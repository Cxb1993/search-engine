?????Abstract? 
 
Nanoscale metallic particle technology is an innovation based upon the conventional zero-valent 
iron method for groundwater remediation. This is a two-year research project. The objective of 
this project is to investigate the feasibility of using nanoscale metallic particle-based permeable 
reactive barrier for in-situ remediation and evaluate design parameters. The major focuses of this 
research are to optimize the synthetic method of nanoparticles, develop a prototype module for 
nanoparticle production, and test the stabilization of nanoparticles using various stabilizers such 
as surfactants, and starch. 
 
Through the first year study, several key findings are reported. It was found that the use of FeSO4 
as a precursor formed a smaller particle size of iron nanoparticles than that of FeCl2 and FeCl3. 
The use Ti (III)-citrate complex as a reductant can not produce zero-valent iron; instead, it readily 
produced zero-valent copper and lead. PAA and biopolymers can serve as a good dispersive 
reagent for stabilization nanoparticles in the aqueous solution. Palladium is a good catalyst and 
the cost-effective dosage of palladium would be 0.1wt%. 
 
In the second year, a 40-m2 pilot-scale field study was conducted to investigate the use of 
palladized nanoscale zero-valent iron (NZVI) for remediation of groundwater contaminated with 
a variety of chlorinated organic solvents including vinyl chloride (VC), dichloroethanes and 
dichloroethylenes in southern Taiwan. Major contaminant is VC that has a concentration ranging 
from 10 to 5000 µg/L. The concentration distribution is depth-dependent at the site where 
contaminant concentrations increased with depth. A total iron mass of about 20 kg on-site 
synthesized NZVI (Pd 0.05 wt%) suspended in 8,500 L water was injected via gravity into the 
sandy aquifer. Twelve multi-level monitoring wells allowing to collect samples from three 
different depths (6, 12, 18 m) were installed. For a monitoring period of 2 months, a spatial and 
temporal decrease in VC concentrations was observed. VC concentrations in upper and medium 
levels were relatively lower (< 100 µg/L) than those in the bottom level (>1000 µg/L). The 
degradation efficiency was greater than 90% at both upper and medium levels but was about 
20-85% at the bottom level. Oxidation-reduction potential (ORP) measurements indicated a 
homogeneous reducing condition (ORP -450 ~ -280 mV) was achieved in the testing field. 
Analysis of total iron concentrations found iron was mainly trapped at the upper level. 
 
Keywords: Nanotechnology, Nanoscale bimetallic particles, Groundwater remediation, 
Permeable reactive barrier, Zero-valent iron, Chlorinated organic solvent, Stabilization, 
Stabilizer

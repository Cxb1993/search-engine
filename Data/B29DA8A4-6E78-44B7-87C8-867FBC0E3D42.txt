on AISI 304 Stainless Steel: Effect of Film Thickness

Abstract 
The aim of this work was to investigate effects of film thickness on   the single layered zirconium oxynitride 
(Zr(N,O)) films deposited on AISI 304 stainless steel substrates using hollow cathode discharge ion-plating system 
(HCD-IP) in an argon-oxygen-nitrogen atmosphere. Three phases (ZrN, Zr2ON2 and m-ZrO2) in the films could be 
identified by XRD and XPS results. The amount of oxide and/or oxynitride phases changed with the different film 
thickness for the Zr(N,O) films. The residual stress of Zr(N,O) films was relieved with increasing oxygen flow rate 
forming oxide in the film, but no specific trends were correlated with the film thickness. 
 
Keyword：Zr(N,O) films、HCD-IP、thickness 
 
 
1. Introduction 
 
Surface coating is an effective method to improve 
mechanical properties and corrosion resistance of the 
substrate material in the aggressive environment. 
Zirconium nitride (ZrN) thin films is usually used in 
decorative applications because of its remarkable 
properties, such as high hardness, wear-resistance, 
corrosion resistance, thermal stability, and golden color. 
Transition metal oxynitride (TMeNxOy) has been 
attracting attention for decorative applications because 
its superior properties such as various colorations, 
acceptable hardness, absorbing layers and wide 
resistivity ranges, lead to a wide variation of multiple 
applications [1-5]. Zirconium oxynitride (ZrNxOy) films, 
is a kind of most common TMeNxOy films with an 
intrinsic color which can vary with the oxygen content 
are good candidates for decorative application [3], [4], 
[6]. The thin films with different thicknesses usually 
have the different structure, mechanical properties, 
corrosion behavior, and colorations [7-13]. 
The main purpose of this work is to investigate the 
effect of film thickness on the composition, structures, 
mechanical properties, color variation and corrosion 
resistance of the ZrNxOy thin films deposited on AISI 
304 stainless steel using an hollow cathode discharge 
ion-plating system (HCD-IP). 
 
2. Experimental Methods 
 
2.1 Specimen Preparation and Coating Process 
 
The substrate material used in this study was 
commercial AISI 304 stainless steel, which has a 
composition of 18.57% Cr, 8.37% Ni, 1.18% Mn, 0.44% 
Si, 0.14% Co, 0.1% Cu, and the balance being Fe. 
Before deposition, the substrates were undergone 
ultrasonically cleaning in acetone and ethanol 
progressively for 5 min respectively, and then dried in 
blowing N2 gas. The deposition process was carried out 
in an HCD-IP system evacuated to a base pressure 8 × 
10-4 Pa (6 × 10-6 torr) to prevent the contamination. 
Prior to deposition, the substrate surface was 
bombarded by Ar ions for 10 minutes at Ar (99.9995%) 
partial pressure of 13.3 Pa (0.1 torr) and the substrate 
bias of -250 V. The gun power was maintained at 6 kW, 
and Zr source (99.2 % in purity) was evaporated by the 
electron beam from the hollow cathode tube. A negative 
bias voltage of 60 V was applied onto the substrate. The 
temperature was fixed at 450  during coating. The ℃
working gas consisted of 10.4 sccm of Ar, 17.5 sccm of 
N2 (99.9995%), and 4 levels of O2 (99.99995% in purity) 
flow rate (0, 2, 5, 8 sccm) was chosen. The total pressure 
was ranged from 1.7 × 10-1 to 2.1 × 10-1 Pa (1.3 × 10-3 to 
1.6 × 10-3 torr). The deposition time increased 15 
minutes per step from 15 to 60 minute for each level of 
O2 flow rate in order to prepare specimens of different 
thickness of films.

The evolution of all the ZrN (220) residual stress of 
Zr(N,O) films as a function of the film thickness is

3.3 Residual Stress of Substrates 
 
Fig. 3 shows the variation of the compressive residual 
stress of the substrate for pure ZrN samples versus the 
film thickness. This result may be explained by 
considering that accumulation of the force which comes 
from the compressive residual stress of the thin film 
increasing with the increases of film thickness. Simply 
stated, the thicker film possessed a higher force to 
deform the substrate than the thinner one, it also 
contributes to the higher compressive stress of the 
substrate. 
The compressive stress of the substrates for samples is 
ranged from 1.4 to 3.4GPa, and the yield stress of AISI 
304 stainless steel is about 0.5GPa. If the stress applied 
on the material is higher than the yield stress of the 
material, the material will be yielded to deformation for 
the relaxation of stress. It could be explained that the 
relaxation of stress near the interface restricted the thin 
film deposited on the substrate, resulting in the higher 
stress of substrate which is higher than the yield stress to 
be measured.

3.4 Corrosion Resistance 
 
The results of potentiodynamic polarization scan of 
Zr(N,O) thin films is listed in Table 2. It can be seen that 
the Icorr of the deposited samples are lower than the Icorr 
of bare 304SS. In other words, the corrosion resistance 
of 304SS is improved by coating Zr(N,O) thin films. 
As the results of potentiodynamic polarization scan in 
1N H2SO4 + 0.05M KSCN solution as shown in Fig. 4, it 
can be noted that there are no specific trend which is the 
variation of Icorr with the film thickness. In Fig. 4 (a), the 
Icorr decreases rapidly with increase of packing density 
then reaches a constant value for pure ZrN films, which 
can be explained by the reason that the corrosion 
protection is improved substantially when the packing 
density exceeds a critical value. In the other samples 
shown in Fig. 4(b), (c), and (d), the Icorr decreased with 
increasing packing density, too. 
It can be concluded that the packing density plays a 
significant role compared with the effect of film 
thickness in corrosion resistance by Zr(N,O) thin films 
coatings.

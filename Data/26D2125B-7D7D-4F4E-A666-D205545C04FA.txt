1. Introduction 
The function of an artificial joint is to restore smooth articulation 
between the bones of the joint. The total hip arthroplasty consists of a 
femoral component with a head and stem and an acetabular component 
with a metal backing and acetabular cup. Medical grade ultra high 
molecular polyethylene (UHMWPE) that articulates well against alloy 
(typically Co-Cr-Mo) is generally used in total joint replacements. The 
clinical performance of the cup mode of UHMWPE is well accepted 
except for its wear behaviour.  
Over the years, the improvement of the wear resistance of 
UHMWPE has not met the expectation [1]. Thus the current trend is to 
replace the weaker component UHMWPE, with a relatively harder 
counter-face. That results in the new generation of total joint 
replacements with metal-on-metal (MOM) sliding pairs, in which 
 6 
DLC coated Ti-6Al-4V sliding against UHMWPE [7, 8]. 
The investigation of the wear of biomaterials conducted by Kim et al. 
[9] showed that the DLC coating dramatically improved the wear 
performance of Ti and Ti-6Al-4V, and protected the substrates from 
corrosion and wear. Sheeja et al. [10] reveal that the tribological 
behaviour of uncoated Co-Cr-Mo and DLC coated Co-Cr-Mo sliding 
pairs clearly shows that the friction of the Co-Cr-Mo sliding pair can be 
significantly reduced by the DLC coating on the sliding surface. The wear 
depth and the roughness of the transfer layer prove that the un-coated 
sliding pairs exhibit a larger wear than that of the DLC coating.          
 The present work is to investigate tribological behavior of DLC 
coated on Co-Cr-Mo and Ti-6Al-4V alloy disks against Co-Cr-Mo rings. 
The characteristics of DLC of different thickness were measured using 
atomic force microscopy (AFM) and nano-indentor. Wear test was 
conducted to show the effect of DLC on tribological behavior of different 
alloy against Co-Cr-Mo ring.

2.1 DLC coating and specimen preparation 
The deposition of DLC was conducted in un-balanced magnetron

3. Results and discussion 
3.1 Raman spectroscopy analyses 
In general, the D peak and G peak of DLC are often located at about 
1360 cm-1 and 1550 cm-1. The DLC film has a mixture of graphite (sp2) 
and diamond (sp3). The increase in ID/IG ratio indicates the degree of 
graphitization of the DLC. As the intensity ratio (ID/IG) of the Raman 
spectra increases, the percentage of sp2 bonds increases.  
Figs.3-4 representatively shows the variation of the DLC structure 
with the coating thickness of sample C1 and T1 (referring to Table 2 for 
symbol meaning) from Raman spectroscopy. The Raman intensity ratio 
ID/ IG is obtained by fitting two Gaussian curves and a linear background 
in the region of 900 cm-1 - 2000 cm-1. Table 2 lists the average ID/ IG value 
of the different coating thickness of DLC. It indicates that thicker DLC 
coating thickness leads to greater ID/ IG value and thereby the bigger 
percentage of graphite (sp2). 
3.2 Observations of the surface of DLC 
 12 
general, the hardness of DLC film on both Co-Cr-Mo and Ti-6Al-4V is 
decreasing with increasing thickness, except for the 0.6ö÷m DLC on 
Ti-6Al-4V. It indicates that the degree of graphitization of the DLC, i.e. 
ID/ IG value, results in the decrease of hardness of DLC on the substrates. 
The higher hardness of the present coating has, the higher ratio of sp3/ sp2 
of the coating has. The graphite could be a good solid lubricants for wear 
resistance.  
3.4 Wear test 
Figs. 7-10 show the typical variation of friction with sliding time on 
ring-on-disk tester representatively for uncoated Co-Cr-Mo sample, 
sample C1, uncoated Ti-6Al-4V sample and sample T1(referring to Table 
2 for symbol meaning). The duplicate tests of specimen C1-1 and C1-2 in 
Fig. 8, and T1-1 and T1-2 in Fig. 10 show the well repeatability of 
coefficient of friction especially at the stable stages. Their friction 
coefficients obtained from wear tests are listed in Table 5. Because the 
degree of graphitization of DLC is increasing with increasing thickness, 
the friction coefficients of samples with DLC on both Co-Cr-Mo and 
Ti-6Al-4V are decreasing with increasing thickness of DLC. DLC film of 
1.2 µm thickness coated on either of Co-Cr-Mo or Ti-6Al-4V disk against 
 14 
a role of lubricant for sliding wear after 4000 s, as well as the original 
DLC did before that time. 
Weight loss is used to determine the amount of wear. Fig.11 presents 
the weight losses of the samples measured after 14,400 cycles. The 
percentage of the weight loss of the DLC coated Co-Cr-Mo disks that ran 
against Co-Cr-Mo rings were 0.03-0.07%, whereas the wear of DLC 
coated Ti-6Al-4V disks were 0.06-0.1%. The weight lose of the film on 
the Co-Cr-Mo disks is always lower than that on Ti-6Al-4V disk. For the 
same substrate, the thicker coated film tends to result in the lower weight 
losses. Thus increasing DLC thickness can provide better protection and 
less weight losses.
  
4. Conclusion 
This study investigated the tribological behavior of diamond-like 
carbon of different thickness on biomaterial alloy. Measurement of 
morphology, nano-indentation test and wear test of the DLC surfaces 
were conducted. The thickness of DLC films, deposited by UBM 
sputtering, affected their mechanical and wear properties. This study has 
revealed significant differences in the friction coefficient from the six 
 16 
than that of DLC coated Ti-6Al-4V substrate. 
The bearing material such as Co-Cr-Mo against Co-Cr-Mo may offer 
a considerable advantage over the Co-Cr-Mo against Ti-6Al-4V. It may 
be concluded that Co-Cr-Mo offers a superior substrate material for 
application of DLC films with regard to bio-medical application. The use 
of DLC coatings is a very promising technology for bio-medical bearing 
surface. The further development of this process will offer the designers 
of bio-medical implants alternative options in achieving longer implant 
lifetime for patients of the future.

4. Conclusions 
To conclude, the successful demonstration of passive synchronization in our experiment should provide a new 
method to directly generate ultrashort femtosecond pulse trains locked at the same repetition rate from synchronized 
two-color (i.e., 1.03 µm and 1.53 µm) P-APM fiber lasers.

1. Introduction 
Coherent synthesis of separated ultrashort mode-locked lasers has been shown to have many potential applications 
including few-cycle pulse generation, optical frequency metrology, and ultrafast pump probe experiments [1]. One 
of the prerequisites for achieving the coherent synthesis mentioned above is to synchronize the pulse repetition rates 
of two mode-locked lasers. Both the active and passive synchronization have been demonstrated to be able to obtain 
the tight synchronization of two ultrashort mode-locked lasers with sub-fs timing jitter [2-4]. For the passive 
synchronization, one of the noticeable characteristics is the non-limited feedback bandwidth when comparing to the 
active synchronization [4]. The technique based on the active-passive hybrid synchronization has also been 
developed to obtain the sub-fs timing jitter with the feedback bandwidth up to 1 MHz between the two-color 
mode-locked lasers. Recently, similar techniques have also been employed in fiber lasers to achieve the passive 
synchronization or timing stabilization by sharing the laser cavities or by injecting the external ultrashort pulse trains 
[5,6]. In this paper, we investigate the possibility to achieve passive synchronization especially between the 
polarization additive pulse mode-locked (P-APM) Er-fiber and Yb-fiber lasers, since the P-APM technique has been 
successfully utilized to generate the shortest pulses both in the Er-fiber and Yb-fiber lasers [7].  
2. P-APM Yb-fiber and Er-fiber lasers  
As shown in Fig. 1, the experimental setup consists of two P-APM fiber lasers. The top and the bottom of Fig. 1 are 
the P-APM Yb-fiber and Er-fiber lasers respectively. The P-APM mechanism is achieved by the nonlinear 
polarization rotation followed by a polarized device, such as the polarization-dependent isolator (PD-ISO) or the 
polarization beam splitter (PBS) shown in Fig. 1. The two mode-locked lasers are pumped individually by the two 
976 nm laser diodes and both can be independently self-started to become mode-locked. The cavity dispersion 
compensation in the P-APM Er-fiber is made by using the Er-fiber with normal dispersion, and in the P-APM 
Yb-fiber the cavity dispersion is controlled by the bulk grating pairs. Without the passive synchronization, the black 
curves in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 show the optical spectra of the P-APM Yb-fiber and Er-fiber laser. The FWHM 
bandwidths of the Yb-fiber and Er-fiber lasers are 6.8 nm and 6 nm respectively. The output power from the 10% 
fiber coupler in the Er-fiber laser is 150 μW with the pump power of 200 mW. For the P-APM Yb-fiber laser, the 
output power from the rejection port of the PBS is 50 mW when the pump power is 300 mW. 1% of the power in the 
Yb-fiber laser cavity is measured to be 23 μW by using the 1% fiber coupler (not shown in Fig. 1) spliced to the 
polarization independent isolator (PI-ISO).  
3. Experimental results of the passive synchronization  
In our experiments the passive synchronization can be achieved as follows. Initially the P-APM Yb-fiber and 
Er-fiber lasers are mode-locked separately. The cavity length of the Yb-fiber laser is detuned to match the pulse 
repetition rate of the Er-fiber laser by moving the stage where the fiber collimator is mounted on. However, the 
pulses of Er-fiber laser start to become unstable when the two pulse repetition rates approach close. This indicates 
that although the interaction of two pulses by cross phase modulation is necessary for the passive synchronization, it 
will also affect the individual fiber laser such that the original P-APM can not maintain stably. Therefore additional 
fine adjustment of the polarization controllers is needed to achieve the stable P-APM and passive synchronization.

This project is to develop a smart 3D body motion process system. A set of four 
optical 3D position locators is developed. A central communication hub controls the 
four slave units and communicates with the LED marker driver wirelessly. The joint 
angles of the body are computed from the captured 3D data from the LED markers. 
A software foundation is developed to encode the body motion into image format.

motion code to any humanoid for replaying the motion task in real time. The 
encoded motion code is the body animation parameter (BAP). In this project, an 
algorithm is developed that combines the BAP (body animation parameters) and 
BDP (body definition parameters) into a vivid body motion display. This system 
provides an automatic pathway for humanoid to perform human’s task. This 
intelligent body motion processor is very useful in the performance enhancement of 
humanoid and in the applications all kind of dynamic human factor designs, physical 
exercise and dancing education, amusement, etc.

Mechatronic Design of an Optical Human Motion Tracking System 
 
Ming J. Tsaia, Yi-Hsun Panb, Han-Wen Jangc, 
 
Department of Mechanical Engineering, National Cheng-Kung University – Tainan, Taiwan 
a Professor, E-mail: mjtsai@mail.ncku.edu.tw 
b Graduate students, ihp14@ra.me.ncku.edu.tw 
c Graduate students, ken24win@ra.me.ncku.edu.tw 
URL: http://ra.me.ncku.edu.tw 
 
 
 
Abstract. Mechatronic design of an optical human motion tracking system is introduced. 
This system consists of 4 optical 3D tracking modules that are placed around an actor. A hub 
controls the data flow from the trackers to a PC. A central communication unit (CCU) on the 
hub generates synchronous signal to the trackers and LED driver. Each tracker has three 
linear CCD cameras to sense infrared emitted by LED markers that are placed on the human 
body. The location of the marker is computed by three planes formed by the peak position of 
the CCD illuminated by the LED marker and the lens axis. A DSP on the CCD board controls 
the exposure of the linear CCD and performs image processing to find the peak position at 
sub-pixel accuracy. Experiments has been setup to verify the feasibility of this system 
 
Keywords. Motion Tracker, Circuit Design, CPLD, LED Driver, CCD module 
 
 
1. Introduction 
 
Human motion is a characteristic behavior and is an 
intellectual property of the human. Human motion 
can be captured by many kinds of motion tracking 
system. The applications of motion tracker are in 
various aspects such as amusement/entertainment 
industry, ergonomic industry, and in medical, 
dancing, and gymnastic trainings, etc. In amusement 
industry, motion trackers capture vivid human 
motions. The motions are then pasted to virtual 
computer models to mimic human like movements. 
Researchers used motion trackers to study gait 
locomotion and analyze the bio-mechanic behavior of 
the human motion. The applications of motion 
tracking systems are constantly increasing. The 
potential applications are recording precious 
performance art, training and correcting movements 
of athletes or dancers with 3D visual analyzed motion 
feedback in real time, automatically copy human 
motions to humanoid for mimic shows. By then, 
guiding a tele-operated robot to perform surgery in a 
remote area or doing critical tasks in a hazardous 
environment by a humanoid instead of by a skilful 
worker is not just a dream.  
Studies on motion tracking systems are currently 
very popular. Three types of motion tracking system 
are available in the market. They are visual marker 
based, non-visual based, and mechanical tracking 
systems. Each of them can be categorized into details 
as shown in Fig. 1. Among them, the marker based 
optical tracking systems are popular in the current 
market due to the convenient of usage.

Three circuit boards are designed to capture image. 
These are central control unit, LED driver board, and 
CCD board. Each circuit board has unique function. 
In the beginning, a PC gives a “start” command to the 
system. Then the CCU board in a hub that is 
connected to the PC will generate a strobe signal 
every 1.25 ms. The LED driver received the signal 
and lights the LED one by one each time. In the mean 
time, the CPLD (complex programmable logic device) 
on each slave board receives the signal and 
distributes it to the three CCD boards. The DSP 
(digital signal processor) on each CCD board controls 
the exposure of the CCD accordingly. The DSP 
receives the image and find the peak position of the 
image. The peak position is then sent to a FIFO (first-
in, first-out) memory in the CCU, as shown in Fig. 5.  
After a frame period, the PC fetches the peak values 
via the USB port that is connected by the hub. A 
frame is defined as the number of markers that are 
placed on the human body. In this system, the 
maximum number of markers is 32, and the period of 
a frame is 40ms.

3.1 Central control unit  
Central Control Unit (CCU) is the control center of 
each tracking module. The main function of the CCU 
is to communicate with all the components of the 
system, including with the PC. Since the light emitted 
from the LED marker should be captured by the CCD 
simultaneously, and each LED just light up only 1.2 
ms, the synchronization of the system is very 
important. The communicate pivot is fulfilled by a 
CPLD. All the timing signals are generated and the 
flows of the signals are controlled by the CPLD.  
To connect with a PC, USB (Universal Serial Bus) 
interface is used. The advantages of using USB are 
that it can be simply plug and play, and it is 
commonly support by the PC industry. The USB chip 
also includes a general programmable interface 
(GPIF) (Cypress, 2002, 2003). The interface let the 
developers to generate codes efficiently. A FIFO 
(First In First Out) memory is connected to the USB 
using GPIF. The FIFO stores data by the CPLD from 
DSP chips and will be sent to the PC via the USB. A 
built in 8051 microprocessor can be used for local 
computation. Detail of the design was conducted by 
Lin (Lin, 2004, Kuo, 2006, Chen 2007). The original 
design was for peer-to-peer devices. Since we have 
four modules to connect with a PC simultaneous, the 
design was modified and a new hub-slave 
configuration was proposed. In the new configuration, 
the modifications are as follows:  
1.  Adding differential transfer IC: Since the hub-
slave architecture should use high frequency 
synchronized signal (2 MHz), single end transfer 
cannot maintain the quality of the signals.  
2. Reducing the number of FIFO: Originally, there 
are 3 FIFOs in the CCU, and has no enough pins 
to accommodate differential transfer IC for 4 
slave modules.  Since one FIFO is plenty enough 
for all the image data, we reduced the number of 
FIFO from 3 to one. Then we gained 37 more pins 
for controlling the differential I/O.  
3. Adding a power supply circuit: The system runs 
in 3.3V nominally. However, the power was 
supplied externally. For single module, there is no 
problem. But for hub-slave architecture, we need

4. Software coding 
 
The software development for the embedded system 
is troublesome comparing to develop software in pure 
PC environment. Especially for this motion tracking 
system, three circuit boards are designed, and three 
kinds of IC chip are embedded. In order to let the 
system work, the firmware for corresponding IC chip 
should also be implemented. The DSP chip in the 
CCD board uses C as the developing language named 
Code Composer (TI, 2001). The CPLD in CCU and 
LED driver board uses VHDL (very high definition 
logic language) (Tang, 2002). A cross compiler in PC 
is available, named Quartus II (Altera, 2009); the 
USB chip in the CCU uses GPIF. Since the 
debugging is performed in PC, and the execution is 
conducted in the circuit board. It is difficult to debug 
the firmware codes since the problem may be caused 
by hardware as well as software. Besides, several 
different sets of firmware should be kept for different 
purpose, such as for debugging, for run time, 
calibration, or single function testing, etc. Including 
the PC side consol software, a total of more then 20 
set of codes should be maintained. Most of the 
firmware codes are written to control the signal flow. 
Main efforts are put on the synchronization of the 
events. Efficient computation of image peak location 
is the most demanding task for the DSP chip. 
 
 
4.1 Software for the CCD board 
In the CCD board, the DSP control the line camera. 
The tasks are: 
1. Signal handshaking.  
2. Generating signals for the camera. The CCD is 
controlled by two signals:ψCLK andψROG. The 
former is a timer and the latter is for exposure. 
3. Controlling the A/D conversion. 
4. Image processing: To reduce the data size and 
increase the efficient in real time tracking, only 
the peak position in every captured image is 
transmitted back to the PC. The computation of 
the peak position should be fulfilled in very short 
time, since the DSP should take care of the next 
exposure on time. Besides, for higher accuracy, 
the peak position should be accurate to sub-pixel 
level.  
 
4.2 Sub-pixel computation algorithm 
Ordinary image process method can only find peak 
position in pixel level. Sub-pixel algorithm uses 
certain mathematical function to fit the peak area, 
after differentiate, the peak value can be found 
between two pixels. The advantage of using sub-pixel 
algorithm is to increase the accuracy of the peak 
position. Our goal is to find the peak exposure 
location in the image beyond the resolution of the 
sensor. However, different sub-pixel algorithm gives 
different results.  Four of the algorithms have been 
investigated: 
1. Parabolic fitting: This is the simplest way to find 
peak position in sub-pixel. Firstly, find the peak 
pixel by search the greatest gray scale (intensity). 
Then select two pixels in the left and right of the 
point. Putting the three points into a parabolic 
function:

5. System validation 
 
To validate the feasibility of the system that can 
simultaneously capture the LED markers by multiple 
slave tracking modules, an experiment has been 
conducted, as shown in Fig. 9. Two motion tracking 
modules are connected to a hub. The hub is 
connected to a USB socket in a PC notebook. A 
calibration tower with 16 LEDs is placed at 45° slant 
angle in front of the tracking modules. When PC 
gives a start command, the hub generates a 40 Hz 
ROG signals to the slave modules via SPI interface. 
The signal also transmitted to the LED driver via RF 
module. The system synchronization architecture 
within those devices is shown in Fig. 10.  The peak 
positions computed by the DSP are transferred back 
to the PC via the USB port. Then the 16 LEDs will 
light one by one in according to the ROG signal 
frequency. In the mean time, the CPLD on the slave 
CCU board receives the ROG signal; it passes the 
clock to the DSP for controlling the CCD image 
capturing. The captured result is shown in Fig. 11. 
The peak data for the LED markers are in right order 
without any losing data or order. It can be concluded 
that the system is working, and it is ready for human 
body capturing.

6. Conclusion and discussion 
 
A low cost optoelectronic human motion tracking 
system has been successfully built. The system 
consists of one hub, four slave tracking modules, and 
one LED driver unit. Four tracking modules that are 
placed around a human body can increase the 
viewing range to minimize the light occlusion 
problem. The labelling problem is solved by lighting 
the LED markers one by one synchronously. The 
synchronization of the LED markers is done via RF 
signal that reduces the impediment to human motion. 
From the test data, some conclusions can be made: 
1. Algorithms that used Sobel mask for pre-
processing gave better results.  
2. If higher the current supply to the LED, the better 
the signal quality, and the higher the s/n ratio of 
the capture image. Therefore, it is suggested to 
provide the driving current to the LED as high as 
the LED can withstand. 
3. The number of markers that can be traced by this 
system is designed to be 32. It may not be enough 
for tracking the whole body motion. To increase 
the number of markers, a higher data rate CCD is 
needed. However, this will reduce the exposure 
time. Then a bigger cell size and higher sensitivity 
CCD should be chosen. 
4. Due to cost consideration, the CCD chip used in 
this system has only 2048 pixels. To increase the 
resolution, higher resolution CCD can be used.  
5. To reduce the interference of ambient light, 
IrLED with weave length at 890nm is used. Since 
the best specular response of the CCD chip is not 
at the this range. The tracking range of the 
tracking module is limited within 2-3 meters. To 
increase the tracking range, a more suitable CCD 
must be selected.

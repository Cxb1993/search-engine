Abstract 
Fully conjugated block copolymers containing electron donating and electron withdrawing phenylenevinylene 
repeating units can be prepared by the sequential ring-opening metathesis polymerization of strained 
cyclophanedienes, initiated by ruthenium carbene complexes (Grubbs metathesis catalysts). The molecular 
weight of the constituent blocks can be tightly controlled by changing the monomer to catalyst ratio and the 
volume fraction of the block copolymers independently tailored by the ratio of the monomers employed. 
Extensive phase separation between the constituent blocks is observed in thin films of these polymers by 
atomic force microscopy and efficient disassociation of excitons between two blocks can be seen in the 
photoluminescence quenching of these materials. 
 
Keywords: block copolymers, electron donating phenylenevinylenes, electron withdrawing 
phenylenevinylenes, cyclophanedienes, ring-opening metathesis polymerization, phase separation.

The optical band gap of the polymers was estimated from the onset of the absorption spectra. The electronic 
properties of homopolymers and block copolymers (Figure 4) were examined by cyclic voltammetry (CV). 
Thin films of the polymers were deposited on a platinum plate and cyclic voltammograms were recorded in an 
acetonitrile solution of [(n-C4H9)4N]PF6 (0.10 M). Films of polymers all exhibited reversible oxidation peaks 
and reversible reduction peaks with a colour change in the film during oxidation and reduction. The energy 
levels of the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital 
(LUMO) for the polymers were estimated from the onset of the oxidation and reduction peaks, respectively. 
The HOMO level of block copolymers is quite similar with their corresponding donor homopolymers and the 
LUMO level is located between two individual donor and acceptor homopolymers.

The research project involved the preparation of soluble poly(p-phenylenevinylenes) 
homopolymers and block copolymers of well-defined molecular weight by ring-opening 
metathesis polymerization of the strained cyclophanedienes. Block copolymer of this type have 
not been previously reported and they are of interest to scientists and engineers as they have the 
ideal microstructure for optimal efficiencies in polymeric light emitting diodes and organic 
photovoltaics devices and little of the defects was found in phenylenevinylenes polymers. These 
polymers will be of tremendous interest to those chemists and physicists interested in organic 
photovoltaics and polymer chemists in general.

II. Abstract 
 
Gait or motion analysis has been used widely in the diagnosis of patients with locomotor 
pathology and the subsequent planning and assessment of treatment.  The application to 
patients with slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) has been very limited.  No study 
has been reported on the gait analysis of patients with SCFE, nor has the outcome of 
surgical treatment in terms of their performance during functional activities other than level 
walking. 
 
The main purpose of the project was to evaluate the performance of Southwick osteotomy 
in treating patients with slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) using biomechanical 
techniques including gait analysis and computer simulation. 
  
In the project, a computer model of the human locomotor system that is capable of 
including the hip joint pathological conditions was developed from an existing model for 
the evaluation of normal and pathological gait.  Motion analysis experiments, including 
level walking, stair-climbing and chair-rising, using ultra-high resolution 
stereophotogrammetry system and forceplates were performed on SCFE patients and 
matched normal group.  The kinematics of the hip and ankle joints and the knee moments 
were found to be significantly different between the two groups during sit-to-stand and 
stair-descending while both kinematics and kinetics during gait were found to be indifferent.  
This suggests that future evaluation of SCFE patients should include sit-to-stand and 
stair-descending movements.  Further force and stress analysis studies are needed for a 
more detailed analysis of level walking. 
 
Keywords: slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE), gait analysis, computer simulation

V. Literature Review 
Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) is a gradual disruption of the proximal femoral 
capital physeal and was described by Muller in 1888 firstly. SCFE was highly occurred in the 
adolescent teenager, corresponding to the average ages of 11~14 years in male and 10~12 
years in female [1-3]. The report incidence of SCFE is 2~3.4 cases per 100’000. SCFE is a 
male predominance and the percentage in boys is about 2.5 times to girls [4-5]. Not only 
overweight is one of the greatest risks in children but the slips are associated with abnormal 
endocrine disease, i.e. hypothyroidism and hypopituiitarism [6].  
 
Clinical categories could be divided into 3 stage, chronic, acute and acute-on-chronic slip 
according to the presentation and duration of the symptoms. Symptoms appears less than 
three weeks is defined as the acute slips, while symptoms appears longer than three weeks is 
deinfe asthe chronic slips. The acute-on-chronic slip is associated with a sudden increase in 
pain preceded by a period of lesser symptoms. Chronic slip is the most common presentation 
(60%) in SCFE patients. Otherwise, Southwick’s method is used to quantified slip severity. 
Fisrt draw a line to be perpendicular to another line connecting superior and inferior margins 
of the capital epiphysis. Then, a line perpendicular to the line connecting these two points is 
drawn to intersect a line parallel to the femoral shaft. The angle of this intersection represents 
the head-shaft angle. Mild grade is corresponding to the angle less than 30o.  Moderate 
grade is corresponding to the angle between 30 o ~60 o .  Severe grade is corresponding to 
the angle greater than 60 o.  
 
The symptoms of chronic slips include aching in the hip or groin and referred pain to the 
thigh or knee. Besides, the limitation of the range of motion (ROM) of patient’s hip joint is 
the important clinical feature. The affected hip joint will lose internal rotation. The pathologic 
sign of SCFE is that hip tends to external rotation while hip flex. Radiographs should include 
an Anterior-posterior view and a flog leg lateral view. The level of slips isn’t easy to assess 
via the AP view radiograph, so a flog leg lateral view may increase the degree of 
displacement when the hip joint is unstable. Therefore, SCFE patients have abnormal 
movement pattern while ambulation, up-and-down stairs and sit-to-stand due to the limitation 
of the hip joint angle.

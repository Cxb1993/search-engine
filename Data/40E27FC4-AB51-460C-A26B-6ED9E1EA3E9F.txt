ABSTRACT 
Identification the topographic characteristics of debris flow origination zone 
 
There are 1,420 streams tendency to initiate debris flow in Taiwan since 2002 till 
now. The potential analysis classified the streams into three categories for high, 
middle and low potential to initiate debris flow according to the topology, geology 
and slope. In recent years, the magnitude of debris flow and landslide hazards has the 
tendency to increase, especially in the off-track mountain area. These slopeland 
related calamities can be attributed to topographic and geologic factors besides 
rainfall factor. However, the long-term (since mid-19th century) Russian experience 
of debris flow research shows that only some of the basins with certain morphometric 
parameters can produce debris flows. The triggering of debris flow are found also 
relates to topographic slope in the source zone and within certain curvature. 
Investigate the characteristics of the initiation zone and the starting points of debris 
flow are important for the potential analysis, prediction and hazard scenario. For the 
sack of related studies and long-term satellite monitoring in domestic, an important 
result of the study is expected in this project. 
This project is to establish such a utilization of aerial imagery. We asses the 
potential uses of the satellite and aerial imagery for monitoring immediate and 
long-term consequences of debris flow disasters, and compare it against the aerial 
imagery and ground surveys. It is expected to determine possible starting points of 
debris flows in channel and evaluate geomorphic conditions to initiate recurrent of 
debris flows. Chenyoulan river watershed in Nantou County, middle Taiwan, is 
selected as the pilot area for the recurrent of debris flows in the watershed since 
typhoon Herb in 1996. The pilot areas in the watershed for study include: Herser No. 
1, Herser No. 3, Chushui, Fongchu, Nanpingkeng, Juinkeng, Erbu, Sanbu, and Tongfu 
stream nine watersheds.

Abstract 
The paper herein studies the correlation of rainfall distribution and initiated landslides in Taiwan 
using statistics of rain gauge data and historical landslides in recent years. Climate changes initiating 
landslides and debris flows are discussed. In review of the landslide characteristics and rainfall 
distribution in Taiwan, it is found that these were affected by the track of typhoon, the following active 
air currents, topographic characteristics and seismic effects, in addition to the fragile geologic 
conditions. 
 
Keywords: landslide, debris flow, topographic effect, climate change 
 
Introduction 
An average of 3.5 typhoons invades Taiwan every year. These typhoons have initiated numerous 
landslides and subsequently caused hazardous debris flows in recent years. These hazardous events 
include the impact of Typhoon Toraji in 2001 (Cheng et al., 2004), Typhoon Mindulle and the 
following storms in 2004 (Chen & Petley, 2005), and Typhoon Aere and Haitang in 2005 (Chen et al., 
2006a). Among these, Typhoon Toraji caused 596 landslides, and Typhoon Mindulle triggered 907 
landslides. These landslides were mainly distributed in the middle of Taiwan in mountainous areas. 
This shows a trend for landslide hazards and climate changes. 
This study collected historical rainfall and landslide data in recent years in Taiwan to clarify the 
trends of the rainfall distribution and the initiation of landslides. The historical of distribution 
landslides and the tracks of Typhoons are discussed in terms of climate changes and topographic effects. 
The correlation between of typhoon rainfall distribution and the initiation of landslides is emphasized. 
 
Landslide Hazards and Economic Losses 
According to the Statistics of the National Disaster Prevention and Reduction Center in Taiwan, 
some severe typhoon rainfall events induced human life losses including: 
‧ Typhoon Herb in 1996: 73 dead/missing and 463 injured. 
‧ Typhoon Xangsane in 2000: 89 dead/missing and 5 injured. 
‧ Typhoon Trami and Toraji in 2001: 219 dead/missing and 192 injured. 
‧ Typhoon Nari in 2001: 104 dead/missing and 265 injured.

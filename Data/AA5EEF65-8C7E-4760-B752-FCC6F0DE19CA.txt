II 
 
摘要 
本研究使用間苯二酚-甲醛(Resorcinol- Formaldehyde, RF)為碳前趨物，製備高比表面積活性碳材，
探討於不同環境濕度(0-90%RH)下，對二氧化碳混合氣體吸附效率的影響。BET結果顯示RF活性碳具
有高比表面積2104 m2/g、平均孔洞約2.6 nm及孔體積約0.214 cm3/g。由SEM得知此碳材為片狀孔洞結
構。為使碳材具有氨官能基，進一步使用氨氣(NH3)對活性碳進行表面氨基化(amination)，表面化學改
質前先使用兩種硝酸溶液(0.1 M, 0.3 M)酸洗，於不同氨氣持溫溫度(600 °C, 700 °C, 800 °C)下，氨基化2
小時。由BET結果得知，隨酸濃度提高，氨基化會加速表面反應，使微孔面積明顯增加。隨溫度提高
時結構主體性破裂不連續，導致比表面積的下降。經由FTIR分析得知氨基化能有效接枝胺類官能基團
於活性碳表面，出現1565 cm-1的NH2官能基。在二氧化碳動態吸附結果顯示RF於50 oC模擬氣體(CO2:N2
＝17%:83%)下對二氧化碳吸附量可達6.5 mmol/g (dry base)。未改質碳材於90%RH濕度下因為水的競爭
吸附，對二氧化碳吸附量由6.5 mmol/g降至4.7 mmol/g；而氨基化後的碳材於90%RH濕度下，因為水參
與吸附反應，使胺類官能基團發揮作用對二氧化碳吸附量仍可達6.6 mmol/g。藉由高比表面積的物理快
速吸附，再擴散至胺類官能基團捕捉提供化學改質吸附，兩者相互配合可達到快速吸附且大吸附量的
協同作用。

一、前言 
活性碳具有高的比表面積且可製作活性碳的含碳材料非常廣泛，製程穩定且簡易，普遍
運用於廢水及空氣污染防治上，為可帶起經濟的有效吸附劑，且可再生利用，為非常環保的
物質。活性碳大致上可分為 4 類，(1)粉狀、(2)粒狀、(3)圓柱狀及(4)纖維狀等形狀之活性碳。
活性碳的高比表面積主要為佔活性碳較多數的的微孔( < 2 nm)所造成，Ryu et al.指出，由於存
在於活性碳內部的孔洞多屬於微孔，且孔洞的形態為片狀，因此，使得活性碳具有降低氣體
擴散阻力、快速吸脫附速率及高吸附容量等特點，而適合作為污染物之吸附劑、觸媒及觸媒
載體等都具有潛在的應用價值，近年來引起廣泛注意[1]。且 Auer et al.表示活性碳則可穩定置
於酸鹼環境中[2]。因此活性碳是目前工業應用最廣的載體。我們已知二氧化碳屬於路易士酸
(Lewis Acid)。因此，預計將路易士鹼(Lewis bases)經由改質到活性炭的表面，可能有利於捕
捉二氧化碳的能力。已有許多研究成功將鹼基改質於活性碳，加強對二氧化碳的吸附能力
[3-6]。雖然氮在碳材上普遍生成較少，但它可以在高溫下被引入到碳的結構中與氨氣反應
[7]。使氨氣在碳表面反應，生成酰胺、腈、吡啶與吡咯等似銨鹽和胺類的官能基[8]。這些官
能基增加活性碳的表面鹼度，可增加捕捉二氧化碳的能力。 
 
二、實驗合成 
研究內容 
本計畫目的為開發可重覆再生且成本低的活性碳吸附材，運用於沼氣中吸附分離
CH4/CO2。研究設計使用原料便宜的酚醛樹脂高分子，製成高比表面積活性碳，利用活性碳
特殊的孔結構，存在大量的微孔和中孔，對CO2進行物理吸附；再利用NH3 氣體對活性碳進
行氨基化(amination)，使活性碳表面帶有鹼性，期望氨基化能增加碳表面的特殊胺類官能基
團對CO2進行化學吸附，藉由物理吸附與化學吸附的協同作用，可高效率地選擇性吸附混合
氣中的CO2。研究過程中使用如表1之FTIR、BET及SEM等儀器分析活性碳氨基化前後，胺基
含量對表面積與物性結構的影響。接著對混合模擬氣(17%CO2/83% N2)，進行動態吸附實驗，
瞭解活性碳固定床吸附分離能力；由活性碳氨基化前後的物化性質與吸附性能測試結果，找
出活性碳在沼氣純化應用上最佳製備條件，最後於不同進氣條件下(不同濕度)測試吸附性
能，瞭解環境因子對吸附CO2能力之影響。探討於不同製程下自製二氧化碳吸附材的物理特
性與吸附能力。

染物的立體空間，而孔徑的大小則影響了吸附污染物的選擇性，孔體積和孔徑則決定了吸附
的效率，合適的大小可明顯的幫助污染物的吸附移動與內部擴散的整體效率。 
 
建議 
由此計畫得知，純粹以物理性質吸附是無法處理不同環境條件下的二氧化碳，表面化學
改質需了解其本身特性才可發揮出應有效果，分別發揮物理及化學兩種吸附物質的優點，藉
由高比表面積的物理快速吸附，再擴散至胺類官能基團捕捉提供化學改質吸附，兩者相互配
合才可達到快速吸附且大吸附量的吸附材。

2. Experimental procedure
2.1. Materials
PAN (Mw 250,000) was provided by Tonghwa Synthetic Fiber
Co., Ltd. (Taiwan). N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMAc) and KOH were
purchased from Sigma–Aldrich Corp.
2.2. Preparation of ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers
Ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers were prepared using a solution blowing
machine (ITRI Material and Chemical Research Laboratories). The
spinning apparatus consisted of a spinneret assembly through
which a polymer solution was fed into a stream of accelerated
air with an air pressure of 1 kg cm2 with a polymer feeding rate
of 2.4 mL min1 from a reservoir using a gear pump. The spinneret
of the solution blowing machine had a width of 10 cm and 128
injection holes (inner diameter: 0.25 mm). PAN powder was ﬁrst
homogeneously dissolved in the N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMAc)
as 17 wt.% solution, which was maintained at 353 K for 3 h with
stirring. A mesh-like plate, a distance of 60 cm between the spin-
neret and the plate, was used as a collector to weave the air-dried
ﬁbers. When the desired amount of ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers had been
collected, the collector was pulled out and distilled DMAc was used
to clean the spinneret assembly.
2.3. Preparation of high-surface-area activated carbon by KOH
activation
Generally, the methods for preparing an activated carbon can be
divided into two categories: physical activation and chemical acti-
vation. Recently, the chemical activationwaswidely used to prepare
high-surface-area activated carbon [13,14]. In the chemical activa-
tion, a raw material is impregnated with activation agents such as
zinc chloride, phosphoric acid and potassium hydroxide, and then
the impregnated material is heat-treated under inert atmosphere.
In this study, the high-surface-area activated carbon was prepared
using a two-step process. The ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers (15 g m2) were
ﬁrst stabilized, soaked in a concentrated KOH solution, over-dried,
and then carbonized. The detailed procedure was as follows:
(i) The ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers were placed in a sealed ceramic
oven and heated to 473 K at a heating rate of 10 K min1
from room temperature. Samples were held at 473 K for
0.5 h and then heated to the deﬁned stabilization tempera-
tures (493, 513, 533, and 553 K, respectively) in an air atmo-
sphere at a heating rate of 2 K min1 and held for 80 min
before cooling. The ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers were under tension
during stabilization.
(ii) The pre-stabilized ﬁbers were impregnated with aqueous
KOH, activating agent, at a weight ratio of KOH/PAN ﬁbers
of 3:1, and subsequently dried at 383 K till constant weight.
The resulting ﬁbers were loaded into the furnace, heated at a
heating rate of 5 K min1 from room temperature until the
carbonization temperature of 1173 K under N2 gas ﬂow
(1.5 L min1), and then held for 30 min. After heat treatment,
the activated material was thoroughly rinsed with deionized
water until the pH of the ﬁltrate was about 6–7.
(iii) Finally the samples were dried at 383 K overnight and
crushed into powders. The samples were classiﬁed according
to the stabilization temperature: AC493, AC513, AC533, and
AC553.
2.4. Characterizations of high-surface-area activated carbon
The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the samples were mea-
sured using an X-ray diffractometer (Rigaku DI-MAX-2500V/PC)
with Cu Ka radiation in the 2h-range from 10 to 50. The morphol-
ogies of pre-stabilized PAN-based ﬁbers and high-surface-area
activated carbon were observed using a ﬁeld-emission scanning
electron microscope (FE-SEM, JEOL JSM-6700F). The global atom
compositions of activated carbon were obtained using elemental
analysis (Elementar Vario EL III); the carbon, hydrogen, and nitro-
gen content of the samples was determined directly, and the oxy-
gen content was obtained by difference. The chemical states of the
nitrogen element of the samples were determined at room temper-
ature using an X-ray photoelectron spectroscope (Kratos Axis Ultra
DLD, Al Ka source). For calibration, the C 1s electron binding en-
ergy was set to 285.0 eV. The surface area and pore volume of
the as-prepared activated carbon samples were determined using
a volumetric sorption analyzer (Micromeritics ASAP 2020). The
activated carbon was degassed at 473 K in a vacuum for a period
of at least 4 h prior to measurements. The nitrogen adsorption/
desorption isotherms were measured over a relative pressure (P/
P0) range of approximately 103 to 0.995. The Brunauer–Em-
mett–Teller (BET) surface area of the activated carbon (SBET) was
calculated from N2 adsorption isotherms with the BET equation
using adsorption data in the P/P0 range of 0.06 to 0.2. The total pore
volumes (Vpore) were estimated from the adsorbed N2 amount at P/
P0 = 0.973. The pore size distribution (PSD) in the micro- and mes-
opore regions was determined by applying density functional the-
ory (DFT) method based on nitrogen adsorption data.
2.5. Gravimetric equilibrium capacity of carbon dioxide
CO2 adsorption capacities were characterized by thermal gravi-
metric analysis (TGA, Perkin–Elmer Pyris 1). Approximately 10 mg
of activated carbon was loaded into a 0.05 cm3 platinum sample
pan used for both adsorption and desorption. Before adsorption,
the activated carbon was ﬁrst pre-heated from room temperature
to 383 K at a heating rate of 10 K min1 under N2 ﬂow
(20 cm3 min1), held isothermally for 60 min to remove any
remaining moisture, and then cooled down to 323 K. Since CO2
emissions are frequently associated with large amounts of nitrogen
for post-combustion separation, a realistic CO2/N2 (about 15:85 v/v)
gas mixture (20 cm3 min1 and 1 Pa total pressure) was employed
for evaluating the adsorption capacity of CO2 at 323 K. For the
desorption of CO2, the sample which adsorbed CO2 was purged in
N2 ﬂow from323 K to 383 K. The adsorption capacitywas calculated
based on the TGA weight gain and reported in terms of mass of CO2
per mass of dry adsorbent, g CO2/100 g carbon (wt.%). To determine
the consistency of the performance of PAN-based carbon, multi-cy-
cle adsorption–desorption studies were carried out at 323 K.
2.6. Dynamic adsorption capacity of carbon dioxide

tubular column of 20 mm i.d. and 120 mm length equipped with
a porous plate located 30 mm from the base of the column, which

through capacity. Since the moisture was contained in a coal-ﬁred
combustion stream, the adsorbent performance measurements
were conducted under the moisture feed mode. In a typical exper-
iment, adsorbents were treated under a nitrogen ﬂow of 100 NmL/
min at 383 K for 1 h, and then cooled to 323 K. To study the effect
of moisture towards the adsorbent capacity, the N2–CO2 gas

3.2.4. XPS spectra of N 1s peak
The high-resolution N 1s spectra of various samples are shown
in Fig. 7. The deconvolution of this peak was conducted using sev-
eral Gaussian curves based on the six forms of nitrogen reported in
the literature: pyridine-type N at 398.4 eV; imines, amines, and

nary-N at 401.1 eV; pyridine N-oxides at 402.8 eV; chemisorbed
nitrogen oxides at 404.5 eV [20,21]. The amount of various N func-
tionalities on the PAN-based carbon was obtained by integrating
the six individual curves, as shown in Table 3. It was found that
with increasing stabilization temperature of the ultra-thin PAN ﬁ-
bers, the surface nitrogen content in activated carbon increased,
passed through a maximum, and then decreased with further in-
crease of the stabilization temperature. AC533 had the greatest
amount of nitrogen species on the surface in the form of quater-
nary-N, pyridine N-oxides, and chemisorbed nitrogen oxides. It
has been reported that the nitrogen atoms of nitrogen-containing
chars could be incorporated into the graphene layers by replacing
carbon atoms, forming more thermostable forms of nitrogen like
pyridine-type N located at the edges of the graphene layers or qua-
ternary-N in the interior [22]. The trend of the nitrogen species on
carbon surfaces measured by XPS closely matches that determined
from bulk elemental analysis (Table 2). Based on the results of ele-
mental analysis and XPS data, the stabilization temperature greatly
inﬂuences the surface chemistry of the PAN-based carbon.

carbon. The adsorption isotherms of MB were plotted to follow

3.4. CO2 adsorption
The adsorption capacities of PAN-based activated carbon were
evaluated by gravimetric equilibrium capacity and dynamic
adsorption capacity. The gravimetric equilibrium capacity, Qeq,
was obtained by TGA in the dry base of a binary mixture of CO2/
N2; the dynamic adsorption capacity, Qdyn, was calculated by the
breakthrough curve in the humid base of N2/CO2/H2O mixture.
In a typical TGA experiment, the recycle tests of CO2 adsorption
were conducted; the total ﬂow rate of the mixture was maintained
at 20 mL min1 during adsorption at 323 K for 120 min, followed
by desorption in a nitrogen ﬂow of 20 mL min1 at 383 K for
90 min. Fig. 8 shows the results of three cycles using AC513. The
CO2 adsorbed could not be desorbed completely during the desorp-
tion process, which might be due to the presence of the nitrogen
functionalities, as indicated in the EA and XPS analysis, on the sur-
face of AC513. It has been reported that the increase in the basicity
of carbon surfaces will strengthen the interaction of CO2 with the
surfaces of the ACs [25–27], and then the retention of CO2 was ob-
served for PAN-based activated carbon after desorption. The
performance values of PAN-based carbon in six consecutive runs
are given in Table 5. The recycle gravimetric equilibrium capacity
of the samples followed the sequence: AC533 > AC513 > A-
C553 > AC493. Based on the analysis of nitrogen functionalities in
Table 3, AC533 had the highest content of pyridine-type N, quater-
nary-N, pyridine-N oxides, and chemisorbed nitrogen oxides.
AC533 thus had the best gravimetric equilibrium capacity, proba-
bly due to the existence of good textural properties and the highest
amount of effective nitrogen species.

4. Conclusion
High-surface-area (over 3000 m2 g1) activated carbon was pre-
pared from ultra-thin PAN ﬁbers via stabilization, activation, and
carbonization. The maximum surface area and pore volume are
3275 m2 g1 and 1.51 cm3 g1 for AC513, respectively, which are
larger than those of commercially available carbon. The pore size
distribution showed that all of samples had pore size distribution
less than 7 nm, ranging from micropores to mesopores, and the re-
sults were consistent with the N2 adsorption/desorption isotherms.
Elemental analysis and XPS results conﬁrm that nitrogen groups
were present in the bulk structure and surface of carbon. The sta-
bilization temperature has a signiﬁcant effect on methylene blue
and CO2 adsorption. The activated carbon prepared from ultra-thin
PAN ﬁbers stabilized at 533 K (AC533) achieved the highest CO2
gravimetric equilibrium capacity of 5.53 mmol g1 in a binary mix-
ture of 15% CO2 in N2 at 323 K, while AC493 had the highest CO2
dynamic adsorption of 2.70 mmol g1 in a N2/CO2/H2O mixture
(83/10/7% v/v) with total gas ﬂow rate of 100 NmL/min at 323 K.
The dynamic CO2 adsorption capacity displays a good correlation
between the micropore surface and micropore volume of the sam-
ple using DFT model derived from N2 adsorption isotherms.

2.6 nm及孔體積約0.214 cm3/g。由SEM得知此碳材為片狀孔洞結構。為使碳材具
有氨官能基，進一步使用氨氣(NH3)對活性碳進行表面氨基化(amination)，表面
化學改質前先使用兩種硝酸溶液(0.1 M, 0.3 M)酸洗，於不同氨氣持溫溫度(600 
°C, 700 °C, 800 °C)下，氨基化2小時。由BET結果得知，隨酸濃度提高，氨基
化會加速表面反應，使微孔面積明顯增加。隨溫度提高時結構主體性破裂不連續，
導致比表面積的下降。經由FTIR分析得知氨基化能有效接枝胺類官能基團於活性
碳表面，出現1565 cm-1的NH2官能基。在二氧化碳動態吸附結果顯示RF於50 oC
模擬氣體(CO2:N2＝17%:83%)下對二氧化碳吸附量可達6.5 mmol/g (dry base)。
未改質碳材於90%RH濕度下因為水的競爭吸附，對二氧化碳吸附量由6.5 mmol/g
降至4.7 mmol/g；而氨基化後的碳材於90%RH濕度下，因為水參與吸附反應，使
胺類官能基團發揮作用對二氧化碳吸附量仍可達6.6 mmol/g。藉由高比表面積的
物理快速吸附，再擴散至胺類官能基團捕捉提供化學改質吸附，兩者相互配合可
達到快速吸附且大吸附量的協同作用。
(英文) Micro-supermicroporous activated carbons with high surface areas were synthesized 
using the copolymer of resorcinol and formaldehyde (RF), followed by chemical 
activation with KOH and carbonization with N2. After pretreatment with nitric acid, heat 
treatment and ammonia treatment (amination) for producing nitrogen-containing 
activated carbons with basic surface were compared. The surface area of the carbon can 
be over 2100 m2g-1. The RF carbonized at 1073 K achieved the highest CO2 dynamic 
adsorption of 6.5 mmol g-1 in a binary mixture of 15% CO2 in N2, while RF had a 
reduced CO2 dynamic adsorption of 4.7 mmol g-1 in a N2/CO2/H2O mixture (83/10/7 % 
v/v) at 323 K. The amination activated carbon had an improved CO2 dynamic adsorption 
of 6.6 mmol g-1 in a N2/CO2/H2O mixture (83/10/7 % v/v) at 323 K. Amination was 
found to be suitable modification technique for obtaining nitrogen-containing activated 
carbons as CO2 adsorbents.

6. 結論 
降溫和升溫負載對類鑽碳膜產生兩種不同形式的殘留內應力，不論是升溫或降溫負載，其溫度負
載越大時，薄膜所產生的殘留內應力也隨之變大。然而，降溫負載所產生的殘留內應力再進行奈米壓
痕測試時，所得到的薄膜硬度是變小的，而升溫負載所產生的殘留內應力會使得薄膜的硬度變大。 
    當均勻線應力作用在結點時，不同於以溫度變化作為負載條件產生的力為不均勻力，當類鑽碳膜
殘留應力為張應力（降溫負載）時，奈米壓痕試驗在薄膜上施加一壓力，此時張應力與壓應力互相抵
消，使得整體薄膜的硬度減小；當類鑽碳膜殘留應力為壓應力（升溫負載）時，奈米壓痕試驗在薄膜
上施加一壓力，使得類鑽碳膜受到擠壓，導致類鑽碳膜的硬度增加。外無論有無熱負載，等效薄膜楊
氏係數隨粗糙度上升而上升，且張應力值會使楊氏係數增加，其間差值為粗糙度所增加之楊氏係數以
及熱負載所增加之楊氏係數之合。另外，在壓痕試驗時，有熱負載由於受到基材粗糙表面的張應力及
升溫負載的壓應力相抵消之故，呈現出無熱負載之硬度值大於有熱負載之硬度值的情況。因此殘留應

High residual stress has been a critical issue that limits the application of DLC ﬁlms. The stress not only
deteriorates the adhesion strength of DLC in different substrates, but also affects the measurement of
mechanical properties such as hardness or elastic modulus. In this study, a two-step ﬁnite element analysis
that simulates the pre-stress induced by a thermal process followed by nanoindentation was carried out to
study the residual stress effects on mechanical properties. Residual stress generated by different substrates,
substrate roughness and non-uniform stressing are compared based on the impact on the mechanical
properties. The results show that positive residual stress tends to increase hardness and elastic modulus while
negative stress generally results in a decrease in hardness and elastic modulus. Different substrates lead to
different degrees of variation but not to any change in the overall trend. The degree of variation is magniﬁed
by substrate roughness. For non-uniform stress distribution, a smooth substrate has small variation in
hardness and elastic modulus from center to edge and such variation is manifest in a rough substrate. These
results indicate that the mechanical properties (hardness and elastic modulus) are affected by the residual
stress. Factors like different substrates, substrate roughness and measurement position cause different
degrees of variation in these properties. Therefore, the interpretation of mechanical properties should be
applied carefully.

1. Introduction
A diamond-like carbon (DLC) ﬁlm is a thin hydrogenated
amorphous carbon (a-C:H) ﬁlm that consists of diamond bonds (sp3)
and graphic bonds (sp2). The carbon and energy sources are necessary
for DLC ﬁlms to have high sp3 content [1]. During the energetic
deposition process, compressive stress develops in the thin ﬁlm and
limits the growth of the ﬁlm thickness. This residual stress can be
related to ﬁlm morphology or microstructure and depends on the
deposition conditions [2]. The sources of residual stress have been
variously identiﬁed with thermal stress (difference in the expansion
coefﬁcient of ﬁlm and substrate), intrinsic stress (energetic particle
bombardment) and extrinsic stress (lattice distortion, voids, and
phase transformation) [3–10]. Many studies that explore the origin of
residual stress focus speciﬁcally on DLC deposition parameters e.g.
pressure [11, 12], substrate bias [13], power density [14] and gas
precursor [15, 16]. The link between residual stress and mechanical
properties has been explored recently. Bai et al. [17] used a
nanoindentation experiment and a ﬁnite element analysis to study
the effect of residual stress onmechanical properties in CNxﬁlms. They
found that with larger compressive ﬁlm stress, both hardness and
elastic modulus increase. Karlsson et al. [18] examined the deposition
conditions in TiCxN1−x that affect the residual stress and correlated
the residual stress to hardness and modulus. The conclusion is that
high negative stress tends to increase the hardness andmodulus.Mani
et al. [19] studied how the residual stress generated by various
pressures affects the TiC mechanical properties. They observed that
hardness and compressive stress increase with decreasing pressure
and that the stress is related to hardness. Chung et al. [20] studied the
effects of residual stress onmechanical properties of Ti ﬁlms on silicon
and discovered that as the internal tensile stress increases, the
hardness and modulus decrease. Huang et al. [21] did research on
SiN ﬁlms on glass and found with decreasing compressive stress and
increasing tensile stress, the hardness andmodulus decrease. All these
works point out that residual stress affects mechanical properties and
such effects depend on the sign of the stress. From an application's
point of view, a thin ﬁlm/substrate analysis is more realistic than one
that focuses on thin ﬁlms alone. However, the existence of substrates
will induce other effects such as substrate effect [22, 23] and substrate
roughness [24–26] that complicate the analysis. A systematic study
should investigate not only the sign or magnitude of residual stress on

4. Results and discussion
4.1. Residual stress effect on mechanical properties for
different substrates
The thermal stress induced by heating or cooling is proportional to
the temperature difference ΔT as indicated by Eq. (9). However, due to
the ﬁnite thickness of the ﬁlm and the constraint of the substrate, the
induced thermal stress is not uniform. This is demonstrated in Fig. 3
where ΔT=±100 K is applied to DLC/Si and DLC/steel, respectively.
For positive temperature difference in DLC/Si, the ﬁlm exhibits negative
shear stress due to a lower CTE and for negative temperature difference,
the shear stress in the ﬁlm is positive. The stress in the ﬁlm is non-
uniform and themaximumstress occurred near the edge of theﬁlm. For
DLC/steel, the sign of ﬁlm stress is the same as that in DLC/Si but the
stressmagnitude is larger due to bigger CTEdifference betweenDLC and
steel. The effects of residual stress on hardness and elastic modulus for
DLC/Si andDLC/steel are shown in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5, respectively. The sign
of the stress has the opposite effects on hardness and elastic modulus.

4.2. Roughness and non-uniform distribution in stress for mechanical
properties
The residual stress effect on hardness and elastic modulus is
manifested by the surface roughness. In modeling this effect, a surface
roughness based on the amplitude surface roughness parameters
(arithmetic Ra) is used (see Ref. [26]). For simplicity, only DLC/Si with
ΔT=−100 K is presented here. The effect of substrate roughness on
shear stress distribution is shown in Fig. 7 for different Ra. Compared to
the smooth surface (Fig. 4a) where the positive stress on the ﬁlm
gradually fades out near the edge of the sample, the existence of
substrate roughness induced positive stress along the radial direction.
As the surface roughness increases, the asperities on the rough surface
act as the stress concentration center. The implication for high residual
stress is the increase in hardness and elastic modulus as shown in
Fig. 8. This indicates that when DLC deposited on a rough surface
during a cooling process (quenching after ion bombardment), the
associated high thermal stress actually increases the elastic modulus
and the hardness. Meanwhile, the correlation between the elastic
modulus (or hardness) and the surface roughness is almost linear. For
roughness substrate (Ra=0.2 μm), the elastic modulus increase
compared to smooth surface (Ra=0 μm) is 20.53% and the increase
in hardness is 5.83%. Another issue that is often neglected is the non-
uniform stress distribution. Different indentation positions might lead
to different elastic modulus and hardness. To investigate such effect,
we perform three different nanoindentation tests along the radial
direction (as shown in Fig. 2). The analysis is carried out with
ΔT=100 K under smooth and rough (Ra=0.2 μm) silicon substrates
for comparison purpose. The variations in hardness and elastic
modulus in different positions for smooth and rough substrates are
shown in Fig. 9. For smooth substrates, the effect of non-uniform stress
distribution on elastic modulus and hardness variations seem to be
linear, while for rough substrates, the effects onmechanical properties
are more signiﬁcant. The results for variations in elastic modulus and
hardness for smooth and Ra=0.2 μm substrates are summarized in
Table 3. For smooth substrates, the maximum variation is 2.41% and
2.5% in elastic modulus and hardness, respectively. In Ra=0.2 μm,
both values nearly double, which demonstrates that substrate
roughness indeed has an effect on mechanical properties.

二、發表論文全文或摘要 
The DLC films made of spin coating carbon nanotubes (CNTs) on silicon substrate and then 
deposited DLC via C2H2 and Ar. The films were examined by Raman spectra, XPS, AFM, 
contact angle test, MProbe reflectometer and nanoindentation to probe the chemical bonding, 
surface, optical and mechanical properties. The results show that for CNTs/DLC films, the 
higher content of CNTs increases the ratio of ID/IG, the sp2/sp3 ratio which leads to a rougher 
surface and hydrophobic surface. For mechanical properties, for CNTs addition, the hardnes 
and elasticity modulus increase compared to undoped DLC film. However, the hardness and 
elastic modulus of higher CNTs contents DLC films is lower than that in lower CNTs contents 
films. For low content CNTs in DLCs results in stress reduction which is advantageous to 
films adhesion. For optical properties, higher CNTs content tends to reflectivity reduction. For 
0.005wt% CNTs/DLC film, the refractive index in the film has less variation for optical 
wavelength from 400 nm to 700 nm. This DLC film seems to have better reflection loss than 
undoped DLC films.

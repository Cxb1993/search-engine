前言 
陰陽離子界面活性劑（catanionic surfactant）是由陰離子型及陽離子型界面活性劑
所組成的新型界面活性劑。當在水相中將陰離子型與陽離子型界面活性劑混合時，由於
二者解離後所帶的電性相反，因此會自行結合形成具有二條疏水性長碳氫鏈結構的高界
面活性物質，這就是所謂的陰陽離子界面活性劑（Jonsson et al., 1991）。由於陰陽離子
界面活性劑具有二條疏水性的長碳氫鏈，因此在水相中的溶解度通常並不高，所形成不
含對離子（counter ion）的沈澱物，則稱為離子對雙親分子（ion pair amphiphile, IPA）
（Tondre and Caillet, 2001; Marques et al., 2003）。 
液胞（vesicle）是指由雙層膜（bilayer）結構所形成的中空球體，而在抗癌藥物或
高級化粧品中所使用的傳輸載體，通常是採用具有二條疏水性碳氫鏈之脂質（lipid）所
形成的液胞，也就是所謂的微脂粒或脂質體（liposome）。由於陰陽離子界面活性劑或離
子對雙親分子，具有與脂質類似的疏水性雙碳氫鏈結構，因此也會傾向於形成具有雙層
膜結構的中空球體－陰陽離子液胞（catanionic vesicle）（Kaler et al., 1989），而成為藥物
傳輸載體的新型材料。且因其雙鏈結構設計容易，原料與脂質相較便宜許多，因此陰陽
離子液胞的生成行為普遍受到重視（Chung et al, 2003; Karukstis et al., 2003; Antunes et al., 
2004）。 
關於陰陽離子液胞的形成，先前的研究多著重於陰陽離子界面活性劑在水相中自發
性形成液胞之行為的探討（Walker and Zasadzinski, 1997; Pasc-Banu et al., 2004）。不過利
用自發性方法所形成的陰陽離子液胞，會明顯受到陰陽離子界面活性劑分子結構特性及
外在環境的影響，因而普遍遇到液胞濃度不高與相態複雜的問題，且不易同時掌控液胞
的濃度與帶電特性，使得其應用受到相當多的限制（Antunes et al., 2004）。 
為改善現有陰陽離子液胞分散系統的相關問題，以離子對雙親分子為材料，利用強
制的方式將其轉變成陰陽離子液胞的狀態，似是一條可行之路，然而其穩定性通常不盡
理想（Bhattacharya and De, 1999; Chung and Chung, 2002; Chung et al., 2003）。為能針對
形成陰陽離子液胞的強制性製程，以及所形成液胞之特性進行更深入的探討，並評估將
所生成的陰陽離子液胞應用於基因傳輸之 DNA 載體的可行性，在此三年的研究計畫
中，第一年度先針對強制性製程的條件進行有系統的分析，以開發出可更精確控制帶電
陰陽離子液胞之生成的強制性製備程序。在第二年度則探討液胞的組成對其物理穩定性
的影響，並評估以添加劑進一步促進陰陽離子液胞之物理穩定性的效果（Eliasz et al., 
1976; Blume, 1980）。

Introduction 
For a surfactant solution, there is an equilibrium relationship between the bulk 
concentration and surface concentration if an adsorption equilibrium is reached at the 
air/liquid interface.  When a new air/liquid interface is created, surfactant molecules in the 
bulk phase will start adsorbing onto the interface in order to establish the equilibrium 
adsorption condition.  As a result, the surface tension will decrease with time or with the 
extent of surfactant adsorption, finally reaching an equilibrium surface tension.  The 
time-dependent surface tension is so-called dynamic surface tension [1,2].  To understand 
the dynamic adsorption or dynamic surface tension behavior is useful for surfactant 
applications, such as foaming [3], laundry process [4], and therapeutic lung surfactant 
preparation [5,6]. 
Some aqueous surfactant or lipid systems may contain an aggregated phase, such as 
microcrystallites, mainly due to solubility limit.  The aggregated phase or particles can be 
considered as the source of free molecules in the bulk phase.  When free surface-active 
molecules in the bulk phase adsorb onto the air/liquid interface because of the new interface 
formation, the dispersed particles can dissolute and provide free molecules in order to resume 
the original equilibrium condition between free molecules and particles.  It has been found 
that when an aqueous surfactant dispersion was filtered and then the dynamic surface 
tensions of the remaining solution were measured, the rate of surface tension decreasing with 
time was significantly reduced, apparently caused by the removal of surfactant particles or 
the sources for free molecules [7].   
For a soluble surfactant, the dynamic surface tension behavior of the aqueous surfactant 
solutions containing monomers and micelles has been extensively studied and can be 
described by theoretical models with a reasonable extent [8].  If only surfactant monomers 
exist in the bulk phase, one usually uses a diffusion-controlled or mixed-kinetic dynamic 
adsorption model to simulate the dynamic surface tension behavior of the surfactant solution

was measured, and the corresponding surface tension value was calculated from the known 
volume of the drop and the capillary diameter.  After the drop detached from the capillary 
tip, the next drop was formed with a slightly smaller volume and the procedure started again.  
By repeating this procedure, the dynamic surface tension behavior of the aqueous catanionic 
surfactant sample was constructed [24].

process will be controlled by the adsorption/desorption step.  Indeed, the sample 
calculations with a much higher k1 value resulted in a curve, curve 3 in Fig. 2, corresponding 
to the limiting case predicted by a dynamic adsorption model with 
adsorption/desorption-controlled mechanism. 
To perform a model analysis on the dynamic adsorption behavior of surface-active 
materials, one usually compares the dynamic surface tension data to the model predictions 
with adjustable parameters and determines the parameters based on the best-fit condition [9].  
For both catanionic surfactants, CP-DS and DTMA-DS, it is reasonable to assume the 
diffusivity D to be 4×10-10 m2/s as compared to other surfactants with known diffusivities.  
In addition, it first appears that one may have at most six adjustable parameters, Γm, KL, ka, 
kd, k1, and k2, in applying the model.  However, since ka and kd are related by Eq (10) and k1 
and k2 are not independent as suggested by Eq (16), there are actually only two and four 
adjustable parameters for the diffusion-controlled and the mixed-kinetic adsorption models, 
respectively. 
To avoid estimating four adjustable parameters, Γm, KL, ka (or kd), and k1(or k2), 
simultaneously from the dynamic surface tension data, one can perform the model analysis 
by determining the equilibrium adsorption parameters, Γm and KL, independently from the 
equilibrium surface tension versus surfactant concentration data.  The dynamic surface 
tension data for a catanionic surfactant solution with a concentration lower than its saturation 
concentration (without aggregates), if they are available, are then compared to the predictions 
by the diffusion-controlled dynamic adsorption model with available D, Γm, and KL values.

Before one applies the mixed-kinetic adsorption model, it has to first establish that the 
adsorption process is not diffusion-controlled.  For typical experiments, the l value in Eq (5) 
or Eq (14) can vary from 1000 μm or more, if there is natural convection and no stirring, to 
about 10 μm, if there is significant forced convection, which produces a diffusion boundary 
layer.  For the following calculations, l is assumed in the order of 102 μm.   
The dynamic surface tensions of aqueous CP-DS and DTMA-DS systems are plotted in 
Figs. 4 and 5, respectively.  For CP-DS, the critical aggregation concentration is estimated 
to be 1.4×10-3 mM from the equilibrium surface tension data.  As a result, the characteristic 
adsorption length la [30], which is Γm/C0 for the case of the Langmuir isotherm, for an 
aqueous CP-DS solution with a concentration lower than Cs is larger than 2.7 mm.  This 
characteristic length defines the region of the subphase containing enough material to cover 
the interface [29], and the surface-active material in farther regions affects the adsorption 
little.  In view of the extremely large la value for an aqueous CP-DS solution, it is unlikely 
that one can obtain the meaningful dynamic tension response for a CP-DS solution with a 
concentration lower than Cs.  Thus, only the dynamic surface tension data for an aqueous 
CP-DS dispersion are reported here and used to estimate the dissolution rate constant by 
comparing with the model calculations.

diffusion-controlled adsorption model taking the aggregate dissolution effect into account

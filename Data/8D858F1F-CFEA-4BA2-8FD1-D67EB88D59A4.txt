Abstract
Graft copolymers comprising acrylic acid
(AAc) and methacryloyl ethyl acrylate (MEA)
units as the polymeric backbone and
poly(N-isopropyl acrylamide) (PNIPAAm) or
both PNIPAAm/monomethoxy poly(ethylene
glycol) (mPEG) as the grafts in aqueous
solution of pH 5.0 undergo thermo-induced
self-assembling into polymeric micelles. The
results of fluorescence and variable temperature
1H-NMR support that in the
poly(AAc-co-MEA)-g-PNIPAAm aqueous
system, the extensive interaction between
unionized AAc residues and partial PNIPAAm
segments induces the polymeric backbones to be
partially embedded into PNIPAAm cores during
copolymer micellization. Therefore, at 60 oC,
the inner core structure of micelles is
constructed from hydrophobic PAAc/PNIPAAm
complexes. On the other hand, the presence of
mPEG grafts of copolymer not only retards
efficiently the formation of PAAc/PNIPAAm
complexes but also prevents the polymeric
backbones from being entrapped into
hydrophobic PNIPAAm cores. The
poly(AAc-co-MEA)-g-PNIPAAm/mPEG
copolymer favors the formation of three-layer
“onion-like”polymeric micelles comprising the
hydrophobic PNIPAAm cores surrounded by
AAc-rich shells and hydrophilic mPEG coronas.
At 60 oC, by addition of ammonium
peroxydisulfate (APS) into micelle solutions,
the free radical polymerization of MEA units in
the AAc-rich shells of onion-like micelles was
achieved to obtain shell cross-linked (SCL)
micelles with ca. 60 nm in their hydrodynamic
diameters. In contrast, the polymerization of
MEA residues in the PAAc/PNIPAAm cores and
AAc-rich shells of micelles was performed to
create the cross-linked (CL) micelles (ca. 160
nm) with PAAc/PNIPAAm complex core
structure. Compared to the SCL micelles, the
CL micelles exhibit significant pH- and
thermo-responsive volume variation due to their
low cross-linking density.
Keywords: stimuli-response, polymeric
micelles, SCL micelles.

 pH-Responsive Polymer Vesicles Prepared from Acrylic Acid/Distearin Acrylate Copolymers 
 
Yi-Fong Huang, Chih-Kai Chuang, Wen-Chia Huang, Hsin-Cheng Chiu* 
Department of Chemical Engineering, National Chung Hsing University, Taichung 402, Taiwan   
 
Introduction 
Since being proposed as a new class of polymer 
assemblies with well defined supramolecular structure, 
polymer vesicles have been found advantageous over 
traditional liposomes in delivery-release of varying 
substances and biomimetics due to their high mechnical 
stability, alterability of chemical constitution and low 
permeability.1,2 In this study, polymer vesicles were 
obtained from self-assembling of an amphiphilic 
copolymer comprising acrylic acid (AAc) and acrylate 
of 1,2-distearol-rac-glycerol (distearin acrylate, DSA). 
The morphology and properties of polymer vesicles 
were characterized by confocal laser scanning 
microscope (CLSM), transmission electron microscope 
(TEM), and scanning electron microscope (SEM). 
Materials and Methods 
Synthesis of Poly(AAc-co-DSA) 
Poly(AAc-co-DSA) was prepared by 
transesterification of poly(N-acryloxysuccinimide) 
(PNAS) with distearin in DMF at 60oC for 120 h, 
followed by thorough hydrolysis of the unreacted NAS 
residues into AAc units. The composition was 
characterized to be 14.2 mol% of the distearin content 
by 1H-NMR. The average molecular weight of the 
copolymer was 3.7 x 105 g/mol. 
Preparation and morphology study of polymer vesicles 
The polymer vesicles were prepared by double 
emulsion (water-in-oil-in-water) and organic solvent 
evaporation, using an organic cosolvent system of THF 
and CHCl3 as the oil phase and acetate buffer (pH 5.0) 
or deionized water as aqueous phase. The polymer 
vesicles were formed upon thorough evaporation of the 
organic phase in emulsion solutions. The structural 
characteristic of the polymer vesicles was studied by 
TEM, SEM, and CLSM, respectively. 
Results and Discussion 
Figure 1 shows the CLSM images of polymer 
vesicles associated with Nile red as a hydrophobic 
probe in the nonpolar regions of the vesicle walls. It is 
obvious that the vesicles exist in unilamellar form. The 
vesicle observation from TEM (not shown) indicates 
that the thickness of the membranes is ca. 25 nm. The 
vesicle size can be controlled in the range from 1 to 20 
μm in diameter by adjusting the ratio of THF in the 
cosolvent system from 5 to 20% (v/v). As THF was 
used solely as the oil phase, the vesicle size was 
reduced to ca 170 nm in diameter. In addition, the 
polymer vesicles are characterized by the 
implementation of pH-responsive transmembrane 
channels permeable for hydrophilic solutes. While 
calcein, acting as a water soluble probe, in the external 
aqueous phase was prohibited to enter the interior 
aqueous compartment at pH 5.0 (Figure 2A), the probe 
molecules can diffuse across the vesicular membranes 
freely at pH 8.0 (Figure 2B). Calcein was confined 
within the compartment by replacing the exterior 
aqueous phase with fresh buffer of pH 5.0 (Figure 2C). 
The results indicate the existence of pH-switchable 
channels embedded within the vesicle membranes that 
control the encapsulation and release of calcein in 
response to pH changes. Based on the observation, a 
tentative molecular packing model is proposed. Due to 
the hydrophobic effect, the distearin grafts associate 
into discrete bilayer islets within the membranes while 
the unionized AAc residues surround the bilayer islets 
and stabilize the membrane structure by extensive 
hydrogen bonding and polymer chain entanglement. 
The two regions are segregated by their hydrophobicity 
difference. The pH-responsive transmembrane channels 
are therefore constructed at high pH. The outer surfaces 
of the membranes are composed of the ionized AAc 
residues, which prevent vesicles from aggregation in 
aqueous phase. 
Conclusion 
   Polymer vesicles were obtained from 
self-assembling of the copolymer comprising AAc and 
DSA residues. The vesicles are characterized by the 
implementation of pH-responsive transmembrane 
channels. The vesicles show great potential in 
biomedical applications by providing a facile control of 
the encapsulation and release of bioactive agents via the 
responsiveness of the membranes to the environmental 
stimulus.

3. Structural optimization of the blades 
The second part of this research utilizes a two-step 
procedure to produce blade designs that are optimal both in 
aerodynamics and in material distribution. The result of the 
first step is the optimum cord lengths and twist angles of 
airfoils of the blade cross-sections along the blade spanwise 
direction. The second step provides optimal material 
distribution for the composite blade. To achieve the objectives, 
a wind field simulation program, TurbSim, a routine to 
calculate the aerodynamic loading on blade elements, 
AeroDyn, and a wind turbine dynamics code, FAST, are 
utilized to perform simulation. The maximum loads on the 
turbine blade are extracted and applied to a parameterized 
finite element model of a blade created in the ANSYS 
environment, and then the mass of the composite blade is 
minimized, while the stress is kept within preset design 
stresses and the natural frequencies of the blade well separated 
from the rotor’s rated speed. Figure 17 shows graphically the 
simulation tools used in this research in sequential order.

minimum blade mass as the design objective, the structural

4. Assessment of the project results 
This project was completed, by the most parts, 
according to the original proposal (the first year). In some 
contents, it even exceeded the proposed plan. The results of 
this project have led to publications of 2 conference papers,

For large horizontal-axis wind turbines, turbine blades are usually made from 
composite materials to reduce the weight while attaining a reasonable strength to 
weight ratio. The design of large wind turbine blades must consider both their 
aerodynamic efficiency and structural robustness. Early designs of wind turbine 
blades utilized airfoils intended originally for airplanes, such as the NACA series. 
Nowadays, most blades use airfoils designed specifically for wind turbines, e.g., the 
NREL S series and DU series. 
A two-step procedure is presented to produce blade designs that are optimal both in 
aerodynamics and in material distribution. The result of the first step is the optimum 
cord lengths and twist angles of airfoils of the blade cross-sections along the blade 
spanwise direction. The second step provides optimal material distribution for the 
composite blade. To achieve the objectives, a wind field simulation program, 
TurbSim, a routine to calculate the aerodynamic loading on blade elements, 
AeroDyn, and a wind turbine dynamics code, FAST, are utilized to perform 
simulation. The maximum loads on the turbine blade are extracted and applied to a 
parameterized finite element model of a blade created in the ANSYS environment, 
and then the mass of the composite blade is minimized, while the stress is kept 
within preset design stresses and the natural frequencies of the blade well 
separated from the rotor’s rated speed. A preliminary result considering only one 
load case with a mean wind speed of 25 m/s is demonstrated as the design 
example, in which the blades, used on a 3 MW wind turbine, contains 21 cross-
sections of NREL S818, S825 and S826 airfoil types. 
The design of large wind turbine blades must consider both their aerodynamic 
efficiency and structural robustness. This paper presents a two-step procedure for 
the optimum design of composite wind turbine blades. A preliminary result 
considering only one load case with a mean wind speed of 25 m/s is demonstrated 
as the design example, in which the blades, used on a 3 MW wind turbine, contains 
cross-sections of NREL S818, S825 and S826 airfoil types. The optimization result 
shows that the initial blade design is infeasible, but eventually the optimization 
process converges to a feasible solution with an optimized mass of 9394.4 kg.

A two-step procedure for designing wind turbine blades has been presented. Loadings 
of forces and moments on the blade were simulated using wind field and wind turbine 
dynamics codes. The maximum loads on the turbine blade were extracted and applied 
to a parameterized finite element model. And finally, optimization process was carried 
out to minimize the mass of the composite blade while the stress kept within preset 
design stresses and the fundamental natural frequency of the blade well separated 
from the rotor’s rated speed. This paper presents some preliminary results of a series 
of research in wind turbine blade design. Important blade design Issues such as fatigue, 
buckling, blade natural frequencies falling at multiples of the rotational frequency, and 
more appropriate failure criteria for laminated composite blades, are part of an ongoing 
research and hopefully will be addressed elsewhere in the near future. A three-blade, horizontal-axis, upwind 
wind turbine with a rated power of 3 MW 
was chosen as the example. Each rotor 
blade, which is 44.175 m long, has a 
circular cross-section from its root to the 
1.753-meter span location. Then the 
circular blade cross-sections gradually 
transition to airfoil sections that have 
NREL airfoil types S818 at 9.648-meter, 
S825 at 32.667-meter and S826 at 
41.873-meter and 44.175-meter. The 
rest sections are hybrid airfoils of two of 
the   three   basic   types   with   different
cross-sectional airfoils and their twist angles are clearly visible. The hollow rotor blades 
are made of composite materials with box-spar reinforcement. Figure 4 illustrates that 
the blade is composed of double-bias (DB), unidirectional, core and lining materials, 
and covered by an exterior coating of gelcoat and a layer of nexus.

The wind turbine is undergone a turbulence with a mean wind speed of 25 m/s, 
simulated using IEC normal turbulence model with the Kaimal spectrum and Type A 
turbulence intensity. The maximum forces and moments, extracted from the FAST 
output, are simultaneously applied to a parameterized finite element model of the 
aerodynamically optimized blade, with its blade root fixed as the boundary condition.
There are 5 design variables defined for the FE model: the first one (x1) is the thickness 
of the double-bias material in the first circular segment; the second (x2) is the thickness 
difference between the first and second segments; the third (x3) is the thickness 
difference between the second and third segments. x4 and x5 are proportional factors 
such that the new thickness values of the unidirectional materials in segments 4 to 9 (x4) 
and segments 10 to 20 (x5) are determined by multiplying their corresponding factor to 
their initial thickness values. After 40 iterations, the best feasible solution was chosen

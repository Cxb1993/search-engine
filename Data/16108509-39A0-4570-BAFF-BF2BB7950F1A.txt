Abstract 
Most study focuses on quality management practices and associated success 
factors, a little study have focused on TQM for quality improvement execution 
frequency and performance of the tools and techniques. To determine the best strategy 
for improving execution performance of the tools and techniques for quality 
improvement, the study addressed this deficiency by integrating the 
Importance-Performance Analysis (IPA) and the Slack’s IPA Matrix to provide a more 
comprehensive performance evaluation matrix for improving execution performance of 
the tools and techniques. The study integration and analysis many literature identified 
the tools and techniques most widely used by business in Taiwan. The tools and 
techniques are then placed on a performance evaluation matrix to determine an 
appropriate improvement strategy.  
 
Keywords: Control chart, Performance evaluation matrix, Total quality management 
(TQM). 
 
1. Introduction 
Most important work is management and improvement of business operating. 
Management is maintaining the present situation of business, but improvement is to 
change the present situation to increase competition advantages. Total quality 
management (TQM) is one of the most popular and durable quality improvement 
concepts. TQM is considered by many as an important quality and business 
performance improvement tool. This position is rooted in its development which has 
passed through a number of phases: Inspection; Quality Control (QC); Statistical 
Quality Control (SQC); Quality Assurance (QA); Total Quality Control (TQC); Total 
Quality Management (TQM) and Six-Sigma. Ever since the late 1980s when the 
positive correlation between introduction of TQM and enhanced competitiveness began 
to be understood, evidence has suggested that the majority of organizations that have

IPA has been applied in a number of settings with relatively little modification in 
form. However, other researchers have extended the basic IPA Model. Although various 
researchers have proposed minor modifications to the technique over the years, the 
basic framework has largely remained the same. Recently, Slack (1994) presented an 
IPA Model in which the underlying framework was changed to consider a relationship 
between importance and performance. Slack theorized that target levels of performance 
for particular product attributes should be proportional to the importance of those 
attributes. Thus, attributes of high importance should have higher performance 
standards than attributes of low importance (Sampson and Showalter, 1999). Modifying 
IPA in this manner changed the prescriptive nature of the model. Slack’s Model was a 
major improvement in face validity over traditional IPA Models (Figure 2).

The tools and techniques items are then mapped onto the performance evaluation 
matrix. The two bold diagonal lines in the performance evaluation matrix indicate the 
limits of the performance control zone (Zone M, or the ‘maintain zone’). The tools and 
techniques items within this zone can be maintained in accordance with the present 
situation. The tools and techniques items that fall into the bottom-right zone (Zone E, or 
the ‘improvement zone’) have greater execution frequency than execution performance; 
more resources must be invested in these tools and techniques to improve execution 
performance. The tools and techniques that fall into the upper-left zone (Zone F, or the 
‘excellent zone’) have less execution frequency than execution performance; indicate 
these tools and techniques execution performance very excellent. (Generally speaking, 
only a few tools and techniques fall into this zone.) Managers should seek to improve 
only those tools and techniques that are located outside the ‘improvement zone’. 
The performance evaluation matrix limits should be adjusted appropriately, according 
to business resources. The closer the performance evaluation matrix limits approach the 
PCCL, the greater the number of the tools and techniques that fall outside the 
‘improvement zone’—thus requiring improvement through investment of more 
resources. The control chart was defined as the performance control line and the target

一、前言 
大眾捷運系統其構成便捷的路網，不僅可有效紓解交通壅塞問題、大幅改善民眾的
生活品質，更兼具提高交通安全、減少道路交通事故，提供民眾密集班次、運量大、速
度快的運輸服務等優點。捷運系統由於具有大量且不特定乘客出入之使用者特性，且基
於土地充分開發利用，多以地下車站設計規劃造成深廣、密閉之空間特性，若加上與地
下街、百貨商場等複合用途建築物，甚至與高速鐵路、台鐵、捷運三鐵的整合規劃相互
共構連結時，形成使用上、搶救上與管理上的複雜性可想而知，一旦發生火災等各種災
變事故，人命安全恐遭受極大威脅；更由先進國家類似空間曾發生火災造成人員嚴重傷
亡之案例，促使如何確保地下捷運空間之大量乘客能在危險環境出現前及時順利避難疏
散，成為設計者、管理者與使用者共同關心的重要課題。 
目前捷運系統多以美國國家防火協會（National Fire Protection Association，NFPA）
130 為避難安全之規劃設計藍本，並採行人員避難容許時間 4 分鐘（離開月台層）、6 分
鐘（離開站體）或 8 分鐘（特殊車站）規定作為避難安全評估基準。此種規格式法規
(Prescriptive Code)雖可提供設計人員與審查者方便驗證該場所之避難安全性，但採預設
固定之參數，且經簡化、概括性的評估避難時間方式，不但無法提出清楚設計理念合理
解釋整個避難過程全貌、也無法調整參數以符合實際發生火災境況分析，故不能說服各
界強烈質疑其合理性與正當性。此外，若以全尺寸（Full-Scale）避難模擬演練來作為
評估避難安全基準有違人道立場，且為花費巨額成本的個案避難試驗，並不符合經濟效
益原則。因此，基於建構安全與符合經濟效益之捷運站體空間考量下，以合理與可行的
評估模式來分析地下捷運車站旅客避難安全，並檢討整體營運安全管理機制，以確保地
下捷運系統安全水平，並為防災機制發展一套有效因應對策，實屬刻不容緩之要務。 
 
二、研究目的 
目前台灣地區對於大眾運輸工具發生重大災害之應變多只限於事故發生後之緊急
應變。然而，在應變器材與資源並不充分的狀況下，若不能有系統的將事前準備工作做
好，往往浪費許多資源及犧牲人員與環境。故如何在複合式車站（捷運、台鐵與高鐵共
構）未發生災害之前，先瞭解災害可能發生的地點與災害時人員避難逃生之動線與時
間，將有助於救災的準備與增加救災的時效性，此即本研究課題之最重要目的。 
 
三、資料及文獻收集 
3.1 避難行為之研究 
有關人員避難逃生之研究可追溯至1955年Kikuji Togawo透過觀察人群流動討論避
難設施，之後在1971 年美國學者Fruin亦透過觀察行人的步行速度，提出人員流動依其
地點、空間、使用性質、密度、速度與流量而不同，並且提出相關之經驗公式。1989 年 
D.Yung 和V.R. Beck 提出人員避難決定與區劃空間中火災成長狀況的發生有關，而火
災成長狀態由火災成長模式來計算，因不同的火災成長狀況可使人員在火場中察覺從他
人的警告或收到警報訊息的狀態。1992 年Sime評估人員因活動力及對建築物之熟識程
度不同而對反應時間長短會有所影響，但由於人員避難時之心理層面相當複雜，目前這
方面的研究尚有不足。1994 年Guylene Proulx 和 George Hadjisophocleous 認知人類在
火災中有不同的複雜反應，而決定發展簡化的避難模式，Proulx 假設居民依照順序對

經以上之災例統計分析可知： 
1.從歐美各國鐵路捷運系統共21件災例分析災害發生原因，可以發現其中發生火
災、爆炸之災例（18件）為最多，而且經起火原因之分析統計，由列車火災件數
達6 件的情況為最多，其次是電扶梯起火的火災5件，由配電室或變壓器起火等
電氣火災2件，顯示火災型態與列車或電氣部分有密切。 
2.從亞洲各國鐵路捷運系統共39件災例分析災害發生原因，其中也以發生火災之災
例（31件）為最多，顯示火災災害型態與歐美國家災例有共通性；同樣經起火原
因之分析統計，與歐美各國所不同的是由配電室或變壓器、空氣壓縮機、空調過
濾機等機械或電氣起火的火災件數達7件的情況為最多，其次是由車輛、列車底
部或車輛馬達等起火的火災共6件。 
3.在上述各國之災例中不難發現，如縱火、丟置爆裂物等惡作劇、惡意犯罪行為在
現代大都會地下車站內是會發生的，針對此種無預警人為因素之防範對策，應加
強車站內初期應變能力與災變管理。 
4.由於時空差異及社會型態轉變之影響，對於越接近當今之災例進行災害發生起因
分析，即對地下車站災害發生情境之掌握越有參考價值。從NFPA 130 對電扶梯
應以不燃材質建造及需設置排水出口以排除積水等設置規定，可以合理排除早期
電扶梯火災具高度火災危險性之看法。 
5.由以上歐美與亞洲各國地下捷運車站的事故發生災例件數比對分析，可以得到的
共同結論為災害事故皆以火災為主，而且在非公共區域電氣設備火災或公共區域
列車起火部分是最主要的起火原因。以此災例統計所得到的結論，將可作為我們
在探討地下捷運空間火災情境假設及分析時重要的參考依據及方向。 
4.2 起火分析 
1.地下捷運車站在營運開放期間，係廣為旅客方便搭乘、使用頻繁的大眾運輸交通
工具，若以火災發生境況為考量，可歸納出下列各項使用特性： 
(1)由於位於市中心交通動線上，平時即是不特定旅客進出，尤其是尖峰時刻，
更是湧進大量人群密集使用的情形，因此對該場所火載量與火災危險度並不
易評估。 
(2)地下捷運車站平時有站務人員實施管理，同時在管理營運機制下該場所消防
安全設備（撒水、排煙設備等）也有專人定期維修保養，故在緊急狀況發生
時，設備動作機率應比一般場所較高，同時有關防火避難設施如走廊通道、
出入口與緊急安全門置放障礙物使避難受阻機率不大。 
(3)一般通行十字旋轉閘門、殘障服務門係供旅客自由進出使用，唯有公務門為
其內部人員進出之管制，因此對防破壞與防縱火措施僅由所架設之錄影監視
系統，或是內部管理巡邏機制來防範。 
(4)由於係屬特殊大型公共場所，故消防安全水平除了應考量火載量與容易逃生
等火災發生造成有形損失之安全影響因素外，更應考量避難動線出入口、通
道之位置、容量大小等空間規劃的影響。

(2)非公共區域

Abstract 
This paper presents a vulnerability-capturing 
formulation for measuring vulnerability responses due 
to geographical differences. Research towards 
generalizing vulnerability analysis remains less 
advanced in comparison to hazard analysis primarily 
because of difficulties involved in expressing and 
validating the interdependency of vast amounts of 
vulnerability factors, and also partly due to the enlarged 
vulnerability scope of including social and economical 
aspects in conjunction with the physical and 
environmental vulnerabilities. This paper will address 
concepts to capture vulnerability by presenting two 
main arguments: (i) the classification of vulnerability 
conditions existing at three or more levels through the 
measuring of the relativity brought forth by changes in 
any of the other vulnerability levels, and (ii) the 
employment of a consistent conversion table to interpret 
vulnerability.  
Keywords: vulnerability, capture, relativity, hazards, 
disaster risk 
1. Introduction 
From a risk-management point of view, the 
decision-maker, who often operates under budgetary 
considerations, cannot afford to act on all identified 
critical infrastructures. Instead, a scheduled protection 
scheme allocating budgeted installments is often 
considered as being a more feasible approach. Decision 
makers placed in charge of overseeing the funding for 
such a protection scheme need, in order to ensure an 
adequate return-from-investment, effective analytical 
tools to provide them with disaster-reduction strategies 
in order to assist in the prevention, reduction and 
mitigation of disaster, all while ensuring a justifiable and

 Disaster risk is often expressed as a product of 
vulnerability and hazard. Vulnerability, according to the 
ISDR definition, is the measure of susceptibility of a 
community to the impact of hazard. The conditions are 
determined by physical, social, economic, and 
environmental factors. Hazard, on the other hand, is 
defined as a potentially damaging physical event or 
phenomenon, human or natural, that may cause the loss 
of life, bodily injury, property damage, social and 
economic disruption or environmental degradation. The 
research of vulnerability aspects is less advanced in 
comparison to hazard analysis primary due to the 
complexity of vulnerability planes. Prior implementation 
of a generalized crisis risk assessment system containing 
both generalized vulnerability and hazard models are 
inevitably required. This paper addresses from a 
systemic engineering perspective, a conceptual 
generalized vulnerability model for crisis risk analysis. 
2. Disaster Vulnerability 
In accordance with the International Strategy for 
Disaster Reduction (ISDR), the definition of 
vulnerability is defined as the conditions determined by 
physical, social, economic, and environmental factors or 
processes, which increase the susceptibility of a 
community to the impact of hazards. In a comprehensive 
review by Cutter [1], three distinguishing vulnerability 
themes were identified: vulnerability as (a) hazard 
exposure; (b) social response; or (c) 
place.  Vulnerability as (a), hazard exposure, focuses on 
both the distribution of hazardous conditions and the 
level of loss derived from a hazardous 
event.  Vulnerability as (b), social response, highlights 
the social constructions of vulnerability such as 
resilience, resistance, response to hazards, and coping 
capacity. And (c) place-specific vulnerability, considers 
an integrated combination of social response and hazard 
exposure within a specific geographic district.  For 
example, the European Spatial Planning Observation 
Network (ESPON) [2] Hazards Project defines 
place-specific vulnerability as a combination of damage 
potential and coping capacity, and is therefore classified 
as place-specific vulnerability research. Overall, 
consideration of the theme of vulnerability as a place is 
of larger scope than the previous two themes. 
 In terms of vulnerability factors, a wide variety and 
combination of factors have been addressed, especially 
from the social-economical plane of vulnerability, such 
as age, gender, race, income, etc. [3, 4]. 
Socio-economical vulnerability factors are classified by 
different stages of a disaster cycle, i.e., before disaster, 
preparedness, response and recovery. For example, 
Schneiderbauer [5] based on this concept, further divided 
vulnerability into susceptibility and resilience. Sutherst 
[6] discussed vulnerability from the adaptive capacity 
point of view and Schmidt [7] classified vulnerability 
factors into either damage potential or coping capacity.

3. Requirements for capturing vulnerability 
Let f1 function interrelates several vulnerability 
conditions at a fixed location; any changes in the 
vulnerability state of a fixed location can directly modify 
the f1 function, thus the f1 function possesses the feature 
of variability. Hence, the corresponding f1 function 
should be updated whenever any vulnerability state is 
altered. The f1 function is a polynomial curve fitting 
based interpolating function. Construction of an f1 
function essentially requires inputs of vulnerability 
conditions measured at a specific location. The inputs 
could be either derived from an historical data set, or if 
scarcity of data circumstance arises, expert judgment can 
be invoked.

The purpose of defining the f2 function is to track 
vulnerability variation and to ensure that these influences 
are updated correctly across all vulnerability levels (i.e., 
across different geographical districts). For example, say 
if under some circumstances the vulnerability at the local 
district elevated to a higher level, it should also influence 
both the regional and national vulnerability level because 
‘everything is connected to everything’. Although an 
increase of high local vulnerability in comparison to 
either a regional or national vulnerability level may be 
negligible or insignificant sometimes, the fluctuating 
trend of vulnerability should be monitored and tracked to 
ensure that it is under control. Doing so would avoid 
initiation of an unexpected concomitant disaster due to 
pre-existing high vulnerability. Another angle by which 
to examine the purpose of defining the f2 function is to 
perceive the set of functions from a top-down 
perspective, e.g., if under a special circumstance, say a 
financial crisis, the nation’s economy exhibits an 
unexpected drop resulting in an increase of the overall 
national vulnerability. As a result of this, through f2 
function, the corresponding rise of national vulnerability 
can be and should be exhibited in both regional and local 
levels. 
4. Time-Dependent Vulnerability 
So far in section 3, two functions f1, f2 have been defined. 
These time-independent functions are important in 
capturing variation of vulnerability either locally, 
regionally or nationally due to alterations in the state of 
vulnerability. Rather than altering the state of time, 
vulnerability should be a function of time since many 
vulnerability factors are time-dependent. For example, in 
the physical planes of vulnerability the physics of 
buildings, infrastructures, roads, bridges degenerate with 
time under the phenomenon known as fatigue. The 
rigidity of materials becomes softer and more vulnerable 
under cyclic or periodic loadings. In other words, the 
physical vulnerability of these infrastructures increase 
with time unless a regular maintenance schedule is 
implemented to constantly monitor and control the rise 
of vulnerability. Environmental vulnerability, too, is an 
increasing function of time. This is because our

5. Vulnerability Conversion 
Vulnerability can be assessed either qualitatively or 
quantitatively. While scarcity of vulnerability data is 
becoming an issue, qualitative vulnerability assessment 
is often adopted as a substitute to explore vulnerability 
condition in depth. Although the qualitative approach 
offers the advantage of in-depth exploration, a 
quantitative approach provides a more accurate 
estimation. Ideally, qualitative and quantitative 
vulnerability assessments should be interchangeable in 
order to provide both an in-depth assessment and, at the 
same time, maintain the accuracy of approximation up to 
a standard. To facilitate conversion between qualitative 
and quantitative assessment, a standard conversion table 
should be produced and applied consistently during the 
interchange process (e.g., see Figure 3).The depth of 
qualitative vulnerability assessment is often dependent 
on the manipulated words or phrases. The indicators 
such as low, medium and high could be used to capture 
the responses to the in-depth question. On the other hand, 
quantitative vulnerability assessment often employs 
statistical analysis techniques to obtain mean, standard 
deviation and covariance for interpreting the relative 
vulnerability level. To facilitate conversion between 
qualitative and quantitative responses, a common set of 
conversion factors provides a consistent filter for 
transforming between qualitative responses and relative 
numerical indicators.

6. Concluding Remarks

vulnerability and hazard, prior generalization of a 
disaster risk analysis is possible, and construction of 
generalized hazard and vulnerability models are 
prerequisites. Comparing the research towards 
generalization, vulnerability analysis is generally less 
advanced compared to hazard analysis primarily due to 
two reasons: (a) the enlarged vulnerability scope to cover 
both social and economic aspects of vulnerability, and 
(b) the complexity in expressing the interdependency 
between vulnerability factors which remains at a 
rudimentary stage. This paper addresses the

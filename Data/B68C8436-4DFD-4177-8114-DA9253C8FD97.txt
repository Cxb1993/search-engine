2摘要
土木建築工程資深工程師因為參與公司專案的次數比較多與時間比較長，所以累積了很多
寶貴的工程知識，然而這些知識，卻都隱藏在資深工程師的腦中，並且隨著他們的退休或離職
而消失。除此之外，營建企業要花相當長的時間培訓新進的工程師，使他們能在短時間之內瞭
解其工作崗位的職責與進行工程專案的技巧。
知識地圖是知識管理領域中最重要而且是最有用的工具之一。知識地圖能夠提供給團隊、
部門、以及企業有效的應用知識管理策略。藉由知識地圖的輔助，使用者不僅可以容易瞭解並
定義內部系統的知識所在，而且能夠決定出什麼是企業中最為重要的智慧資產。在傳統的營建
產業領域中，若能透過知識地圖作為應用的工具，對產業導入知識管理將可帶來很大的效益。
為了要將解決上述的兩個問題，本研究希望能藉由知識地圖的建立，提供轉換隱性知識為顯性
知識的方法，並且讓新進工程師能夠更有效率的學習工程實務，提昇工作效率。
所以本研究的主要目的包含（1）以 IDEF0 分析營建知識地圖的建置與應用程序，有系統的
探討知識地圖建置過程中關鍵要素。（2）探討營建工程中所適合的知識地圖應用模式。（3）探
討營建工程中不同角色所需建立與應用的知識地圖。本研究主要藉由文獻回顧、專家訪談以及
IDEF0 的研究方法進行研究。最後由情境分析的方式演練營建產業如何建立知識地圖，以及如
何透過知識地圖分享企業中的知識，以藉此讓企業中的知識不斷的延伸發展下去，進一步提升
營建業的生產力。
由於土木建築的工程類型相當多樣，如房屋建築、辦公大樓、橋梁、隧道、機場等等，所
以每一種建物所需要的知識都不相同，因此需要選定一個適當的建物作為研究的重點。本研究
選擇以橋梁建築作為研究的主軸，主要的原因是橋梁建築，是連結地區與地區之間的重要通道，
是區域之間經濟流通的重要樞紐。然而，工程生命週期成本之中，工程維護的費用的比重相當
龐大，使得維護管理的研究越來越受到重視，以台灣為例，全國有兩萬五千多座橋需要進行維
護，因此本研究主要的研究範圍，著重在橋梁工程的維護與管理。

6資訊形象化
資訊形象化是利用電腦技術的支援，將摘要以視覺化之呈現方式描述對數據
的認知。然而，知識地圖就是資訊形象化的一種展示，為展現知識地圖所表達之
知識。目前應用資訊形象化的領域有搜尋引擎，如 Kartoo，財務金融，如
SmartMoney 等。經由文獻回顧及檢視現有的知識管理商業軟體後，歸納出下列
數種主要型態之知識地圖：檔案總管型、思考管理型、網狀地圖型、星狀地圖型、
點狀地圖型、條列地圖型、等高線地圖型。
知識地圖的應用
概念式
概念式的知識地圖，知識的型態及其來源以外顯知識為主。一般而言，概念
式知識地圖最廣泛應用於網站地圖，多以概念的分類來呈現，將網站的架構分門
別類地進行歸類，各個類別之間並沒有從屬關係。
作業流程式
作業流程式的知識地圖，知識的型態及其來源包含了外顯、內隱。如包含了
工作流程的順序關係，建立使用者對工作流程規劃的整體概念，當使用者對其中
的「流程」有所疑惑時，便可據此快速地找到相關的文件或專家。
階層架構式
階層式的知識地圖，知識的型態與其來源以專家知識為主。大多數的組織架
構圖、分工結構圖、成本分解圖等都可以是階層式的知識地圖。

研究方法
研究方法以深度訪談法為主，訪談的對象高工局局長、各級管理者、實際維
護管理工程師，最後透過 IDEF0 系統化的分析方法，將訪談的結果整合於知識地
圖的應用架構。使用深度訪談主要的目的在於，深度訪談能發覺人們內心身處的
動機和態度，當人們有時不願在一群人面前公開談論某些動機或表示某些意見
時，該方法常常可以收集到很多重要的的資訊。使用 IDEF0 的主要目的在於，能
夠透過系統化的架構，以階層式的方式一層一層表達出知識地圖實際應用在工程
案例的方式，並讓工程師能夠快速的學習知識地圖建構的核心。
研究成果
本章節的內容主要包含了橋梁維護管理知識分類邏輯，並針對新進員工的知
識需求，透過 IDEF0 的方式展現如何建立知識地圖。根據文獻的回顧發現，新進
的工程人員需要學習的知識相當多元，除了基礎的橋梁維護知識、實際的操作流
程、還有橋梁維護管理系統內的資料分析，因此新進的員工需要花費相當多的時
間學習現有的知識與資訊系統。本研究所提出的知識地圖應用於新進工程人員的
機制，即是以分類邏輯作為核心，串聯現有的實際作業流程、知識文件、以及資
訊系統，用以降低新進員工的學習負擔，提升學習的效率，並讓知識能夠不斷地
重複使用。

2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
As more and more academic studies indicate that the physical and mental elements of the evacuee are the
keys to evacuation planning, this study discusses design-phased legislations and system stimulation,
reflecting on the investigation methods for hypothesized problems. （Butcher and Parnell, 1983; De Cicco,
1990; Jennings, 1999）
2.1 Current Problems of Evacuation Legislation
Mental elements are excluded from the consideration for most legislation, for the reason that human
characteristics and behaviors are uneasy to be confined and controlled. Hence, normally, legislation and
academic studies on the standard width and length of the egress route are more than on the discussion of the
evacuee’s characteristics and interaction. In addition, more and more researchers find that the interaction
relation between the evacuee and the environment is the effective value for evacuation capacity. （Butcher
and Parnell, 1983; De Cicco, 1990; Jennings, 1999）
Ding YuQun once indicated that controlling the amount of through stairs by fixed area and walk distance
fails to identify the difference between the density and the usage, thus causes blocking and danger to
evacuation （Ding and Jiang, 2000）.Chuldey believes that the design of egress routes needs to take user’s 
types into account, emphasizing the user’s capacity（Chudley, 1990）. Paul studied the connection between
people’s move and the design of theexits, bringing up the importance of the exits location in the whole
floor（Paul, 1984）. Sime questions the narrow aspects of Britain legislation to regulate the egress time
merely in accordance to human physical fitness, proposing the importance of the connection between “the 
exit location design of a room” and “the capacity the user tends to use in emergency evacuation” （Sime
and Kimyra, 1988）. It is perceived that both the user’s physical fitness and behavior characteristics make a 
great influence to evacuation, and that in comparison with the standard step length in the legislation, it
takes different time for different people to walk for the same distance. Consequently, every advanced
country in the world start moving forward to meet the need of safety by verifying safe design with
computer estimate system, based on science.
2.2 Current Problems of Evacuation Simulation
Generaly, when the architect’s working on functional design of a building, how can he enable the user to 
safely and effectively evacuate to a place of safety? There are two methods to meet the need. One is the
full-scale evacuation drill, and the other is to comply with the building laws (Gwynne et al., 1999). A
full-scale drill might confront moral and financial feasibility problem, on the other hand, complying with
the building laws can merely reach its lowest design standard without safety assurance. Current computer
evacuation simulation provides the possibility for solution, and satisfies the designer’s needs（Still, 1993）.
Pooch considers that model simulation contains the following disadvantages：1. significant hypotheses may
increase the diversity between the model and the reality；2. the parameter of the model may be difficult to
initialize with a need of lots of time to collect, analyze, and interpret（Pooch and Wall, 1993）. Despite that
models have been applied to numerous building designs excluded from building laws, they are not
accordingly suitable for every case (Chen, 2003). That proves the suitability of the inserting data during
computerized simulation has an extreme influence the out-coming results.

3.3 Evacuation Behavior Planning and Research
1. Questionnaire Method: Owing to the restricted labor and financial capacities, the test taker are simply
those who participate in the practical evacuation drill. Processing as general survey, all the test takers
received an explanation to the questionnaire before providing them, and the questionnaires are required to
be taken home to fill out and mail them back to the research team for collection. There are at last 835
feedback questionnaires collected with 738 effective ones out of all. The actual reply rate is 51%. Amid the
testers, there are, by sex, 31.4% males and 66.4% females; by educational background, 76.4% high school
graduates, 12.1% college graduates, and 11.5% masters; and by age, 75% under 17 and 25% between18-65.
2. Observation Method: Considering the insufficient mobilization, the exits of the highly dangerous area
(3-5F) in the arena are set up as the video recording observation area, and half of the area is chosen to be
the field observation area (see Figure 2 (C)) for the symmetrical stand with the exits and the identical width
of every exit. 12 staffs participate in the video recording observation. And 11of they do the recording with a
video camera respectively at 5 floor exits, 1 walkway, three stairs, 1 safety inspection gate, and 1 main exit
while the rest stay in the central control room to do synchronous recording with the others by radio at the
same time the evacuation drill begins.
4. DATA ANALYSIS
4.1 Questionnaire Result Analysis
Mental independence during the evacuation determines one’s evacuation behaviors; consequentialy, this 
study tends to find out the way people think during the evacuation through the questionnaire, used to
investigate the difference between the design, legislation, and operation of the arena and the practical
evacuation psychology.
4.1.1 Design and Simulation Investigate
In general, people usually go watch a show in company with the others; therefore, when a fire burst out,
the evacuation behavior will possibly be the flow in numerous small groups. The study shows that 3/4
testers will take the security of their companions and family in to consideration of the evacuation way.
Relevant studies indicate that the general flow rate of the children and elderly is 1.10 m/s and the flow rate
is 0.8 m/s (Building Research Institute, 1977) , and those of adults are 1.52 m/s and 1.0m/s while the flow
rate of women with children is merely 0.72m/s (Chen, 2003). Apparently, it slows down the egress time of
adults when evacuating with children and the elderly in smal groups, and when evaluating the building’s 
safety with functional legislations for design, to prevent over optimistic evaluation results, the architects
should take the influence of the average age of the smal groups instead of that of the whole. On “egress 
route” item, the higher proportion (516 people, 80.8%) of the testers choose “folow the directions of 
emergency guiders” rather than the two egress characteristics, “run to the near” (223 people, 34.9%) or 
“homing”(52people, 8.1%) (Yoshiteru Murosaki, 1993), found in the past researches to the evacuee
characteristics. This allows a rethought of the researcher on the input values adjustment in the simulation
analysis.
4.1.2 Legislation Discussion
Fire alarm, broadcast, and exit indicators are the most important “trigger” during the whole evacuation. 
Regarded as an environmental condition in a static drill with no presenting shows or activities, the fire
alarm in this case is not heard by up to 31% (221 people) and definitely higher under an activity or a show.
Furthermore, 5.6% unaware of the broadcast, 19.4% vague about the broadcast content, and 25.1% has
difficulty in deciphering exits indicators. It is perceived, by a cross analysis of the above three, that 31.8%
(235 people) fail to hear either the alarm or the broadcast thoroughly, 20.5% (151 people) fail to hear the
broadcast with a clear understanding, 35.4% (261 people) fail to clearly hear the broadcast and understand
the exit indicators. That is to say, over 1/3 of the testers fail to acquaint themselves with the egress
information, which influences the egress judgments, upon this result, the architects should rethink about the
efficiency of volume design, broadcasting method and exit indicator design.
As the slope design of the highly dangerous area (3-5F) is concerned, up to 49.6%(366 people) think it’s 
poorly designed, and over 20% fell down or staggered. Based on evacuation viewpoints, steep slopes cause
the evacuee to have fear or fall down. The revise of the design standard is needed.
4.1.3 Operation Management Discussion
As to the familiarization of the building, up to 59.4% testers are unfamiliar with each entrance and exit of
the arena possibly because of first-time admission or less observation. Improvement of exit instructions
before the show is accordingly required.

4.2.2 Drill Result Analysis
Comparing the flow rates of the drill and the leaving situation, it is perceived that the flow rate in the drill
(the average at each exit is 2.37m/s, 1.51m/s in the walkway, 0.58 m/s downstairs) is faster than after a
performance (the average at each exit is 0.84m/s, 0.64m/s in the walkway, 0.46 m/s downstairs), revealing
an easier leaving in the drill. The result is in concern with the low presence (71.7%) of the drill participants.
Instead of the supposed 2556 participants to fill up the highly dangerous area (2170 people), only 1556
people present, and the low density of people directly affects the flow rate. When doing full-scale or
regional drill, the pretence should be regarded as basic information for analysis, avoiding from result values
being misapplied by the researcher or the architect.
5. DISCUSSION AND SUGGESTION
From the analysis and discussion described above, this study, based on three aspects, the input values,
legislations and operation management, concludes the following discussions and suggestions for the
architects and operators as references.
1. Design and Simulation
(1) System simulation is operated to directly define the age level, neglecting “smal groups’ evacuation 
identity” through practical performance observation – That’s, evacuating with children and the elderly
slows down the speed of adults. In consequence, to prevent from misapplying over-optimistic assumed
input values and diverging form the practical situation, it’s suggested to differentiate activity types and to 
evaluate possible condition spread of small groups when doing evacuation simulation.
(2) It’s perceived through this study analysis that when the ratio of total width of every exit to the stairs at 
specific areas reaches 1.7 (bottleneck ratio), the flow rate downstairs is close to the minimum rate input in
SIMULEX, indicating a high risk in a practical evacuation. The architects should reduce the chance of
bottleneck in egress route as possible or reconsider to establish the history values of bottleneck ratios.
(3)To the problems of follow-the-crowd, the architects should best define the width of the exit as the
width causing less follow-the-crowd condition. For those builds with completed design or under operation,
the direction of guiders in an emergency can be considered to prevent form the possibility to lengthen
egress distance because of automatic extraction or movement.
2. Regulations
(1) To the problem about the uneasily- perceived alarm activities, it’s suggested to revise the standard of 
fire alarm and exit indicator clearness to enable a clear hearing and vision of fire alarm and the content or

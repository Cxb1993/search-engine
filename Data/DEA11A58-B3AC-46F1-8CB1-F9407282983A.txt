Abstract  
The objective of this 3-year project is to find a way of using spent activated clay (SAC) as adsorbent. The 
1st year was focus on: investigation of the suitable pretreatment methods for SAC, determining the 
physical-chemical properties of the treated-SAC, and testing the adsorption characteristics of dyes and Cu onto 
SAC. Results show that surface area of SAC increased from 1.85 to 7.42 m2/g after pretreatment. Kinetics 
experiments indicated that equilibrium adsorption could be attained within one hour. Modified Freundlich 
model fitting results showed that steam-treated-SAC exhibited a higher adsorption rate than the others. The 
equilibrium adsorption data were correlated well to the Langmuir isotherm. Results also showed that the 
adsorption affinity and adsorption maximum of methylene blue (MB) is greater than that of Congo red. The 
2nd year was focus on the following targets: further developing a best regeneration method for SAC, 
characterize the adsorption of MB, Pb, and Ni onto the SAC. It was found that the SAC treated by pressure 
cooker (SP) exhibits a highest MB removal. Double exponential model fitting show that the amount of MB, 
Pb, and Ni adsorption and mass transfer rates speed up as increased with increasing pH and temperature. The 
equilibrium data was well fitted by multilayer adsorption isotherm. The adsorption capacities for MB ranged 
from 9.70×10-5 to 3.41×10-4 mol/g. The MB adsorption is spontaneous and endothermic. Results reveal that 
the removal efficiency for Pb is higher than that of Ni. MB exhibits a stronger adsorption tendency in the 
competitive adsorption system of MB+Pb+Ni. Results indicate that intensity of competition has the following 
order: MB > Pb > Ni. The 3rd year was focus on studying the adsorption characteristics of Cr(VI), Cr(III), and 
F- ions onto SAC. The kinetic experimental data could be described well by pseudo 1st order equation and the 
equilibrium data was all fitted to Langmuir isotherm. Cr(VI) and F- have better effect in the acidic solution 
while Cr(III) has better adsorption in the alkaline solution. It is concluded that the SAC can be used as as 
adsorbent for treating the wastewater containg dye, divalent metals, chromium, and fluoride ions. The 
information should provide an important scope for utilization of SAC in metal-dye-bearing wastewaters. 
 
Keywords: adsorption, chromium, competition adsorption, copper, fluoride, dye, lead, heavy metal, nickel,  
spent activated clay

吸附法為可行之廢水處理技術，可為三級處理程序之選項。選用適當之吸附劑，不但可有效移除
廢水中重金屬及色度，具有污泥量低及可回收金屬之優勢，已廣泛被廢水處理廠採用。目前，最常用
之商業用吸附劑為活性碳，但此吸附材昂貴、再生程序繁瑣且維護操作費也高。整體上，非傳統性吸
附材在廢水處理之操作維護費低，有時候不必考慮再生，很適用於非開發中國家。過去研究指出，利
用廢棄物或低成本材料做為吸附劑可有效移除水中污染物，諸如綠藻(Donmez et al. 2002)、汙泥灰
(Selvaraj et al. 2003)，木屑(Garg et al. 2004)、椰子殼纖維(Verma et al. 2006)等、廢棄農業產品(Marshall et 
al. 2001)、骨炭(Cheung et al. 2001)、紅泥漿(Yunus et al. 2002)。最近 Mohan 及 Pittman Jr. (2006)彙整去
除 Cr(VI)之非傳統性吸附材，大致可分三大項: (1) 生質、(2) 農業及工業廢棄物、 (3) 奈米級顆粒。  
本計畫全期共 3 年，主要研究目的為將廢棄土 (SAC)資源再生為吸附劑，學術研究價值性，
未來實際應用上具高度期望。。第 1 年針對下列主題進行研究：1.探討前處理程序對 SAC 之物化性
質之影響，2.測試白土及經不同處理方式後之 SAC 對染料及重金屬之吸附狀況。第 2 年度針對下列主
題進行研究：1. 測試再生廢白土對 MB 之吸附效能，進而確定最佳再生操作方法；2. 利用再生效
率最佳之廢白土吸附 MB、Pb 及 Ni，並探討單一與競爭吸附行為。第 3 年針對探討再生廢白土對污
染物(Cr(VI)、Cr(III)、F-)之吸附特性。

並測定MB濃度。 
MB 吸附試驗: 將廢白土再生後，經由其對於 Methylene blue 之吸附試驗，可得壓力鍋再生後

4.1.1 特性分析 
圖8為不同pH值下，活性白土、廢白土及壓力鍋再生廢白土之界達電位。隨著溶液pH値增加，其

當pH値大於2.4時，活性白土及廢白土之表面帶負電荷；當pH値低於2.4時，其表面則帶正電荷。顯示廢
白土所吸附之油脂及色素不影響其表面帶電情形。經壓力鍋清洗處理之廢白土，在pH値為2至10之範圍
內皆為負電荷，即-50 ~ -100 mV。故可預期其對於陽離子型污染物能有較高之吸附移除效率。

4.1.2 再生廢白土對 MB 之動力吸附試驗 
研究中廢白土之再生方式可分為酸或鹼液清洗、界面活性劑清洗、超音波震盪、加熱及利用壓力
鍋等處理方式進行清除廢棄活性白土中所含之雜質或油脂等殘留物質。為瞭解廢白土以不同再生法處
理後作為吸附劑之吸附效能，故以 Methylene blue 染料作為指標性吸附質，於溶液 pH 値為 5.5，0.2 g
之再生廢白土及 MB 與離子強度(NaNO3)之濃度分別為 3×10-5 及 5×10-2 M 之操作條件下進行動力吸附
試驗。由圖 9 可顯示再生廢白土對 MB 之動力吸附情形可依再生方式分為兩部份探討，第一部份為以
去離子水清洗、加溫沖洗及超音波震盪等物理性再生處理；第二部分係利用酸、鹼液或界面活性劑清
洗，屬化學處理程序。經第一部分再生後之廢白土對於 MB 之吸附效能明顯優於第二部份之再生方式，
亦即以物理性方式處理廢白土之再生效率較高，且不會產生酸鹼等有害廢液，故亦不會對環境造成二
次污染。

4.6.1 MB 動力競爭吸附系統 
MB 之競爭吸附系統係指當 MB 溶液中共存 Pb 或 Ni 時，其對於 MB 吸附量或吸附速率之影響，

4.6.2 Pb 動力競爭吸附系統 
圖 24 為 Pb 系統之動力競爭吸附試驗結果，競爭吸附質為 MB 與 Ni。由圖可知單一 Pb 溶液之吸

4.1.1 不同再生方式之廢白土對 Cr(VI)之動力吸附 
為了解顆粒活性碳、活性白土、SAC與不同處理方式再生廢棄活性白土對於吸附溶液中Cr(VI)動力
吸附之影響，遂以顆粒活性碳、活性白土及經不同方式處理之廢棄活性白土，其處理方式有超音波、
壓力鍋(Pressure cooker)、RO純水、陽離子型界面活性劑CPC、陰離子型界面活性劑SDS、咖啡機(Steam)、
熱處理(Heat)、酸洗(0.1N HCl)、鹼洗(0.1N NaOH)、高溫焚化(600oC)、高溫焚化後以咖啡機清洗(600oC
＋Steam)等不同方式，以了解不同處理方式再生之吸附劑對Cr(VI)之動力吸附情形，並選擇其中再生效
率最高的再生SAC作為本研究之吸附劑。 
動力實驗結果如圖26 所示，實線為pseudo 1st order模式模擬所得結果，結果顯示，14種不同吸附
劑皆能將Cr(VI)吸附去除。在初始濃度3.5 mg/L、溶液pH值2.0、離子強度0.05M NaClO4及24oC條件下，
反應90 min後，反應趨於平衡。實驗結果嘗試以Freundich、pseudo 1st order、pseudo 2nd order模式進行模
擬，所得之模擬結果，其中以pseudo 1st order模式模擬結果最佳，故本研究均以pseudo 1st order模式模擬

method used for compliance with regulation limits. This method is accomplished by two steps: (1)

Adsorption offers an alternative technology for removal of heavy metals and dyes from wastewater. 
With the selection of a proper adsorbent, the adsorption process can be a promising and effective 
technique for the removal of certain types of contaminants.3 Since adsorption is capable of 
removing trace amounts of organics and metals from aqueous solution, it has long been used as a 
tertiary treatment process in many wastewater treatment plants to comply with stringent effluent 
regulations. Adsorption can also allow metal recovery and generate less sludge as the adsorbent can 
be regenerated. As one of the most effective adsorbents to remove a variety of contaminants, 
activated carbon has been broadly applied for wastewater treatment. The activated carbon upon 
reaching its adsorption capacity, is either disposed to landfill or regenerated with elevated 
temperature. The use of activated carbon has the following disadvantages: high adsorbent cost, loss 
of adsorption capacity after each regeneration, and difficulties of operation. Thus, many researchers 
have focused on seeking the low-cost adsorbents for decontamination purposes. In particular, one 
thorough review paper 4 has looked into the low-cost adsorbents for heavy metal removal. To keep 
this issue on track, most recently, Mohan and Pittman Jr.1 have published one fruitful review paper 
on chromium removal by adsorption.  Basically, the broad low-cost adsorbents can be divided into 
3 categories: (1) biomass, (2) agriculture and industrial wastes, and (3) nano-sized particles.  
Despite the fact that low-cost adsorbents have been intensively studied for many years, study of the 
use of spent activated clay (SAC), an industrial waste, in treating wastewater enriched with Cr(VI) 
has not yet been found in literature. 
 
The present paper reports the results for kinetic and equilibrium studies of Cr(VI) adsorption onto 
SAC. Since the effectiveness of adsorption relies much on operational parameters, parameters 
including pH, temperature and ionic strength were evaluated. Both kinetic and equilibrium isotherm 
models were applied to establish the rate of adsorption, adsorption capacity, and the mechanism of 
Cr(VI) adsorption onto SAC. Results of this study will be useful for future scale up and can also 
serve as base-line data for using this waste as a low-cost adsorbent for treating Cr(VI)-rich 
effluents.

Equilibrium studies 
Effect of pH on the adsorption equilibrium 
Solution pH is the most important variable affecting the adsorption characteristics. Fig. 4a depicts 
the percentage of Cr(VI) removed as a function of equilibrium pH with 1 g/L of SAC solution 
containing 4.12 mg/L of Cr(VI). Blank test without adsorbent in the mixed suspension was run in 
parallel to monitor the interference of the adsorption procedure being conducted. As seen in Fig. 4a, 
no removal can be found for the blank test indicating that the SAC is the sole adsorbent proving for 
Cr(VI) removal.  Fig. 4a shows the adsorptive removal is small in the alkaline region. The marked 
adsorption was observed under acidic condition and the amount adsorbed decreased with increasing 
pH.  Up to 100% of Cr(VI) removal was achieved with 4 g/L of SAC in a solution containing 4.12 
mg/L Cr(VI). In general, chromate ion adsorption decreased abruptly at pH 2.0 – 4.0 value and then 
progressively decreases or remains almost constant over a wide pH range (4.0 – 7.0).The results 
shown in Fig. 4 demonstrate that the adsorption was closely related to the surface charge of the 
SAC. As mentioned previously, montmorillonite is the main component of activated clay. Due to 
ionization of functional groups developed on the surface of the clay edges, the surface charge of this 
clay is pH-dependent. Therefore, when the solution pH is less than the value of pHzpc (3.8), the SAC 
possesses a positively charged surface which would be favorable for anionic ion adsorption. The 
active surface functional groups at the clay edges has been well recognized as the silanol (Si–O-) 
and aluminol (Al–O-) sites as the main constituents of this clay are SiO2 (65 – 75%) and Al2O3 (15 – 
20%)8. Based on the speciation diagram shown in Fig. 4b, the major chromate ions coexisting in 
amphoteric surface functional groups. In this work, at pH value below pHzpc (3.8), the positively 
charged SAC surface (S–OH2+) would be favorable for the anionic hydrochromate, HCrO4−, 
adsorption whereupon the coulombic interaction force can readily take place. Noted that the 
hydro-chromate, HCrO4− (Fig. 4b), ion is now the dominant species involved in the formation of 
surface complexes below pH 3.8.  Hence, the adsorption capacity of HCrO4− will decrease due to 
the decrease of attractive surface charge of SAC. In Fig. 4, one can still see that a small amount of 
Cr(VI) was removed between pH 3.8 and 7.0 whereupon pH is greater than pHzpc. At this region, 
the neural surface hydroxyl functional group, S–OHo, may be the sole active site providing for both 
HCrO4− and CrO42− adsorption.  
 
As the solution pH increases, not only less functional group (S–OHo) is deprotonated but also more 
OH- is now competing with the coexistence of HCrO4− and CrO42− ions (Fig. 4b) for the active 
surface sites. Consequently, it is difficult for them to form complexes and the adsorbed amount will 
decrease. Thus, the possible mechanisms of surface complex reactions for the pH-dependent Cr(VI) 
adsorption onto SAC are proposed as follows:  
S−OH2+ + HCrO4−  =  S−HCrO4   + H2O ; Ks1  For pH < pHzpc (3.8)    (8a)  
or 
S–OHo   + HCrO4− =  S−HCrO4   + OH−  ; Ks1  For pH > pHzpc (3.8)   (8b)  
S–OHo  + CrO42−   =  S−CrO4−   + OH− ; Ks2  For pH > pHzpc (3.8)    (9)  
where S−HCrO4 and S−CrO4− are the formation of the bonding complexes; Ks1 and Ks2 (L/mol) are 
the stability constants of adsorbed species HCrO4− and CrO42−, respectively.

ABSTRACT 
The ability of an unconventional bio-adsorbent, pineapple leaf powder (PLP), to adsorb cationic dye, 
methylene blue (MB), from aqueous solution was studied. The adsorption characteristics affected 
by the factors: dye concentration, ionic strength, mixing speed, pH, and temperature were evaluated. 
It was observed that intraparticle diffusion is involved in the adsorption process and the data 
conformed well to a pseudo-2nd-order equation. Fitting parameters revealed that the rate of 
adsorption increased with decreasing dye concentration and ionic strength while the mixing speed 
did not show a significant effect. Batch equilibrium results indicated that the adsorption was 
favorable at higher pH and lower temperature. The adsorption equilibrium data was well fitted by 
the Langmuir adsorption isotherm. The PLP adsorption capacities of MB varied from 4.68×10-4 to 
9.28×10-4 mol/g in the pH range of 3.5–9.5. Values of ΔGo ranging from -24.76 to -28.19 kcal/mol 
suggest that the adsorption reaction is a physical process enhanced by the electrostatic effect. The 
values of ΔHo and ΔSo are -5.93 kJ/mol and 76.4 J/K-mol, respectively. The studies presented 
herewith revealed that adsorption of the MB dye increases with a decrease in the temperature, 
thereby indicating the process to be exothermic in nature. Work carried out has shown that this 
agricultural waste could be fruitfully employed as an alternative to existing commercial adsorbents 
for the removal of cationic dyes from wastewater.  
 
INTRODUCTION 
Various dyes are widely utilized as coloring agents in industries such as textiles, cosmetics, pulp 
mills, leather, dye synthesis, printing, food, and plastics, etc. Because many organic dyestuffs are 
harmful to human beings and hazardous to aquatic living organisms, removal of dyestuffs from 
wastewater has received considerable attention over the past decades.  Amongst the developed 
methods of decontamination, adsorption was recognized to be a cost-effective and useful process to 
remove colors from aqueous solution, in particular for those that are difficult to biodegrade. 
 
Even though activated carbon is the most widely employed adsorbent for the removal of color from 
textile due to its effectiveness and high adsorption capacity, its use is still restricted because of high 
operating cost. The need of regeneration and difficulty in separation from the wastewater after use

Effect of agitation speed 
In the batch adsorption system, agitation speed plays a significant role in affecting the external 
boundary film and the distribution of the solute in the bulk solution.16 Figure 4 shows the effect of 
agitation on the removal of MB onto PLP at three speeds, 200, 400, and 500 rpm. As can be seen 
from Fig. 4, with increasing agitation speed the adsorption equilibrium of MB almost remains 
unchanged. A similar trend was observed for various agitation speeds at different time. These 
observations can be explained by the fact that the boundary layer resistance is very small and the 
mobility of the system is high. In other words, the diffusion of MB ion from the solution to the 
surface of the PLP and into the pores occurs easily and quickly. The values of MFE fitting results 
are tabulated in Table 1.  The values of r2 for this kinetic model were found to be high (close to 
unity) along with the small value of normalize standard deviation s (%). Meanwhile, the calculated 
qe,cal values also agree well with the experimental value of qe,exp. It appears that the adsorption 
kinetic obeys the MFE for the entire sorption period. Normally, the rate of adsorption and adsorbed 
amount would increase with increasing the degree of agitation because the film boundary layer 
surrounding particles was reduced by increasing degree of agitation, thus the external film transfer 
coefficient increases, and hence the adsorption capacity.16 As shown in Table 1, the rate constant, k, 
and the uptake of MB were not influenced much by the degree of agitation.  An agitation speed of 
200 rpm was therefore chosen for further experiments.

Intra-particle diffusion (IPD) 
Normally, if the plot of the amount of dye adsorbed against t1/2 presents two or more intercepting 
lines, this indicates that the adsorption steps are independent of one another.24 Fig. 9 shows the IPD 
plots for the effects of initial MB concentrations. The results clearly exhibit that the plots presented 
two distinguishable intercepting lines which indicated that two steps occurred in the adsorption 
process.  The first intercepting line represents surface adsorption while the second one IPD. The 
slope of the second linear portion gives the intra-particle rate constant, kp, and the intercept of this 
portion is proportional to the thickness of boundary layer.   Table 4 lists the corresponding model 
fitting parameters.  The r2 values are high and the values of s are low for the second stage 
indicating that the adsorption mechanism follows the IPD process after 10 min.  Application of the 
IPD model has indicated that the MB adsorption onto PLP is the rate-determining step. It was found 
that the values of kp range from 9.90×10-9 to 5.29×10-7 mol/(min1/2-g) and increased with increasing 
initial MB concentration. The driving force of diffusion is very important for adsorption processes 
and is associated with the adsorbate concentration in the bulk solution. Generally, the increase of 
adsorbate concentration would result in an increase of driving force, which will increase the 
diffusion rate of MB.25 Since the results shown herewith do not go through the origin, this indicates 
that the intra-particle diffusion is not the sole rate limiting step.26 This confirms that the adsorption 
mechanism was a multi-step process, involving adsorption on the external surface and diffusion into 
the internal pores of the PLP.   As mentioned previously, the values of C (i.e., intercept) give an 
idea about the effect of boundary layer. As shown in Table 4 the values of C increase with 
increasing initial MB concentration. This indicates that a higher initial MB concentration would 
result in a greater boundary layer effect in the adsorption process. The increase in the value of C 
indicates the abundance of solute adsorbed on the boundary layer. A similar result of such effects 
show significant effect on the uptake of MB by PLP. With pH varying from 1.5 to 10.0, the 
adsorption increased with increasing solution pH. Particularly the adsorption increased rapidly as 
pH increased from 1.5 to 6.0. The extent of adsorption was found to remain nearly constant at pH 
values greater than 6.0. About 90% MB was removed within the range of pH 6.0-10.0. The 
adsorption of MB onto PLP appears affected much by the solution pH. Such dependence of 
adsorption on pH could be due in part to the fact that the surface charge of PLP was greatly affected 
by solution pH. Results of FTIR indicate that PLP comprised of various functional groups, such as 
hydroxyl (-OH) and carbonyl (>C=O) which could be affected by the pH. At alkaline pH, a 
significant high electrostatic attraction exists between positively charged cationic dye and the 
negatively charged PLP surface, due to the ionization of these functional groups of PLP. As shown 
previously the pHzpc of PLP particles was 2.3. The PLP has positively charged surface at pH less 
than the pHzpc. On the contrary, when pH is greater than the pHzpc, the negatively charged PLP 
surface is favorable for the adsorption of cationic MB dye. Thus the adsorption of basic MB dye 
decreased at a lower pH. Also, lower adsorption of MB at acidic condition is attributed in part to the 
presence of excess H+ ions destabilizing basic dye and competing with cationic dye ions for the 
adsorption sites. The effect of pH on the solid-liquid equilibrium may be described by the following 
possible mechanisms: 
(1). chemical interaction between hydroxyl and carboxyl groups of active component of PLP and 
the reactive group (Cl-) of MB. 
S−O− + MB+   = S−O−MB            (12) 
where S represents the active surface sites of PLP; S−O− refers to ionized surface functional groups 
of PLP. 
(2). weak electrostatic interaction between electron-rich sites of the PLP surface and MB.

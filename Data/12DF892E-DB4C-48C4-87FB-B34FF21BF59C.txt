typical office life events based on ubiquitous computing technologies.   
In brief, this study focuses on office space type to explore issues of smart space design based 
on a situated-life pattern approach by applying data mining techniques. The design problems of 
integrating ubiquitous computing devices with physical spatial components are explored and 
discussed.  
Keywords: Smart Space Design, Data Mining, Interactive Space Design 
 
三、前言 
近年來，有關智慧化空間或互動式空間設計的課題，正吸引眾多研究者的注意與探討，學術
單位積極透入，結合機電、設計與自動控制等專長的研究者，進行跨領域的研究合作。政府機關也
積極推動 E 化與 M 化的資訊基礎建設，內政部建築研究所也從建築永續與智慧化技術結合的觀點，
著手推動智慧化生活空間的願景與發展。伴隨資訊科技的發展，以及高齡化社會時代的來臨，建築
物在面臨諸如安全、便利、健康與舒適的問題時，如何規劃與設計智慧化空間，以符合未來智慧化
生活環境需求，已成為重要的研究課題。 
有關智慧化互動式空間的研究發展，大致可分為兩個方向：1) 技術發展導向；與 2) 空間設
計導向。技術發展導向普遍從資訊科技應用的觀點切入，發展各類型具有自動感知與網路通訊能力
的資訊裝置設備，提供具有諸如便利、安全、節能或舒適的所謂智慧化的資訊設備與裝置；空間設
計導向則是從空間設計應用的觀點，安裝智慧化資訊設備裝置於建築室內空間，探討智慧化資訊設
備與裝置的應用性與潛力。觀察這兩個研究向度，可發現技術導向普遍欠缺從使用者的觀點出發，
探討智慧化資訊設備與裝置的使用者行為與適用性；而空間設計導向雖然理論上設計者應該為空間
使用者把關，但在資訊科技的洪流下，一般設計者亦不免被技術牽引，僅能從技術應用的觀點，探
討傳統空間設計應用的方式。換言之，一般以技術導向觀點所開發的智慧化資訊設備裝置，由於欠
缺從空間設計者與空間使用者的觀點，進行智慧化資訊設備裝置的適用性評估，因此往往難以符合
空間設計者與空間使用者的需求，造成使用者接受度與滿意度上的相關問題。 
資料採礦（Data Mining）是從資料中發現隱含的知識型樣（Patterns），例如法則、限制與規
則等，以彌補專家知識取得不易的難題。資料採礦所發現的隱藏知識，可以提供作為決策參考
[Adriaans and Zantinge, 1996]。情境式生活模式設計觀點，則以日常生活需求為分析基礎，探討居
住者日常生活中特定發生的事件，據以進行以使用者需求導向的空間設計。針對前述欠缺使用者導
向的智慧化空間設計問題，本研究擬採用資採礦技術結合情境式生活模式分析，進行智慧化空間設
計的使用者行為與空間環境資料分析，期望以資料運算的觀點，找尋隱含的、使用者導向的智慧化
空間設計知識，以輔助空間設計者進行智慧化空間設計。  
四、研究目的 
本研究認為智慧化空間在技術層面發展有其重要性，也值得持續關注，但在人性化科技的潮
流下，現階段更應該回頭檢視空間使用者真正的需求為何？空間互動的意義與價值到底為何？智慧
化生活環境所揭示的「遍佈運算（Ubiquitous Computing）」所代表的空間設計意義到底是什麼？智
慧化生活概念對於空間設計是否有革命性的變革與指引？這些都是本計畫企圖找尋解答的研究問
題。

決策參考。 
六、研究方法 
本計畫「資料採礦輔助智慧化互動式空間設計之研究」，以資料採礦技術結合情境式生活模式
分析的觀點，進行使用者導向的智慧化空間設計課題探討，研究方法包括有文獻回顧、設計實驗、
系統開發與設計創作等。各方法要點與原因說明如下： 
z 文獻回顧：本計畫蒐集國內外有關「智慧化互動式空間設計」之重要研究文獻，鎖定重
點研究單位、研究團隊、SCI 學術期刊與國際學術研討會等，進行相關文獻收集。其中
美國麻省理工學院媒體實驗室(Media Lab)、卡內基美侖大學智慧化工作場所(Intelligent 
Work Place)、喬治亞理工學院環境智慧研究群(Ambient Intelligence Group)，以及國內成
功大學資訊建築實驗室(Information Architecture Lab)等，將列為主要文獻收集對象。本計
畫藉由文獻收集與回顧的研究方法，釐清研究課題與探討內容，作為研究立論的基礎。 
z 設計實驗：本計畫探討辦公室類型的智慧化互動式空間設計的使用者行為課題，擬透過
設計實驗方法，針對辦公室類型的使用者空間，進行智慧化互動式空間設計實驗。本計
畫將架設安裝互動式設備裝置，蒐集辦公空間中使用者行為的相關資料，然後再以資料
採礦技術進行使用者空間互動資料分析；本計畫企圖透過空間設計實驗的方法，探討使
用者導向之智慧化互動式空間設計所面臨的問題，並提出解決方案。

(三)日常生活模式難以定義的本質 
本計畫透過情境式生活模式的分析觀點，雖然空間系統雛型開發的空間組件，包括智慧門、
智慧牆與智慧桌等，能夠提供支援辦公室特定生活模式的需求服務。然而，空間使用者的生活模式，
就如同設計本質一樣，具有難以定義與預測的特性。在辦公室使用者日常生活中，往往有某些非預
期的事件發生，例如臨時性的會議、學生有急事討論或者突發事件等，本計畫開發的智慧化空間系
統雛型尚無法處理非預期的辦公室生活事件。因此，後續研究可針對本計畫開發的辦公空間系統雛
型，進行智慧化空間非預期空間事件之問題探討，並提出有效解決方案，以改善目前本研究辦公空
間系統雛型的智慧化性能表現。 
十、結論 
本計畫提出以情境式生活模式分析觀點，進行使用者導向的智慧化空間設計，並且結合資料
採礦與遍佈運算技術進行一個智慧化辦公空間的更新設計案，研究發現情境式生活模式的分析觀
點，對於設計符合居住者需求的智慧化空間深具潛力。本計畫辦公空間系統雛型開發的智慧門、智
慧牆與智慧桌等空間組件，能夠支援辦公室特定生活模式的使用者服務需求，並且整合實體空間元
素產生人性化的空間介面，提供智慧化空間設計與資訊互動的有效解決方案。而有關智慧化空間面
臨的設計問題，例如空間系統雛型效能表現、人性化空間介面設計以及日常生活事件難以定義的特
質等，也在研究中進行課題討論並提出後續研究之建議。 
透過本計畫對於智慧化空間設計的探討，可發現隨著遍佈運算技術的發展，可預見未來空間
設計也將產生很大的變革，人類將會越來越習慣與實體空間元件進行資訊互動，透過空間元件進行
資訊處理以支援日常生活需求，而如何整合空間設計與遍佈運算科技以進行設計構想與設計決策，
也值得設計師與電腦工程師進一步共同探究。

3. A Situated Life Pattern Approach 
To design a smart space to support daily lives, the study believes the key 
factor is to map occupants’ situated-life patterns to the computing 
infrastructure and the physical space. To illustrate the design ideas, this pilot 
study presents a smart space project for a typical office daily life in the paper. 
An office of a college teacher is re-designed as an example to illustrate how 
to integrate ubiquitous computing technologies with physical space to serve 
the needs of occupants. The regular office life patterns of Prof. Lan can be 
described as the following scenario: 
Everyday in the morning, Prof. Lan walks into his office. When he stands in front 
of his office, he is identified and allowed to get into his office by the smart door 
at the entrance. His status of “inside office” triggers a spatial event to display 
the daily schedule on the smart wall. He notices his students will come to his 
office for group meeting later. After he works for a while in front of his computer, 
the computer shows a video image to inform him that his students are just in 
front of his office. He sends a command from his computer to the smart door and 
let them get into his office. During the group meeting, the students present their 
projects by plugging a tablet computer into the smart wall. The smart wall 
shows the related information regarding with specific design project. Lan uses 
an electronic pen to open a design drawing and add some annotations on the 
smart wall during the discussion. These annotations are stored and linked with 
the design drawing for further reference. In the afternoon, during the tea time 
break, one of Lan’s colleague stops at his office. He is unscheduled but still 
welcome to enter Lan’s office by the smart door. He wants to chat and discuss 
with Lan about a new design project he is doing and asks for some comments. 
Lan uses a smart table to connect to the website of the new design project in his 
colleague’s computer. They use the smart table to display and discuss related 
design information of this project. They use electronic pens to draw sketches and 
add annotations on the smart table. These sketches and annotations can also be 
stored with the related design information of the project.  
By observing the scenario, we can find there are typical office events 
occurring regularly. These patterns of events are underlined in the scenario 
and could be roughly classified as personal identification, daily schedule 
noticing, group meeting, project presentation, information processing, and 
social interaction. In order to narrow down the research scope, the paper 
only explores three typical office life patterns, including user identification, 
group meeting and social interaction. These three office life patterns are the 
main design objectives to serve for the occupant in developing the smart 
space project.

6. Conclusion 
This paper proposes a situated life pattern approach to design a smart space 
to support typical office daily life based on ubiquitous computing 
technologies. The situated life pattern approach provides a potential way to 
design a smart space to fit in with occupants’ needs. Based on the situated 
life pattern approach, the study develops a smart space project with smart 
door, smart wall, and small table to verify the design solutions to support 
office daily life. The design problems encountered and important issues 
regarding with system performance, natural spatial interfaces, and the ill-
defined features of life patterns are discussed.  
In the study, we can envision there is likely a great change in space 
design with the development of ubiquitous computing technologies. People 
will get used to interact with physical spatial components for information 
processing to support the needs of everyday life. The design strategies and 
design concepts to integrate ubiquitous computing technologies are needed 
to study and explore further.

2 Literature review 
Work design is defined as designing work environment (Kishimoto, Naka et 
al., 2007). The work design contains four aims: including interaction, 
visualization, inspiration trigger, and tracking. Becker and Steele indicate 
there are three key elements related with work decisions, including work 
processes, information technology, and physical settings (Becker and Steele, 
1994).  
Goleman indicates emotion is a feeling and distinctive thought, which 
reveals the psychological state, biological state, and the behavioral 
propensity of a person (Goleman, 1995). Although there are many more 
subtleties of emotion than we have words for, however, Goleman classified 
emotions into eight families and argued that all the emotions should belong 
to one of these families. These families of emotions are: anger, sadness, fear, 
enjoyment, love, surprise, disgust, and shame. In turn, our emotions change 
the way we think, serve as constant guides to appropriate behavior, steers us 
away from the bad, and guides us toward the good. Human being is 
emotional and emotions thus influence our everyday behavior and as a 
consequence emotions affect our work (Martinez-Miranda and Aldea, 2004).  
Based on the literature reviews, we can see the emotions influence the 
behavior of a typical designer during design processes. Of course, there are 
various types of human emotions. Some emotions appear positive to assist 
design work. On the other hand, some emotions seem negative, which may 
affect designers to do unusual work or even lead to bad design results. Of 
course, sometimes, a negative emotion, such as anxiety, may also push a 
designer to work with effectiveness. These literature reviews form the 
foundations to explore the research issues in the study.  
3 The Research Scope 
In this study, we are interested in how to assist design activity from an 
emotional approach in developing a smart workplace project. The key 
problems are how to detect the designer’s emotional state and how to 
response wisely to assist design activity by applying ubiquitous computing 
technologies. The research scopes then include:  
z How to analyze and classify the emotional state of a designer? 
z How to detect the emotional state of a designer in a workplace? 
z For each emotional pattern detected, what kind of smart mechanism 
should be provided to support design activity?

approach is to augment ubiquitous computing devices into a designer's 
workplace. We monitor and record the design activities of a typical designer 
during the design processes. Based on the recorded data, an intensive survey 
to the designer followed to discover the emotional patterns during the design 
processes. These discovered patterns are analyzed and classified based on

4.3 SPATIAL EVENTS TO ASSIST DESIGN ACTIVITY 
In order to explore the research intention of assisting design activity from an 
emotional approach, there are two assumptions to establish the research 
foundation in the study. The first assumption is designers can be more 
creative while they are at positive emotional states in a personal workplace. 
The second assumption is a negative emotional state of a designer should be 
improved in order to support positive design activity via a proper spatial 
event.  
Based on the two assumptions, the study starts to explore how to 
response a typical emotional pattern of a designer appropriately by a 
corresponding spatial event. A set of spatial event scripts corresponding with 
various emotional patterns of a typical designer then are set up to support the 
needs of the designer from a human-sense approach. At current stage, the 
human senses of sight, hearing and smell are explored to support the 
capabilities of improving the emotional state of a designer by a typical 
spatial event. Two spatial events corresponding with positive and negative 
emotional state to assist design activity are briefly described as follows.  
For instance, once the designer is detected at a positive emotional state, 
such as relaxed, that means the designer desires to see some beautiful 
environmental scenery, to hear some joyful music and to smell some nice 
fragrance. The corresponding spatial events then are triggered to support the 
needs of the designer. On another hand, when a designer is detected at a 
negative emotional state, such as sad, based on the triggered spatial event, 
the system will try to comfort him by showing some proper message, giving 
him some advices to take a rest, or playing some comfortable music, to 
improve the bad emotional state. The notion of assisting design activities

5 Summary 
The research adopts an emotional approach to develop a smart workplace in 
assisting design activities during design processes. In this pilot study, the 
overall research scopes are explored. The emotional patterns of a typical 
designer are analyzed and labeled for pattern recognition. These emotional 
patterns can be detected by the developed mechanism. Besides, based on 
some assumptions, for each emotional pattern detected, a corresponding 
spatial event will be triggered to assist design activity during design process. 
A spatial system prototype with overall framework of personal workplace is 
developed to verify the research intention.  
For further studies, the spatial system prototype need to do more rigorous 
evaluation tests since it is developed based on some subjective assumptions. 
The system prototype can only show the preliminary achievements of overall 
research scopes. Besides, based on our observations, the spatial system 
prototype could successfully detect the emotional state of a typical designer 
and trigger corresponding spatial event. However, the designer shows low 
satisfaction to the system performance from various points of views. 
Therefore, a more complete understanding of the empirical study to support 
the design work from an emotional approach is still need to be studied 
further in the future.

3. A Situated Life Pattern Approach 
To design a smart space to support daily lives, the study believes the key 
factor is to map occupants’ situated-life patterns to the computing 
infrastructure and the physical space. To illustrate the design ideas, this pilot 
study presents a smart space project for a typical office daily life in the paper. 
An office of a college teacher is re-designed as an example to illustrate how 
to integrate ubiquitous computing technologies with physical space to serve 
the needs of occupants. The regular office life patterns of Prof. Lan can be 
described as the following scenario: 
Everyday in the morning, Prof. Lan walks into his office. When he stands in front 
of his office, he is identified and allowed to get into his office by the smart door 
at the entrance. His status of “inside office” triggers a spatial event to display 
the daily schedule on the smart wall. He notices his students will come to his 
office for group meeting later. After he works for a while in front of his computer, 
the computer shows a video image to inform him that his students are just in 
front of his office. He sends a command from his computer to the smart door and 
let them get into his office. During the group meeting, the students present their 
projects by plugging a tablet computer into the smart wall. The smart wall 
shows the related information regarding with specific design project. Lan uses 
an electronic pen to open a design drawing and add some annotations on the 
smart wall during the discussion. These annotations are stored and linked with 
the design drawing for further reference. In the afternoon, during the tea time 
break, one of Lan’s colleague stops at his office. He is unscheduled but still 
welcome to enter Lan’s office by the smart door. He wants to chat and discuss 
with Lan about a new design project he is doing and asks for some comments. 
Lan uses a smart table to connect to the website of the new design project in his 
colleague’s computer. They use the smart table to display and discuss related 
design information of this project. They use electronic pens to draw sketches and 
add annotations on the smart table. These sketches and annotations can also be 
stored with the related design information of the project.  
By observing the scenario, we can find there are typical office events 
occurring regularly. These patterns of events are underlined in the scenario 
and could be roughly classified as personal identification, daily schedule 
noticing, group meeting, project presentation, information processing, and 
social interaction. In order to narrow down the research scope, the paper 
only explores three typical office life patterns, including user identification, 
group meeting and social interaction. These three office life patterns are the 
main design objectives to serve for the occupant in developing the smart 
space project.

6. Conclusion 
This paper proposes a situated life pattern approach to design a smart space 
to support typical office daily life based on ubiquitous computing 
technologies. The situated life pattern approach provides a potential way to 
design a smart space to fit in with occupants’ needs. Based on the situated 
life pattern approach, the study develops a smart space project with smart 
door, smart wall, and small table to verify the design solutions to support 
office daily life. The design problems encountered and important issues 
regarding with system performance, natural spatial interfaces, and the ill-
defined features of life patterns are discussed.  
In the study, we can envision there is likely a great change in space 
design with the development of ubiquitous computing technologies. People 
will get used to interact with physical spatial components for information 
processing to support the needs of everyday life. The design strategies and 
design concepts to integrate ubiquitous computing technologies are needed 
to study and explore further.

2 Literature review 
Work design is defined as designing work environment (Kishimoto, Naka et 
al., 2007). The work design contains four aims: including interaction, 
visualization, inspiration trigger, and tracking. Becker and Steele indicate 
there are three key elements related with work decisions, including work 
processes, information technology, and physical settings (Becker and Steele, 
1994).  
Goleman indicates emotion is a feeling and distinctive thought, which 
reveals the psychological state, biological state, and the behavioral 
propensity of a person (Goleman, 1995). Although there are many more 
subtleties of emotion than we have words for, however, Goleman classified 
emotions into eight families and argued that all the emotions should belong 
to one of these families. These families of emotions are: anger, sadness, fear, 
enjoyment, love, surprise, disgust, and shame. In turn, our emotions change 
the way we think, serve as constant guides to appropriate behavior, steers us 
away from the bad, and guides us toward the good. Human being is 
emotional and emotions thus influence our everyday behavior and as a 
consequence emotions affect our work (Martinez-Miranda and Aldea, 2004).  
Based on the literature reviews, we can see the emotions influence the 
behavior of a typical designer during design processes. Of course, there are 
various types of human emotions. Some emotions appear positive to assist 
design work. On the other hand, some emotions seem negative, which may 
affect designers to do unusual work or even lead to bad design results. Of 
course, sometimes, a negative emotion, such as anxiety, may also push a 
designer to work with effectiveness. These literature reviews form the 
foundations to explore the research issues in the study.  
3 The Research Scope 
In this study, we are interested in how to assist design activity from an 
emotional approach in developing a smart workplace project. The key 
problems are how to detect the designer’s emotional state and how to 
response wisely to assist design activity by applying ubiquitous computing 
technologies. The research scopes then include:  
z How to analyze and classify the emotional state of a designer? 
z How to detect the emotional state of a designer in a workplace? 
z For each emotional pattern detected, what kind of smart mechanism 
should be provided to support design activity?

4.3 SPATIAL EVENTS TO ASSIST DESIGN ACTIVITY 
In order to explore the research intention of assisting design activity from an 
emotional approach, there are two assumptions to establish the research 
foundation in the study. The first assumption is designers can be more 
creative while they are at positive emotional states in a personal workplace. 
The second assumption is a negative emotional state of a designer should be 
improved in order to support positive design activity via a proper spatial 
event.  
Based on the two assumptions, the study starts to explore how to 
response a typical emotional pattern of a designer appropriately by a 
corresponding spatial event. A set of spatial event scripts corresponding with 
various emotional patterns of a typical designer then are set up to support the 
needs of the designer from a human-sense approach. At current stage, the 
human senses of sight, hearing and smell are explored to support the 
capabilities of improving the emotional state of a designer by a typical 
spatial event. Two spatial events corresponding with positive and negative 
emotional state to assist design activity are briefly described as follows.  
For instance, once the designer is detected at a positive emotional state, 
such as relaxed, that means the designer desires to see some beautiful 
environmental scenery, to hear some joyful music and to smell some nice 
fragrance. The corresponding spatial events then are triggered to support the 
needs of the designer. On another hand, when a designer is detected at a 
negative emotional state, such as sad, based on the triggered spatial event, 
the system will try to comfort him by showing some proper message, giving 
him some advices to take a rest, or playing some comfortable music, to 
improve the bad emotional state. The notion of assisting design activities

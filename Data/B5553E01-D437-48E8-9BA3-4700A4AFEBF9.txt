IV. THEORETICAL ANALYSES
In order to realize the carrier transport
property in the TW heterostructures, we start
with the calculation of transmission coefficient
T*T through the TW structures by using the
transfer matrix approach and then figure out the
tunneling current with the T*T calculated. When
a TW structure is under bias, the wave function
of an electron in any layer can be expressed as a
linear combination of Airy function Ai() and
complementary Airy function Bi() [12]. By
solving Schrodinger’s equation in each layer and 
then matching the continuities of wave function
and probability flux at each interface, the
calculation is accomplished. The influences of
temperature and conduction-band
nonparabolicity are also taken into account.

respectively. Owing to the electrons injecting
from the cathode layer cancel the electric field in
W1 [13], the voltage drop is expected to take
place not in W1 but mostly in W2 and W3 , as
shown in Fig. 1(b). Taking the conduction
band-edge potential on the left-hand side of the
TW structure as reference, the one-dimensional

and transmission coefficients T*T of the TW
structure of device R502 under different external

By using the wave functions calculated, the
electron concentrations in wells 1W , 2W , and
3W can be estimated. The peak value of the
electron concentration in 1W (
317 cm101  ) is
much larger than that in 2W (
315 cm108  )
and that in 3W (
315 cm105  ) due to the
relation among the subbands and the Fermi
energy ( 302010 EEEE F  ). As an external
bias is applied to the TW structure, a voltage
drop is expected to occur almost in 2W and 3W ,
as the previous mentioned, because the electric
field applied to 1W is largely screened by the
injected electrons. Thus, we assume that the
band diagram under an external bias can be
approximated as shown in Fig. 3(b). When the
subbands E10 in W1 , 20E in 2W , and E30 in
W3 are in alignment, the T*T will reach 100%,
as shown in Fig. 3(c), and thus a peak current
can be obtained. Then, increasing external bias
will result in a decreasing current due to the
electrons tunneling with decaying transmission
probabilities. If most electrons tunnel through the
TW structure with the valley range in the
transmission resonance spectra, a valley current
can be obtained. Thus, an N-shaped NDR
phenomenon is expected in the I-V
characteristics of device R502. Fig. 4 shows the
calculated I-V characteristics of the TW structure
of device R502. An N-shaped NDR performance
is obtained.
Fig. 5 shows the conduction band
diagrams and transmission coefficients T*T of
the TW structure of device R505 under different
external bias voltages V A at 140K. Fig. 5(a) is
the conduction band diagram and T*T at
VA 0mV. As a bias is introduced to the TW
structure, a non-equilibrium condition is
established. When the subband E10 in W1 is in
alignment with the subband E30 in W3 , the
T*T will reach 100%, as shown in Fig. 5(b), and
thus a peak current will be obtained. Increasing
external bias will result in a decreasing current
due to the electrons tunneling with decaying
transmission probabilities, as shown in Fig. 5(c).
When the bias is further increased, the subband
E10 may align with the subband E20 in W2 , as
shown in Fig. 5(d), and the second peak current
will then be observed. Thus, a double N-shaped
NDR phenomenon is expected in the I-V
characteristics of device R505.

A resonant-tunneling optoelectronic device
Der-Feng Guo
Department of Electronic Engineering
Air Force Academy
Kaohsuing, Taiwan
e-mail: g6f6guo@ms5.hinet.net
The illumination effect on the switching performance is presented for a triangular-barrier
resonant-tunneling diode (TBRTD). In the structure center of the TBRTD, a delta-doped (-doped) quantum
well is inserted. Owing to the resonant tunneling through the miniband in the quantum well, an N-shaped
negative-differential-resistance (NDR) phenomenon is observed in the current-voltage (I-V) characteristics.
The device shows a flexible optical function related to the cap-layer conductivity and potential barrier height
changeable by incident light.
A schematic sketch of the device is shown in Fig.1. Figure2 shows the band diagram of the device with
incident photons under a positive drain-to-source bias voltage DSV . The -doped sheet inserted in the center
of the InGaAs well forms two successive V-shaped wells. A miniband is formed in the successive wells. As
photons with energies higher than the band-gap energy of GaAs inject from the source, because the well
region has a thickness of only 20nm, electron-hole pairs will be photogenerated mostly in the source and drain
regions. In the source region, photogenerated electrons will transport over the gate, and holes will move in
opposite direction for the forward-bias condition. In the drain region, photogenerated electron-hole pairs will
be separated by the existing electric field. Moreover, part of the holes will be accumulated in the ( p )
InGaAs well, as shown in Fig.2. The accumulated holes will lower the potential barrier of the gate. If the DSV
voltage is sufficient to cause the Fermi level of the n -GaAs layer on the source side being in alignment with
the miniband in the InGaAs well, a peak current will be obtained due to the resonant tunneling effect. With
further increased DSV voltage, the current is expected to drop. If most of the electrons tunnel through the
miniband with the valley range in the transmission resonance spectra, the valley current can then be obtained.
Thus an N-shaped NDR phenomenon can be observed in the I-V characteristics. Because the characteristics of
the device are somewhat bound up with the input-light power, the switching is controllable by changing the
input-light.
Figure 3 shows the I-V characteristics of the device under illumination (solid lines) and dark (dashed
lines). N-shaped NDR performances are observed under both conditions. Compared to the dark characteristics,
the illuminated characteristics present a higher peak current, higher valley current, lower peak-current voltage,
and lower valley-current voltage. These phenomena will be interpreted by the variation of the triangular
barrier height and cap-layer conductivity.

INTRODUCTION 
The de facto standard method for the 
multi-objective optimization problems 
(MOOPs) in recent years is using evolutionary 
algorithm or genetic algorithm (GA) 
incorporating Pareto optimality criterion [1-3]. 
The solution of an MOOP is a Pareto curve 
which usually consists of more than one 
non-inferior design. As indicated by the studies 
[4-6] for bearing design, the usual treatment of 
an MOOP in Tribology field is considering a 
weighted sum of several objectives of the 
interest. If the single-objective optimization is 
conducted by using the GA, a premature 
solution may be reached. In order to obtain 
multiple non-inferior solutions, not one 
solution, the standard approach of using GA 
with the concept of Pareto optimality was 
applied [4-6]. They show that the marrying of 
the Pareto rank-based selection in the GA 
better maintains the diversity of population 
members than using the selection based on a 
combined fitness of several objective functions. 
Because of the robustness of the GA the results 
are generally accepted as the global optimal 
solutions with high probability. But, the 
robustness of the GAs in global optimization 
comes with the penalty of extended 
computation. 
An attempt to alleviate the computational 
loading in calculating the objective functions in 
an MOOP was presented recently [7]. In that 
study a Kriging metamodel assisted 
multi-objective GA is applied for design 
optimization. They show that the method can 
significantly reduce the number of 
objective/constraint function calls. An 
approximately 50% fewer number of 
simulation calls compared to a conventional 
GA was reported. Therefore, a practical method 
in solving a tribological MOOP is to find the 
potential optimal solution set rapidly.  
Despite a continuous improvement of 
computing power of modern computers, the 
computational demand in engineering 
optimization is constantly increasing. To have a 
reliable result in a multi-objective GA 
optimization, a moderate size of population and 
number of generation are usually required. As a 
result, a large amount of calculations is needed. 
With the help of parallel computing for an 
MOOP, the computation of the objective 
functions in a generation may be conducted 
concurrently [8-10]. Nevertheless, the speedup 
of the computation is limited by the number of 
generations required in the GA, which is not a 
small number for most cases. 
Lately, Zhu and Bogy [11] applied the 
DIviding RECTangle (DIRECT [12]) and 
simulated annealing (SA) algorithms to 
optimize the air slider in a hard-disk drive. 
Both the DIRECT and SA algorithms were 
used as the single-objective multi-factor 
optimization methods. They found that the 
DIRECT algorithm is extremely efficient as 
compared with the SA method when solving 
the nonlinear slider optimization problem 
subjected to bounded constrains. They also 
modified the DIRECT algorithm to limit the 
manufacturing tolerance as well as to deal with 
hidden constraints in several applications. Due 
to its effectiveness the DIRECT algorithm is 
also successfully applied in other tribological 
designs [13,14] with single objective. 
This study proposes a new global search 
algorithm, hypercube-dividing method (HDM), 
for MOOPs. The HDM is modified from the 
DIRECT algorithm, which is then incorporated 
with Pareto optimality criterion to perform 
multi-objective optimization. Therefore, the 
commonly used GA as a search tool in an 
MOOP is replaced by the HDM in this study.

DIRECT-algorithm alike. But, the criterion for 
selecting the hypercubes to be divided is 
modified for multi-objective optimizations. In

To illustrate the solution procedure of the 
HDM for an MOOP a two-factor two-objective 
air bearing optimization is presented. The goal 
is to maximize the stiffness and the load carry 
capacity of the air bearing simultaneously. The 
two design variables are width of square 
porous pad, and distance of porous pad edge to 
bearing edge. The MOOP was solved by 
incorporating the concept of Pareto optimality 
into the HDM. Then, a comparison with a 
previous study [4] using GA as the 
optimization method is conducted. The goal is 
to maximizing the bearing stiffness while 
minimizing the air flow to the bearing 
concurrently. The four design variables are 
pressure of supply air, width of square porous 
pad, distance of porous pad edge to bearing 
edge, and permeability of the porous material.   
In an MOOP the main bottleneck in the 
solution procedure is the calculation of 
objective functions. The execution time spent 
on the algorithm of an optimization method is 
usually trivial. Also, the execution time is 
affected by many factors in a simulation, such 
as coding, compiler, and hardware. In this 
sequential computation study, therefore, the 
comparison is based on the number of 
objective-function calls, instead of execution 
time. 
 
THE HYPERCUBE DIVIDING METHOD  
When performing a global optimization 
some effort has to allocate global search to 
ensure that potentially optimums in the design 
space are not overlooked. On the other hand, 
some effort has also to place on local search 
near the current best solution for an efficient 
exploration. In the DIRECT algorithm, these 
two operations are carried out by the procedure 
of n-rectangle (n-dimensional hyper-rectangle) 
dividing. And the selection of the n-rectangle to 
be divided is based on (1) the size of the 
n-rectangle (for global search), and (2) the 
objective function value of that n-rectangle (for 
local search).  
To solve an n-dimensional problem in the 
HDM, variables are normalized respectively to 
their lower bound of zero and upper bound of 
one. Because of the normalization only one 
n-cube (n-dimensional hypercube) exists at the 
initial step. The dividing scheme of the HDM 
for MOOPs is derived from the DIRECT 
algorithm. In order to extend the global search 
uniformly around the selected subspaces, the 
n-rectangle trisecting in the DIRECT is 
changed to n-cube bisecting in the dividing 
process. 
In the HDM, the selection of the n-cube to 
be divided is based on (1) the size of the n-cube, 
and (2) the Pareto rank of the n-cube in the 
same size group. Therefore, the search can be 
conducted in an unexplored space as well as 
near the n-cubes with the current best rank. 
Only one hypercube is selected in each size 
group, which has the lowest Pareto rank. When 
two or more hypercubes having the same 
lowest rank in a size group, a hypercube is 
selected by random choice for dividing. Note 
that the objectives are calculated at the center 
of each n-cube for Pareto ranking. 
In the HDM, the number of the n-cubes 
divided is a function of the amount of dividing 
steps. In each new step the dividing occurs in 
each size group. The total number of objective

CONCLUSION 
For many years, GAs have been 
successfully applied in many tribological 
optimizations with single or multiple objectives. 
To have a reliable result moderate numbers of 
population and generation are usually required 
in the GAs. Accordingly, the main drawback of 
GA as a global optimization tool is 
computational inefficiency. In this study, a new 
approach to deal with MOOPs is proposed. The 
approach is illustrated by a simple air bearing 
design. A comparison between the HDM and 
the GA for solving a four-factor MOOP is also 
presented. When the help of parallel computing, 
a concurrent computing of GA can be 
conducted in a generation. Similar parallel 
computing can also be used in a search step of 
the proposed HDM.  
It is very difficult to evaluate the 
effectiveness of a method for MOOPs by only 
studying limited cases. The difficulty also 
comes from the fact that the Pareto solutions 
are not the same, in terms of min-max solution 
and the shape of Pareto front, for different 
methods. In this study the Pareto front in the 
tenth step of the HDM has more solution 
selections (Fig. 10) mainly due to an efficient 
global search. Therefore, if the bearing 
stiffness is preferred in a final decision, the 
solution obtained by the HDM can provide a 
better design than by the GA.  
More importantly, the number of objective 
function calls in the HDM can be greatly 
reduced. It is also illustrated that the global 
search capability of the HDM can be enhanced 
if a suitable minimum size of hypercube is set. 
This mechanism of limiting the search to some 
useful resolution can greatly improve the 
search efficiency of the HDM. A similar result 
is very difficult to achieve by using GA. This 
study proposes an alternative method to treat 
MOOPs such that a reliable Pareto solution set 
based on a predetermined resolution in the 
design space can be obtained.

INTRODUCTION 
 Numerical simulation in many tribological 
studies requires the solution of Reynolds 
equations for the fluid-film pressure 
distribution and the consequent performance 
evaluation. In these cases an efficient 
numerical method to the solution of Reynolds 
equation is of practical importance. Due to the 
lack of analytical solution the Reynolds 
equations are usually solved numerically. The 
discretized equation of a two-dimensional 
Reynolds equation of elliptic type can be 
viewed as a sparse non-symmetric matrix 
problem. From a mathematical point of view, 
this is a trivial problem, which can be solved 
by using a direct method (e.g., Gaussian 
elimination method). However, for a large 
system of equations the solution by using direct 
methods may be required to consider several 
factors, such as execution time, memory 
storage requirement, and maybe more 
importantly, accumulated round-off errors from 
a large number of floating point operations. As 
a result, iterative (indirect) methods are the 
dominated solvers over direct methods for 
Reynolds equations. 
 Because of lacking closed-form solutions 
the analysis of errors caused by numerical 
methods in solving Reynolds equations is 
generally unattainable and uninterested. In 
early days for the numerical solution of 
Reynolds equations, the major advantage of 
using iterative methods as oppose to direct 
methods is that round-off (rounding) errors do 
not accumulate in iterative methods. Also, 
some iterative methods are relatively easy to 
implement and require much less memory 
storage than direct methods. In the case of 
journal bearings where incompressible fluid is 
applied, the Reynolds boundary conditions for 
cavitation can be easily satisfied when 
successive over relaxation (SOR) methods are 
used. 
In general, when an iterative method is 
used to solve Reynolds equations the solution 
in each iterative step is improved by modifying 
the solution with the solution residual. As a 
result, the accuracy of the solution is improved 
as the iteration increases. The numerical 
solution reaches its best possible result after 
some certain iteration. If more iteration is 
carried out, no further improvement is possible. 
However, a manual determination of the 
stopping point by trial-and-error is also 
troublesome for using iterative methods. The 
determination of the efficiency and 
effectiveness of an iterative method for solving 
Reynolds equation, thus, is influenced notably 
by the stopping (terminating) criterion.  
A usual procedure to the solution of a 
Reynolds equation is to discretize the equation 
into difference equation to establish a system of 
linear algebraic equations. Usually, the system 
of equations is solved by iterative methods as 
seen in many recent studies ((1)-(22)). The type 
of lubrication model, solution method, and 
convergence criterion in these studies are noted 
in Table 1. In some studies ((1), (4), (5), (8), (9), 
(11), (13), (14), (16), (17), (20), (21)) where the 
convergence criterion is not specified, a 
trial-and-error convergent test might have been 
conducted to determine the required iteration to 
have a satisfactory solution. The other studies 
((2), (3), (6), (7), (10), (12), (15), (18), (19), 
(22)) do specify the allowable tolerance for 
convergence in the corresponding iterative 
methods. Nevertheless, the tolerance used was 
not justified. 
In 2005, Allwood (23) presented a detail 
discussion of solution methods for elastic 
rough contact problems. It is noted that 
whenever an iterative solver has been used to 
solve the linear system at the heart of the 
contact problem, the Gauss-Seidel (GS) 
method has always been chosen. In that study 
(23), three state-of-the-art algorithms were 
examined. In the two iterative conjugate 
gradient (CG) methods presented, the 
recommended relative errors used to assess 
convergence of the methods are the square of 
the tolerance specified (around 10-6 to 10-12). It 
is shown that the CG method outperforms the 
GS method when dealing with the contact 
problem in an active set algorithm. On the 
other hand, a study (7) found that the GS 
method can be a much faster solver than the 
CG method for contact modeling. If the 
stopping criteria were consistent when 
comparing efficiency of the GS and CG 
methods in sequential computing, the 
disagreement of the method efficiency between 
Refs. (7) and (23) might have been minimized.

二  、 與會心得 
    此次大會的磨潤學論文均以口頭發表，同時在多個演講廳舉行。今年
的主要內容包括下列磨潤學相關的重要領域：能源動力，表面工程，齒輪，
非鐵金屬，軸封，磨耗測試，潤滑學基礎，奈米潤滑，液膜潤滑，金屬加
工，表層鍍膜，引擎及動力系統，合成液壓潤滑劑，風力渦輪機之磨潤應
用，滾動軸承，固態潤滑劑等。另外在大會會場內亦有多場事先註冊且須
付費之專業課程，供在職之工程師或學生參加。 
     由於研究議題廣泛，筆者於會議前一天到達，並依據大會手冊中各場
次的論文摘要說明來預定擬參與的場次，主要相關的場次為液膜潤滑、潤
滑學基礎及引擎、動力系統。此次會議的口頭報告，筆者提出一個新的迭
代法收斂條件，其基本原理為數值分析時的截斷誤差分析，本研究的結果
在液膜潤滑的研究上（通常都是使用迭代法求解各種的雷諾方程式）建立
一個在學理上可遵循的迭代收斂法則。由於此次的報告為星期三中午前的
最後一場，在報告結束前、後，引起多位與會者的發問及討論，尤其是年
輕的參與者在實務上碰到的數值不收斂的問題。本論文已於出國前被STLE
之Tribology Transactions接受，筆者並將以此論文為基礎，近期的相關研究
為進一步的探討多種迭代法用於求解雷諾方程式的效率及優缺點。

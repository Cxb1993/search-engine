4一、緒論
可變訊息標誌（Variable Message Signs；VMS 或稱 Changeable Message
Signs；CMS）是種可程式化的交通管制設施，透過文字或圖形符號來傳達各
項動態的控制或警告訊息給道路使用者，使用路人得以避開擁擠路段與時間，
作為個人行車路徑規劃之參考，並讓有限的運輸資源得以發揮最大的效用，為
我國公路智慧型運輸系統(Intelligent Transportation System, ITS)中，先進旅行
者資訊系統（Advanced Traveler Information System, ATIS）設施之ㄧ，在 ITS
中與駕駛者關係最為密切。許多國內外相關的研究也都指出此種設施對用路人
之指引及整體道路系統之使用效率有相當的助益(郭伊琳，民 94；王端丙，民
97；Benson, 1996)。
隨著國內高速公路路網逐漸擴充完成，在交通路網建設愈形複雜與路況資
訊亦日益增加的情況下，傳統的文字可變訊息標誌以文數字顯示資訊的方式囿
於字數上的限制，已無法滿足資訊呈現的需求。因此包括歐洲、北美、日本、
泰國等國家皆已發展以圖形顯示方式的圖誌路徑資訊看板(Graphical Route
Information Panel；GRIP)。目前國內在部分較複雜之系統路網上亦嘗試設置類
似功能的圖誌可變標誌(Changeable Graphics Signs；CGS)。
雖然圖誌可變標誌可ㄧ次同時呈現不同路徑的資訊，然而受限於道路行車
速度與連續性，過度複雜或呈現不當訊息的圖誌可變標誌，如以路徑顏色加上
整合路徑時間或路徑時速來表示路網資訊，可能導致用路人無法及時接收或處
理其中的訊息。Alkim 等人(2000)的研究即指出，一般駕駛人對文字可變訊息
標誌之反應時間較圖誌可變標誌為快。Richards 等人(2005)則提出整合圖誌可
變標誌與可變訊息標誌的設計方向與訊息內容分配的問題，是改善可變交通標
誌的重要方向。高公局的研究(高公局，民94)亦指出在交流道前設置文字型可
變訊息標誌與具路徑顯示的路徑導引標誌，可補充文字型因字數受限在敘述上
的不足，致用路人閱讀困難，兩者可收相輔相成之效，以達到集體路徑導引的
目的。現行各國使用或測試之圖誌可變標誌也有部份包含了可變訊息標誌的整
合。
然而圖誌可變標誌與可變訊息標誌兩種設計的整合，涉及相當多的影響變
數，對駕駛人反應與理解能力的影響更為複雜，而這些變化是否有助於駕駛人
的路徑選擇，或者潛藏可能的風險，都必須予以進一步探討。目前國內外相關
研究主要針對以文數字顯示為主的可變訊息標誌與呈現圖形化刺激的圖誌可
變標誌作探討，鮮少探討兩者之搭配設計。本研究即在探討整合圖形與文字之
可變訊息標誌之資訊組合與呈現位置對駕駛者反應之影響。

賴建榮與嚴國端(民 92)針對國內高速公路已有的可變訊息標誌作全面性
的調查，並配合問卷調查分析用路人對此類標誌的認識與反應情況。調查結
果發現有 66.13﹪的駕駛者會去注意可變訊息標誌，駕駛者在字型方面較偏好
標楷體與中黑體，顏色則偏好紅色與橙色，顯示方式偏好前導式。駕駛經驗、
路線熟悉程度、交通壅塞程度皆會影響駕駛者對 VMS 之注意。年齡、教育
程度、地區別及駕駛經驗對 VMS 理解偏好與反應亦有顯著影響。另外，駕
駛者對不同資訊來源的理解與偏好程度也有不同。另外，透過實驗研究，發
現 VMS 之呈現以橙色搭配中黑體較佳，呈現行數以單行時，受試者反應時
間較短，字體大小以 70×70CM 時反應時間較快，而在動態訊息呈現方式上，
閃現式在反應時間與正確率的表現皆優於前導式與快速連續式(賴建榮、嚴國
端與柯威，民 94； Lai, 2006；Lai,2008； Lai 與 Yen, 2004；Lai, Yen 與 Wang,
2006)。
(2) 有關 CGS之研究
有關圖誌可變標誌之研究，高公局在「高快速公路整體路網交通管理系
綜合規劃成果報告」中，闢有專章說明各項資訊顯示系統設置的考量，並指
出為提高用路人對路網控制運作的接受度，能在比較各路徑之旅行時間資訊
後選擇行駛路線，提昇路網控制策略成效，建議在路網主要轉向點增設可顯
示指示路徑及行駛時間的路徑導引標誌作為輔助，其內容以綠色路段表示行
駛速度 40 公里以上，黃色路段表示行駛速度 20 公里到 40 公里，紅色路段表
示行駛速度 20 公里以下。此外，交流道前設置文字型 VMS 與具路徑顯示的
路徑導引標誌，可補充文字型因字數受限在敘述上的不足，致用路人閱讀困
難，兩者可收相輔相成之效，以達到集體路徑導引的目的 (高公局,民 94b)。
賴建榮(民 97)及 Lai 與 Tsai (2008)參考人因工程圖誌顯示原則，進行不
同路網複雜程度與呈現方式的圖誌可變標誌設計與對駕駛人影響的實驗驗
證。實驗研究發現路網型態與可變資訊內容對受試者的反應有顯著影響，在路
網型態方面，受試者對三角形路網較四角形路網有較高的正面決策比例與較
短的反應時間；在可變資訊內容方面，受試者對「路徑顏色」有較高的正面
決策比例與較短之反應時間，而對「路徑顏色+個別路徑時速」的正面決策
比例較低，「路徑顏色+整合旅行時間」的反應時間較長。賴建榮(民 97)同時
採用狀態偏好法針對結合圖誌與文字資訊之圖誌可變標誌進行探討，結果發
現資訊組合為「路徑顏色+旅行時速」與「路徑顏色+旅行時間」為駕駛者自
認為理解且正面決策比例最高的兩個資訊組合，其次是「路徑顏色+整合旅
行時間」，最後是「路徑顏色」。

關於圖誌可變標誌的研究，Alkim 等(2000)先引用人因工程對圖誌設計的
原則，簡化修正圖誌可變標誌，再透過模擬實驗與偏好問卷調查，比較圖誌
可變標誌與一般文數字 VMS 對受試者理解程度的影響。實驗研究結果發現
受試者對圖誌可變標誌的理解程度低於一般文字 VMS，但是受試者很快學習
熟悉圖誌可變標誌，而在路徑選擇上，受試者對圖誌可變標誌優於一般文數
字 VMS。同時在主觀偏好上，受試者反應也顯示快速地喜好可變圖誌，而一
般文數字 VMS 之優勢快速消失。研究指出此種現象可能係受試者對圖誌可
變標誌平常較少接觸，較不熟悉，導致其優勢不明顯，但適當的認識後，其
優勢即快速顯現。另外，在受試特性上，對圖形具有良好閱讀能力者，對可
變圖誌標誌的反應與偏好，顯著優於圖形閱讀能力不良者。
Atkins (2003)接受英國高速公路局委託執行 GCDP 的研究，透過問卷、
小組討論與實驗室實驗，探討伯明罕地區(Birmingham Box)GCDP 設置位址
與訊息內容之設計。研究指出對多重路徑選擇的路網，GCDP 較 VMS 容易
理解，而僅有二種路徑選擇的狀況，則建議採文字為主的 VMS 較佳。另外
研究亦指出呈現 GCDP 路徑旅行時間的必要性。Schönfeld 等人(2000)亦透過
問卷與實驗探討德國慕尼黑(Munich)地區 GCDP 的設計，研究建議在路徑顏
色上採二種色彩(紅色表道路壅塞，黑色表路況正常)，可限制看板資訊的複
雜度。同時在GCDP前應設置固定式標誌以提示駕駛人注意前方GCDP資訊。
Richard 等人(2005）針對 20 種不同的圖示顯示看板（Graphical Congestion
Display Panels；GCDP）進行模擬駕駛實驗，由受試者以口頭回答及填寫問
卷，以探討駕駛者對 CGS 呈現訊息的理解，研究結果發現不同訊息量與受試
者特性皆會影響受試者對 GCDP 的理解，過多的訊息，受試者的反應速度較
慢且正確率較低；年輕族群對 GCDP 有較高的實用性評價與反應正確率，而
中老年族群較容易理解 GCDP 內容；擅於閱圖者對 GCDP 有較高的理解力與
正確率。研究亦指出歐洲地區各國採用 GCDP 時，路徑顏色符碼的整合有待
進ㄧ步研究，另外在 GCDP 與 VMS 整合上，VMS 訊息如何作為 GCDP 路徑
選擇的誘導刺激，亦有待進ㄧ步探討。

One picture is worth a thousand words. By displaying part of a road network, including the
location of congestion, an overview of the current traffic situation can be given. The potential of the
human mind to absorb graphical information is a distinct advantage for a CGS over a VMS which
can display text message only. A CGS can display information regarding different routes
simultaneously. Because the road network is displayed, including the locations of congestion,
motorists receive information on all possible routes, regardless of their destination. This can be
important when the network is complex or when congestion occurs on several locations within the
network. CGSs also serve a larger group of motorists, and particularly motorists who are not
familiar with the roadways will have fewer problems. They will not need to know what the
descriptive terms on text VMSs stand for.
Presenting route information in a graphical way can give more information to drivers at a time.
Along with the advantages of graphical display, there are also disadvantages. A problem that
remains is whether or not more information can be processed fast enough by drivers. Results of
some studies showed that integration of CGS and VMS may be an alternative for improving the
effectiveness of traffic variable signs (Alkim et al., 2000). However, there are many variables which
could affect the effect of integration. Drivers’comprehension and response to the integrated signs
will become more complex. Further research on the integration of CGS and VMS is needed. The
main purposes of this study were to investigate the effects of display information and relative
position on integrated changeable graphical sign CGS with Variable message signs VMS for
driver’s response using a simple driving simulator experiment.
Methods
Experiment design
A driving simulator was used to examine the effects of display information and relative
position on integrated changeable graphical sign (CGS) with variable message signs (VMS) for
driver’s response using a simple driving simulator experiment. The independent variables were CGS
information (road color, road color with driving journey time), VMS information (no information,
simple information, detailed information), and position of VMS relative to CGS (top, bottom, right,
left). Illustrations of two kind of CGS information with various VMS information and position of
VMS are shown in Table 1.
A within-subjects design was conducted in the experiment. 24 treatment groups represented
the combinations of the three within-subjects factors Each participant went through 72 randomized
stimulus presentations, that is, 24 CGS (shown in Table 1) with 3 different response choice in each
test. The dependent measures collected in this experiment were response time and optimum route
choice percentage. Response time was the time between the start of presentation of a CGS stimuli
and the moment of a participant’s response to the information of the CGS. Optimum route choice 
percentage was 100 times the number of optimum responses divided by the total number of
responses. The optimum response is the choice to the fastest route among all alternatives.
Apparatus and stimuli
A P4-2.4 PC with 1G ram was used to process the experimental task program for the
participants. A Microsoft side winder force feedback wheel, replacing the steering wheel, was
instaled in front of the driver’s seat in the ergonomic laboratory. The stimuli were displayed on a
22”screen (47.5 cm wide × 30 cm high).
The stimuli used in this study were composed of a sequence of computer-generated CGS
merged with a real drivers’ view video representing freeway driving at 100 km per hour. The CGS 
panel used green color for the background, red for heavy congestion road section, yellow for light
congestion section, and black for normal conditions. Driving journey time was designed in
accordance with the color displayed on each CGS.

Figure 1. The experimental task configuration
Before starting the experiment, each participant was briefed on the purpose and procedures of
the experiment. To prevent visual fatigue, participants were asked not to do any reading tasks for an
hour before the experiment. A warm up session was conducted in the beginning to familiarize
participants with the response task. With the participants’ consent, the actual experiment began. The
complete experiment took about half an hour.
Participants
25 college students (12 female and 13 male) between 19 and 28 years old (M=23, SD=3.1)
participated in the experiment. All had 0.8 corrected visual acuity or better and normal color vision.
Each participant had a valid driver’s license and driving experience on the freeway for at least one 
year. They were paid for their participation.
Results and discussion
The means and standard deviations of response time and optimum route choice percentage
under each level of the independent variables are shown in Table 2.
Results of the analysis of variances on response time showed the contents of CGS information
( F(1, 24)=54.168, p<0.001), position of VMS ( F(3, 72)=6.323, p<005) were significant. However,
analysis of variances performed on optimum route choice percentage showed no significant effect.
Participants responded more quickly for road color only CGS (8.648 sec.) than for road color with
journey time CGS (10.065 sec.). Participants had the shortest response time for VMS on the right
position of CGS (9.245 sec.), however, they had the longest time for VMS on the bottom position
(9.637 sec.). The results of Duncan multiple-test demonstrated response times for VMS on the right
and top position were significantly different from the left and bottom positions.
The interaction of content of VMS information and position of VMS relative to CGS were
significant on participants’response time. Response times for detail information with the bottom
position of VMS relative to CGS were shortest. Figure 2 showed response time for three levels of

The main purposes of this study were to investigate the effects of display information and
relative position on integrated changeable graphical sign (CGS) with variable message signs (VMS)
for driver’s response using a simple driving simulator experiment. The independent variables were
CGS information (road color, road color with driving journey time), VMS information (no
information, simple information, and detail information), and position of VMS relative to CGS (top,
bottom, right, and left). Computer generated CGS and VMS merged with a driver’sview video was
projected onto a screen in front of the test vehicle. Participants were asked to select a route
according to the destination on the optimal route choice rule. Participants’response time and
optimum route choice percentage were collected for the analysis of drovers’response and
comprehension. Results of analysis showed that CGS information and position of VMS were
significant for subjects’response time. Participants’responded quicker for road color than for road
color with driving journey time. Participants had the shortest response time for VMS on the right,
however, they had the longest time for VMS at the bottom. The interaction of the CGS information,
VMS information and the position of VMS were also significant on participants’response time.
Response times for detail information with the bottom position of VMS relative to CGS were
shortest. Analysis of variance for route choice percentage was not significant.
Introduction
Variable message signs (VMS) are programmable message bulletin boards that are used to
provide real-time information about changing condition on roadways to inform drivers and improve
operations. It is critical in informing motorists of various situations, especially in high volume
traffic and congested zones. In the early periods, VMS is usually presented in the alphanumeric
format. However, the amount of text which can be display is limited. It is not always possible to
present all relevant information to drivers at a certain location. However, the amount of text which
can be displayed is limited. The multitude of different routes existing in a road network would
require directing different route recommendations at drivers with various destinations. Using signs
to display graphical information may be a possible solution for this problem. The concept of using
graphical signs to display traffic information is now becoming increasingly popular. A graphical
route information panel (GRIP) or a changeable graphical sign (CGS) with graphical information
has been developed recently and is also now becoming increasingly popular. For instance, in the
Netherlands, prototypes of graphical panels have been developed with implementation expected in
the future and in Germany, a pilot scheme has been in operation since April 2003 (Richards et al.,
2005). A graphical panel was prototyped in an off-road experiment as part of a project in France,
and Atkins transport systems were commissioned by the Highways Agency to undertake a concept
study into graphical congestion display panel in England (England Highway Agency, 2003). In
Taiwan, the National Freeway Bureau has been researching the use of Changeable Graphical Signs
(CGSs) to display real-time congestion and journey time information relevant to drivers on sections
of the trunk road network (Http://www.freeway.gov.tw). A CGS can be has various names, such as
GCDP (Graphical Congestion Display Panels) in the UK, or GRIP (Graphical Route Information
Panel) in the Netherlands.

Task and Procedure
A test participant, siting in the driver’s seat, would see the driving video on the screen with 
CGS stimuli appearing on the road center gradually increasing in size. The time allowed for each
CGS presentation was 15 seconds and would end when a response was made during the presentation.
Before the presentation of a CGS stimuli, an instruction message related to the destination would
appear. Participants were asked to choose a route according to the destination by their free will.
After a response was made, the CGS stimuli disappeared, and the next instruction and stimuli would
appear after a random lapse between 0 and 10 seconds. Each participant was instructed to respond
by turning the steering wheel with his or her hand or by pressing the pedal with foot to signify her
or his route choice for a specific message during each trial. Three response modes were designed to
match the alternative route choice for different type of CGS (turn right, left or driving straight).
Participants’ response times and route choice were recorded by the computer automaticaly. Figure 1

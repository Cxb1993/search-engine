II. GREEDY FORWARDING AND LOCAL MINIMA 
 
A. Greedy forwarding scheme 
For the greedy forwarding scheme, a packet is marked by its 
sender with the destination location. After that, a forwarding 
node can make a locally optimal, greedy choice in choosing 
the packet’s next hop. It means that a node must know its 
radio neighbors’ locations to make the locally optimal choice 
of the next hop is one of its neighbors whose geographical 
distance to the packet’s destination is the shortest. There are 
two operating modes of the greedy forwarding scheme: 1-hop 
greedy and 2-hop greedy. The main difference is that a node 
selects the next hop of a forwarding packet by considering 
either its 1-hop neighbors or 2-hop neighbors. 
Figure 1 illustrates an example of 1-hop greedy forwarding 
scheme. Node A sends a packet to node J. Node A considers 
its 1-hop neighbors {B, E} and then selects node B 
as the next hop for forwarding the packet because the distance 
between B and J is less than that between E and J. Then node 
B makes its locally optimal choice to select C as the next 
forwarding hop, and continues the process until the packet 
reaches J. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fig. 1. 1-hop Greedy Forwarding scheme 
 
Figure 2 illustrates an example of 2-hop greedy forwarding 
scheme. Node A sends a packet to node J. Here node A 
considers its 2-hop neighbors {C, G, H}, instead of its 1-hop 
neighbors,  and decides  to  select  node  C  as  the  next hop 
because the distance between C and J is the shortest one. Then 
continues the process until the packet reaches J. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fig. 2. 2-hop Greedy Forwarding scheme 
B. The Local Minima Problem 
Most existing geographic routing protocols use greedy 
scheme to forward data packets to the destination, that is, the 
forwarding node selects one of its neighbors whose distance 
to the destination is the shortest one, as the next-hop node. 
However, the barriers (holes) on the natural topography often 
cause greedy forwarding scheme to fail to discovery a path. 
This is because if “holes” exist on the natural topography, 
some node may not be able to find its next-hop forwarding 
node since there are no neighbors closer to the destination than 
from the node itself to the destination. This problem is called 
“local minima problem”. When the local minima problem 
occurs during forwarding a packet, some geographic routing 
protocols just discard the packet even though a path to the 
destination existing. 
An  example  of  the  local  minima  problem  is  dictated  in 
Figure 3. Node A sends a packet to node J. When node D
receives the packet, the local minima problem occurs since the 
1-hop neighbors {C , G} of node D have a longer distance  to 
node J than the distance from D to J. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fig. 3. The Local minima problem 
 
III. THE DRQC PROTOCOL 
 
A. Assumptions 
This DRQC protocol has the following assumptions: 
(1) Each  node  is  equipped  with  a  GPS  to  get  its 
geographic location and each node broadcasts 
periodically its GPS locations to its 1-hop neighbors. 
(2)  Each node has the same communication range. 
(3)  The barrier on the geographical topology is static. 
(4)  We do not consider the power consumption problem on  
       each node 
 
B. Terminologies 
The data packet generator is called as Source and the packet 
receiver is called as Destination. A node which is executing 
the selection of next-hop forwarding node is called Prober, and 
the node which is selected by the prober to be the next- hop is 
called Forwarder. Figure 4 shows that a packet is generated by 
node S (Source) and will be delivered to node D (Destination). 
Node X and node Z are Forwarder when they are selected by 
their Prober S and X. 
If node v can’t find a route to vd from its Neighbors 
Table and Routing Table, node v starts to select a Forwarder 
from  its  two  hop  neighbors  denoted  by  N2(v).  The  first 
selection criterion is that the two hop neighbor must be red 
and be at the same quadrant area with the destination vd. If 
more than one candidate exists, then select the one having the 
shortest distance to vd as the Forwarder (denoted as vm), and 
records the route to vd via vm into its routing table (line05- 
line09 of Fig. 11). 
The second criterion is to select a two hop neighbor which 
is white and is at the same quadrant area with the destination 
vd. If more that one candidate exists, then select the one having 
the shortest distance to vd as the Forwarder and records the 
route (line10-line14 of Fig. 11). The last criterion is to select a 
two hop neighbor who has the shortest distance to vd as the 
Forwarder (line15-line19 of Fig. 11). 
The worst case is that the node v can’t find a Forwarder to vd
from its two hop neighbors. Under this case, node v is called 
as Blocker and it will generate a DETOUR message and 
send it to the Previous Forwarder vp of this packet (line20- 
line21 of Fig. 11). 
Part 2: Handling DETOUR message is shown in Fig.12. 
 
When a node v receives a DETOUR message with the 
contained data packet sender Detour_vs, the packet receiver 
Detour_vd, and the node (denoted as Blocker) who can not 
find a Forwarder to the destination Detour_vd, the node v
selects a Forwarder from its two hop neighbors which haven’t 
been selected as the Forwarder of the packet since last time. 
Such information is maintained at the Candidates field of the 
routing table. The DETOUR message generator also puts its 1- 
hop neighbors into the DETOUR_Filters field of the message 
to indicate the node v not to consider those as Forwarders 
because there are no routes via them (line00-line01 of Fig. 12). 
After that, node v selects a Forwarder from the Candidates, 
which has the shortest distance to Detour_vd (line02 -line05 of 
Fig. 12). If no one of the two hop neighbors could be selected 
as a Forwarder, node v becomes a Blocker and generates a 
DETOUR message and sends it to the Previous Forwarder vpf
of this packet (line06-line09 of Fig. 12).

Fig. 23~Fig. 27 showed the simulation results about the 
packet delivery rate. In this case, we disable the recovery 
mechanism of three methods. and observe whether packet 
delivery rate of DRQC is superior to other methods. 
By the simulation, the longer communication  range  of  
node  results  in  higher  packet delivery rate. This is because a 
node would have more neighbors with a longer communication 
range, and the number of local minima problem will be 
decreased with more neighbors. Also the more nodes deployed 
in network would have more neighbors, and which results in 
higher packet delivery   rate.   On   average,   the   simulations   
showed   the proposed  DRQC  protocol  has  higher  packet  
delivery  rate (96%) than the 1-hop greedy method (79%) and 
the 2-hop greedy method (87%). 
Fig. 28 showed  the simulation results about the average 
packet delay(Hop count), which is measured by the hop 
count needed to deliver a  packet  from  the  source  to  the  
destination.  It  is obvious that packet delay (Hop  count) 
decreases when the communication range of nodes increases. 
By the simulation, the average packet delay of DRQCૠ1-
hop greedy and 2-hop greedy are almost the same.

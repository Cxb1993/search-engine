Abstract 
Edge type dislocation is the dominating dislocation type in InN thin film grown on Si (111) 
substrate. These vertical dislocation lines tend to glide along >< 0211 directions and form a slip 
system on the )0002( plane. Mismatch dislocation lines will form on the growth basal plane if film 
thickness exceeds critical thickness. Continuous growth will trigger the formation of threading 
dislocations along growth direction. Periodic features with triangular symmetry are designed and 
fabricated on InN film toallow edge type dislocations to annihilate by gliding a small distance toward 
the edge of the film. The glide of dislocations can happen during epitaxy at a temperature close to 
InN dissociation temperature or be triggered by post-growth thermal annealing. Periodic holes were 
implemented by etching Si substrate. Epitaxy of InN by plasma-assisted molecular beam epitaxy 
system (PAMBE) is performed on patterned substrates. Alternatively, direct etch of the MBE-grown 
InN thin film followed by a post-growth annealing can also trigger dislocation glide. The edge type 
dislocation density estimated by x-ray diffraction was reduced for 10% and the )2110( rocking curve 
width was reduced from 8.71° to 6.43°.  
 
Keywords: InN, dislocation, dislocation glide, MBE

Abstract 
Room temperature polarized infrared (IR) reflectance technique is employed to study the optical properties of wurtzite InN epilayers on 
Si(111) grown by molecular beam expitaxy (MBE). The reflection spectra are compared to the calculated spectra generated based on the 
anisotropic dielectric function model. Good agreement between the measured and calculated spectra is obtained. From the fit of the 
experimental curve, the reststrahlen parameters at the center of Brillouin zone, the carrier concentration and mobility as well as the 
epilayers thicknesses are determined. The values of the carrier concentration and mobility are in good agreement with the results 
obtained from the Hall effects measurements.

3. Theory

theoretical polarized IR reflectance spectra for the studied structure are calculated by using the standard transfer matrix 
formulation based on five layers model, i.e., air/HT-InN/LT-InN/AlN/Si(111). The model takes into consideration of the 
lattice vibrations and the free carrier contribution.

4. Results and Discussion 
Fig. 2 shows the room temperature experimentally (solid line) and theoretically (dotted line) IR reflectance spectra with 
(a) s- and (b) p-polarization for the HT-InN/LT-InN/AlN/Si(111) in the range of 100 – 1500 cm-1. The insert figures show 
the polarized IR reflectance spectra (experimentally and theoretically) in the range of 100 – 7800 cm-1. Also shown in Fig. 
2 are the phonon frequencies of the InN and AlN. 
From Fig. 2, it can be clearly seen that both polarized spectra are dominated by a high reflection background in the 
range of 100 – 250 cm-1 and two high reflection peaks (A and B). However, the p-polarization spectrum has a pronounced 
dip C near 890 cm-1. The high reflection background is due to the absorption of the free carriers in HT-InN epilayer as 
well as the Si(111) substrate. The origin of the peaks A and B is the ωTO,⊥ for the InN and the AlN, respectively. The dip C 
in the p-polarization spectrum is due to the ωLO of the AlN. This dip is located within the ωLO,|| and ωTO,⊥ of the AlN. The 
appearance of this dip is known as Berreman effect [16]. The dip originated from the ωLO of the InN is undetected because 
it has been suppressed by the large γLO,|| value as well as the LO phonon-plasmon (LPP) coupling effects [11,17]. Note that 
the spectral strength of this LO dip is very weak in nature and is sensitive to the LPP coupling effects. 
Let examine the oscillation features in the insert figures in Fig. 2. The oscillation fringes in the 2000 – 7800 cm-1 region 
in both spectra are due to the multiple reflection of the light beam between the interfaces of the epilayers. It is interesting 
to note that the oscillation fringes in the region above 5000 cm-1 are attenuated and became flat. Similar observation has 
been reported by Ishitani et al. [10,11] and Thakur et al. [12]. According to Ishitani et al. [10,11], the flatten of the 
oscillation fringes is mainly due to the absorption of the photon energy by excess carriers (electron and holes) in the InN 
epilayers. Generally, the photon energies in the range of 5000 – 7800 cm-1 are correspond to 0.62 – 0.97 eV, i.e., around 
the band-gap energy of the InN. Consequently, excess carriers (electron and holes) in the InN epilayers are photo-excited 
into permissible excited states. When these carriers return to their equilibrium states, the excess energy is released. This 
We consider now the theoretical modeling of the polarized IR reflectance spectra. Typically, the reflectivity for 
s-polarization measurement is only depend on ε⊥, thus one set of phonon parameters is sufficient to fit the s-polarized IR 
reflectance spectrum. However, for p-polarization measurement, the reflectivity depends on both ε⊥ and ε||, thus two set of 
phonon parameters are required to fit the p-polarized IR reflectance spectrum. For these reasons, the s-polarized spectrum 
is fitted prior the p-polarized spectrum.  
During the fitting, the fitting parameters (i.e., the ωTO,||(⊥), ωLO,||(⊥), γTO,||(⊥), γLO,||(⊥), ωp,||(⊥), and γp,||(⊥)) are adjusted until 
the theoretical spectra give the best fit to the experimental spectra for both the s- and p-polarization measurements. The 
ε∞,||(⊥) used to model the theoretical polarized IR spectra are obtained from Refs. [18-20]. While the values of m*||(InN) = ~ 
0.06 mo,  m*⊥(InN) = ~ 0.07 mo, and m*||(⊥)(Si) = 0.31 mo are taken from Refs. [21,22]. It should be pointed out here that 
all the parameters are interrelated and the choice of the parameters will affect simultaneously the features of the spectrum. 
Therefore, this leads to a restriction of the permitted parameter values. Nevertheless, the greatest advantage of this fitting 
method lies in its ability to give unambiguous results.  
As shown in Fig. 2, the fitting of theoretical spectrum to the measured spectrum for both the s- and the p-polarization is 
good in each case. The best-fit parameters unambiguously assigned to HT-InN, LT-InN, AlN buffer layer, and Si(111) 
substrate, are listed in Table 1. Note that the discrepancy between the theoretical and experimental spectra above 5000 
cm-1 (as shown in the insert figures in Fig. 2) can be attributed to PL effects as mentioned previously.

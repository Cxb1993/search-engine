EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS 
The laser epi-structure is grown on (100) oriented Si-doped GaAs substrate by molecular beam epitaxy. The QDs are 
self-assembled using Stranski-Krastanov growth method. The nominal thickness of InAs QD is 2.6 ML and the single-
layer dot density is about 51010 cm-2. To change the peak wavelength of QD emissions, InGaAs strain-reducing layer 
(SRL) of different thickness is deposited immediately after the dot formation. The In composition is fixed at 0.15 in this 
work. Figure 1 shows the dependence of photoluminescence (PL) at room temperature (RT) for the ground-state (GS) 
and excited-state (ES) emission peaks of a single InAs QD layer as function of the thickness of In0.15Ga0.85As SRL (d). 
The data points are based on our separate growth and measurement as well as published results in Ref [11]. As d is 
between 0 and 5 nm, the PL peak wavelengths are fitted by the following expressions,

Figure 1. RT-PL for the GS and ES emission peaks of single QD layer as function of the thickness of InGaAs SRL. 
 
The active region consists of 10 stacking layers, each of which includes self-assembled InAs QD layer, capped 
In0.15Ga0.85As SRL of varying thickness for adjusting the peak emission wavelength, and GaAs space layer of 33 nm. 
Three chirped wavelengths of longer, medium, and shorter wavelength range are designed with In0.15Ga0.85As SRL 
thickness of 4, 3, and 1.5 nm, with stacking numbers of 4, 3, and 3 layers, and designated as 4*QDL, 3*QDM, and 3*QDS, 
respectively. The detail crystal growth can be found in [12]. From Eq. (1) and (2), the calculated GS/ES emission peaks 
are 1270/1191, 1246/1169, and 1202/1132 nm for longer, medium, and shorter wavelength QD-stacks, respectively. The 
experimental data may have uncertainty of 10 nm due to wafer non-uniformity as well as calibration factor of thickness 
and composition. 
The wafer is patterned into ridge waveguides of 5-µm width by lithography and wet etching process, and then cleaved 
into laser bars with different cavity length. No package is applied to laser bars when doing probe measurement for light-
current-voltage (L-I-V) and spectrum characteristics under continuous-wave operation at RT of 20 ˚C. The front facets of 
laser bars are deposited with broadband anti-reflection (AR) coating (R < 1% from 1050 nm to 1350 nm) in order to 
suppress double-cavity effect, while the rear facets are deposited with high-reflection (HR) distributed Bragg reflector 
coating (R > 99%) in order to reduce cavity loss and form the end mirrors of ECLs. 
Grating-coupled external-cavity arrangement in the Littrow configuration [13] shown in Figure 2 is built to investigate 
three coated QD laser bars with different cavity length of 1.5, 2.0, and 3.0mm. In the setup, collimation lens with high 
numerical aperture of 0.68 to collect the wide angle emission from edge emitter, and external grating with a groove 
density of 1200 lines/mm and blazed wavelength of 1.0 µm, are utilized. The first-order diffracted light from the grating 
provides the optical feedback, while the zeroth-order diffraction is coupled into multimode optical fiber which connected 
with the optical spectrum analyzer (Ando AQ6315E) at a resolution of 0.05 nm. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4. (a) L-I-V characteristics of solitary AR/HR coated QD lasers for three cavity lengths of 1.5, 2.0 and 3.0 mm. (b) 
The threshold lasing spectrum for 2-mm QD emitter before and after facet coatings. 
 
The tuning characteristics of QD ECLs are then presented. In order to avoid the competition from free-running lasing 
modes of original FP emitter, it is suggested that the QD ECLs be operated below or at least not well above the threshold 
condition of solitary coated devices since the AR coating is not low enough to inhibit the lasing emissions. Figure 5 
shows the lasing spectrum of 2.0-mm QD ECL tuned from 1160 to 1290 nm under 100mA by manually rotating the 
diffraction grating. The lasing peaks are corresponding to the resonance of the external cavity. Their spectral linewidths 
are less than 0.5 nm and their intensities are about 35 dB higher than their side mode emission almost across the tuning 
range of 130 nm. The lasing linewidth can be further reduced by packaging the laser devices and enclosing the entire 
external cavity with acoustic and thermal isolation [15]. 
 
Figure 5. Lasing spectra of 2-mm QD ECL, tuned across 1160-1290 nm wavelength at constant current of 100 mA. 
 
Figure 6 shows the threshold current and threshold current density of tunable QD ECLs as function of tuning wavelength 
for three cavity lengths. The minimum threshold currents are achieved around 1260 nm which is the spectral gain peak of 
as-cleaved emitters. There is no significant jump in threshold current as tuning wavelength goes from minimum toward 
longer and shorter wavelength sides. The spectral gain dip between the GS and ES, which is prominent in conventional 
unchirped QD structure [7,8], is absent in our chirped multilayer QD structure. For 3.0-mm ECL, the tuning range is only 
63 nm (from 1218 to 1281 nm), in which the maximum current is close to its solitary threshold current. For 1.5-mm ECL, 
it can be turned across 150 nm (from 1143 to 1293 nm) under maximum threshold current of 90 mA (1.2 kA/cm2), which 
is a little lower than its solitary threshold current. The short-wavelength and long-wavelength ends are therefore limited 
by mode competitions from free-running lasing modes of internal Fabry-Perot (FP) emitter.

Slow-light-based photonic crystal 
waveguides with cavities for all-optical 
switching applications
V . Janyani, N . Janrao, Malaviya National Institute of Technology 
(India)
Optical switching is one of the important functions required for optical 
signal processing, optical networks and modern telecommunications 
for long distances . Out of the many switching mechanisms available, 
the directional coupler technique is widely used . The use of slow light 
using photonic crystals proposed in this paper can further enhance the 
utility of directional coupler type switching . The device exhibits good 
switching characteristics between the two output ends . At around 
1 .55μm wavelength, which is the wavelength commonly used in long-
haul communication systems, it is usually possible to design directional 
couplers having coupling length of the order of 800μm by conventional 
means . However, instead of using normal dielectric waveguides, if slow-
light waveguides are used, the size of directional coupler can be reduced 
up to 5μm . This is smallest optical switch used in optical communications 
and networks . In the early stage the electromagnetically induced 
transparency, (EIT) has been used to achieve the slowing down of light 
speed, but this method has disadvantages constrained by the property 
of the material’s absorption lines and is sometimes not practical . With the 
rapid development of photonic crystals waveguides which can be used to 
control the light, a new era has opened . In comparison to other methods 
of generating slow light, photonic crystals waveguide-based slow light 
generation is more suitable, efficient and practical, and at the same time 
tenability of the characteristics can be incorporated in the device .
In this paper we propose slow light generation method using photonic 
crystal waveguide which has direct application in directional coupler type 
switching . We demonstrate that the designed switch has much smaller 
length while maintaining the switching speed desirable .

Giant tunable optical dispersion using 
chromo-modal excitation of a multimode 
waveguide
E . D . Diebold, K . Y . Hon, Z . Tan, J . Chou, T . Sienicki, C . Wang, B . 
Jalali, Univ . of California, Los Angeles (United States)
The ability to control chromatic dispersion is paramount in applications 
where the optical pulsewidth is critical, such as chirped pulse 
amplification and fiber optic communications . Typically, devices used 
to generate large amounts (>100 ps/nm) of chromatic dispersion are 
based on diffraction gratings, chirped fiber Bragg gratings, or dispersion 
compensating fiber . Unfortunately, these dispersive elements suffer from 
one or more of the following restrictions: (i) limited operational bandwidth, 
(ii) limited total dispersion, (iii) low peak power handling, or (iv) large 
spatial footprint . Here, we introduce a new type of tunable dispersive 
device, which overcomes these limitations by leveraging the large modal 
dispersion of a multimode waveguide in combination with the angular 
dispersion of diffraction gratings to create chromatic dispersion . This 
device, which may aptly be called the chromo-modal dispersion device 
(CMD), maps different wavelength components of an optical waveform 
to distinct modes of a multimode waveguide . The CMD then exploits 
the large modal dispersion inherent in a multimode waveguide to create 
chromatic dispersion (GVD) . We characterize the device’s dispersion, 
and demonstrate its ability to stretch a sub-picosecond optical pulse 
to nearly 2 nanoseconds in 20 meters of multimode optical fiber . As an 
application of this device, we demonstrate single-shot, time-wavelength 
atomic absorption spectroscopy of rubidium vapor at a repetition rate of 
90 .8 MHz .

150-nm continuous tuning of external-cavity 
quantum-dot lasers operated below 90-mA
Y . Chen, Union Optronics Corp . (Taiwan) and National Chiao Tung 
Univ . (Taiwan); G . Lin, P . Su, H . Cheng, National Chiao Tung Univ . 
(Taiwan)
Low threshold and widely tunable quantum-dot (QD) external-cavity 
lasers (ECLs) are implemented with Littrow configuration . The broad and 
flat gain spectra are achieved in novel chirped multilayer InAs/InGaAs/
GaAs QD gain media . We discuss the effect of cavity length on tuning 
characteristics of tuning range and tuning threshold . The tuning range 
of 3-mm ECL is only 63 nm (from 1218 to 1281 nm) with maximum 
threshold near its solitary threshold current, while the 1 .5-mm ECL can 
tune across 150 nm (from 1143 to 1293 nm) under recorod low injection 
current of 90 mA (1 .2 kA/cm2), which is a little below its solitary threshold 
current . The short-wavelength and long-wavelength ends are therefore 
limited by mode competitions from free-running lasing modes of internal 
Fabry-Perot emitter . As regard to the 2-mm ECL, the maximum threshold 
current of 2-mm ECL is no more than 90 mA (0 .9 kA/cm2) across the 
130 nm tuning range; nonetheless, the maximum threshold at tuning-
wavelength ends is about 1 .2 times its solitary threshold current of 
75 mA . With clever choice of cavity length, the short-wavelength and 
long-wavelength tuning ends are optimized under injection current even 
slightly above the threshold current of solitary device . We discuss the 
underlying mechanism and propose the strategy to further extend the 
tuning wavelength . It can be realized by the epitaxial growth design 
through optimization of QD-stacks of three or more chirped wavelengths, 
each with separate number of stacking layers .

Strongly asymmetric electromagnetic field 
distribution in chirped photonic crystal 
waveguides
A . E . Akosman, Bilkent Univ . (Turkey); D . Erbas, H . Kurt, TOBB 
Univ . of Economics and Technology (Turkey); E . Ozbay, Bilkent 
Univ . (Turkey)
In this study, we report a novel approach to the asymmetric light 
propagation by utilizing the two dimensional chirped photonic crystal 
waveguides . The asymmetry in the field distributions for forward and 
backward transmission of the modes in the chirped photonic crystal 
waveguide originates from the non-symmetric unit cells of the photonic 
crystal along the waveguide direction . The allowed waveguide modes 
are modified in each super cell of the chirped waveguide to ensure an 
effectively asymmetric propagation of the waveguide modes . The chirped 
waveguide is formed by modifying the distances between each neighbor 
columns of rods of square lattice photonic crystal waveguide . The 
design is optimized as to achieve a stop band and waveguide band for 
the operating frequencies for backward direction and forward direction, 
respectively . Thus, the operating frequencies are allowed to propagate 
along the waveguide until they reach to the stop band region for forward 
direction . Then, the modes are forced to leak out from the waveguide 
to the side paths of the photonic crystal structure . The similar path is 
followed by the light for the backward direction . The modes are forced 
to propagate through not the waveguide but the photonic crystal part 
until the pass band occurs for the waveguide region .Then, the modes are 
leaked into the waveguide and the highly asymmetric field distribution 
occurs at the outputs of the waveguide . The numerical results are verified 
with the experimental studies in micro-wave regime .

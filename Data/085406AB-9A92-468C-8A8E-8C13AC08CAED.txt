Handique et al. (2001)探討直流道的構型的
微液滴混合器。當微液滴流過直流道接觸流道壁
面，液滴內會產生一雙對稱的再循環流，但無法
快速且有效的達到完全混合。Song et al. (2003) 
為了加強微液滴內流體的接觸並觀察混合作
用，設計由四個轉彎組成的流道構型，當液滴流
經轉彎的流道，會產生不對稱的混沌對流，使微
液滴內的流體產生拉伸(Stretch)與折疊(Fold)。
Tung et al. (2009)在液滴內加入微粒子，利用不同
截面積的蜿蜒流道使液滴內產生非對稱流，並觀
察微粒子在微液滴內部的光強度數據與分佈情
況分析其混合結果。Sarrazin et al. (2007)以對稱
的流道結構使液滴融合，利用轉換結合(Shifted 
coalescence)設計使液滴與液滴垂直的撞在一
起，完全混合所需的時間接近於曲折的流道。
Hsieh et al. ( 2009)用液滴式微流體系統分析DNA
形態，將分子信標Molecular beacon (MB)，當作

拓展了更具實務的價值。 
本計劃為兩年的研究，第一年(98.08-99.07)
主要以注射式幫浦(Syringe pump)驅動流體形成
乳化液滴，並於液滴內部進行混合反應，觀察液
滴生成之物理特性與混合反應之機制，搭配數值
模擬分析，探討液滴形成與混合反應的影響參
數。第二年則將利用旋轉微流道驅動流體形成乳
化液滴。 
本期計劃中，我們利用流道結構，配合流率
的變化，生成包覆著兩種混合反應液體的液滴，
再以可視化技術，定性及定量探討液滴內混合反
應的現象。本實驗將兩種混合反應的液體分別注
入 Y 型流道，互相接觸後做為消散相流，再以油
為連續相流，利用流體聚焦 (Flow-focusing)方
式，生成被油包覆的雙液體微液滴。我們分別以
直流道及 U 型流道連接生成液滴的聚焦匯流
區，觀察液滴流經上述流道動過程中，混和反應
的現象。同時，我們也探討液滴大小對流體混合
流動與混合反應的影響。流體聚焦的流道設計
中，兩側的連續相流以壓力及剪力擠壓與拉伸位
於中央的消散相流體，產生頸縮並斷裂生成液滴 
(Haeberle et al. 2006)。除了，實驗觀察與量測液
滴內混合反應外，本研究亦利用數值方法，模擬
兩種混合反應的液體於十字型聚焦流道，由成
長、頸縮至斷裂生成液滴的流場與濃度場的時變
結果。

四、結論 
本研究利用壓力驅動流體注入實驗晶片，以
可視化設備觀察比較微液滴於直流道與 U 型流
道的混合反應。本實驗以化學藥劑反應後顏色深
淺的變化量化求出混合效率，比較液滴尺寸與流
道幾何的影響。將目前實驗所得的結果及要點歸
納整理如下： 
(1)由流體聚焦(Flow-focusing)的結構頸縮消散相
流體可以生成微液滴，縮短擴散距離，並由液
滴成長誘發之渦漩得到 60% 以上的混合效
率。搭配下游雙 U 結構的混合器可有效加強混
合作用。 
(2)固定消散相流率，生成液滴之長度(直徑)隨著
連續相流率增加而變小，而液滴生成頻率則隨
連續相流率增加。較小的液滴，在剛形成液滴
時，混合反應的效率越好。 
(3)液柱流經直流道混合區產生對稱的渦漩；在低
流率時，僅能達到 75%的混合效率；提高流率
後，可達到 90%的混合效率。 
(4)液柱流經 U 型流道混合區的每個轉角時，由  
於受到不對稱的慣性力及離心力，使得流體界
面有翻轉、扭曲及拉伸的現象，增加流體間的
接觸，加速混合反應。可視化實驗結果顯示 U 
型流道混合效率最終均能提升至 95%達到接近
完全混合，不受流率高低的影響。

ABSTRACT 
This paper reports flow visualization experiments of droplet 
emulsions in the microfluidics that is composed of a flow-
focusing (cross-junction) structure with a converging-diverging 
nozzle shaped section. The channel structure was made of 
polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) with a channel of 73 μm in depth 
and 100 μm in width. Oil as the continuous phase and water as 
the dispersed phase were injected into the cross-junction inlets 
to form water-in-oil droplets. It is found that adding the 
converging-diverging section to the cross-junction significantly 
reduces the droplet size without largely increases the overall 
flow resistance and still achieve excellent uniformity (with 
coefficient of variation less than 3.5%). Throat of the 
converging-diverging section determines the breakup location 
of droplet emulsion. The size of droplets is controllable in a 
wide range (6-116μm) through the variation of throat width 
between 23 and 100 μm in conjunction with the flow rates of 
continuous and dispersed phases. This emulsion technique 
demonstrates that droplets with diameter as small as 10 μm or 
less can be produced in microchannels having dimensions on 
the order of 100 μm without use of surfactants. 
 
INTRODUCTION 
Dispersions of micrometric droplet in two or more 
immiscible liquids are in general referred to as emulsions. 
Droplet emulsions have been frequently used in pharmaceutics, 
cosmetics, and food industry for a long period of time. Recently, 
droplet-based microfluidics has shown promising applications 
in chemical synthesis, protein crystallization and screening, and 
microanalysis [1-4]. In droplet microfluidic systems, 
micro/nanoliter-sized droplets are generated, transported, mixed, 
reacted, synthesized, or analyzed on a unit-packed device called 
lab-on-chip. A crucial process in droplet-based microfluidics is 
to precisely control the droplet volume. Droplet emulsions in 
microchannels have been reported to achieve good uniformity 
either for forms of water-in-oil or oil-in-water [5-11].  
One of the most popular microfluidic devices used for 
droplet generation is the one with a simple geometry of T-
junction. In T-junction microfluidics, formation of droplets is 
basically controlled by the surface force ratio of shear stress to 
interfacial tension and the flow rate ratio of dispersed phase to 
continuous phase [7-11]. The surface force ratio is referred to as 
capillary number. At low capillary numbers, pressure difference 
between the two immiscible fluids rather than the shear stress 
dominates the breakup process ‘squeezing’ the dispersed phase 
fluid to form droplets of plug type [7]. Formulations of simple 
scaling laws for the squeezing mechanism have been analyzed 
to predict the size of droplets [7-10,12]. At higher capillary 
numbers, shear stress of the continuous flow becomes to 
dominate the break up process and a transition from squeezing 
(pressure-dominated) to dripping (shear-dominated) mechanism 
occurs. Droplets of smaller size may be produced in a higher 
capillary number range through an increase of continuous phase 
viscosity and flow rate. In a T-junction microchannel, Nisisako 
et al. [13] has reported generation of droplets having a minimum 
diameter of 40 μm, which is however still on the order of the 
channel dimension. Notably, increasing viscosity and flow rate 
leads to a large increase in flow resistance and pressure drop. 
Another most employed device for droplet generation is 
cross-junction microfluidics in flow-focusing geometries. 
Formation mechanism of droplets generated in a symmetrical 
cross-junction is in many ways (e.g. the dependence of capillary 
number and flow rate) similar to the one in T-junction. 
Likewise, formulation of simple scaling laws was reported for 
prediction of squeezing break-up droplets at lower capillary 
numbers [14]. However, comparing to the T-junction much 
smaller size of droplets may be generated in flow focusing

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
Effect of Continuous Phase Flow 
Figure 3 shows visualization images of droplets generated 
at different continuous phase flow rates in the emulsion 
microfluidics with throat width Wt = 45 μm. It can be seen in the 
images of Qo = 0.05 and 0.15 ml/hr that liquid front of the 
dispersed phase (water) becomes narrow and necking as 
entering the converging section where the continuous phase 
(oil) flow makes a right angle turn and begins to accelerate 
creating a focused velocity gradient. As a result, the droplet 
breaks up in a close vicinity of the throat. The droplet size is 
approximately at the order of the throat width. When the 
continuous phase flow rate increases, smaller droplets are 
formed. At Qo = 0.25 and 0.35 ml/hr, droplets generated are 
found to have a diameter smaller than the width of throat. 
Uniformity of the droplets is of primary concern in the 
present experiments. Quantitatively, droplet uniformity is 
examined based on coefficient of variation (CV) that is defined

Effect of Throat Position 
The converging-diverging nozzle shaped section plays a 
crucial role in generation and control of droplet emulsion in 
cross microchannels. It has been observed that the throat 
determines the breakup location of the droplet during the 
emulsion process. Figure 8 shows the formation of droplets for 
different throat positions (Lt = 96 and 170 μm) but with the 
same throat width. It can be seen that necking occurs when the 
dispersed phase flow penetrates through the throat and the 
droplet breaks up at a rather fixed position due to the focused 
velocity gradient created by the nozzle shaped geometry. Figure 
9 compares variations of droplet size with continuous phase 
flow rate for the cases of Lt = 96 and 170 μm with the same 
throat width of Wt = 45 μm at Qw = 0.05 ml/hr. In both cases, the 
droplet size is reduced by an amount of about 55%. This is an 
indication that the throat position has indiscernible effect on 
droplet size.

CONCLUSIONS 
Visualization experiments have been carried out to 
examine the droplet emulsions in the microfluidics that is 
composed of a flow-focusing (cross-junction) structure with a 
converging-diverging nozzle shaped section. Oil as the 
continuous phase and water as the dispersed phase were injected 
into the cross-junction inlets to form water-in-oil droplets. No 
surfactants were used in the present experiments. It is found that 
adding the converging-diverging section to the cross-junction 
significantly reduces the droplet size without largely increases 
the overall flow resistance and still achieve excellent uniformity 
(with coefficient of variation less than 3.5%). Throat of the 
converging-diverging section determines the breakup location 
of droplet emulsion. The size of droplets is controllable through 
the variation of throat width along with the flow rates of 
continuous and dispersed phases. It is found that droplet size 
decreases with increasing continuous phase flow rate. At the 
lowest dispersed phase flow rate Qw = 0.01 ml/hr, the droplet 
size reduces more rapidly than the higher dispersed flow rates 
but droplets are formed in a small range of continuous flow rate 
(Qo ≤ 0.15 ml/hr). At higher dispersed phase flow rates Qw = 
0.05 and 0.10 ml/hr smaller droplets can be generated in the 
relatively higher range of continuous flow rates Qo = 0.05-0.40

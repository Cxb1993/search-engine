第一章 前言 
1.1 前言 
近年來隨著科技的進步與環境的發展，工業界開始致力於高產量、低成本與高速化方面
之發展，但是其生產設備、機械零件與加工刀具將面臨到高壓、高溫與高轉速的問題。對於
如何防止機械零件因為高速摩擦及模具加工所造成的磨耗損失與高溫腐蝕，而導致機械零件
使用壽命縮短，將是一個重要的探討的重點。而鍍膜技術對材料而言除提供裝飾性外，還提
供保護性及功能性，保護特性的鍍膜具有抗磨耗、耐衝擊、抗腐蝕等特性，因此以物理氣相
沉積(PVD)或化學氣相沉積(CVD)法鍍製高硬度、高韌性之奈米複合薄膜之研究已愈來愈受到
重視。此類種技術可製作陶瓷材料鍍層，如 TiN、TiC、TiCN、TiAlN、CrN 等，且鍍層硬度
高，與基材附著強度好[1-4]。一般針對提升材料耐磨或耐熱性質的表面改質分為兩大類[5]，
一種是擴散滲透法，透過熱擴散的機制，將金屬或非金屬元素由外滲透進入金屬基材內部，
如滲鋁、滲氮、滲碳等，可在材料表面形成化合物層，但需在高溫環境下進行，易導致材料
產生變形。而另一種表面改質為披覆處理，在材料表面披覆一層硬質鍍膜例如氮化鉻、氮化
鈦等，而披覆的方式又分為濕式與乾式兩種，濕式需要透過溶液作為介質來成長鍍層，例如
電鍍法、溶膠凝法等，此種方式易產生大量污水，且硬度、附著性較差，僅要求條件不高的
場合才會使用。而乾式披覆則包含了物理氣相沉積(PVD，Physical vapor Deposition)及化學氣
相沉積(CVD，Chemical vapor Deposition)兩種。此類種技術可製作陶瓷材料鍍層，如 TiN、
TiC、TiCN、TiAlN、CrN 等，且鍍層硬度高，與基材附著強度好。其中化學氣相沉積利用反
應氣體加熱產生化合反應，在基材上生成鍍層；雖均勻性高，但加熱溫度一般處於 800~1000oC 
以上，基材選擇受限，且因化學反應過程中易產生腐蝕汙染物較不環保。而物理氣相沉積，
利用真空及電漿活化反應原理，可以有效降低製程溫度，且過程中也沒有污染物產生的問題，
是一種較環保的技術，且透過製程的設計，可製作多層或複合薄膜，是一種相當具發展潛力
的表面改質方法。  
 
1.2 研究動機與目的 
    氮化鉻薄膜具有很好的抗腐蝕和抗磨耗特性，已廣泛的運用在表面改質上[6]，且鍍覆在
一般鋼基材上也有很好的附著性[6]，氮化鉻在高於 800oC的環境中才會開始氧化，並在表面
生成緻密的 Cr2O3[8]而使之有更好的擴散阻絕與耐磨耗能力，應用上常用於壓鑄模(Die 
Casting Mould)的改良[9]。然而氮化鉻薄膜硬度約 18GPa，相較如氮化鈦的 21~23GPa[10]，硬
度略低於其他硬膜，為了保留氮化鉻薄膜原有的特性，而再進一步提升其硬度、抗磨耗等性
能，需透過 Veprek[11]及 Musil[12]等人曾提出的奈米晶複合材料(nanocrystalline composite 
materials)的設計概念，目前此類奈米複合系統分為 Veprek[11]提出之奈米金屬氮化物與硬質
相之奈米複合薄膜(nc-MeN/hard phase, nc-為 nano crystalline, Me為 Ti, Zr, Nb, Cr等)，以及
Musil[12]提出的奈米金屬氮化物與軟質相複合薄膜(nc-MeN/soft phase, soft phase為 Cu, Ni, Y
等)。從 nc-TiN/a-Si3N4硬質相奈米複合薄膜的研究中發現，鍍膜具有 40~60GPa 的高硬度，
主要因素為 TiN奈米晶粒及非晶質的 Si3N4之間互相束縛，避免了晶界滑動的影響[10]，而矽
元素的添加，對降低其高溫(500oC)磨擦係數也有相當的助益，改善了原 TiN 的抗磨耗性能
[11] 。

2.1.1 直流磁控濺鍍系統 
濺鍍的基本原理為利用自由電子加速撞擊工作氣體(如氬氣)，將之解離形成正離子；正
離子被負電位的靶材吸引，撞擊濺射出靶材原子使之沉積於基材。而磁控濺射則是於靶材後
方加裝磁場設備，在靶材前形成磁力場，利用磁場束縛靶材表面電子運動軌跡，此時電子受
到磁場與電場的作用，而成螺旋狀運動，進而提高電子與工作氣體碰撞解離的機率。但傳統
磁控濺鍍法有著電漿距離過短的問題，原因為磁力線被束縛在靶前面的小空間，對於沉積複
雜形狀的工件會有鍍層不均勻的問題。故為改善此缺點，設計出非平衡磁控濺鍍法[15]，其
機制為透過磁極重新排列，將外圍磁場強度增強後，可以有效的將電子導引至基材表面，如
圖 2-1所示。

一般磁控濺射可分為直流式(DC)與射頻式(Radio Frequency, RF)，前者針對導電之金屬靶

後者。使用射頻磁控濺鍍時，利用介質靶沉積之薄膜成份，其金屬與氣體原子化合比，往往
會偏離原靶材之化合比，故仍然必須添加少量的反應氣體，如 O2、N2等來彌補損失的氣體原
子。與直流式磁控濺鍍相比，製作介質膜時，同樣是採用添加反應氣體的活化反應完成，但
直流式磁控濺鍍所使用的金屬靶，遠較介質靶容易製作而且廉價，加上導熱性佳，可承受高
功率電源仍不容易龜裂。因此在高產能的磁控濺鍍系統均採用直流磁控的活化反應製程取代
射頻製程來鍍覆介質膜，以大幅降低生產成本[16]。

應、陽極消失、電弧放電等問題。遲滯效應指的是濺鍍過程中，兩種型態的變遷，初始反應

2.2.2 氮化鉻鍍膜組成 
氮化鉻的組成一般分為Cr2N的六方結構，以及CrN的氯化鈉結構。以磁控濺鍍法為例，
其組成的結構與鍍製過程中氮分壓的改變相關，較高的氮分壓產生CrN，較低的氮分壓則產
生Cr2N[22]，根據Chiba等人[23]的研究發現，基材偏壓與氮分壓的改變亦會影響CrN鍍膜的優
選方向(Preferred orientation)，其關係如圖2-3所示。當氮分壓及基材偏壓增大時，其優選方向
從(200)至(111)至(220)之間轉變，一般而言CrN的硬度與其形貌組織及優選方向相關[24-25]，
當其優選方向為(111)時，硬度約12至14GPa，而優選方向改變為(200)時則能提升至18.5GPa。
而不同優選方向對硬度所造成的影響以(111)為例，其原因為(111)優選方向與氯化鈉結構的主
要滑動面相同所致，因而導致硬度下降[26]。氮分壓與基材偏壓除了會改變CrN鍍膜的優選方
向，對其化學成分的組成亦有所不同[23]，如圖2-4所示，經由改變分壓與基材偏壓的不同，
會分別形成Cr+CrN、Cr+Cr2N以及CrN三種型態之鍍膜。

2.3.2 氮化鉻矽奈米複合薄膜特性 
 
在文獻報導中[27]，氮化鉻矽奈米複合薄膜具有34GPa的硬度，與氮化鉻(18GPa)以及氮
化鈦(21~23GPa)[10]相比硬度高出許多，對於改善傳統氮化物膜硬度相對較低的缺點有一定的
幫助。而其磨耗性質如圖2-9所示[39]，在低轉速(400rpm)與AISI4340鋼對磨的磨耗試驗中，
磨擦係數明顯低於氮化鈦、氮化鉻以及經離子氮化處理之鋼材。此種低轉速低磨擦係數的特
性，應用在液壓幫浦的零件表面改質上有很大的幫助，由於液壓幫浦在高速磨擦時，是屬於
流體壓力行為，與材質並無一定的關係。而在低速旋轉時，常因油膜保護不足，需要低磨擦
係數的材質來避免幫浦啟動瞬間過大的磨擦力造成能量損失，故氮化鉻矽在此領域具有很高
的應用性。而不同矽含量的氮化鉻矽薄膜所對應的磨擦係數如圖2-10所示[27]，當矽含量增加
則平均磨擦係數從CrN的0.5降低至0.2，矽含量的增加導致磨擦係數的減少，是由於非晶質相
在磨擦反應下會形成平滑的表面，這種現象常常發生在陶瓷材料，例如Si3N4和H2O反應會產
生SiO2或是Si(OH)2的磨耗層，而SiO2和Si(OH)2則是已知對自我磨潤有作用的材料[40]，透過
矽的添加可以促進此類材料的形成，提升其磨耗性能。 
在抗高溫氧化能力方面，氮化鉻矽薄膜同樣具備良好的特性[41]，從圖 2-11 高溫氧化溫
度及時間對應增重結果研究發現，氮化鉻矽薄膜的增重明顯小於氮化鉻薄膜，且其抗高溫氧
化能力與矽含量有其關連性。CrN 薄膜於高溫氧化時會形成 Cr2O3層於表面，使其有較好的
抗氧化能力，而 Cr-Si-N 薄膜除了一樣能形成 Cr2O3也可能會形成 SiO2氧化層保護底材。研
究中也發現 Cr-Si-N 所形成的氧化層厚度較 CrN 薄，可提前而且更有效的阻止底材繼續被氧
化，使其具有更好的抗高溫氧化能力。

非晶質相包圍奈米晶粒的情形，但是隨著矽含量增加，其晶粒尺寸又變大，在 Ti-Si-N薄膜之

4.1.4硬度分析 
各薄膜之硬度、彈性係數與薄膜第三元素添加量的關係分別如圖 4-45(a)、(b)所示，由圖
4-45(a)可發現當薄膜摻雜極少量矽元素(約 0.7 at.%)時硬度值增高至 21GPa，接著隨矽含量增
加而硬度下降至 16.5 GPa，而隨著矽含量增加至 10.1 at.%時，薄膜硬度值大幅度上升至 24.6 
GPa，此硬度增加為晶粒細化強化所致。但是當矽含量增加至 12.9 at.%，硬度卻遽降至 12GPa。
矽含量與 Cr-Si-N薄膜硬度之關連性在相關文獻[81，82]亦有類似之報導。 
當薄膜摻雜銅元素約 2.14~2.57 at.%時具有 18~19 GPa的最大硬度，但是隨著銅含量增加
至 13.58 at.%時，薄膜硬度值大幅度地下降至 9GPa，此現象與Musil[83]與 Baker[84]在氮化鉻
摻雜銅時所提出之結果相符，當銅摻雜量極少時將有助於提升鍍膜的硬度；而當銅含量增多
達 3.7~14.9 at.%時，將造成薄膜硬度下降。但是當薄膜銅含量自 14.9 at%增加至 21.9 at%時，
其平均硬度值卻上升至 17.3GPa，此一硬度值隨銅含量增加而往上升的趨勢應與薄膜銅原子
已足夠將 CrN晶粒包圍而抑制晶粒尺寸，使整個奈米複合薄膜獲得晶粒細化強化效果有相當
之關聯性。 
Cr-X-N薄膜之彈性係數與矽或銅元素添加量的關係相當一致，均隨著第三元素添加量增

形成的晶界，亦能成為擴散阻礙層，導致擴散速率下降[85]。 
本研究使用能量散佈光譜儀(EDS)對高溫氧化後薄膜表面進行定量分析。定量分析區域如

鍍膜表面之 Cr2O3顆粒則較為鬆散，除了顆粒狀 Cr2O3之外，底層則偵測到少量的矽成份。推
測由於 Cr-Si-N鍍膜中矽原子的添加，能提早阻擋氧的內擴散，使得 Cr原子無繼續外擴散形
成大量緻密的 Cr2O3。由鍍膜高溫氧化剝落後邊緣多層型態的成份定量分析觀察，剝落部分均
均含有較高含量的矽，應是電子束穿透偵測到矽晶片基板所致。 
整體而言，由 EDS表面元素分析，僅觀察到 Cr2O3氧化物的形成，並未發現 SiO2的成份。
由於 Cr2O3其活化能低於 SiO2，高溫氧化下形成機會較高，而非晶質 Si3N4的共價鍵結亦會使
得矽原子較難以擴散，因此於高溫氧化下，Cr 的擴散速度相對於 Si 會更快的在表面形成
Cr2O3[86]。

關。整體而言，Cr-Si-N 鍍膜確實比 CrN 鍍膜更能阻擋氧元素的內擴散，進一步保護底材。

少，原因可能為 CrSi中間層影響，或是矽晶片矽元素在高溫下向鍍膜內部擴散所致。

發明人/創作人 李志偉、郭裕竹、張育銓 
本技術利用電漿放射監控系統來穩定控制鍍膜製程，使用非對稱脈
衝直流磁控濺鍍法分別沉積氮化鉻銅與氮化鉻矽奈米複合薄膜。本
技術利用妥善的鉻/銅(或矽)靶材功率控制，以及適當的試樣-靶材
距離，可獲致所需之各種不同銅/矽含量的氮化鉻銅/氮化鉻矽奈米
複合薄膜薄膜，本技術並提供各種銅/矽含量之 Cr-Cu-N或 Cr-Si-N
薄膜的相對應微結構特性、機械性質與耐磨耗性質，可提供模具、
醫藥等相關產業應用。其中含適當銅含量的氮化鉻銅奈米複合薄膜
可特別應用於抗菌用途；含適當矽含量的氮化鉻矽奈米複合薄膜具
有抗高溫氧化應用之潛力。 
技術說明 The nanocomposite Cr-Cu-N/Cr-Si-N thin films have been deposited 
by a bipolar asymmetric pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering 
process. A plasma emission spectrometer was employed to monitor the 
stability of sputtering processing. Various Cu/Cr contents of 
Cr-Cu-N/Cr-Si-N films can be obtained through suitable controls of 
Cu/Si target powers and distances between sample and target. The 
microstructures, mechanical and tribological properties of Cr-Cu-N 
/Cr-Si-N coatings can be provided by this technique, which can be 
applied in the die and medicine industries. The application of the 
Cr-Cu-N thin films with suitable Cu contents to the antibacterial field 
is achieved. It is also observed that the anti-oxidation performance of 
the Cr-Si-N coatings with sufficient Si contents is promising.

The concept of nanocrystalline transition metal nitride
(nc-MeN) nanocomposite hard coatings incorporated with
soft metal has been proposed by Musil and Vlček since 1998
[1]. Many efforts have been devoted to investigate such
nanocomposite thin films, for example, Zr–Cu–N [2,3], Ti–Cu–
N [4] and Cr–Cu–N [5,6] coatings. Sufficient hardness, good wear
and tribological properties were achieved through proper adjust-
ments of deposition parameters and soft metal contents in the
nanocomposite coatings. In a previous study [6], a high hardness and
with around 2.1 to 2.6 at.% Cu was prepared by a pulsed DC
magnetron sputtering system. The hardness of the Cr–Cu–Ncoating
system decreased with increasing Cu content up to 14.9 at.%.
However, a slight increase of hardness was observed in the Cr–Cu–
N coating with 14.9 at% Cu [6].
In the present work, the influences of higher Cu content, up to
24.3 at.%, on microstructures and nanomechanical properties of
Cr–Cu–N thin films were explored. It is also well known that
copper is one of the antibacterial metals, which is already adopted
in the stainless steel [7,8] and TiO2 photocatalysts [9,10] to
improve their bactericidal properties successfully. The antibacte-The nanocomposite Cr–Cu–N thin films with Cu content ranging from 1.6 to 24.3 at.% have been deposited by a bipolar asymmetric pulsed
DC reactive magnetron sputtering system. A strong CrN (200) texture is found for each coating. The columnar structure was replaced by a dense
and compact morphology when the coating contained more than 8.2 at.% Cu. An unusual hardness enhancement is observed on the coating with
15.7 at% Cu, which is possibly due to its grain refinement effect. For the coating containing 15.7 at.% Cu, a combination of high hardness, low
coefficient of friction and good antibacterial properties are achieved. The Cr–Cu–N nanocomposite thin film developed in this study with
sufficient hardness and antibacterial abilities can be used as a promising candidate to deposit on the surfaces of medical apparatus and food
processing equipments.

Abstract 
The nanocomposite Cr-Si-N thin films with various Si contents have been prepared by a 
bipolar asymmetric pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering system. The Cr-Si-N coatings 
exhibit a mixed CrN and Cr2N phases when Si contents is lower than 3.1 at.%, whereas a 
single CrN phase is observed for coatings with higher Si content. The crystallite size of 
Cr-Si-N decreases with increasing Si content and reaches to a minimum of 5.2 nm when the 
Si content is 10.1 at.%. A high hardness, low friction coefficient and good nanowear 
resistance are found in the Cr-Si-N coatings with around 10.1 at.% Si in this work. 
Keyword: Cr-Si-N thin film, pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering, nanoscratch, 
nanowear 
1. Introduction 
Chromium nitride coating has been characterized for its sufficient mechanical properties, 
good corrosion resistance and adequate wear performance [1-3]. Recently, according to the 
concept of superhard nanocomposite hard coatings proposed by Vepřek and Reiprich in 1995 
[4], the addition of Si to form amorphous silicon nitride phase has been adopted to improve 
the mechanical properties and oxidation resistance of binary chromium nitride coatings 
successfully [5-15].  
The abilities to eliminate the arcing problem and having a high sputtering rate have been 
reported on the ceramic hard coatings deposited by a pulsed DC magnetron sputtering system 
[16]. Sufficient adhesion properties of CrN and Cr-Cu-N films deposited by this method were 
also explored by Lee et al. [17-19]. In this work, the ternary Cr-Si-N thin films with various 
Si contents were prepared by a pulsed DC magnetron sputtering system. The influences of Si 
on the phase, microstructure, mechanical and nanotribological properties of Cr-Si-N films 
were investigated.

300W and 100W power to pure Cr and pure Si targets, respectively. The purity and diameter 
of Cr and Si targets are 99.99 wt.% and 76.2 mm, respectively. The pulse frequency was kept

4. Conclusions: 
A series of ternary Cr-Si-N thin films with Si contents ranging from 0.3 to 12.9 at.% 
have been prepared by the pulsed DC magnetron sputtering system. It is observed that the 
columnar structure of the coating refines with the addition of Si. The surface roughness, grain 
size, hardness, residual nanowear depth and friction coefficient of Cr-Si-N films decrease with 
increasing Si content. For Cr-Si-N coatings with 10.1 at.% Si, a sufficient mechanical and 
nanotribological properties including an average hardness of 24.6 GPa and friction coefficient 
of 0.08 has been achieved in this study.

The effect of Cu content on the microstructures, mechanical and antibacterial properties 
of Cr-Cu-N nanocomposite coatings deposited by pulsed DC reactive magnetron 
sputtering 
Yu-Chu Kuo1, Jyh-Wei Lee2,3,4,#, Chaur-Jeng Wang1, Yu-Jie Chang5

Abstract 
The nanocomposite Cr-Cu-N thin films with Cu content ranging from 1.6 to 24.3 at.% 
have been deposited by a bipolar asymmetric pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering system. 
A strong CrN (200) texture is found for each coating. The columnar structure was replaced by 
a dense and compact morphology when the coating contained more than 8.2 at% Cu. An 
unusual hardness enhancement is observed on the coating with 15.7 at% Cu, which is possibly 
due to its dense and fine structure. For the coating containing 15.7 at.% Cu, a combination of 
high hardness, low scratch coefficient and good antibacterial properties are achieved. The 
Cr-Cu-N nanocomposite thin film developed in this study possessed of sufficient hardness and 
antibacterial abilities is a promising candidate to be coated on the surfaces of medical 
apparatus and food processing equipments. 
Keyword: Cr-Cu-N thin film, pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering, nanoscratch, 
antibacterial properties 
1. Introduction 
The concept of nano crystalline transition metal nitride (nc-MeN) nanocomposite hard 
coatings incorporated with soft metal has been proposed by Musil and Vlček since 1998 [1]. 
Many efforts have been devoted to investigate such nanocomposite thin films, for example, 
Zr-Cu-N [2,3], Ti-Cu-N [4] and Cr-Cu-N [5,6] coatings. Sufficient hardness, good wear and 
tribological properties were achieved through proper adjustments of deposition parameters 
and soft metal contents in the nanocomposite coatings. In previous study [6], a high hardness 
and low scratch coefficient Cr-Cu-N thin film with around 2.1 to 2.6 at.% Cu was prepared by 
a pulsed DC magnetron sputtering system. The hardness of the Cr-Cu-N coating system 
decreased with increasing Cu content up to 14.9 at.%. However, a slight increase of hardness 
was observed in the Cr-Cu-N coating with 14.9 at% Cu [6].  
In present work, the influences of higher Cu content, up to 24.3 at.%, on microstructures 
and nanomechanical properties of Cr-Cu-N thin films were explored. It is also well known 
that copper is one of antibacterial metals, which is already adopted in the stainless steel [7,8] 
 22
A strong preferred orientation of CrN (200) is observed in each coating. For the C1 and C2 
films, the Cr2N (002) and (111) reflections are also found. Due to the Cu (111), SUS 420 (110) 
and CrN (200) intensities reflections are coincide at 2θ=43.3~43.8o ranges, the crystalline Cu 
phase is characterized based on Cu (200) reflection at 2θ=50.43o. When the specimens with 
more than 8.2 at.% Cu (samples C3~C6), the Cu (200) peak is more obvious with increasing 
Cu content. No reflections of Cu are found on C1 and C2 specimens possibly due to a low Cu 
content (<3.7 at.%), which could not cause reflection. It is argued that the Cu phase may exist 
and possibly the Cu atoms just form amorphous structures or are trapped in the Cr-N lattice. A 
possible mechanism for Cr2N and CrN phase evolution in the Cr-Si-N coating system was 
suggested by Lee and coworkers [12]. The Si acts as a substitutional solid solution element in 
the Cr-Si-N film and extra Cr ions will react with Cr-N phase to form the Cr2N. As the Si 
content is higher, amorphous SixNy phase will thus produce and the Cr2N phase will disappear 
[12]. Similarly, the limited Cu content may also be trapped in the Cr-N phase and cause 
additional Cr ions to form Cr2N phase. When the Cu content reaches 8.2 at.%, the Cu 
crystalline phase produces and the Cr2N phase thus vanishes. 
Typical fine granular structure is observed on surface of each film. The cross-sectional 
morphologies of C1 (Cu=1.6 at.%) and C4 (Cu=15.7 at.%) coatings are shown in Figs. 3 (a) 
and (b), respectively. A thin Cr-Cu mixed interlayer around 250 nm in thickness is also 
indicated. The thickness of coating ranges from 1.6 µm (C1) to 3.0 µm (C6) due to the 
difference of Cu target power as listed in Table 1. Fast deposition rates from 0.56 nm/s to 1.15 
nm/s are observed for the nitride coatings. As compared to the columnar structure shown in 
Fig. 3(a), a dense and compact microstructure is shown in Fig. 3 (b) for the C4 coating 
containing 15.7 at.% Cu. The columnar structure is no longer observed. For the coatings with 
less than 8.2 at.% Cu (C1~C3 coatings), the columnar structure is clean to be seen. The 
columnar structure can be ceased by high Cu content to stop the grain growth and to stimulate 
a renucleation of the grains to form a globular structure [4]. The critical Cu content 
incorporated to form a dense and fine grain structure without a columnar morphology is 7 
at.% in the Zr-Cu-N system [3] and around 7-11 at.% in the Cr-Cu-N films [6]. Through this 
work, the critical Cu content is narrow down in the range of 8.2 to 11 at% in the Cr-Cu-N 
coating system. 
The plan-view TEM micrographs of C6 film at lower and higher magnifications are 
shown in Figs. 4(a) and (b), respectively. In Fig. 4(a), large grains around 100 nm in diameter 
are observed. In addition, many dark particles around 40-50 nm in diameter are uniformly 
distributed in the substrate, which are further identified to be the CrN grains by the diffraction 
patterns inserted in the upper right corner in Fig.4(a). The dark CrN grain in high 
magnification is presented in Fig. 4(b). According to the EDS data, the chemical composition 
of this phase is 40.1%Cr-40.2%N-19.7%Cu (in at.%), which is denoted as region A in Fig. 
4(b). On the other hand, the substrate (region B) adjacent to the grain consists of 
35.9%Cr-38.0%N-26.1%Cu (in at.%). Apparently, the Cu content in the substrate is higher 
than that in CrN grains. The TEM images and EDS data exhibit quite different information as 
 24
when Cu exists as a Cu-rich or Cu-containing phase and distributes uniformly [15]. If Cu 
exists as a solute in a solid solution, the antibacterial property of the Cr-Cu-N coating will be 
limited. As compared with the XRD, TEM and EDS data shown in Figs. 2 and 4, respectively, 
the Cu-rich substrate and Cu-rich CrN grains are responsible for the excellent antibacterial 
properties of C6 coating. It can be concluded that the application of the hard and bactericidal 
Cr-Cu-N nanocomposite coatings on the surfaces of medical apparatus and food processing 
facilities is promising. 
4. Conclusion 
The Cr-Cu-N thin films with various Cu content has been prepared by a bipolar 
asymmetry pulsed DC reactive magnetron sputtering system in this study. The typical 
columnar structure vanished when the coating contained more than 8.2 at% Cu. An unusual 
hardness enhancement to 21 GPa in maximum and a lower scratch coefficient to 0.13 is 
observed on the coating with 15.7 at% Cu. A 100% bactericidal rate against E. Coli is 
achieved on the Cr-Cu-N coating containing 24.3 at.% Cu. For coatings with more than 15.7 
at.% Cu, a combination of sufficient hardness and good antibacterial properties are observed 
in the Cr-Cu-N nanocomposite thin films, which make them to be promising candidates 
applied on the surface of medical and food processing equipment.

 Although the HEA system is designed to be a possible candidate for high temperature 
material, the application of the aluminizing process to this novel system has never been 
reported. In this study, a high entropy alloy was pack cementation aluminized at 900oC for 4 
hrs. A static oxidation test at 1100oC for 196 hrs was held to explore the influence of the

2. Experimental Procedure 
The high entropy alloy was melted, in a 25-kilogram induction furnace, in air. Pure iron, 
pure nickel, pure aluminum, pure cobalt and pure chromium pellets with more than 99.9% 
purity were used as raw materials. The liquid alloy was then poured into a 10-kilogram 
ceramic shell mold at 1650oC. The chemical composition of the as-cast alloy was analyzed as 
16.9%Fe-35.0%Cr-10.1%Al-22.2%Ni-15.8%Co (in at.%). A FCC and BCC mixed structure 
was identified with an X-ray diffractometer (XRD, Rigaku, Model DMAX-B) equipped with 
a Cu target. HEA specimens with dimensions 9.6 mm×3.7 mm×2.3 mm were pack 
cementation aluminized at 900oC for 4 hrs. Detailed pack aluminizing process has been 
reported elsewhere [7,8].  
A static oxidation test of untreated substrate and aluminized HEA was conducted in 
alumina crucibles at 1100oC for 196 hrs. Weight changes were measured before and after tests 
with a precision electronic balance with 1x 10-4 g accuracy.  
Surface and cross-sectional morphologies of each specimen were further analyzed with a 
field emission electron probe microanalyzer (FE-EPMA, JXA-8500F, JEOL). The chemical 
compositions of specimens were also detected by FE-EPMA and EDS with the aid of a 
ZAF-corrected program. Crystal structures of specimens were identified by XRD. 
Microhardness of each specimen was measured with a Vickers microhardness tester 
(Matsuzawa, DMH-1) under 0.1N load. 
3. Results and Discussion 
3.1 Microstructures, phases and hardness of the aluminide layer 
The cross-sectional backscattered electron (BSE) image of an untreated HEA is revealed 
in Fig. 1. A mixed structure with two phases is obvious. The island-like white region, 
indicated as A with the average chemical composition of 9.0%Al-30.6%Cr-19.1%Fe 
-16.4%Co-24.9%Ni (in at.%) is analyzed to be the FCC phase, whereas the average chemical 
composition of the grey region B is 12.0%Al-38.8%Cr-16.3%Fe-12.8%Co-20.1%Ni (in 
at.%), which is the BCC phase due to its lower content of Ni and higher contents of Cr and 
Al. In addition, very fine grey particles around 1-2 µm in diameter are well dispersed in 
region B with the average content of 29.6%Al-13.9%Cr-10.1%Fe-11.0%Co-35.4%Ni (in 
at.%), which are possibly the Ni3Al phase. The microhardness values of the region A and B 
are HV177±2 and HV 405±17, respectively. For the aluminized specimen, an aluminide 
layer around 25.9 ± 2.1 µm was observed on the surface of HEA as indicated with a white 
arrow in Fig.2. No apparent interdiffusion zone is found between the aluminide layer and

Almost all surface oxides spalled, except a strip of porous oxide (indicated by an arrow in 
region C) and an oxide layer (region D) underneath are found. The imprint of the oxide grains 
around 2 µm in diameter formed during oxidation is clearly to be seen on the substrate surface. 
The average chemical composition of the porous oxide is 57.0%O-19.5%Al-4.0%Cr -7.0%Fe 
-5.8%Co-6.7%Ni (in at.%), which is the AlFeO3 phase according to the XRD result. The 
AlFeO3 phase is a solid solution with high contents of Cr, Co and Ni dissolved. For the oxide 
underneath, it is the α-Al2O3 phase based on the chemical analysis and XRD. It is believed 
that the non-protective and loosely adhered porous AlFeO3 phase formed on the outermost 
region of the untreated HEA during 1100oC oxidation test. The oxygen thus penetrated the 
porous oxide and formed a protective α-Al2O3 layer between the porous oxide scale and 
substrate. The porous AlFeO3 scale spalled after cooling to room temperature. The 
cross-sectional morphology of the oxidized HEA still exhibits two phase structure similar to 
that shown in Fig.1. The chemical composition of the FCC structure is almost the same. 
However, the content of the BCC structure changes into 4.5%Al-50.2%Cr-20.0%Fe-15.8%Co 
-9.5%Ni (in at.%). which is rather low in Al and Ni contents. The average microhardness 
values of FCC and BCC phases are Hv139 and Hv407, respectively, which are almost the 
same as compared with these before oxidation test. 
Huang et al. [3] investigated the high temperature oxidation behavior of thermal sprayed 
AlSiTiCrFeCoNiMo0.5 and AlSiTiCrFeNiMo0.5 HEAs at 1100 oC for 150hrs. The weight gain 
data around 8 to 10 mg/cm2 was observed due to less protective titanium oxide formed on 
HEA surfaces. In this study, although porous oxide was formed during oxidation test, a rather 
low weight change is found on the HEA substrate due to protective α-Al2O3 layer formed 
beneath the oxide scale.

2. Experimental details  
The Mo–Ru alloy coatings were fabricated onto the tungsten carbide (WC) substrate by 
RF magnetron sputtering technique with Cr and Ti interlayers deposited by DC magnetron 
sputtering. Pure metal targets of Mo, Ru, Cr and Ti with diameters of 76.2 mm, were adopted 
as source materials for sputtering. The target-to-substrate distance was kept at 200 mm for all 
sputtering guns. The chamber was evacuated down to 1.33×10−3 Pa followed by the inlet of 
argon gas as a plasma source. Before deposition, all the targets were pre-sputtered for 5 min 
in order to clean the target surfaces. The substrate holder was rotated with a speed of 6.0 rpm 
and heated to 600°C during sputtering. The working pressure was 1.33×10−1 Pa. The Ti and 
Cr interlayers were deposited successively for 45 min with an input power of 100 W. Then, 
Mo and Ru are co-sputtered with 265W and 465W power, respectively, for 35min. The 
individual thickness of laminated Ti, Cr and Mo-Ru coatings was 0.13µm, 0.21µm and 
0.65µm, respectively.  
To investigate the stripping phenomenon, the chemical wet stripping process was 
performed by immersion coating assemblies in a selective Cr etching solution, which is a mix 
of 6% (NH4)2Ce(NO3)6 + 8% CH3COOH + H2O and denoted as CAN-HAc in this paper.

points for the etching pits, cracking and stripping of the coating. The XRD pattern of the

The Mo-Ru coating was completely stripped after immersion in the etchant for 32 min. 
Very fine granular structures are found on the stripped surface. A rather smooth surface with 
roughness values, Ra 2.86 ± 0.10 nm and Rq 3.70 ± 0.11 nm, is observed. According to the 
chemical analysis data and XRD pattern shown in Fig. 2, the Mo-Ru coating and Cr interlayer 
were detached completely. Only a smooth Ti interlayer is found on the WC substrate. No 
apparent damage of the WC substrate was observed. Obviously, the CAN-HAc etching 
solution is a successful and efficient stripping recipe to detach the Mo-Ru coating without 
damage to the WC substrate.  
3.3. In-situ AFM observation of chemical stripping process    
The in-situ AFM images of the specimen immersed in the diluted etchant for 49, 121 and 
223 mins are shown in Figs. 4(a) to (c), respectively. In Fig. 4(a), a surface crack was 
observed in the center. The coating is still adhered with the interlayers and WC substrate. 
After etching for 121 mins, parallel lines are found on both sides of the crack. It is suggested 
that the coating is not adhered firmly to the substrate due to the dissolution of Cr interlayer 
underneath. The cracked coating thus vibrates when the contact mode AFM cantilever tip 
scans on the surface back and forth. The parallel lines formed on the AFM image due to the 
periodic vibration of the loosely adhered coating caused by the several tens nN contact force 
of AFM tip. When immersion for 233 min, the cracked coating finally breaks away and 
delaminates as shown in Fig.4 (c). Figure 4 (d) illustrates the secondary electron (SE) image 
of the in-situ AFM observed region, in which a partial stripped coating after etched for 233 
mins is indicated with an arrow. Again, circular cracks and spalled coating debris are obvious 
on the surface of specimen. A similar crack blister and brittle circular fracture occurred on the 
corroded hard coating has been reported by Komotori et al. [13]. It is also noticeable that the 
concentration of etchant plays an important role to the etching rate in this study. For the 
normal concentration of CAN-HAc, the etching rate is about 0.34 nm/s at 20oC. However, the 
etching rate decreases to around 8 or more times slower than normal condition when the 
concentration of etchant dilutes to half of original ones. 
3.4. Stripping mechanism of Mo-Ru coatings  
Since the CAN-Hac solution is formulated to selective etch the Cr metal [14], the Mo-Ru 
coatings are deposited on tungsten carbide substrates with Ti and Cr interlayers intentionally.

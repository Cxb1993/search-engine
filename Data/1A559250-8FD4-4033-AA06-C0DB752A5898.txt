InGaAs-Capped InAs/GaAs quantum-Dot Infrared Photodetectors with 10.4 μm Responses 
Wei-Hsun Lin, Chi-Che Tseng, Kuang-Ping Chao, Shu-Chen Mai and Shih-Yen Lina), Research Center for Applied 
Sciences, Academia Sinica, Taiwan, a)shihyen@gate.sinica.edu.tw 
    Quantum-dot infrared photodetectors (QDIPs) have been widely investigated in recent years. 
For most of the QDIPs reported in literature, the responses of the devices are usually limited in the 
mid-wavelength infrared wavelength range (MWIR; 3-5 μm). To improve the disadvantage, several 
reports regarding dot-in-a-well (DWELL) have been proposed to achieve long-wavelength infrared 
(LWIR; 8-12 μm) wavelength detection [1]-[2]. However, for such devices, an additional InGaAs 
layer prior to the QD growth is always required to achieve the devices operated at longer detection 
wavelengths. The additional InGaAs layer may be disadvantageous for uniform QD formation and 
may also result in strain accumulation in the QD structures. In this report, the influence of InGaAs 
capping layer on the performances of 10-period InAs/GaAs QDIPs is investigated. With an 
additional 8 nm In0.15Ga0.85As capping layer grown after 2.5 ML InAs QD deposition, 8.4 μm 
response is observed for the device. The results suggest that the dominate transition mechanism is 
changed from the QD excited state-wetting (WL) state to the QD excited state-QW ground state in 
the InGaAs layer. To further expand the detection window to a longer wavelength, a 2.0 ML 
InAs/GaAs QDIP with the same 8 nm In0.15Ga0.85As capping layer is prepared. The detection 
wavelength of the device is expanded to 10.4 μm. Due to smaller QD heights are observed for the 
2.0 ML InAs QDs, higher energy states in the QD structure have resulted in a reduced energy 
difference between the QD excited states and the InGaAs state. In this case, LWIR response at 10.4 
μm is observed for the InAs/GaAs QDIP. Similar normal incident absorption of the device 
compared with the conventional QDIPs has proved that the unique property of QDIPs is not 
influenced by the additional capping layer. All samples investigated in this report are grown on 
(100)-oriented semi-insulating GaAs substrates by Riber Compact 21 solid-source molecular beam 
epitaxy system,The device structures are shown in Table I. Three samples with no InGaAs capping 
layer, 8 nm In0.15Ga0.85As capping layer/2.5 ML InAs QDs and 8 nm In0.15Ga0.85As capping 
layer/2.0 ML InAs QDs are referred to as samples A, B and C. The normalized 10 K spectral 
responses of devices A and B at 2.0 V are shown in Fig. 1. As shown in the figure, with the 
additional InGaAs capping layer, the dominate detection wavelengths have changed from ~ 6 μm to 
7.9 μm. According to our previous report, the main transition mechanism of QDIPs is the transition 
between the QD first excited state to the wetting layer state [3]. It is reasonable to assume that the 
presence of the InGaAs layer has provided an additional lower-energy state compared with the WL 
state such that longer detection wavelength is observed for the device with the InGaAs capping 
layer. Therefore, by keeping the same InGaAs state, it is possible to achieve even longer detection 
wavelength by increasing the QD energy levels. The simplest approach to do this is to reduce the 
QD coverage. The AFM images of the 2.5 and 2.0 ML InAs QDs are shown in Fig. 2. As shown in 
the figure, 3.35 x 1010 and 1.62 x 1010 cm-2 dot densities with average dot heights 6.9 and 4.4 nm 
are observed for the 2.5 and 2.0 Ml InAs QDs, respectively. With the same 8 nm In0.15Ga0.85As 
capping layer and reduced InAs coverage 2.0 ML, device C has exhibited a longer detection 
wavelength at 10.4 μm. The 10 K spectral response of device C at 2.0 V is shown in Fig. 3. 
Although the dominate response of device C is at 10.4 μm, a weaker response at 5.7 μm is still 
observed for the device, which is attributed to the QD first excited state-WL state transition. In 
conclusion, long detection wavelength up to 10.4 μm is achieved by reducing the InAs coverage 
down to 2.0 ML with an additional In0.15Ga0.85As capping layer. The high responsivity 1.2 A/W of 
the device has exhibited high crystal quality of the sample. Similar normal incident absorption of 
the device compared with the conventional QDIPs has proved that the unique property of QDIPs is 
not influenced by the additional capping layer.

Abstract 
Detection wavelengths in the long-wavelength infrared range of InGaAs-capped 
InAs/GaAs quantum-dot infrared photodetectors (QDIPs) are observed. Compared 
with the standard QDIPs without the InGaAs-capped layer, a longer detection 
wavelength of 10.4 μm is obtained for the device with 2.0 mono-layer (ML) InAs 
coverage. The phenomenon is attributed to the lower energy difference between the 
quantum-dot excite state and the quantum-well ground state of the InGaAs-capped 
layer. Similar dark currents and high normal incident absorption of the 
InGaAs-capped QDIP have demonstrated its potential in the application in 
high-performance and grating-less FPAs operated at high temperatures.

III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
The 10 K spectral responses of the standard QDIP at + 2.0 V are shown in Fig. 1 
(a). As shown in the figure, symmetric responses are observed at different voltage 
polarities. Wide detection window ranging from 3-9 μm with peak detection 
wavelength at 6 μm is observed for the device. The performances of the standard 
QDIP are generally observed for such devices.11 Also observed in the figure are the 
high peak responsivities up to 4-5 A/W of the device. The results suggest that 
optimized growth conditions are obtained for the standard QDIP. To investigate the 
transition mechanisms of the standard QDIP, the 10 K photoluminescence excitation 
(PLE) spectrum of the standard QDIP sample is shown in Fig. 1 (a). The detection 
energy is set at the PL peak position 1.121 eV of the sample. As shown in the figure, 
there are three major peaks observed in the PLE spectrum, which are (a) the first QD 
excited state EQD,1, (b) the second QD excited state EQD,2 and (c) the wetting-layer 
(WL) state EWL. According to our previous report, the main transition mechanisms 
responsible for the spectral responses of the standard QDIP are the transitions 
between the QD excited states and the WL state.12 The energy differences of 
EQD,1-EWL and EQD,2-EWL extracted from the PLE spectrum are 0.230 and 0.155 eV, 
respectively. In this case, the detection wavelengths of the standard QDIP should 
cover from 0.230 to 0.155 eV (5.4-8 μm), which is consistent with the spectral

The other important characteristic for QDIPs is their potential in 
high-temperature operation. To achieve high-temperature operation, one important 
issue is the depression of dark currents. The 10 K dark currents of the standard and 
InGaAs-capped QDIPs are shown in Fig. 3. As shown in the figure, similar I-V curves 
are observed for the two devices. The phenomenon suggest that with the insertion of 
InGaAs capping layers in the QD structures, no significant dark current enhancement 
is observed for the InGaAs-capped QDIP. The activation energies of the two devices

IV. CONCLUSIONS 
In conclusion, the InGaAs-capped QDIP operated at LWIR range is investigated. 
With the insertion of additional InGaAs capping layers and reduced QD coverage, a 
LWIR response at 10.4 μm is observed for the InGaAs-capped QDIP. Compared with 
the standard QDIP, similar dark currents and high normal incident absorption of the 
InGaAs-capped QDIP have demonstrated its potential in the application in 
high-performance and grating-less FPAs operated at high temperatures. The tunable 
detection wavelengths of the device from MWIR to LWIR ranges are also 
advantageous for the development of multi-color QDIPs.

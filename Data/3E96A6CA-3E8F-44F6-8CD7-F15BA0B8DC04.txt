Abstract 
Minimization is the ultimate target of the next generation batteries. Batteries with the 
characteristics of low weight, small volume and high energy density have always been pursuing by 
both industries and academia. The minimization of batteries is best achieved by thin film technologies. 
Thin film electrodes of a few microns and hundreds nanometers can have many practical applications 
from micro-batteries to large area cell devices, due to efficient material usage, and high energy density.  
Currently, the commercialized anode materials are lithium metal, carbon and graphite. Lithium 
metal is highly active, and has serious safety concerns. Lithium metal can also crystallize on carbon 
and graphite surface, causing the same safety issues. Therefore, it is of great interest to search for new 
effective anode materials. 
The goal of this project is to develop a reactive e-beam evaporation/ sputtering technique for 
fabricating nano-composite thin films. This is a two-years project devoted in three parts, which are 
nano-composite of Li/tin oxides, Wood’s metals, and inter-metallic alloys. For the results of 1st year, Sn 
coating avoided damaging due to the electrolyte. The interface between Si and Sn suppressed the 
lithium inert/de-inert and decreased the irreversible reaction and SEI formation. The electrochemical 
properties of Si thin films could be improved. For the results of 2nd year, the Cu6Sn5 thin film anodes 
were prepared by using magnetron co-sputtering technique. Films deposited without substrate bias 
showed nanosized particles on the surface. The bias induced ion bombardment has resulted in structural 
evolution. The 150VDC bias deposited anodes show the lower initial reversible capacity, which however 
was stable during cycling test.  
 
Keywords?Li secondary batteries, anode materials, nano-composite thin film, wood’s metals, 
inter-metallic alloy.

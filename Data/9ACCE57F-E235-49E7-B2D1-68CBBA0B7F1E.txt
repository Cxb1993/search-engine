Ambipolar Pentacene-based Thin-Film Transistors with  
Polymeric Buffer Layers  
Liang-Yun Chiu1, Wei-Yang Chou1and Horng-Long Cheng*1 
1Institute of Electro-Optical Science and engineering, Advanced Optoelectronic Technology Center,  
Nation Cheng Kung Universtity, 
Tainan 701, Taiwan 
Tel.: +886-6-2757575 ext 65252 ext 40, E-mail: l78981076@mail.ncku.edu.tw  
 
Keywords: organic semiconductor, ambipolar transistor, polymeric buffers  
 
Abstract 
 
  We studied the ambipolar characteristics of pentacene-
based thin-film transistors by using several different 
insulating polymers as the buffer layers, including 
polymethylmetacrylate (PMMA), poly(vinylidene fluoride) 
(PVDF), and poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA). By using the 
PMMA, the devices exhibit good ambipolar electrical 
characteristics. In contrast, when PVDF and PVA are used, 
the devices show unipolar hole electrical properties instead 
of ambipolar properties. Finally, the morphologies of the 
corresponding pentacene films on these polymer buffer 
layers were also studied.  
 
Introduction 
For more than two decades, scientific researchers have 
focused on organic thin-film transistors (OTFTs) and their 
commercial applications. Nowadays OTFTs are used for 
traditional display drivers and memory cells or sensors as 
well as inverters for more complicated and logical circuits 
with ambipolar characteristics, thanks to a conspicuous factor 
within the organic semiconductors [1]. In terms of organic 
semiconductor material study, pentacene (PEN), which is a 
common small molecule, exhibits high performance of 
unipolar hole transport that is sufficiently comparable to the 
inorganic amorphous silicon when applied to the transistor 
configuration. In literature, several studies have reported that 
most organic semiconductors are intrinsically capable of hole 
and electron transport, which can be estimated using 
theoretical charge transfer calculations, as in the case of PEN 
[2]. However, N-type characteristics of PEN-based devices 
are rarely found to have an important limit, i.e., the interface 
between PEN and gate dielectrics with full electron trap sites 
[3]. Therefore, to solve the interface problem, this work aims 
to evaluate three polymeric buffers filmed on the traditional 
trap-rich (i.e., silanol group, SiOH-) silicon dioxide. Further, 
atomic force microscope (AFM) images demonstrate the 
surface morphology of thick PEN deposited on the buffers. 
From the electrical curve observation, ambipolar phenomenon 
was demonstrated with the case of PEN only on 
polymethylmetacrylate (PMMA).. 
Experiment setup 
Amorphous insulating polymer, PMMA, and ferroelectric 
poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF), and hydroxyl group–rich 
poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) buffer layers were prepared by spin 
coating. Pen films were evaporated onto the buffer layers. The 
device consisted of p-doped silicon, silicon dioxide (SiO2), 
and silver as bottom gate electrode, main gate dielectric, and 
top contact source-drain electrodes, respectively. All devices 
were post-baked and then sent to N2-filled dry glove box. The 
electrical measurements were carried out using a 
semiconductor parameter analyzer (Keithley4200-SCS). 
Result and discussion 
Figure 1 shows the electrical curve of the ambipolar PEN-
based OTFT with the PMMA buffer layer. Figure 1a shows 
the typical typical output curve. The obvious gate-driven field 
effect can be traced by each step of gate bias on both the P-
type and N-type operations (i.e., P-type: gate bias small than 
0 V; N-type: gate bias greater than 0V). It should be noted 
that the diode curve was also clearly observed, which 
probably originated from the p-n junction constructed by the 
meeting of induced carrier and opposite local charge near the 
drain. Figure 1b shows the saturated transfer curve. Similarly, 
the specific current curve with a “V” shape further 
emphasizes the two kinds of carrier transports in the PEN 
channel from the dynamic sweep scan of gate bias at the fixed 
drain-source voltage of ± 50V.          
Figure 2 shows the summary of two device electrical 
parameter values of PEN-based OTFTs on P-type and N-type 
operation modes, namely, saturated mobility (ut), and 
threshold voltage (Vt). Clearly, only PMMA buffer-based 
device possesses ambipolar characteristics, and the 
corresponding transport efficiencies of the holes or electrons

Polymer Thin-film Transistors with a Solution-processed 
Passivation Layer 
 
Fu-Chiao Wu, Yu-Ta Chen, Yaw-Wen Jang, Horng-Long Cheng* 
 
Institute of Electro-Optical Science and Engineering, Center for Micro/Nano Science and Technology, and 
Advanced Optoelectronic Technology Center, National Cheng Kung University, Tainan 701, Taiwan 
 
ABSTRACT 
Poly(3-hexylthiophene)-based (P3HT) thin-film 
transistors (TFTs) made from different solvents were 
fabricated. After covering the P3HT active layers 
with a solution-processed passivation layer, the 
mobility of P3HT TFTs was enhanced. We suggest 
that some vacancies in the P3HT active layers 
should be filled with solvent molecules to promote 
carrier transport. 
1. INTRODUCTION 
Recently, there have been many studies in the 
field of organic thin-film transistors (OTFTs). 
Compared with inorganic TFTs, OTFTs possess the 
advantages of a simple and low temperature 
process; they are easy to fabricate on large areas 
and flexible substrates; and they are inexpensive to 
fabricate [1]. Hence, OTFTs are very appropriate for 
flexible electronics and displays. When OTFTs are 
applied to circuits or integrated devices, the active 
layers of OTFTs must be shielded from damage 
during processes or degradation by moisture and 
oxygen to improve the stability and lifetime of the 
devices. Therefore, passivation layers with good 
properties, such as being friendly to active layers 
and insensitive to surroundings, are necessary [2,3].  
For simple and low-cost fabrication, 
solution-processed passivation layer materials, such 
as polymer materials, are needed. However, if 
passivation layers are deposited by solution 
processes, whether solvents used to dissolve the 
passivation layer materials damage active layers, 
especially polymer active layers, and then give rise 
to poor performance of OTFTs must be considered. 
Titanium sub-oxide [3], poly(4-vinylphenol) [4,5], and 
poly(vinyl alcohol) [6] have been used as passivation 
layers for polymer TFTs by solution processes. 
Those solution-processed passivation materials 
have good properties that can improve the lifetime of 
polymer TFTs. However, after a polymer active layer 
is covered with a passivation layer even by dry 
processes, the output on-current (Ion) of a polymer 
TFT is often lower than that without a passivation 
layer [3,4,6,7]. Increasing the Ion of a polymer TFT 
after a passivation layer is deposited on top of a 
polymer active layer is a challenge. Accordingly, the 
solution-processed passivation layer is a very 
important issue in the performance of polymer 
TFTs. 
In this study, we used chloroform and 
1,2,4-trichlorobenzene (TCB) as solvents to 
deposit regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) 
as active layers of TFTs. We then covered 
P3HT-based TFTs with poly(vinylidene fluoride) 
(PVDF) dissolved in N,N-dimethylformamide 
(DMF) as a passivation layer. The electrical 
characteristics of P3HT-based TFTs and 
photophysics of P3HT thin films before and after 
being covered with a passivation layer were 
discussed. We propose that the solvent-induced 
vacancies in the P3HT active layers can influence 
carrier transport and the performance of 
P3HT-based TFTs. 
2. EXPERIMENTAL 
The active layer material P3HT (head-to-tail 
regioregularity = 98.5%) was purchased from 
Aldrich. Two kinds of solvents with different boiling 
points (b.p.) containing chloroform (b.p.: 60 oC) 
and TCB (b.p.: 213 oC) were used to dissolve 
P3HT to form 0.34 wt% solutions. The passivation 
layer material PVDF was dissolved in DMF with a 
concentration of 1 wt%. 
The TFT device with bottom-gate and 
bottom-contact architecture was adopted in this 
study, as shown in the inset of Fig. 1(a). The 
dielectric layer was silicon dioxide. Indium tin oxide 
was used to replace metal as the gate, source, and 
drain electrodes because of its durability [8]. The 
channel length and the channel width were 37 m 
and 20 mm, respectively. Before deposition of 
P3HT, the ITO glass substrate was cleaned and 
treated with oxygen plasma. Afterwards, P3HT 
was spin-coated on the substrate at 2000 rpm for 
30 s and then baked at 120 oC for 2 h. Finally, 
PVDF was spin-coated on top of the P3HT at 3000 
rpm for 30 s and then baked at 150 oC for 2 h in 
vacuum. The deposition of P3HT and PVDF was 
performed in a N2-filled glove box. 
The optical properties and molecular 
microstructure of P3HT thin films were studied 
using absorption and Raman spectra, respectively.

4. CONCLUSIONS 
We fabricated P3HT-based TFTs using different 
solvents to deposit P3HT active layers and 
covered P3HT with a PVDF passivation layer. 
Before depositing PVDF on P3HT, the C-film (from 
a lower b.p. solvent) had more vacancies than the 
T-film (from a higher b.p. solvent), causing lower Ion 
and sat of the C-TFT. After the P3HT active layers 
were covered with a PVDF layer, the Ion and sat of 
the T-TFT and the C-TFT all increased. We 
suggest that the DMF molecules may permeate 
into the vacancies of the P3HT thin films to 
promote carrier transport. The C-TFT specifically 
had greater increased magnitude in Ion and sat 
than the T-TFT because the C-film had more 
vacancies to be filled with DMF molecules. The 
solvent-induced vacancies in an active layer could 
hinder carrier transport and result in the poor 
mobility of an OTFT. However, selecting a suitable 
solvent to deposit a passivation layer could induce 
solvent-filled vacancies in an active layer to 
facilitate carrier transport and enhance the mobility 
of an OTFT.

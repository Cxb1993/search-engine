Erkebispegården, The Archbishop's Palace is the oldest secular building in Scandinavia. Work on it
started in the 12th century, and it was the Archbishop's residence until the Reformation in 1537. From
1566 it was the residence of Danish governors. Later, in about 1700, it was taken over by the military
authorities. The oldest part, the north wing, is now used by central and local authorities for official
receptions. The east and south wings were rebuilt in 1995/96 after a fire in 1983, and they now house a
museum and a concert hall.
The new museum was constructed on the site of the fire, after five years of archaeological excavations,
and it is built around the rich discoveries that had been made. In the basement, the archaeological
exhibition takes us through the history of the Archbishops’ Palace. The most unique discovery is the

Rockheim (Rock House) is the national experience centre for pop and rock. Rockheim shall collect,
preserve and safeguard the Norwegian popular music from the 1950s and up to the present day.
Rockheim is situated on Brattørkaia in Trondheim, and opened its doors 5 August 2010.
Rockheim is one of the nation's largest managers of Norwegian popular heritage. Behind the scenes
museum takes place both archiving, research and management of Norwegian music history materials.
Rockheims permanent exhibition is designed as a journey through contemporary Norwegian music and
culture, spread on 1320 square meters. Here you move from decade to decade, from 1950's nascent
youth culture of today's digital media diversity. The exhibition raised bands, artists and industry
forward in light of the various decades’ cultural history.

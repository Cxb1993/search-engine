a. 找出病灶的邊界(藍線)，邊界可由人工手繪或電腦自動偵測。b. 電腦自動標
記病灶區域為黑色部分。c. 將病灶區與附近健康皮膚的畫素同時作主成分分析
計算顏色變異數。e. 使用病灶旁的健康皮膚作主成分分析。 f. 僅使用病灶區作

左上: 數位相片

新穎性與實用性的總結: 
 
1. 主成分分析可使用在一般數位相機的皮膚病灶相片分析，但是也可適用在其
他的皮膚影像(像是皮膚鏡影像)，做顏色變異數的分析 
2. 第一、第二主成分分析可以消除毛髮、角質反光對皮膚病灶區的顏色變異數
計算的不良影響(圖三) 
3. 第三主成分的顏色變異數可有效評估皮膚病灶的惡性傾向 
4. 主成分分析可在 RGB顏色空間使用，也適用於其他顏色空間像是 CIELab 
5. 加入適量的病灶周圍的正常皮膚做主成分分析可增進皮膚病灶的惡性傾向評
估的正確率 
6. 此分析方法適用於其他非黑色素細胞的皮膚腫瘤像是基底細胞癌(basal cell 
carcinoma)、鱗狀上皮細胞癌(squamous cell carcinoma)、Kaposi肉瘤。(圖四)

CAPILLARY DETECTION FOR CLINICAL IMAGES OF BASAL CELL CARCINOMA
Adam Huang1,2,3, Wen-Yu Chang4,5, Hsin-Yi Liu1,2, and Gwo-Shing Chen6
1Research Center for Adaptive Data Analysis/2Center for Dynamical Biomarkers and Translational 
Medicine/3Graduate Institute of Biomedical Engineering, National Central University, Jhongli, Taiwan
4E-Da Hospital, I-Shou University, Kaohsiung, Taiwan
5Graduate Institute of Medicine, Kaohsiung Medical University, Kaohsiung, Taiwan
6Department of Dermatology, Kaohsiung Medical University Hospital, Kaohsiung, Taiwan
ABSTRACT
Dilated capillaries are an important characteristic of basal 
cell carcinoma (BCC). Detecting capillaries in images can 
improve a computer-aided skin cancer diagnosis system. In 
this study, we investigate the feasibility to extract capillaries
from clinical images of skin lesions recorded by a regular 
digital camera.  First, we used a compact set of 1 curvilinear 
and 2 color parameters to train a support vector machine
(SVM) classifier to identify capillary pixels. Second, the 
identified pixels were grouped by a region-growing 
algorithm to form capillary candidates. Last, the likelihood
to be a true capillary was estimated based on the distance to 
the red color in the “CIE Lab” color space. The method was 
tested on a dataset of 21 BCC images with visible capillaries
and 28 benign pigmented lesions without visible capillaries.
The accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity of the proposed 
method were 89.8% (44/49), 90.5% (19/21), and 89.3%
(25/28) respectively. We found capillaries recorded by a 
regular digital camera can be detected successfully.
Index Terms— Computer-aided detection, basal cell 
carcinoma, vascular pattern detection, color segmentation,
support vector machines
1. INTRODUCTION
Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is a common malignant skin 
cancer with locally invasive properties. BCC characteristic 
features including arborizing vessels, ulceration, large blue-
gray ovoid nests, leaflike areas, multiple blue-gray globules 
and spoke-wheel areas [1].  The presence of certain capillary 
patterns is crucial for clinical diagnosis of BCC.  Arborizing
vessels (Fig. 1), which are characterized by focused vessels 
with different diameters and tree-like branches, have been 
reported with a presence frequency of 60.7% [1] of BCCs 
and have  positive predictive value of 94.1% [2], [3].
Automatic detection of arborizing capillaries can potentially 
improve a computer-aided diagnosis system for skin cancer.
Although the vascular patterns in BCC are visible to the 
naked eye and can be captured by regular digital 
photography, the majority of recent studies in vascular
patterns of BCCs have been conducted by using dermoscopy
[1]-[3]. Dermoscopy is a new technique to improve 
diagnostic accuracy of pigmented skin lesions because it 
allows in vivo evaluation of colors and microstructures not 
visible to the naked eye.  As dermoscopy has become a 
popular modality in diagnosing pigmented skin lesions, 
fewer new studies have been devoted to improve the 
diagnostic performance of a computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) 
system that processes more easily accessible clinical images 
taken by regular digital cameras.
In our hospital, clinical images are constantly taken for 
observation and recording purposes. Therefore, combining 
information extracted from both dermoscopic and clinical
images could possibly improve a CAD system’s accuracy.
Further, a specialized CAD system devoted to analyze 
clinical images has the potential to improve health care 
quality for rural hospitals that lack of dermatological 
specialists and dermoscopic equipments.
In this study, we investigate the feasibility of detecting 
capillaries in skin lesions by using regular digital 
photographic images that are more accessible in most 
hospitals.
2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1. Data Acquisition
The digital pictures of skin lesions used in this study were 
selected from a skin lesion database from the Kaohsiung 
Medical University Hospital in Taiwan.  In this database, a 
suspicious region was photographed by a dermatologist with 
a Nikon D70 6.1 megapixel digital single-lens reflex (SLR) 
camera and sigma 18-50mm F2.8 macro lens for recording 
purposes before biopsy.  Biopsy was performed and 
histopathology result was available for each lesion.
From the database, a training dataset of 25 BCC images 
with visible, various capillary and pigment patterns were 
selected to train the proposed capillary pixel identification
algorithm.  A separate dataset of 21 BCC images with 
visible capillaries and 28 (15 nevi and 13 seborrheic

trained classifier to build the precomputed SVM score table,
a 256x256x256 matrix.
2.3. Region Growing
Once the curvilinear responses and color a, b components in 
an image were computed, the SVM score for each pixel was 
obtained from the precomputed table.  (The SVM scores for 
Fig 1 is shown in Fig 4)  The top 2% of pixels with highest
SVM scores were selected to form capillary clusters.  
Clusters with a population lower than 100 pixels were 
removed.  The operating parameters of 2% and 100 pixels 
were chosen empirically.  Last, the survived clusters were 
dilated by one pixel to form the final capillary candidates
(shown as green areas in Fig 5).
2.4. Likelihood Estimation
In this step, the likelihood of a candidate to be a true 
capillary was estimated based on the mean distance to the 
red color for all the color pixels within the capillary 
candidate.  (The red color is (255, 0, 0) in the RGB color 
space, and (138, 209, 198) in the CIELab color space.)
Finally, the likelihood of an image with visible capillaries 
was estimated by averaging the mean distances to the red 
color from the top three capillary candidates within the 
image.
Three distance computations were tested in this study: 1)
Cartesian distance in the 3D CIELab space, 2) Cartesian 
distance in the 2D space of color components ‘a’ and ‘b’,
and 3) difference in color component ‘a’ only. We used the 
“area under the ROC curve” (short noted as AUC) to 
compare the performance of three different distance 
computations.
3. RESULTS
The test results for three distance computations were 
illustrated in Fig 6.  The better ROC curve (blue curve in Fig 
6), with a higher “true positive rate” and a lower “false 
positive rate”, is located on the upper left side.  The AUC 
values of 3D Lab space (red curve), 2D a-b space of (green 
curve), and 1D along the ‘a’ direction were 0.863, 0.877, 
and 0.923 respectively.  The distance computed on the color 
axis ‘a’ in the Lab space performed significantly better than
the other two approaches.  The accuracy, sensitivity, and
specificity of the blue curve at the upper left corner were 
89.8% (44/49), 90.5% (19/21), and 89.3% (25/28) 
respectively.
4. CONCLUSIONS
We found capillaries in clinical images recorded by a regular 
digital camera can be accurately detected by a SVM 
classifier using a compact set of 3 parameters (2 Lab color 
components and one matched filtering response). This 
compact set allows us to build a precomputed SVM scoring 
table to shorten computation time for processing a high 
resolution digital image.

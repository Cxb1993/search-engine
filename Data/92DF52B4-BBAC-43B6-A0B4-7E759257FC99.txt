本研究著重在按鍵設計上，我們依照現有按鍵排列，創造新型按鍵的群組概念，評估
使用者的打字績效和舒適度，期望藉此研究減少文字按鍵所佔面積。由研究結果得知，數

傳統按鍵的疲勞度差不多。如果要應用四種按鍵設計於小型文數字輸入產品上，以直式的
整體績效較佳(反應時間最快、疲勞度最低、整體滿意度最高)。
關鍵詞：按鍵設計、文數字輸入介面、群組按鍵
Abstract
This research emphasizes on key design, according to current key allocation. We introduce
the concept of new-fashioned group key, evaluate users’typing efficiency and comfort level, and
hope to reduce some space through our effort. Our research indicates the module with separation
of digits and letters gets much shorter in terms of typing reaction time. Characters on keys
arranged in horizontal or in 45° make no difference in the test. The accuracy in typing has
significant higher difference between new-pattern keys and the traditional one. All four new
patterns are similar on fatigue scale. If we apply four kinds of key design into the small character
input product, the overall achievements appears the best among all while typing with B type key.
Keywords: key design, character input interface, group key

早期有關按鍵設計的研究多是探討按鍵的移動距離與反應時間，也有針對字母的排列
進行數學的演算，提出較佳的按鍵配置組合，然而，很少研究是統整按鍵特性、排列法與
手指移動從事使用性評估。因此，本研究為了讓符合使用者的對按鍵的依賴以及配合標準
鍵盤打字的方式，透過人因理論的觀點，針對按鍵的特性，在小型行動文數字輸入產品上
提出績效較佳的按鍵與按鍵設計之建議。
二、設計概念與研究目的
本研究為了改善前述的問題而提出四種新式按鍵，藉由縮減按鍵數，減少原先按鍵佔
用的面積，並且符合 QWERTY 既有的配置以因應使用者的習慣性需求，而達到易於學習
的目的，以 QWERTY Keyboard 的一般按鍵績效作為標準(包括:一般按鍵的反應時間、正確
率、主觀滿意度)，來檢視四款新式按鍵之績效達成率，從而提出績效較佳的新式按鍵，以
應用在未來小型行動產品的文數字輸入按鍵設計上。
按鍵形狀有各種的變化，在 PC 電腦操作介面中，按鍵形狀的圖示邊長大多是 10-15mm
且一般都是正方形或矩形。有研究指出方形目標的點選時間皆比圓形目標的點選時間好，
顯示目標形狀對點選時間有顯著差異(Whisenand 和 Emurian, 1999)。因此，本研究選擇以
方形和長方形為按鍵的基本設計雛形。由於字元的排列組合多達數千種，即使以 QWERT
排列來進行設計，亦有數十種組合方式，本研究選擇形狀與字元排列二種變項，產生出四
種無視覺邊角按鍵設計(圖 1)。

3. 疲勞度
長方形，適合手指前後移動的生物特性。而 C、D 則是以手指以原地轉動的方式來移動，
过於微小的移動使得受試者很受窘。(B>A>C>D)
四種按鍵設計的正確率沒有顯著差異，但平均數都接近八成以上，A、B、C、D 型按
鍵(82.81、88.91、90.31、90.08)與標準鍵盤的單一按鍵的正確率(97.03%)有顯著差異。新按
鍵與傳統單一按鍵之間的差距可能是因為新按鍵的練習只有 3 次。在評估心理的舒服度、
生理的疲勞度上四種按鍵設計亦無顯著差異，四種按鍵的疲勞在心理上與績效上是一致
的。四種按鍵的疲勞度與傳統按鍵的疲勞度無顯著差異，但是 B、C、D 按鍵的疲勞度平均
數比傳統按鍵來得低。A 按鍵則可能因為錯誤字數多而感到疲勞。
在主觀滿意度中，B、C 按鍵的速度感有顯著差異(p=0.009)，而 C 按鍵分別與 A、B 按
鍵的學習性滿意度有顯著差異(pA=0.036, pB=0.001)，C 的學習性滿意度皆比 A、B 按鍵評價
低。在訪談過程中，受試者表示 C 按鍵不易記憶。在整體滿意度的評分上四種按鍵有顯著
差異，B 與 A、C 有顯著差異，但是 AC 之間沒有顯著差異，C 與 B、D 有顯著差異，但是
BD 之間沒有顯著差異。依整體滿意度評分的高低為 B＞D＞A＞C
(62.50>58.75>55.31>48.44)。
受測者重視的項目與在整體滿意度的評分上，字元排列法明顯影響受測者的心理評
價，應更進一步的以其它實驗來釐清字元排列方向與字元位置的相對關係。
六、結論
由研究結果得知，長形按鍵(A、B 按鍵)的反應時間比方型按鍵(C、D 按鍵)短，即手指
前後移動的反應時間較手指近距離的轉動來得好。而數字與字母分開的設計反應時間較
快，字元在按鍵上水平排列與呈 45 度角排列沒有差異。四種按鍵的正確率與傳統按鍵的正
確率有顯著差異，需要更多的時間去適應新式按鍵，但在錯誤率皆在二成以下。四種按鍵
的疲勞度與傳統按鍵的疲勞度差不多。如要應用四種按鍵設計於小型文數字輸入產品上，
以 B 的整體績效較佳(反應時間最快、疲勞度最低、整體滿意度最高)。本研究的「無視覺
邊角按鍵」輸入裝置，具有(1)縮短鍵盤輸入十隻手指移動距離、(2)運用手指觸覺與自體感
覺之定位，可降低輸入作業時之視覺需求、(3)大幅減少按鍵數、(4)大幅縮減鍵盤必須面積、
(5)配合現有鍵盤安排，縮減學習時間。等多項特點，未來若能與其他產品進行結合發展，
相信能成為舉足輕重之創新設計。在未來仍會持續產生更包羅萬象的資訊產品，我們可預
見其功能必定在資訊傳遞上更為龐大且複雜，人們沒有辦法避免這資訊潮流的來襲，所以
設計者應該要思考這些重要的人因相關建議，將其合適的套用在未來即將誕生的種種資訊
產品之上，以造福人類。

中文：（100~500 字）
本新穎鍵盤乃配合大眾對 QWERTY 鍵盤的認知，將其每行四個
鍵置於一個按鍵之四個角，可節省鍵盤 3/4 的體積，縮減按鍵輸入
之手指移動距離，及移動時間，並可讓使用者使用觸覺接觸到邊角
即可確認位置。 本創作意含將手機 10 個數字鍵及二個符號，置於
三個按鍵之四個角落。此鍵盤優點為：
（1）提高輸入效率及減少誤按之發生。
（2）減小鍵盤之體積，增加其方便性。
（3）提高學習效率，不增加使用者學習上之負擔。

(3) enhancing efficiency and shortening users’learning curve on the

The direct observation aimed to identify the preferred operational hand gesture based 
on the observation of the hand gestures during the experiment. These were recorded

The hand and the forearm were parallel to, and fully supported by, the armrest of the 
chair when using the engineering mock-up. Thus, various gesture patterns were likely to 
be observed during the pre-/post-studies, and the accumulation of a number of gesture 
photos was likely to identify the most preferred gesture pattern. For the subjective 
assessment, a five-point scale questionnaire with an open-ended comment section was 
used to collect the end-user’s opinion and satisfaction with the design and the user 
experience (i.e. the higher the score, the greater the satisfaction), and the fatigue level in 
the body regions (i.e. the higher the score, the greater the fatigue) (Chen et al, 2007;

terms of positive and negative comments associated with product features (Chen et al,

1. To total the number of positive comments, Pc, and the number of the negative 
comments, Nc, for each of the product features; 
2. To calculate the critical margin Cm = Pc – Nc for each design feature; 
3. To propose design solutions by dealing with critical design features having high 
values of Cm.

Experimental procedure  
The experimental procedure in both pre- and post-studies involved two learning blocks for 
warm-up, three learning blocks, with a break time of one min between blocks.  
At beginning, participants were instructed before the experiment. During the 
experiment, participants made simple point-and-click between a permanent blue square 
target and a red square target for varying target conditions generated by FLG within each 
block. All combinations of 32 graphic task conditions were represented in random order 
within each block. Furthermore, the objective variables were collected via FLG within each 
learning block. In regard to the direct observation, the operational gesture of the arm and 
hand were captured by a digital camera located on the right hand side of each participant: if 
participants changed the gesture at each learning block, a photo was taken and the 
observation note was taken using pen and paper. Once the experiment was completed, 
participants were asked to fill out the subjective questionnaire and were encouraged to write 
down open-ended comments about the design failures of the engineering mock-up and the 
experiment design. In addition, the experimental procedure was under the control of FLG, 
thus the process bias could be reduced. 
 
 
Design case study 
 
Based on the ‘STEPC’, an engineering mock-up (version I) was designed. The pre-study 
was conducted with the engineering mock-up (version I) with one hundred participants: 
participants were instructed before the experiment that each trial should be ‘As fast as and 
as accurate as possible’. Furthermore, participants were instructed that the ‘Preferred hand 
gesture could be applied according to their own feeling, except that the forearm must lie on 
the armrest of the chair in order to reduce arm fatigue’. In total, pair n = 32 target conditions 
× 3 learning blocks × 100 subjects = 9,600 objective data were collected with the 
engineering mock-up (version I). In regards to the direct observation, in total, n = 3 learning 
blocks × 93 subjects = 279 photos were taken (i.e. seven cases were excluded owning to 
their poor quality photos). Moreover, the pre-study had the mouse tested with the first 
seventeen participants, and eight learning blocks were introduced in order to validate 
hypothesis (H1) and the consistency by comparing the results with those of the earlier 
study. In total, pair n = 48 target conditions × 8 learning blocks × 17 subjects = 6,528 
objective data were collected with the mouse. Having completed the pre-study, the 
preferred gesture patterns were identified: 72.8% of gesture photos were found to be Type I 
(i.e. θ1 ≈ θ2 ≈ 0o and θ1 ≈ θ2 <= 15 o). Furthermore, the possible design failures were 
identified by following the above systematic evaluation process, and an engineering mock-
up (version II) was designed based on a proposed design solution. After that, the post-study 
was conducted with the second engineering mock-up (version II) one month after the pre-
study (i.e. one month was spent to design the engineering mock-up based on the proposed 
design solution). The post-study was conducted with forty-three participants randomly 
selected from the same population as the pre-study. The experimental conditions were the 
same as the pre-study. Participants were instructed before each trial to be ‘As fast as and as 
accurate as possible’. Furthermore, participants were instructed to ‘Please use a Type I hand 
gesture’. In the post study, totally pair n = 32 target conditions × 3 learning blocks × 43 
subjects = 4,128 objective data.

Discussion 
 
This study aimed to propose and validate a user-centred design case study of a novel 
gesture-based pointing device based on the implementation of the user-centred design 
approach. Based on the results analysis, the subjective feeling about the design was that the 
clearness, ease-of-use and usefulness of version II were more satisfactory than for version I. 
Furthermore, it was obvious that the end-users preferred version II because it offered more 
accuracy and efficiency than version I.  Therefore, the study has achieved one of its aims: 
the usability of version II was enhanced based on the design solution to deal with critical 
design failures of the version I. Furthermore, it had also proved that the UCD approach can 
play a highly significant role in new product development. 
In this study, encouraging participants to write down their opinions was a success 
because end-users did take time to reflect on how to communicate with the experimenter 
(i.e. in this study, the designer). However, the end-users need to be encouraged to fill out 
the open-ended comment section, thus the verbal and nonverbal communication takes place 
before the participant picks up the pen. Under such circumstances, the experimenter must 
take a friendly attitude toward the participants, at the same time not acting ‘too emotionally’ 
since it might affect the participant’s intentions and feelings about the design. Sometimes, 
encouraging the user to draw pictures might also be even helpful. Although the time taken 
for the writing process was more than five mins in average, the analysis of the open-end 
comments was efficient. In addition, each general issue and special issue occurring during 
the experiment was also noted by using pen and paper, which allows the experiment to 
reflect the situation and was really helpful to enforce the description of persona of end-
users. 
By recommending the Type I gesture pattern to the participants in the post-study, the 
discomfort levels in the body regions were rated below the average. However, its limitation 
needs to be pointed out: the design difference between the two versions might have affected 
the effect of the gesture patterns on the fatigue. 
It must be noted that for all the experiments involving rapid pointing movements, the 
time length of the experiment had its impact on the participant’s motor system. The time 
length of the experiment did not exceed 15 mins, as recommended by ISO 9241-9 (2000). 
However, for a novel device of which the participant has no previous experience, the time is 
very easily spread out, and this was the reason for reducing learning blocks to three for the 
study. Even so, the time taken to complete the same task varied individually. 
To sum up, it is likely that the success of the implementation of the UCD approach 
relied on the rich information contained in the end-users’ opinions collected from the open-
ended comments. The role of the objective measurement and the subjective assessment 
were mainly for the purpose of assessing the validity of the results in this paper. Based on 
further analysis of the metadata, there is opportunity to use metadata concept to model 
human performance and cognitive skill acquisition in order to predict user behaviour across 
design change(s), and the visualization of models across design change(s) based on 
ontology might enforce the description of persona of end-users which facilitates creativity 
for designers. 
Furthermore, the following are the ergonomic design guidelines of the GBPD for future 
study: Firstly, based on the context of use, the GBPD was not so intuitive and that required 
end-users to practice which might make them less willing to purchase the product. 
Secondly, although the angle at the extremity of the forearm and of the wrist was designed 
not to exceed five degrees, it was found that the radial and ulnar deviation of the hand might 
need to be further investigated. Thirdly, the effect of the discontinuous cursor movement

on the observation of the hand gestures during the experiment. These were recorded

The hand and the forearm were parallel to, and fully supported by, the armrest of the 
chair when using the engineering mock-up. Thus, various gesture patterns were likely to 
be observed during the pre-/post-studies, and the accumulation of a number of gesture 
photos was likely to identify the most preferred gesture pattern. For the subjective 
assessment, a five-point scale questionnaire with an open-ended comment section was 
used to collect the end-user’s opinion and satisfaction with the design and the user 
experience (i.e. the higher the score, the greater the satisfaction), and the fatigue level in 
the body regions (i.e. the higher the score, the greater the fatigue) (Chen et al, 2007;

1. To total the number of positive comments, Pc, and the number of the negative 
comments, Nc, for each of the product features; 
2. To calculate the critical margin Cm = Pc – Nc for each design feature; 
3. To propose design solutions by dealing with critical design features having high 
values of Cm.

Experimental procedure  
The experimental procedure in both pre- and post-studies involved two learning blocks for 
warm-up, three learning blocks, with a break time of one min between blocks.  
At beginning, participants were instructed before the experiment. During the 
experiment, participants made simple point-and-click between a permanent blue square 
target and a red square target for varying target conditions generated by FLG within each 
block. All combinations of 32 graphic task conditions were represented in random order 
within each block. Furthermore, the objective variables were collected via FLG within each 
learning block. In regard to the direct observation, the operational gesture of the arm and 
hand were captured by a digital camera located on the right hand side of each participant: if 
participants changed the gesture at each learning block, a photo was taken and the 
observation note was taken using pen and paper. Once the experiment was completed, 
participants were asked to fill out the subjective questionnaire and were encouraged to write 
down open-ended comments about the design failures of the engineering mock-up and the 
experiment design. In addition, the experimental procedure was under the control of FLG, 
thus the process bias could be reduced. 
 
 
Design case study 
 
Based on the ‘STEPC’, an engineering mock-up (version I) was designed. The pre-study 
was conducted with the engineering mock-up (version I) with one hundred participants: 
participants were instructed before the experiment that each trial should be ‘As fast as and 
as accurate as possible’. Furthermore, participants were instructed that the ‘Preferred hand 
gesture could be applied according to their own feeling, except that the forearm must lie on 
the armrest of the chair in order to reduce arm fatigue’. In total, pair n = 32 target conditions 
× 3 learning blocks × 100 subjects = 9,600 objective data were collected with the 
engineering mock-up (version I). In regards to the direct observation, in total, n = 3 learning 
blocks × 93 subjects = 279 photos were taken (i.e. seven cases were excluded owning to 
their poor quality photos). Moreover, the pre-study had the mouse tested with the first 
seventeen participants, and eight learning blocks were introduced in order to validate 
hypothesis (H1) and the consistency by comparing the results with those of the earlier 
study. In total, pair n = 48 target conditions × 8 learning blocks × 17 subjects = 6,528 
objective data were collected with the mouse. Having completed the pre-study, the 
preferred gesture patterns were identified: 72.8% of gesture photos were found to be Type I 
(i.e. θ1 ≈ θ2 ≈ 0o and θ1 ≈ θ2 <= 15 o). Furthermore, the possible design failures were 
identified by following the above systematic evaluation process, and an engineering mock-
up (version II) was designed based on a proposed design solution. After that, the post-study 
was conducted with the second engineering mock-up (version II) one month after the pre-
study (i.e. one month was spent to design the engineering mock-up based on the proposed 
design solution). The post-study was conducted with forty-three participants randomly 
selected from the same population as the pre-study. The experimental conditions were the 
same as the pre-study. Participants were instructed before each trial to be ‘As fast as and as 
accurate as possible’. Furthermore, participants were instructed to ‘Please use a Type I hand 
gesture’. In the post study, totally pair n = 32 target conditions × 3 learning blocks × 43 
subjects = 4,128 objective data.

Discussion 
 
This study aimed to propose and validate a user-centred design case study of a novel 
gesture-based pointing device based on the implementation of the user-centred design 
approach. Based on the results analysis, the subjective feeling about the design was that the 
clearness, ease-of-use and usefulness of version II were more satisfactory than for version I. 
Furthermore, it was obvious that the end-users preferred version II because it offered more 
accuracy and efficiency than version I.  Therefore, the study has achieved one of its aims: 
the usability of version II was enhanced based on the design solution to deal with critical 
design failures of the version I. Furthermore, it had also proved that the UCD approach can 
play a highly significant role in new product development. 
In this study, encouraging participants to write down their opinions was a success 
because end-users did take time to reflect on how to communicate with the experimenter 
(i.e. in this study, the designer). However, the end-users need to be encouraged to fill out 
the open-ended comment section, thus the verbal and nonverbal communication takes place 
before the participant picks up the pen. Under such circumstances, the experimenter must 
take a friendly attitude toward the participants, at the same time not acting ‘too emotionally’ 
since it might affect the participant’s intentions and feelings about the design. Sometimes, 
encouraging the user to draw pictures might also be even helpful. Although the time taken 
for the writing process was more than five mins in average, the analysis of the open-end 
comments was efficient. In addition, each general issue and special issue occurring during 
the experiment was also noted by using pen and paper, which allows the experiment to 
reflect the situation and was really helpful to enforce the description of persona of end-
users. 
By recommending the Type I gesture pattern to the participants in the post-study, the 
discomfort levels in the body regions were rated below the average. However, its limitation 
needs to be pointed out: the design difference between the two versions might have affected 
the effect of the gesture patterns on the fatigue. 
It must be noted that for all the experiments involving rapid pointing movements, the 
time length of the experiment had its impact on the participant’s motor system. The time 
length of the experiment did not exceed 15 mins, as recommended by ISO 9241-9 (2000). 
However, for a novel device of which the participant has no previous experience, the time is 
very easily spread out, and this was the reason for reducing learning blocks to three for the 
study. Even so, the time taken to complete the same task varied individually. 
To sum up, it is likely that the success of the implementation of the UCD approach 
relied on the rich information contained in the end-users’ opinions collected from the open-
ended comments. The role of the objective measurement and the subjective assessment 
were mainly for the purpose of assessing the validity of the results in this paper. Based on 
further analysis of the metadata, there is opportunity to use metadata concept to model 
human performance and cognitive skill acquisition in order to predict user behaviour across 
design change(s), and the visualization of models across design change(s) based on 
ontology might enforce the description of persona of end-users which facilitates creativity 
for designers.

study: Firstly, based on the context of use, the GBPD was not so intuitive and that required 
end-users to practice which might make them less willing to purchase the product.

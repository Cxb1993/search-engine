This  project  proposes  a  new  approach  to  evaluating  design  creativity  by  metaphor.  We  argue  design 
creativity  comprising  originality  and  metaphorical  significance  (synthesis  of  object  similarity,  category 
dissimilarity,  and  salience  imbalance).  Participants  assess  products  through  feature  listing,  similarity 
judgment and salience ranking. Raw data  is analyzed by similarity‐based method, and several comparisons 
are conducted to classify product into quadrants. Correlations between factors are submitted for statistical 
analysis. Significant  correlations are also  found between  these  factors, especially  salience  imbalance and 
metaphorical significance. The results show a good metaphorical design is viewed as appropriate degree of 
similarity, high degree of salience imbalance, category dissimilarity and originality.   
 
Keywords: design creativity; metaphorical significance; originality; similarity 
 
1. Introduction 
Design creativity can be measured by originality and significance (Smith, 2005). Originality of a product  is 
generally  dependent  on  similarity  between  the  product  and  other  existing  products  (Casakin,  2008). 
Theoretically the similarity can be measured by comparing all of the existing products (Goel & Craw, 2005). 
However,  the number of  the existing products could be very  large, so  that  it  is difficult  to ascertain  the 
similarity.  For  human  evaluators,  the  comparison  obviously  varies with  the  size  of  the  set  of  existing 
products  in  their  long‐term memory, and  thus originality  is  somewhat of  relative  judgment.  In contrast, 
significance  is a  rather subjective  judgment concerning about practical, commercial values, or  figurative, 
symbolic meaning perceived by individuals (Dorst & Cross, 2001). In this sense, judgment of significance is 
as relative as that of originality. Since metaphor is often used in design for transmitting meaning, like visual 
design or computer network (Blackwell, 2006; Holyoak & Thagard, 1995; Müller, 2001; Refaie, 2003), this 
study  focuses  on  aspects  of  metaphorical  significance  and  originality  in  design  creativity.  Similar  to 
originality, metaphorical  significance  of  a  product  is  likely  to  be measured  based  on  similarity. Many 
theories have been developed for explaining metaphorical design (Gordon, 1961; Lakoff and Johnson, 1980; 
Fauconnier  and  Turner,  2003),  yet  the  relation  between  originality  and metaphorical  significance  is  still 
unclear. In this study, we review literature on similarity‐based metaphor theories, and then propose a new 
method for measuring design creativity by metaphor. 
 
2. Literature review 
Metaphor  is not only a style  in speech and writing, but also a resourceful method of human’s thinking  in 
daily life (Lakoff and Johnson, 1999). A general definition of metaphor is a figure of speech by which a word 
or phrase is transferred in application from one object to another (Joy, 2009). That means metaphor allows 
us to understand or create an unfamiliar thing (target)  in terms of one familiar thing (source). The target 
provides dimensions  for  attribution, while  the  source provides properties  to be  attributed  to  the  target 
(Cupchik, 2003). Aristotle noticed a value of metaphor is to express novel ideas (Halmiton, 2000), but more 
important,  the  function of metaphor  is  to create similarity  rather  than merely express existing similarity 
(Black, 1979). It has been thought the kernel ability of creativity that helps us to creatively put two things 
from two different domains together into a new one (Ricoeur, 1981; Seitz, 1997).

However,  taking distinctive attributes  into account  in  the above  two equations are not quite applicable. 
First, to enumerate the most attributes of an object is always very tedious, if not impossible. An alternative 
way is to limit the number of attributes to a certain number. For example, the number is set to seven due to 
some limits on our capacity for processing information. Second, the effect of distinctive attributes is not as 
significant  as  expected,  because  the  purpose  is  to  compare  the  similarity  of  two  objects,  common 
attributes are more obviously. Accordingly,  the weights  α and  β  in  the equation  (2) are usually  set  to 0 
(Wang and Liao, 2009; Wang and Chou, 2010). 
 
Opposed  to  Tversky’s  attribute‐based  approach  to  humans’  perception  of  similarity,  Gentner  (1983) 
stressed on the role of structure mapping. By definition of structure mapping theory, originated by Gentner 
(1989), structure refers to structural relation between attributes of objects. Take a lamp consisting of a red 
lampshade and a vase‐shape lamp base for example. Attributes of the lamp can be described in a form of 
predicates in first‐order logic, such as red(lampshade) and vase‐like(lamp_base) that illustrate the attributes 
of color and shape, respectively. Likewise, relations can be binary predicates, such as on_top(lampshade, 
lamp_base)  that depicts  the  attribute of  configuration.  For  convenience  reason, we define  attributes  as 
attribute and relation in this paper. 
 
Structure  mapping  theory  indicates  that  similarity  comparison  always  starts  with  attribute  mapping, 
followed by  structure mapping. Moreover,  abstract  concepts  are  frequently  represented by  similarity of 
structural relation rather than surface attributes. For example, to create a metaphorical product, a designer 
may  retrieve  sources of metaphor  from  short‐term memory by  comparing  the attributes  such as overall 
shape, color, and texture between the target object and another potential source object. Later on, he or 
she may  compare  relations  such  as  product  architecture  (i.e.,  structural  relation  of  components)  and 
behavior (i.e., operational relation of product use or assembly).

2.5 Summary 
The  degree  of  objects  similarity  is  dependent  on  the  types  of  matching  attributes  (features  about 
appearance  or  surface,  and  relations  about  structure  comprising  features)  of  the  source  and  target. 
Previous studies (Wang & Chou, 2010; Wang & Liao, 2009) suggested a good metaphorical product always 
has the quality that its target and source are considerably similar in relation, its target and source should be 
identified before we compare the similarities (Figure 3). The target object of a metaphorical product that 
belongs to a category  is unnecessarily the typical product of that category.  Instead,  is could be an object 
that has some common attributes of the categorical typical product, and some distinctive attributes of the 
products that belong to other categories. To avoid a similarity measure that highly depends on the human 
evaluator’s expertise on  the existing products and sources of metaphor, notion of  the  typical object of a 
category is useful.   
 
Although the similarity‐based theories reviewed in this paper have limits, it is possible to adapt them into a 
practical measure  for originality and metaphorical  significance of products. Given  that  the metaphorical 
significance of a product, p, of which the target object x and source object y are members of the categories 
X and Y, respectively. As shown in Figure 4, the metaphorical significance of the product, p, which is the first 
factor of design creativity, can be assessed by 
M= αS(x, y)+ βD(X, Y)+ γI(x, y)                            (9) 
where   
S(x, y) is the object similarity, 
D(X, Y) is the category dissimilarity, 
I(x, y) ) is the salience imbalance, and 
α, β and γ are weights, 0< (α+β+γ) ≤1. 
The  second  factor  of  design  creativity,  originality  of  the  product,  p,  can  be  measured  by  the  object 
dissimilarity between  the  categorical  typical product  and  the product, p,  as obtained by  calculating  the 
dissimilarity between the typical product, t, and the product, t, (i.e., D(t, p) shown in Figure 4). By doing so, 
relations between originality  and metaphorical  significance  can be determined,  and design  creativity of 
metaphorical products can be explored as well.

3. Method 
 
The purpose of  this  study  is  to develop  an  approach  to measuring design  creativity by  significance  and 
originality of metaphorical products, and test it based on empirical data.

Surface  feature  facilitates participant  to  recognize  the  similarity of  two objects; however,  relation makes 
more sense of similarity. We observed  that  if source and  target were similar  in both surface  feature and

of CD player is broadcasting music, they are both the same by producing something which is invisible, they 
are both driven  through  the center of  the axle. Although CD player and electronic  fans are dissimilar  in 
surface, participants could perceive the similarity of relation. 
 
However,  product  with  high  object  similarity  might  not  belong  to  metaphor.  In  the  classification  of 
similarity  (see  Figure  4), we  circled  eight  products  as metaphor  based  on  the  argument  proposed  by 
Gentner and Markman(1997). Products belonged to the scope of metaphor were not the highest similarity 
among 15 products. Product with high degree of similarity belonged to literal; on the contrary, product with 
low degree of  similarity belonged  to anomaly.  In other words, over‐high  similarity may  cause  the  lower 
degree of metaphor as well as over‐low similarity may cause unreasonable metaphor. Only  the products 
with proper degree of similarity close to the central area were regarded as metaphorical design. 
 
Metaphor based on the same structural relation of source and target, but different appearance of them. 
Took Hannibal for example, tape dispenser (target) and elephant (source) were not similar in appearance, 
but the relation between base and tape of dispenser were similar to body and  long nose of an elephant. 
These findings supported that a good metaphor is based on the balance of similarity of appearance feature 
and relation. 
 
5.2 Category dissimilarity 
Category  dissimilarity  was  the most  difficult  factor  to measure  and  the  result  was most  divergent.  A 
metaphorical product was not  scored  the highest on  similarity; however,  it was dissimilar between  two 
categories. Comparing  the  scattergrams between object  similarity and category dissimilarity, we claimed 
that Excalibur toilet brush, Bilancia uova, and Hannibal which were not only within the scope of metaphor 
but  also  with  far  distance  between  categories.  These  products  were  regarded  as  good metaphor.  For 
instance, the source of Bilancia uova was egg carton. It belonged to food category and also a production of 
organism, it was hard to associate with household appliance, so two categories were dissimilar. 
 
Category  dissimilarity  was  significant  correlated  only  with  ‘metaphorical  significance’.  Casakin  (2005) 
argued that between‐domain metaphor (two objects belong to two different domains) ensured the design 
more innovative and meaningful. The finding supported this argument. 
 
5.3 Salience imbalance 
Since metaphorical design was integrated two objects into one product, the feature of source was obviously 
salient than feature of target. In quadrant of literal (product with high similarity), the salience of matching 
feature of source and of target might be equal; then the difference (i.e.  imbalance) would be unobvious. 
Little difference of salience might not become good metaphor. However,  if salience  imbalance  is high but 
similarity  is  low, that would not be thought of good metaphor. Only a product with proper similarity and 
high salience imbalance would be regarded as a better metaphor design.

feature  is salience for source but not for target, so the salience  is  imbalance. Moreover, salient feature of

An Approach to Measuring Metaphoricity of Creative Design 
Hung-Hsiang Wang and Jung-Hsuan Chan 
 National Taipei University of Technology, Taiwan  
Abstract. Metaphor is central to design creativity as it 
involves processes of discovering two different objects 
which are similar in some perspectives and combining them 
together into a new and meaningful one. This study argues 
the degree to which a design contains metaphor is a good 
indicator of the creativity of the design. In this paper the 
strength of metaphoricity is a function of feature similarity 
between its target and source entities, as well as the domain 
dissimilarity between the two entities. The situation of 
metaphoricity is the salience imbalance of the similar 
features between of its target and source entities. To test the 
argument, five award winners of various well-known 
creativity-oriented design competitions are accordingly 
presented to twenty-six design students to assess the 
metaphoricity strength and saturation, and the creativity on a 
subjective base. Results reveal the creativity has significantly 
positive relation between the object similarity and 
metaphoricity saturation.  
Keywords: design creativity, metaphor, similarity, industrial 
design  
1 Introduction  
Metaphor is not only a style in speech and writing but 
a resourceful method of human’s thinking in daily life 
(Lakoff and Johnson ,1999). It has been thought the 
kernel ability of creativity that helps us to creatively 
put two things from two different domains together 
into a new one (Seitz, 1997; Ricoeur, 1981). 
Metaphors have long been recognized to play an 
important role in industrial design (Hey and Agogino, 
2007). Metaphor is often used at earlier stages of 
conceptual design to solve problems or interpret 
meaning in a creative way. The conceptual design 
starting with an initial design goal, through ideation, 
evaluation, and finalization can be seen as a process of 
defining a target, searching sources to construct pairs 
of the target and sources as alternatives, and selecting 
a satisfactory one from these alternatives. 
Although there are some metaphor theories based 
on similarity measures, few of them have been applied 
to the area of design creativity. Moreover, empirical 
evidence supporting the positive relation between 
design metaphor and design creativity has rarely been 
reported. Therefore, this study aims to provide 
empirical evidence regarding design metaphor 
measures and its implications to design creativity. 
Quantitative results of questionnaires for assessing 
design metaphor and creativity are presented following 
a short literature review. 
2 Metaphoricity in Design 
2.1 Metaphorical Design 
Metaphors are represented by the form “A is B”, 
where B is called the source of the metaphor, and A is 
the target. Metaphor can be used for understanding of 
an unknown situation A in terms of one familiar thing 
B (Gentner 1983; Gentner and Markman, 1997; 
Novick, 1988; Vosniadou, 1989; Ortony, 1993). 
Interpretation of a metaphor is a process of discovering 
which features of the source may be valid and useful to 
understanding the target. To construct such a 
metaphor, one needs to find out the source B that is 
similar to the target A in some perspectives but 
dissimilar to each other in terms of membership of 
certain categories. The similarity maintains a 
reasonable mapping from the source B to the target A, 
while the dissimilarity promises an unusual mapping. 
The search for sources is thus described as a mapping 
of the target and sources based on their common 
features. As long as the mapping is reasonable but 
unusual to a certain degree, the conceptual design is 
said to be creative in terms of the processes or the 
products. A creative design is identical to a both new 
and meaningful design. 
Take Alessi's Anna G corkscrew, designed by 
Alessandro Mendini for example. The main goal of 
this project is to design a new object that belongs to 
the target domain of wing corkscrew. As it has the 
salient feature of two wing-like levers, it also called an 
angel corkscrew or butterfly corkscrew. Thus, dancers 
are selected as the source domain, and a female

3.2 Domain Dissimilarity 
In addition to the similarity between the target and 
source objects, the dissimilarity between the target and 
source domains also plays an important role in 
metaphorical design. Winner (1985) suggests a good 
metaphor have a sufficiently long distance (i.e., higher 
dissimilarity) between the domains to which the target 
and source objects correspondingly belong. Casakin 
(2005) points out that the degree of difficulty to 
establish a metaphor is mainly determined by how 
remote the source is from the target. Michalko (2001) 
also determines a positive relationship between the 
probability of inspiring new concepts by metaphors 
and the domain dissimilarity. 
This study measures the distance between the two 
classes or categories of which the target and source 
objects are members, respectively, to obtain the 
domain dissimilarity. For the target, Industrial and 
Business Taxonomy, developed by Ministry of 
Economic Affairs of Taiwan, is a practical domain 
classification. For example, the classes can be home 
accessories, 3C-electronics, transportation, fashion, 
and sport and entertainment. In contrast, the source 
domains are much more diverse. They may range from 
nature to artificial, from creature to non-creature, or 
from tangible to intangible classes. 
The domain taxonomy seems to be arranged in a 
hierarchical structure, which is typically organized by 
supertype-subtype relationships. In such an inheritance 
relationship, the subtype by definition has the same 
features as the supertype plus one or more additional 
features. For example, corkscrew is a subtype of wine 
accessory, but not every wine accessory is a 
corkscrew. Hence, a type must satisfy more features to 
be a subtype than to be a supertype. Theoretically the 
domain dissimilarity can be computed not only by the 
inversed similarity, but also by the depth and width of 
the supertype-subtype relationships. 
Sometimes it is hardly to consider such 
relationships because of the difficulty of specifying the 
consistent supertype of the target and source objects.

3.4 Metaphoricity 
As previously described, the salience imbalance, I(x, 
y), is practical for identifying whether or not an object 
is a member of metaphorical design. For a typical 
metaphorical design, its salience imbalance value is 
supposed to be as greater than 0 as possible. Also, the 
metaphoricity strength, T(x, y), of a design can be 
thought as a function of the feature similarity and 
domain dissimilarity between the target and the source. 
This study defines it as 
T(x, y)= (α×S(x, y)+β×D(x, y))/ (α+β) (6) 
where α, β are the weights for the feature similarity 
and domain dissimilarity, respectively (α+β≠0) 
By the definition, the design example shown in

study calls this characteristic “saturation”. The 
metaphoricity is extremely saturated, because the

5 Conclusion 
This research has proposed a feature-based approach 
to measuring metaphoricity of designs, including 
measures of the object similarity, domain dissimilarity, 
and salience imbalance. The strength of metaphoricity 
is defined as a function of feature similarity between 
its target and source entities, as well as the domain 
dissimilarity between the two entities. The saturation 
of metaphoricity is the salience imbalance of the 
similar features between of its target and source 
entities. To test the argument, five award winners of 
various well-known creativity-oriented design 
competitions are accordingly presented to twenty-six 
design students to assess the metaphoricity strength 
and saturation, and the creativity on a subjective base. 
Results reveal the creativity has significantly 
positive relation between the object similarity and 
metaphoricity saturation. In this sense, creative 
designers are those who learn how to maximize the 
similarity between the target and source objects, the 
dissimilarity between the target and source domains, 
and the salience imbalance, in order to create both new 
and meaningful solutions. Nonetheless, relation 
between the creativity and the domain dissimilarity 
might not be a perfect linear correlation, which is 
much more uncertain than predicted. The strength of 
metaphoricity remains to be determined in further 
studies. The limitation of this method is that features 
of target and source may hard to indicate by general 
participants with non-design background, and 
participants with different culture would evaluate 
metaphorical design differently. To sum up, 
metaphoricity measures have potential to develop 
alternative tool for assessing the creativity of designs.

Hung-Hsiang Wang and Jung-Hsuan Chan

Abstract. Metaphor is based on the similarity of two things. 
It’s the center of creative thinking. The  purpose of this study 
is to understand humans’ perception about similarity. This 
study uses Tversky's contrast model to discuss the similarity 
of product images with three issues: the importance of 
common features for similarity and dissimilarity; the 
asymmetry of similarity; the degree of similarity of two 
images in different context. Through three experimental 
investigations with chair and product images as material, 
different assumptions were tested respectively. The results 
show: (1) common features are more important while 
choosing similar source; (2) more salient images are 
regarded as a referent and comparison of similarity is 
asymmetry; (3) salience of features might be changed in 
different context. The results can be applied to metaphorical 
products and creative thinking to perceive common and 
salient fetures between different domains, and make a 
innovative connection. 
Keywords: similarity, contrast model, product design, 
metaphor 
1 Introduction 
Metaphor helps the designer seek two different things 
to be integrated into a novel one during the earlier 
stages of design process(Casakin, 2007). Most of these 
scholars believe that similarity has significant meaning 
while comparing two objects, but they didn’t discuss 
further by similarity. That makes us neither indistinct 
about concept of pictorial similarity, nor understand 
the cognitive situation while humans comparing two 
objects. Not only similarity is important for construct a 
metaphorical image but also the degree of similarity is 
a main factor for humans’ perception. Thus, we 
thought it is necessary to explore humans’ 
interpretation of product image by similarity. 
Similarity is the basis of metaphor, and it also 
constrains the selection of particular linguistic 
expression to talk about something else (Kovecses, 
2002). Similarity plays an important role in human 
perception ( Melara, 1992; Tversky & Gati, 1978) and 
information organization and retrieval. 
Previous models of similarity have its own 
weakness (Attneave, 1950; Shepard, 1964; Rorissa, 
2007), like geometric/spatial, and assume humans pay 
equal attention to the various dimensions while 
judging similarity. Similarity can be measured until 
Tversky(1977) proposed contrast model. 
Contrast model defines stimuli as sets of features 
and the similarity of any two stimuli as a linear 
function of a measure of their common and distinctive 
features. This influential model has provided a basis 
for inquiries into the nature psychological similarity 
and has been incorporated into models of other 
cognitive processes (Markman & Gentner, 1993). 
Many researches proved that contrast model is indeed 
helpful for comprehending similarity (Chan, 2000; 
Gati & Tversky, 1984; Johnson, 1986; Rorissa, 2007; 
Tversky & Gati, 1982). 
The main purpose of this study is to understand 
humans’ perception about the similarity of product 
images. According to Tversky’s contrast model, we 
propose three issues that are: the relationship of 
judgments of similarity and difference; the direction of 
asymmetry of pictorial similarity; and the different 
context effects. We used three investigations to test 
whether the three issues can be explained similarity of 
product images, and used different images of chair and 
product as material to test different hypotheses.

2.3 Context Effects 
The scale ƒ is generally various with respect to 
changes in context or frame of reference. Namely, the 
salience of features may vary widely depending on 
implicit or explicit instructions and on the object set 
under consideration. 
Tversky & Gati (1978) proposed the salience of 
features is determined in part of by their diagnosticity, 
i.e., classificatory significance. A feature may acquire 
diagnostic value in a particular context if it severs as a 
basis for classification in that particular context, then 
the value may become more salient. 
When the context is extended by enlarging the 
object set, some features that had been shared by all 
objects in the original context may not be shared by all 
objects in the broader context. These features then 
acquire diagnostic value and increase the similarity of 
the objects that share them. Consequently, the 
similarity of a pair of objects in the original context is 
usually smaller than their similarity in the extended 
context. In other words, when enlarging the types of 
compared objects, the degree of similarity of a pair 
will become higher than in the context which is only 
one type. This study would like to test whether the 
degree of similarity of product images changes in 
different contexts. 
3 Similarity of Product Images 
3.1 Similarity versus Difference 
The purpose of the experiment is to understand 
whether humans’ judgments of similarity and 
difference of images affected more by common 
features and its relative weight.Based on the purpose, 
four hypotheses are formulated:  
1. People attend more to the common features in 
judgments of similar images, i.e., images with 
more common features are considered more 
similar. 
2. People attend more to the distinctive features 
in judgments of difference of images, i.e., 
images with more distinctive features are 
considered more different. 
3. The relationship of similarity and difference 
are not complementary, i.e., θ>λ. 
4. Familiarity with images affects the choice of 
similarity or difference. 
To investigate the relationship of similarity and 
difference, the material of this experiment is 20 sets of 
4 images. Each set included two pairs of image of 
chair, one pair is classical or famous chair image, 
another pair is general or infamous chair image. The 
images of classical chair were obtained from textbook 
of design history and internet photos, while images of 
general chair from catalogs, magazines or internet. 
The image choice follows these principles: similar 
appearance in the same pair, a designer’s different 
products in different period, and the same series of 
products. A set is also composed by the same principle: 
similar appearance or property (armchair or stool) 
between famous and general pairs. These images were 
represented by gray to reduce the interference. The 
final material was completed after two preliminary 
tests to delete or amend the inappropriate image. For 
example, if the probability of a pair been selected as 
more similar or dissimilar is 100%, while the other 
pair is 0%, that indicated that the two pairs of images 
were not suitable for comparison in the same set, then 
the image should be removed or replace. 
All subjects were presented with the same 20 sets 
in the same order. The famous pair(F) and general 
pair(G) within each set were arranged on the left side 
and right side randomly. Two groups of 18 subjects 
participated in the experiment. First, all subjects 
needed to select one pair of each set that is more 
famous. Then, one group of subjects- the similarity 
group- was asked to select one pair of chair images 
that are more similar in each set. Another group of 
subjects- the difference group- was asked to select one 
pair of chair images that are more different in each set.

3.3 Context effects 
The salience of features varies with different objects 
under consideration or classifications which object 
belongs to while comparing two similar images, and 
affects the degree of similarity between images. The 
hypothesis of this study is: the similarity of a pair of 
images in the original context is usually smaller than 
their similarity in the extended context. 
The comparison of similar images is not only the 
definitely referent, but the degree of similarity is 
obviously different. The main reason is the reputation 
and influence of classical chair is more than generals, 
participants may have a thought that general chair is 
imitated from the classical. That’s why the degree of 
“famous chair is similar to general chair” is relative 
low. The result proved that direction of similarity of 
images is asymmetry.

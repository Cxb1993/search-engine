The result of the project is a thermodynamic description of the Ag 
– Cu – In – Sn system. The obtained Gibbs energy functions 
allow for describing thermodynamic properties of the phases, phase 
equilibria, solidification path and, together with mobility data, 
for a prediction of kinetic predictions of the solder joints. The 
obtained results can be used by scientist for the future projects 
as well as by engineers for managing technological processes.

4. Modeling procedure. 
 The thermodynamic parameters for all phases in the system were optimized using 
ThermoCalc software[3].  For this optimization, thermodynamic data for the liquid phase, 
invariant reactions liquidus/solidus and solid phases equilibrium were used. To each piece of the 
selected information was given a certain weight based on personal judgment.  The optimization 
was carried out step by step in agreement with Schmid-Fetzer’s et al [4]. guideline.  First, the 
optimization of the liquid phase was performed, and then the solid phases were assessed.  All 
parameters were finally evaluated together to provide the best description of the system.  The 
calculated interaction parameters are shown in Table 2. For checking of the results of 
optimization the system was also calculated using Pandat [5] software. The flow-chart of the 
used CALPHAD method is shown in Figure 1.

Appendix 2 
Thermodynamic description of the quaternary Ag – Cu – In – Sn system. 
 
Wojciech Gierlotka 
Chemical Engineering and Material Science Department, Yuan-Ze University, Yuan-tung Rd. 135, Chungli, Taiwan 
 
 
Abstract 
Due to new regulations, the classic, Sn37Pb solder must be replaced by a lead-free material. 
There are many alloys that could be used instead of the classic lead solder, and one of them is a 
quaternary Ag – Cu – In – Sn alloy. The Calphad method was used for the thermodynamic 
description of this quaternary system. Good agreement between calculation and available 
experimental information was found. Solidification of the promising lead-free solder Sn-1.5Ag-
0.7Cu-9.5In wt% was performed using Scheil approach and good agreement between this 
calculation and DTA experiment was found. The obtained set of Gibbs energy functions can be 
used in the future for expanding the quaternary system to high-order ones. 
 
Keywords: lead-free solder; Calphad approach; silver; copper; indium; tin. 
 
 
 
1. Introduction. 
Soldering technology is very important for the electronic industry. Recently, due to new 
regulations the classic, Sn37Pb solder must be replaced by lead-free material. There are many 
candidates that be used instead of the classic lead solder and one of them is a quaternary Ag – Cu 
– In – Sn alloy. This alloy was patented in Korea as a new type of solder with the following 
composition: silver (Ag) of about 0.3 wt. % or more, and less than about 2.5 wt. %, copper (Cu) 
of about 0.2 wt. % or more, and less than about 2.0 wt. %, indium (In) of about 0.2 wt. % or 
more, and less than about 1.0 wt. % or less, and a balance of tin (Sn) [1]. There is the possibility 
of adding other components to the quaternary solder to obtain better properties i.e. Ge or Al to 
improve anti-oxidation properties or Bi or Zn to drop the melting point of the solder. Taking into 
Iwase et al. [35] at 973 K by Kameda et al. [36] at 1173 K. Yamaji and Kato [37] at 1423 used 
the Knudsen cell technique combined with mass-spectrometry analysis for the determination of 
Ag activities in the liquid phase. The heat of formation of the FCC_A1(Ag), HCP_A3, Ag3Sn 
phases were determined by the calorimetric method by Kleppa [4], Witting and Gehring [21] and 
by Laurie et al [22]. The heat capacities of the Ag3Sn phase were determined by Wallbrecht et al. 
[38]. The liquidus/solidus and phase equilibria in the binary Ag – Sn system were determined by 
Heycock and Neville [39], by Murphy [40] by Petrenko [41] by Hume – Rothery and Reynolds 
[42] and by Umansky [43]. The binary Cu – In system is more complicated than previously 
described binaries. Besides terminal FCC_A1(Cu) and Tetragonal_A6(In), the system includes 
BCC_A2, CUIN_DELTA, CUIN_GAMMA, CUIN_ETA and CUIN_THETA. The 
thermodynamic properties of the, liquid were described by many researchers. The mixing 
enthalpy of the liquid phase was measured by Kleppa [44] at 723 K, Beja [45] at 999 K, by 
Azakami and Yazawa [46] at 1273, by Itagaki and Yazawa [47] at 1373 K, by Kang et al [48] at 
the temperature range 903 K – 1348 K and by Kang et al [49] at 1373. The activities of 
components were determined by Azakami and Yazawa [46] at 1273 K, Jagannathan and Gosh 
[50] at 1073 K, by Kang et al. [49] at 1373 and by Jacob and Alcock [51] at 900 K. The phase 
equilibria in the binary Cu – In system were investigated by Weibke and Eggers [52], by Hume-
Rothery et al. [53], by Reynolds et al. [54], by Jain et al. [55], by Owen and Morris [56], by

5. Results and discussions. 
Calculated binary systems superimposed with experimental data are shown in Figures 1 a – f. 
Figure 1a exhibits calculated binary Ag – Cu system. The calculation was made based on Hayes 
et al.’s [3] proposition. Figure 1b shows calculated Ag – In binary system superimposed with 
experimental data obtained by Weibke [16], by Campbell [17] and by Moser et al. [15]. As can 
be seen from the figure, calculation reproduces experimental data well. The next Figure 1c 
displays binary Ag – Sn system together with experimental data obtained by Heycock and 
Neville [39], by Murphy [40], and by Petrenko [41]. The picture exhibits very good agreement 
between calculations and experiment. Figure 1d shows calculated phase diagram of the binary 
Cu – In system superimposed with data obtained by Weibke and Eggers [52] and by Jonesand 
[57]. Very good agreement between the calculation and the experiment was obtained; however, 
for a low concentration of indium the calculated liquidus line lies slightly above the experimental 
data. The experimental information obtained by Weibke and Eggers [52] exhibits in this region 
less smooth characteristic than calculation. Figure 1e shows calculated binary Cu – Sn system 
with experimental data obtained by Hamasumi and Takamoto [77], by Bastow and Kirwood [85], 
by Bauer and Vollenbruck [80], by Haase and Pawlek [84] and by Vero [83]. The system Cu –

the polymorphic transformation CUSN_ETACUSN_ETA_PRIME was neglected. From

Ono et al. [71] and by Sengupta et al [73]. The calculation agrees well with experimental data

Moelans et al. [87]. Figure 3a shows the calculated isothermal section of the Ag – Cu – In system. 
The calculation agrees with Bahari et al.’s [110] work. There is a big difference between the 
phase equilibria presented in Bahari et al. [111] and those presented in Woychik and Massalski’s 
[110] work. Woychik and Massalski showed that CUIN_GAMMA and Ag2In phases make a 
continuous solid solution through the whole system; however, Bahari et al [111] presented that 
those phases are even not in equilibrium. Bahari et al [111] proposed equilibrium between Ag2In, 
Cu11In9 and CUIN_ETA/ETA_prime and equilibrium between CUIN_GAMMA, HCP_A3 and 
FCC_A1(Cu). Since these two works show different equilibrium between phases then it should 
be driving force for future work on the Cu – In - Sn ternary system. Figure 3b shows calculated 
liquidus projection of the ternary Ag – Cu – Sn system superimposed with data obtained by 
Woychik and Massalski [110]. As can be seen from this figure, even the phase equilibrium at 
780 K agrees with Bahari et al. [111] work, the liquidus projection agrees with Woychik and 
Massalski’s [110] data. Figures 3c and d show isothermal section at 673 K and liquidus 
projection of the ternary Ag – In – Sn system. The isothermal section is compared with data 
obtained by Liu et al. [116]. From this figure one can see that the calculation reproduced the 
experimental information well. Figure 3d shows the calculated liquidus projection of the ernary

superimposed with the experimental data obtained by Wierzbinska – Miernik [107]. From this 
figure it can be seen that the proposed thermodynamic description reproduces enthalpy of mixing 
well. Similar, Figure 4b shows very good agreement between calculated and measured activity of 
In in liquid Ag – Cu – In at 1173 K for an intersecton x(Ag) – x(Cu) = 0 by Wierzbinska – 
Miernik [107] . Figures 4c and 4d show the calculated enthalpy of mixing and activity of In in 
the ternary Ag – In – Sn system, respectively. The enthalpy of mixing was calculated for 
intersection 4*x(Sn)-x(In) = 0 at 1253 K and superimposed with experimental data obtained by 
Gather et al [86]. Activity of indium was calculated at 1273 K for an intersection x(Ag)-x(Sn) = 
0. From both figures it can be seen that the calculation reproduced experimental data well. Figure 
4e shows the calculated mixing enthalpy of liquid Ag – Cu – Sn at 1173 K for an intersection

The AgGaSe2 as well as AgGaS2 has been showed to be efﬁcient frequency doubling crystals for infrared
radiation such as the 10.6m CO2 laser. The production of these crystals needs good knowledge of the
phase relationship. The thermodynamic description of the binary Ag–Ga system has been chosen as a

1. Introduction
The AgGaSe2 as well as AgGaS2 has been showed to be efﬁ-
cient frequency doubling crystals for infrared radiation such as the
10.6mCO2 laser [1]. Theproductionof these crystals needs a good
knowledge about the phase relationship. For the description of the
phase equilibrium in both cases: Ag–Ga–Se and Ag–Ga–S it is nec-
essary to know the phase diagram of the Ag–Ga binary system. In
this case, the optimization of thermodynamic parameters of the
binary Ag–Ga phase diagram has been choosing as a ﬁrst step of
the thermodynamic evaluation of the Ag–Ga–Se and the Ag–Ga–S
systems.
2. Literature information
The thermodynamic properties of the liquid phase using elec-
tromotive force (EMF) measurement have been investigated by
Danilin andYatsenko [2] at 980K, Predel and Schallner [3] at 1000K
and Jendrzejczyk-Handzlik and Fitzner [4] at 973K. Besides EMF
measurements, the activities of silver and gallium were measured
by Qi et al. [5] at 1200K by Knudsen effusion method. The exper-
imental data sets described activities of the elements are in good
agreement with one another. The enthalpy of mixing of the liquid
phase was investigated by Predel and Stein [6] at 1323K, Itagaki
and Yazawa [7] at 1243K, Jendrzejczyk-Handzlik and Fitzner [4] at

Strong effect of Pd concentration on the soldering reaction
between Ni and Sn–Pd alloys
Cheng En Ho,a) Wojciech Gierlotka, and Sheng Wei Lin
Department of Chemical Engineering & Materials Science, Yuan Ze University, Chungli City,
Taiwan, Republic of China
(Received 5 June 2010; accepted 9 August 2010)
The effect of Pd concentration on the soldering reaction between Ni and Sn–xPd alloys
(x ¼ 0–0.5 wt%) was investigated in this study. When the Pd concentration was low
(x  0.05 wt%), the predominant reaction product was a layer of Ni3Sn4. In contrast,
an additional (Pd,Ni)Sn4 layer deposited over the Ni3Sn4 in the case of above 0.2 wt%.
This microstructure evolution significantly weakened the strength of the interface,
deteriorating the reliability of solder joints. A Pd–Ni–Sn isotherm simulated by the
CALPHAD method was used to rationalize the above transition in the reaction product(s).
Concern of “black pads” in Ni(P) metallization due to
galvanic hypercorrosion resulting from Au plating pro-
cess has disturbed the microelectronic industry for over a
decade. To diminish such reliability concerns, the depo-
sition of a protective layer of Pd between the Ni(P) and
Au was considered and adopted recently.1 In the newly
developed trilayer structure, i.e., Au/Pd/Ni(P), the thick-
nesses of Au and Ni(P) are customarily less than 0.1 mm
and more than 5 mm, respectively, but the intermediate
Pd layer has not yet been specified. The recommended
Pd is deposited within the range of 0.05–0.3 mm primar-
ily based on cost-effective considerations.1 During sol-
dering, the Pd film would quickly react to form Pd–Sn
(or Pd–Ni–Sn) intermetallic(s) at the interface and was
subsequently alloyed with molten solder at the early
stage of soldering,2,3 followed by the exhaustion of the
surface Au. This allowed the underlying Ni to be in
contact with the solder containing different Pd contents,
resulting from various Pd thicknesses. The purpose of
this study is to investigate the reaction product(s) of
different Pd concentrations between Ni and the Sn–Pd
alloys as well as to examine the effect of a minor pertur-
bation in Pd concentration on the reaction.
Experimentally, the soldering reactions between Ni
metallization and Sn–xPd solders at 250 C were exam-
ined. The x for Sn–xPd is 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, and 0.5 wt%,
respectively, corresponding to 0.056, 0.11, 0.22, 0.33,
and 0.56, in at.%. The Sn–xPd solders were prepared
from pure Sn (99.99%) and Pd (99.95%). The Ni was
electrolytic type and was P- or V-free accordingly. After
reaction for 2 min, solder joints were quenched in water
and subsequently were cross-sectioned to reveal the in-
terior microstructures. The compositions of the phases
were determined using a field-emission electron probe
microanalyzer (FE-EPMA) operated at 12 keV. This low
accelerated voltage produced a much smaller interaction
volume between the incident electron beam and the mea-
sured specimen, so that accurate composition measure-
ments for tiny phases could be performed. Additionally,
electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) was further used
to identify the crystal structure of the reaction product.
Figures 1(a)–1(c) show the cross-section of theSn–xPd/Ni
interfaces (x ¼ 0.05, 0.1, and 0.2 wt%) after the 2 min
reaction. The amount of reaction products significantly
increased with the Pd concentration can be noted in these
figures. A more interesting finding is the type of reaction
products was strongly Pd concentration dependent. In the
Sn–0.05Pd/Ni reaction [Fig. 1(a)], only a thin layer of
reaction product grew at the interface. This layer was
carefully identified using FE-EPMA to be Ni3Sn4 with a
rare quantity of Pd (0.2 at.%). The growth of the Ni3Sn4
was not surprising in the case of the Sn-based alloys
joined to Ni,4,5 with the exception that other active ele-
ments, such as Cu4–6 or Zn,7 participated in the reaction.
Interestingly, a slight increase in the Pd concentration to
0.1 wt% made the situation distinctly different. The reac-
tions of Sn–0.1Pd/Ni produced two kinds of products, a thin
continuous layer with scattered chunklike phase domains, at
the interface [Fig. 1(b)]. With the aids of FE-EPMA and
EBSD analysis, the former was identified to be Ni3Sn4
[Figs. 2(a)–2(b)], which was the same as the reaction prod-
uct resulting from the Sn–0.05Pd/Ni reaction. In contrast,
the latter was identified to be the PdSn4-based structure
(space group: Aba2), as evidenced by the EBSD results in
Figs. 2(c)–2(d). The composition of the PdSn4 was then
determined using EPMA to be 5.6 at.% Pd, 15.5 at.% Ni,
and 78.9 at.% Sn on average, suggesting that numbers of Ni
had been incorporated into PdSn4 by replacing many Pd
sublattice sites. Consequently, this compound should be
more properly referred to as (Pd0.26Ni0.74)Sn4.

As depicted in Fig. 3, the phase fields in equilibrium
with the liquid Sn phase (Sn), comprise one three-phase
field, (Sn) þ (Pd,Ni)Sn4 þ Ni3Sn4, and two two-phase
fields, (Sn) þ (Pd,Ni)Sn4 and (Sn) þ Ni3Sn4. When the
Pd concentration in the Sn–xPd alloy approaches zero,
e.g., 0.05 wt%, the Sn–xPd/Ni interface represents a tie-
line in the (Sn) þ Ni3Sn4 two-phase field, indicating that
the growth of Ni3Sn4 phase is stable with the (Sn) phase.
When the Pd concentration is high, e.g., 0.2 wt%, the
Sn–xPd/Ni interface represents a tie-line in the (Sn) þ
(Pd,Ni)Sn4 two-phase field, and the growth of the (Pd,Ni)
Sn4 next to the (Sn) phase becomes stable. When the
Pd concentration is in between (e.g., 0.1 wt%), the (Sn) þ
(Pd,Ni)Sn4 þ Ni3Sn4 three-phase field dominates, and
both (Pd,Ni)Sn4 and Ni3Sn4 coexist with the (Sn)
phase. Also note that the narrow (Sn) phase provides a
good explanation for why a slight change in the Pd con-
centration resulted in a distinctly different reaction prod-
uct(s).
For industrial applications, the Pd concentration in
solder is varied according to the solder joint geometry
and the used Pd thickness. In a 300 mm (diameter) solder
joint with a 250 mm (diameter) pad combination, the Pd
concentration is estimated to be as high as 0.11 wt%.
This estimation is based on the assumptions that a 0.2 mm
thick Pd layer was deposited and the entire Pd could
dissolve into the solder during soldering. This concentra-
tion increases inversely with joint size and becomes a
double (i.e., 0.22 wt%) with adoption of the same film
to the joints with a half size. The shifting in the Pd
concentration alters the reaction product(s) from one sin-
gle Ni3Sn4 layer to a bilayer of (Pd,Ni)Sn4/Ni3Sn4 that
dramatically degraded the mechanical property of the
interface, affecting the reliability of solder joints.
In summary, the soldering reaction between Ni and
Sn–Pd alloys was sensitive to the Pd concentration. At a
low Pd concentration (0.05 wt% or below), only a contin-
uous layer of Ni3Sn4 formed. As the Pd concentration was
slightly increased to 0.1 wt%, a continuous layer of
Ni3Sn4 along with discontinuous (Pd,Ni)Sn4 grains
coexisted at the interface. When the Pd concentration was
further increased to 0.2 wt% or higher, the (Pd,Ni)Sn4
became a continuous layer deposited over Ni3Sn4. The
formation of the (Pd,Ni)Sn4/Ni3Sn4 structure was very
poor for the mechanical property of the interface, deterio-
rating the reliability of solder joints. This suggests that the
Pd concentration/thickness applied to solder joints should
be reduced and tightly controlled to produce joints with
strong and consistent interfacial properties.

3. Experimental Information
Taking into account the associate solution model which
was used in this work for description of the ternary Ag-Pb-
Te system, one should mention that binary Pb-Te and Ag-Te
systems had to be also remodeled. For reoptimization of
these binary systems we used exactly the same experimental
sets as in our previous works[8,9] where an ionic model was
used. The binary Ag-Pb system was also recalculated using
the same literature information as Lee et al.[10] used.
Experimental information about Ag-Pb-Te system is very
limited. Enthalpy of mixing of the liquid phase was measured
by Romermann and Blachnik.[11] They measured the heat of
mixing for ﬁve sections: Te-Ag08Pb02, Te-Ag06Pb04,
Te-Ag05Pb05, Te-Ag04Pb06, Te-Ag02Pb08, at temperatures:
973, 1073 and 1173 K. No information about either activities
or chemical potentials of components in the ternary liquid
alloys was found. The DTA information was provided by
Blachnik and Gather[12] and Blachnik and Bolte.[13] Blachnik
and Gather[12] investigated the cross-section Ag2Te-PbTe.
Besides DTA experiments they also identiﬁed the phases
present in the system using x-ray and metallographic
methods. Blachnik and Bolte[13] examined two isopleths in
Ag-Pb-Te system. The ﬁrst one is Ag2Te-Pb section, and the
second one is the PbTe-Ag section. Similarly, as in previous
paper,[12] DTA, x-ray and metallographic methods were used
for the determination of phase equilibria. Unfortunately, the
isopleths don’t agree with each other. In the cross point of the
two isopleths a difference between liquidus temperatures is
about 100. In our case for optimization we used the
information obtained from PbTe-Ag isopleth.
4. Optimization Procedure
The thermodynamic parameters for all phases in the system
were optimized using ThermoCalc software.[14] For this
optimization, thermodynamic data for the liquid phase,
invariant reactions and phase equilibria were used. To each
piece of the selected information a certain weight was given
which was based on personal judgment. First, Ag-Te, Pb-Te
and Ag-Pb binary systems were optimized, and these optimi-
zations were incorporated into optimization of the ternary
Ag-Pb-Te system. The optimization was carried out step by
step in agreement with Schmid-Fetzer’s et al.[15] guideline.
First, the optimization of the liquid phase was performed, and
then the solid phases were assessed. All parameters were
ﬁnally evaluated together to provide the best description of the
system. The calculated interaction parameters are shown in
Table 2. For checking of the results of optimization the system
was also calculated using Pandat software.[16]

6. Conclusions
The thermodynamic modeling of the ternary Ag-Pb-Te
system was performed based on the literature information
and a set of thermodynamic parameters was obtained which
were then used to reproduce known data. Good agreement
between calculations and experimental results was found.
With the obtained thermodynamic description of the liquid
phase, lead inﬂuence on tellurium pressure over Ag-Pb-Te
alloys was shows that an increase in lead content of an alloy
increases the vapor pressure of tellurium.

The calculated phase diagram superimposed with experimental data obtained by Pellini 
and Vio [14], by Kimata [15], by Lanyon [16] and by Kotkata et al [17] is shown in Figure 1 and 
2. Figure 1 exhibits the phase diagram together with experimental data of liquidus line; Figure 2 
shows the phase diagram with experimental information about solidus. In addition, Figure 2 
presents the phase diagram proposed by Gosh et al [21] (marked by dashed lines). The calculated 
solidus and liquidus lines agree with data obtained by Lanyon [16] and Pellini and Vio [14] but 
lay above the data proposed by Kimata [15] and by Kotkata et al [17]. The shape of liquidus and 
solidus slightly differ from those proposed by Gosh et al [21]. Due to strong undercooling effect 
and big discrepancy of the experimental information about liquidus and solidus it is hard to 
describe the real shape of these lines. Figure 3 shows calculated enthalpy of mixing of the liquid 
Se – Te at 733 K  system together with data obtained by Maekawa et al [5], by Mouloudi et al. [6] 
and by Morgant and Legendre [7]. From this figure one can see that proposed thermodynamic 
description agrees well with experimental data. In the same Figure 3 is shown description of 
enthalpy of melting proposed by Gosh et al [21]. It is clear that higher number of coefficients in 
RK polynomial give in this case better description of the enthalpy of mixing of the liquid phase. 
The activities of Te and Se reveal positive deviation from Raoult’s law what suggests stronger 
interaction between the same type of atoms (Se – Se and Te – Te) than between different type 
(Se – Te) what does not agree with the shape of enthalpy of mixing ( which can suggest a strong 
interaction Se – Te). Calculated chemical potentials of selenium and tellurium at 773 K are 
shown in Figure 4. For comparison of different experimental results all chemical potentials have 
been recalculated to 773 K. From Figure 4 one can see that the calculated chemical potential of 
tellurium agrees with Moloudj et al [8] results more than with data obtained by Nanjo et al [11].

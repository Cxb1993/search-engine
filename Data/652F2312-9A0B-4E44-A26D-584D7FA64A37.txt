Abstract   
 
According to the maximum stress theory and Tsai-Wu criterion, the fiber direction and stacking sequence designs 
for the bicycle frame made of the carbon/epoxy composite laminates have been studied in this paper. Using the finite 
element method, the stress and failure index are obtained for the design estimation. The better design cases are selected 
from seven different loading conditions applied on the bicycle frame. As a result, the stacking sequences [-45/45/0/90]s, 
[-45/45/90/0]s and [45/-45/0/90]s are good stacking designs for the composite bicycle frames. On the contrary, the 
uni-directional laminates, i.e. [0/0/0/0]s, [90/90/90/90]s, [45/45/45/45]s and [-45/-45/-45/-45]s, are bad designs. 
   
Keywords: composite materials, stacking, bicycle frame, finite element

2. PROBLEM DESCRIPTIONS 
 
The configuration of the problem is illustrated in Fig. 1. A metal plate with a central crack is 
subjected to a remote stress σ0 and a DC current i0. This thin plate is made of mild steel with 
dimensions 2W×2L and small thickness e. The crack length is 2a. 
The thermo-electro-structural coupled-field problem in Fig. 1 will be solved by the finite 
element method. The plane stress, two-dimensional thermo-electric conditions, linear elastic and 
isotropic properties are assumed. The phase change effect can be ignored. To simulate more 
practical conditions, the temperature-dependent material properties in Table 1 (Tsai et al. 1991) 
should be adopted in the analysis. The use of the temperature-independent material properties 
may lead to incorrect results. This issue will be discussed in this paper. 
Under thermo-electric Joule heating, the current-induced thermoelastic problem is transient. 
Contact conditions between both crack surfaces are considered in this study. The electric current 
and heat flow can pass through the crack surfaces when the crack contact occurs.

5.2. Hot Spot around Crack Tip 
 
When the electrical energy is applied on the metal plate, the temperature field increases with 
time. Fig. 4 depicts the history of the temperature contour during t =0 ~ 0.08 s. At the crack tip, 
the Joule heating effect causes a high temperature area or a hot spot. It demonstrates the 
existence of the melting crack tip (red color area) when enough electrical energy is provided. 
In Fig. 5, it shows the electric current density vectors near the crack tip at t = 0.08 s. Due to 
the opening condition of the crack, the vectors can not pass through the crack surfaces. It is 
noted that there is a field concentration at the crack tip. Similar to the elastic stress field, the 
electric current density also has the r−1/2 singularity at the crack tip. Under the Joule heating 
effect, this current density concentration induces the hot spot around the crack tip.

5.3. Crack Welding under Compressive Load 
 
In this section, the electric current i0=25 kA and remote compressive stress σ0= −40 MPa are 
applied on the plate. Due to the remote compressive stress, the crack faces will contact to each 
other. Then the electric current density vectors can pass through the crack. Similar to the 
resistance spot welding process, the crack surfaces can melt and weld together. The numerical 
results in Fig. 6 show the temperature field and melted region. 
In Fig. 7, the electric current density vectors can pass through the crack. Along the contact 
region, the electric current and contact resistance induce the hot region (as shown in Fig. 6). The 
hot contact region along the crack will melt and then weld together with enough electric energy. 
The crack faces can bond together after the cooling process. This crack welding process can 
bond the crack surfaces and repair the damaged structures.

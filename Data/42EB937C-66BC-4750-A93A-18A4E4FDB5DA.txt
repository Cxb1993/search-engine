亂數產生器在統計、模擬、博奕，特別在密碼學方面，有很重要的地位；不論是對
稱/非對稱加密、雜湊函數、串流加密、one-time pad 等，均依賴亂數產生器來產生金鑰。
目前所看到的真亂數(True Random Number)產生器，多需精密的儀器或高深的知識才能
產製，例如收集外太空的電磁訊號或 RLC 振盪電路等等。所以本計畫研發運用個人裝
備即能夠產製夠水準、能通過檢測的亂數，以供個人資料保護之用。首先，我們擷取雙
影像，影像分別取自 Webcam(低解析度視訊鏡頭) 、IPcam(高解析度監視鏡頭)及相聲
VCD，均可產製夠水準、能通過檢測的亂數，其中運用 Webcam 或 IPcam 可產生真亂數；
而用相聲 VCD 當訊號源則可產生擬真亂數(Pseudo Random Number)，使用 NIST SP

 Random number is very useful in cryptography, chaos theory, game 
theory, information theory, pattern recognition, probability theory, quantum 
mechanics, statistics, and statistical mechanics.  In this work, we propose 
audio or video or dual-mode hybrid random number generator which includes 
1) Dual mode white noise extraction mechanism, 2) Sampling point filter

2. Related works 
2.1. Random number 
The random number could be generated from two major 
ways – true random number generators (TRNGs) and 
pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs).  TRNGs often 
generate random number sequences from nature phenomena 
such as dice, coin flipping, flip-flop circuit, oscillator, 
electromagnetic wave, sound, image, video… etc.  As a 
result, the random number sequences generated from TRNGs 
cannot be reproduced [1].  On the other hand, PRNGs often 
generate random number sequences from mathematical 
functions such as linear congruential to simulate real 
randomness. 
A linear congruential random number generator [5] 
represents one of the best-known PRNGs and were firstly 
broken by Jim Reeds [6] and then by Joan Boyar [7].  
Researchers develop the feedback shift register since then [8].  
In 2010, He Debiao, Chen Jianhua, and Hu Jin (2010), 
proposed “A Random Number Generator Based on Isogenies 
Operations.”  They used character of elliptic curves to 
generate random sequences which is also PRNG method to 
pass the NIST SP 800-22rev1a 15 statistical tests. 
The random number sequences generated from PRNGs 
are reproducible. On the contrary, TRNGs must be 
unpredictable. No matter generated from PRNGs or TRNGs, 
random number sequences must pass specific statistical tests 
to prove their randomness. 
In 2007, Salih Ergun and Serdar Ozoguz proposed 
“Truly Random Number Generators Based on A 
Non-autonomous Chaotic Oscillator,” [10] then improved 
their method in “Truly Random Number Generators based on 
Non-autonomous Continuous-time Chaos” [11] in 2008.  
Salih Ergun and Serdar Ozoguz combined RLC (resistance, 
inductor capacitor circuit) chaotic oscillator, operational 
amplifiers and Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA); then 
uploaded the binary data from PCI interface.  The statistical 
tests pass rate of Salih Ergun and Serdar Ozoguz’s method 
were from 96% to 99%; however, their method based on both 
electronic circuit and PCI interface knowledge is not suitable 
for common computer users. 
In 2009, Shelan Khasro Tawfeeq proposed “A Random 
Number Generator Based on Single-Photon Avalanche 
Photodiode Dark Counts” [12] which produced nearly 50% 
0’s and 50% 1’s.  Yuki Yamanashi and Nobuyuki Yoshikawa 
proposed “Superconductive Random Number Generator 
Using Thermal Noises in SFQ Circuits” [13] which used the 
superconductive single-ﬂux-quantum (SFQ) circuits and 
thermal noises to produce random number sequences.  
Nevertheless, those two methods also require specific 
equipment and electronic circuit knowledge to simulate 
random sequences. 
Yas Abbas Alsultanny proposed another TRNG called 
“Random-bit Sequence Generation from Image Data” in 2008 
[14].  Alsultanny used simple operation like XOR to 
generate random bits from images, but only 94% of the 
generated random bits could pass at least four statistical tests 
[3] as well as 79% pass all five tests seems not feasible 
enough.  Though Alsultanny’s results are not good enough 
but this method indeed inspire us to generate random bits 
from video sources.  Then, we propose the random number 
sequences generated from white noise of webcam as followed 
[2]. 
2.2. Statistical Test Suite for Random Number Generators 
The qualified random number sequences should satisfy 
unpredictability.  For randomness, Alfred J. Menezes, Paul 
C. van Oorschot, and Scott A. Vanstone proposed five 
statistical tests in Handbook of Applied Cryptography, 1996 
[4]; but this verification method is not enough to approve the 
randomness.  Therefore, NIST published Special Publication 
SP-800-22 (A Statistical Test Suite for Random and 
Pseudorandom Number Generators for Cryptographic 
Applications) in 2001, and revised in August 2008 and April 
2010 [5].  The SP-800-22 rev1a developed 15 statistical 
tests to verify the randomness of binary sequences produced 
by either PRNGs or TRNGs.  A qualified random number 
generator should pass all 15 tests listed in the following: 
1. The Frequency (Monobit) Test 
2. Frequency Test within a Block 
3. The Runs test 
4. Test for the longest-Run-of-Ones in a Block 
5. The Binary Matrix Rank Test 
6. The Discrete Fourier Transform (spectral) Test 
7. The Non-overlapping Template Matching Test 
8. The Overlapping Template Matching Test 
9. Maurer’s “Universal Statistical” Test 
10. Linear Complexity Test 
11. The Serial test (two-bit test) 
12. The Approximate Entropy test 
13. The Cumulative Suns (Cusum) test 
14. The Random Excursions test 
15. The Random Excursions Variant test 
 
Above 15 statistical tests are commonly used for 
determining whether the random number sequences possess 
some speciﬁc characteristics that a true random number 
sequence could possibly exhibit.  In addition, the NIST 
issues the 140 Publication Series to coordinate the 
requirements and standards for cryptographic modules.  The

3Development of electrochemical method to determinate the residues of sulfite in
dried daylily (Hermerocallis fulva L.)
Abstract
Sulfites have been widely used as additive in food to prevent oxidation, bleach, inhibit bacterial
growth, and control enzymatic and non-enzymatic reactions during production and storage.
Nevertheless, excessive sulfites might cause allergic reactions, such as asthma, dyspnea and death.
Taiwan Food and Drug Administration (TFDA) issued a limited standard of food additives which
dried daylily of sulfur dioxide residues should be less than 4g/Kg (4000 ppm). Traditional method of
detection in sample testing is not only complex devices, but also time consuming. In this work, using
a platinum electrode as the amperometric biosensor to determinate the residues of sulfite in dried
daylily. Calculate the concentration of sulfur dioxide by the difference current levels. The optimum
experiment condition were 0.7 V, 40oC and pH 6.0. The optimum soaking solution condition were
40oC and 1 hour. Under the optimum experiment condition, the detection was determined in sulfite
concentration range of 0-105 ppm (r2 = 0.9925) (S/N=3), and response time 20 s. This studied
biosensor showed high sensitivity and quick response time to the residues of sulfites.
Keywords : Daylily(Hermerocallis fulva L.), Sulfite, Sulfur dioxide, Electrochemical detection

2. Determination of AA and UA concentrations by using OP-SPCEs SPCE
As shown in Figure 1B and 1D, the oxidative peaks of AA and UA in a composite solution could be clearly distinguished and the
UA responses were linearly proportionally to the concentrations of UA from 25 to 200 M (Figure 1B). From Figure 1D, it was
also shown that a linear regression equation of the plot of oxidative peak currents vs. AA concentration was determined to be y =
0.0348x + 0.0533 (R2 = 0.9995) in Eqn(3) in Table 1. From Eqn (1) and Eqn (3), it is observed that both the linear regression
equations for the plots of oxidative peak currents vs. AA concentration with and without the presence of UA were virtually identical.
This result also indicates that the peak currents of AA were not interfered with by UA at all and the AA concentration in any
composite solution should be calculated as the first step toward determining the accurate concentrations of AA and UA, as the the
analysis procedure we propose in Table 2. In addition, the degree of cross-talking interference from AA was linearly proportional to
the concentration of AA with a regression equation of y = 0.0226x + 2.421 in Eqn(4). Therefore, the second step of the analysis
(Step 2 in Table 2) was to determine the degree of cross-talking interference caused by AA.
After the degree of interference caused by AA was calculated, the estimated cross-talking-interference-free UA peak current
(i.e. without interference of AA) (Step 3 in Table 2) is determined by subtracting the level of AA interference. The estimated
interference-free UA peak current is used as y value to solve the x value according to Eqn(2) , where x is the correct concentration
of UA.

Another series of composite solutions of 100 M UA and various AA concentrations (ranging from 25 to 300 M) were
prepared. The solutions were analyzed with the procedure shown in Table 2, and recoveries ranging from 96% to 99% were
obtained (data not shown).
The reproducibility of OP-SPCE inter-electrodes for the determination of UA was further investigated by monitoring the
voltametric behavior of UA when measured by three distinct OP-SPCEs. When measured with different OP-SPCEs, the peak
currents for 25, 50, 75, 100, 150, 200 and 300 μM UA exhibited RSDs of 13.8%, 12.3%, 12.0%, 12.0%, 11.8% and 11.7% in the
CVs, respectively. The poor reproducibility of preparing OP-SPCE could be caused by the organic oil and pollutants on the surface
of the SPCE, incompletely cleaned by the oxygen plasma treatment. This indicates that further research are needed in order to
optimize the conditions by which oxygen plasma cleans the surface of SPCEs [2, 4].
CONCLUSION
In a composite solution containing of UA and AA, the cross-talking response of AA may interfered with the UA. When UA (100
μM) was mixed with various concentrationsof AA (ranging from 25 to 300 μM), the peak current of UA, in response to the increase 
of AA concentration in the same solution, was elevated. The degree of cross-talking can be minimized if the SPCE is treated with
oxygen plasma and a fitting procedure of subtracting this cross-talking response was used to correctly analyze the concentration of
UA in a composite solution. Oxygen plasma treatment can not only remove the organic oil and impurities from the surface of
SPCEs, but it may also add oxygenated functionalities to the surface. These cleaning and modification effects of oxygen plasma
caused the oxidation peaks of AA and UA in the cyclic voltammograms to became well-defined, with a peak-to-peak potential of
200 mV, and subsequently reduced the cross talking effects. The OP-SPCE also exhibited consistency when detecting
concentrations of UA and AA, with an RSD of 1.4% and 2.2%, respectively. Overall, we combined an effective surface-cleaning
method, which improved the electrochemical properties of the SPCE, and a fitting procedure, which subtracted the cross-talking
response, to analyze the concentration of UA. The OP-SPCE exhibits a high sensitivity towards UA, suggesting its great potential
for determining UA concentrations in real samples. Compared with other methods, oxygen-plasma treatment is relatively simple
and easy to operate. The fitting procedure proposed provides an efficient and convenient way to solve the issue of cross-talking
responses. In the presence of a high concentration of AA (100μM), recoveries for UA concentrations between 50 and 200 μM range 
from 106% to 97%. This range meets the clinical requirement for the abnormal elevation of UA in serum.

ABSTRACT (Key Words: liquid-liquid 
equilibrium  methanol  organic carbonates  
water  hydrocarbons) 
Experimental liquid-liquid equilibrium (LLE) 
data are presented for two quaternary systems 
water−methanol−(50 mol% n-heptane + 50 
mol% toluene) or (50 mol% cyclohexane + 50 
mol% p-xylene).  The LLE data were also 
reported for six quinary systems water−(75 
mol% methanol + 25 mol% dimethyl carbonate), 
(50 mol% methanol + 50 mol% dimethyl 
carbonate), or (25 mol% methanol + 75 mol% 
dimethyl carbonate)−(50 mol% n-heptane + 50 
mol% toluene) and water−(75 mol% methanol + 
25 mol% diethyl carbonate), (50mol% methanol 
+ 50 mol% diethyl carbonate), or (25 mol% 
methanol + 75mol% diethyl carbonate) 
water−(50 mol% cyclohexane + 50 mol% 
p-xylene).  The experiments were conducted at 
298.15 and 313.15 K (or 318.15 K) under 
atmospheric pressure.  Each system exhibited a 
large partially miscible region and the region of 
heterogeneity was found to decrease with an 
increase of temperature.  These new LLE data 
were correlated with NRTL and UNIQUAC 
models obtaining a very good reproduction of 
the experimental behavior.  The densities, 
viscosities, refractive indexes, and surface 
tensions of each conjugated phase located in the 
isothermal binodal curve have been determined 
for these multicomponent systems.  It is 
observed that these data of each conjugate phase 
are valuable indicators for the degree of 
solubility of the multicomponent systems. 
1. INTRODUCTION 
Oxygenated compounds such as methanol can 
be used in the reformulation of gasoline to 
improve the octane rating and pollution-reducing 
capability.  However, methanol presents partial 
miscibility with aliphatic hydrocarbons and this 
solubility is even lower if water is present since 
alcohol preferentially goes into the aqueous phase.  
Hence, in the presence of methanol, even trace 
amounts of water can cause a separation of two 
phases in gasoline. 
Dimethyl carbonate (DMC) and diethyl 
carbonate (DEC) have a good blending octane, 
they do not phase separate in a water stream like 
some alcohols do, and they are both low in 
toxicity and quickly biodegradable [1].  It is 
therefore of interest to undertake the study the 
liquid-liquid phase equilibrium of representative 
gasoline compounds (mixtures of a naphthenic 
and an aromatic hydrocarbon) in the presence of 
methanol + water with the DMC or DEC as an 
oxygenate additive  for overcoming phase 
separation.

The internal standard method was applied to

Equilibrium compositions of sampled liquid 
were analyzed with a Perkin-Elmer Autosystem 
GC.  Good separation of the five components was 
obtained on a stainless steel (3.5 m long, 2.1 mm 
i.d.) column packed with Porapak QS (80/100 
mesh, SUPELCO, PA/USA).  The constant 
heating method was adopted for the GC analysis.  
The Helium carrier gas flow rate was 
electronically kept constant at 40 mL/min 
working with a split ration of 12:1.  The injector 
and oven were maintained at 473 K and 503 K, 
respectively.  Detection was carried out by a TCD 
at 493 K with a current value of 80 mA.  Three or 
four analyses were performed for each sample in 
order to obtain a mean mass fraction value with 
repeatability better than 0.1%.   
The GC response peaks were integrated by 
using Perkin-Elmer Turbochrom software.  Each 
calibration curve contains 15 experimental points 
and each of these points is the result of at least five 
injections into chromatograph.  To know the 
possible errors involved in using these calibration 
curves, several quinary mixtures of well-known 
concentrations were prepared.  Based on these 
analyses and the standard deviations provided in 
the measurements, we estimated the uncertainty 
of the equilibrium concentration in mole fraction 
of the experimental tie-lines obtained in this work 
to be ±0.0085 for water, ±0.0022 for methanol, 
±0.0002 for DMC, ±0.0003 for DEC, ±0.0031 for 
cyclohexane, ±0.0030 for n-heptane, ±0.0005 for 
toluene, and ±0.0035 for p-xylene. 
Density, viscosity, refractive index, and 
surface tension of each conjugate phase were 
investigated for each equilibrium tie-line.  
Densities ρ were measured with an Anton Paar 
DMA-5000 vibrating-tube densimeter 
(Anton-Paar, Graz, Austria).  Viscosities η were 
determined with an automatic microviscometer 
(Anton Paar type AMVn), which uses the 
rolling-ball principle.  Refractive indices nD were 
measured with an automatic Anton Paar 
RXA-156 refractometer, which runs with the 
wavelength of 589 nm corresponding to the D-ray 
of sodium.  Surface tensions σ were measured 
with an automatic tension meter Model CBVP-A3 
(Kyowa, Japan), which works by the 
Wilhelmy-plate method. 
All samples were prepared by mass using a 
Mettler Toledo XS 205DU scale with a precision 
of 0.01 mg.  The uncertainty in the composition is 
estimated to within ±1×10-4 mole fraction. All 
liquids were thermostatically controlled to within 
±0.01 K, ±0.05 K, ±0.03 K, and ±0.05 K for ρ, η, 
nD, and σ measurements, respectively.  All 
measurements were performed at least four times 
under atmospheric pressure (100.8±0.4) kPa, and 
the results were averaged to give the final values.  
The uncertainties of ρ, η, nD, and σ were 
estimated to be ±3×10-5 g⋅cm-3, ±0.004 mPa⋅s, 
±0.00005, and ±0.2 mN⋅m-1, respectively.

4. CONCLUSION AND SUGGESTION 
The effect of oxygenate addition on 
hydrocarbon + water mutual solubility of 
representative multicomponent systems that are 
of interest for the reformulation of oxygenated 
gasoline has been investigated through the study 
of the liquid-liquid equilibrium.  It is observed 
that the systems exhibit a large partially miscible 
region.  The system containing DMC possesses a 
partially miscible region which is evidently 
smaller than that of the corresponding water + 
methanol + hydrocarbon systems.  The same 
behavior can be observed for the system 
containing DEC with the mole fraction of DEC is 
less than 0.50 when mixing with methanol. 
We found that the experimental liquid–liquid 
equilibrium data for the multicomponent systems 
studied can be accurately reproduced with either 
NRTL or UNIQUAC models.  The results and 
analysis presented in this work are of scientific 
interest and should also be of interest for gasoline 
reformulation using methanol as an additive under 
extreme conditions that involve the undesirable, 
although sometimes unavoidable, presence of 
water. 
The density, viscosity, refractive index, and 
surface tension of the conjugate phases are 
properties intimately related to the solubility 
behavior of the studied multicomponents systems 
since their values also depend on the 
concentration of the oxygenate additives.  Hence, 
these properties of each conjugate phase for each 
equilibrium tie-line were measured.  We observed 
that these properties of the conjugate phases 
approach each other and should become equal at 
the plait point.

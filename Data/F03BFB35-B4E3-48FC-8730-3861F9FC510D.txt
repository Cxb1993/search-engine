第一章  前言 
    在高密度開發之都會區內，可以藉由更高的建築以及往地下發展來創造更多的空間
利用。例如，英國地鐵系統及日本的地下車站與商店街都是地下空間開發與利用的典
型。台北101 大樓則是往空中發展而拓展空間的著名案例，當然所有的高樓建築必定伴
隨相當深度的地下室或基礎。這種地下開發而增加空間的方式，是解決土地面積不足的
方案之一，然而，所有地下空間的開發利用都伴隨較高的工程災害風險。例如，台北捷
運主要施工期間發生數次規模較大之地下工程施工災害，而高雄捷運在93-94 年間陸續
亦發生地下工程施工災害達十次。 
    在地下空間的開發利用或災害防治過程中，調查規劃、分析設計、工程施工、營運
維修等任一環節出問題都可能衍生災害。充分且詳細的地質與大地工程調查是第一要
務。在大地工程的現地調查技術中，圓錐貫入試驗(Cone Penetration Test, CPT)是一種簡
單、快速、可靠及經濟的調查方法。此種調查方法可取得垂直向近似連續的地層剖面資
料，有助於深入瞭解地層結構的特性，特別適用於軟弱的黏性土層或是疏鬆的粉質細砂
或砂質粉土層。不過，CPT調查結果之資料量遠大於傳統鑽探資料，在電腦工業尚未成
熟的年代，其應用受到相當的限制。近年來電子資訊科技進步快速，電腦資料處理能力
與速度提高許多，且國內個人電腦甚為普及，使得CPT 應用於國內大地工程調查及檢
驗工作有逐漸增加之趨勢。Schmertmann (1988)認為CPT與DMT是相輔相成的試驗方
法，因為CPT是評估土壤強度的很好方式，而DMT則適用於調查土壤的壓縮性
(compressibility)與現地應力(in-situ stresses)，這些參數都是大地工程分析及設計重要且
基本的資料。 
    根據本研究計劃主持人近年來在國內許多地方的調查結果發現，乾淨砂或純黏性土
壤的CPT 試驗結果相當清楚明顯，其辨識與應用較為容易。然而，細料含量較高的粉
土或黏土質砂性土壤的孔隙水壓激發情形不若乾淨砂或純黏土明顯，而且其貫入阻抗亦
有別於乾淨砂或純黏性土壤。粉土或黏土質砂性土壤的孔隙水壓激發情形或許與細料含
量或細料成分有關，其工程行為並不是很清楚。此種粉土質土壤常出現於國內西部平原
之沖積層，而且對大地工程設計具有相當之重要性，所以必須對此類土壤的特性加以探
討。 
    本研究計劃為整合型計畫「地下空間開發與防災之創新科技研究」的子計畫「科技
化的土壤地盤現地調查技術之研究與應用」。本計畫著重於圓錐貫入試驗 (Cone 
Penetration Test, CPT)相關之研究與應用，配合孔隙水壓之量測，詳細而深入的探討土壤
地盤的貫入阻抗、孔隙水壓激發與細料含量之相關性；同時，以平鈑膨脹儀試驗
(Dilatometer Test, DMT)試驗結果，來測定土壤的變形特性，並嘗試建立兩者之關聯性；
此外，亦將進行平行孔的鑽探調查及SPT試驗及採樣，以進行兩種貫入試驗結果之比較。
本研究期望在既有CPT相關研究之成果下，結合DMT及鑽探取樣之結果進行土壤地盤
CPT試驗的特性與應用方面深入研究，以期對此科技化的調查方法有更完整、更進一步
的了解，綜合各種研究成果提供工程界應用CPT資料進行大地工程分析與設計之參考，
並對地下空間開發與防災有所貢獻。

E.  CPT 試驗設備之改進 
CPT 試驗採用應變控制方式，採用定速率(20±5mm)貫入而量測其貫入阻抗，而
DMT試驗亦可採用CPT 貫入系統。目前，國內CPT 系統中均無推進力量的量測系統，
如果可以取得貫入過程中之推力大小，有助於試驗結果之判讀及控制。本研究計劃擬改
良目前使用中之卡車式CPT 系統，使其具有量測推進力量的能力。 
 
3-2 研究方法-平行調查資料之篩選 
本研究主要利用以 CPTu 試驗為主之平行孔(鑽探與 DMT)的調查案例，進行前述各
項主題的探討。 
鑽探之標準貫入試驗與取樣通常間隔 100 - 200cm 進行乙次，SPT-N 值為貫入 30
公分的累積打擊數(N2+N3)，而採取之土樣通常為兩個 7 公分左右之銅圈樣品，其概略
位置如圖 1 所示。由於 CPTu 每隔 5cm 深度記錄一筆資料，每一筆資料包括錐尖阻抗、
摩擦阻抗及孔隙水壓三種數據。兩種試驗資料的取樣頻率及代表範圍不同，無法直接採
取一對一的資料比對，因此必須決定如何選取對應資料進行後續比對探討。通常多對一
資料的相關性探討會採取多筆資料平均值與單一資料的比較方式，此方式在均質土壤層
的應用可行，但對於夾薄層或互層的土層而言，可能產生較大偏差。例如砂性土層的錐
尖阻抗與孔隙水壓分別為10MPa與80kPa，粘性土層之錐尖阻抗與孔隙水壓分別為1MPa
與 800kPa，兩筆資料的錐尖阻抗與孔隙水壓平均值分別為 5.5MPa 與 440kPa，此平均值
的代表性顯然不恰當。 
本研究採用鑽探取樣深度對應之單筆 CPTu 數據進行兩者資料之相關性探討，而且
此單筆 CPTu 資料足以代表此範圍內之土層試驗結果方為有效比對資料。由於貫入錐前
端之土層性質會影響試驗結果，其影響深度達貫入錐直徑之 2 至 4 倍(約 15 公分)，而試
驗深度(錐尖)後 30 公分則為 SPT-N 值採計範圍，所以較嚴謹的考量方式為採計 45 公分
內的 CPTu 資料(10 筆)與單筆的 SPT 資料對應(圖 1)。CPTu 的試驗數據中以錐尖阻抗較
具代表性(準確度最高)，故以錐尖阻抗值為有效與否之篩選依據。 
一般 SPT-N 的變異係數為 26%，而 Standard cone test 的變異係數為 37 (Schultze，
1975)，所以，應用類似的觀念，差異量在代表值的 30%以內應該是可以接受的有效資
料。也就是說，對應深度內(45 公分)之所有錐尖阻抗與選取錐尖阻抗資料之差值(絕對值)
與選取錐尖阻抗之比值必須小於 30%視為有效數據；但當選取錐尖阻抗值小於 1MPa
時，其差值小於 0.3MPa 亦可視為有效數據。圖 2 為有效代表資料之示意圖，圖中實線
上任一符號點代表選取資料之錐尖阻抗，對應深度內土層之所有錐尖阻抗值落在線上符
號點與上下虛線範圍內，表示此筆資料可為此深度土層之有效代表。圖 3 為實測 CPTu
資料之篩選說明，圖中標準貫入試驗的試驗深度分別為 17.55-18.0 公尺及 19.05-19.50 公
尺，選取的對應 CPTu 資料點深度分別為 17.9 公尺及 19.4 公尺。深度 17.9 公尺的錐尖
阻抗值為 10.12MPa，其篩選的上下限錐尖阻抗值分別為 13.16MPa 及 7.09MPa，深度
17.70-18.15 公尺內之所有錐尖阻抗值均在上下限範圍內，此筆平行調查資料為有效數
據。深度 19.4 公尺的錐尖阻抗值為 7.37MPa，其篩選的上下限錐尖阻抗值分別為 9.58MPa
及 5.16MPa，深度 19.2-19.65 公尺內之部分錐尖阻抗值落在上下限範圍之外，此筆平行
調查資料不視為有效數據。

4-3  CPTu 孔隙水壓特性 
CPT試驗過程中所量測的孔隙水壓(Pw)並不是土壤真正的孔隙水壓，而是試驗過程
中水壓計感應到的水壓大小，量測值與水壓計的安置位置有關，目前經常使用的為透水
元件安置在緊接錐頭之後的方式，一般以孔隙水壓(u2)表示之。圖20為為篩選後各類土
壤之孔隙水壓係數與其細料含量之相關性，圖中可以發現細料含量約在90%以下之砂性
土壤(SP-SM、SM)及粉土(ML或CL-ML)的孔隙水壓係數幾乎接近0，而細料含量約在90%
以上之大部分的粘性土壤(CL)及部分粉土(ML或CL-ML)的孔隙水壓係數介於0至1之
間。圖20之結果說明一般砂性土層大都不會激發超額之孔隙水壓及正常壓密粘性土層明
顯激發超額孔隙水壓之之特性。由圖中亦可發現部分低塑性粉土(ML)類似砂性土壤的無
孔隙水壓激發的性質，部分ML土壤則類似粘性土壤(CL)的高額孔隙水壓激發特性。因
此，CPTu試驗量測到之明顯超額孔隙水壓，即顯示此試驗土壤應具有粘性土層的性質，
此項孔隙水壓激發特性應可應用於粉土質土壤之砂性土壤或粘性土壤屬性之評估。圖21
為篩選後各類土壤之孔隙水壓係數(Bq)與土壤行為分類指數(Ic)之相關性，由圖中可以了
解土壤行為分類指數(Ic)大於2.95之Zone 3(粘性土壤)土壤之孔隙水壓激發特性。 
CPT資料之應用時通常採用修正之錐尖阻抗(qt)，由於砂性土壤通常不會激發超額
孔隙水壓，而且孔隙水壓值遠小於錐尖阻抗(qc)，所以，考慮孔隙水壓修正之錐尖阻抗
(qt)幾乎等於錐尖阻抗(qc)，所以一般的砂性土壤之孔隙水壓係數Bq 大都小於0或相當接
近0。圖22為篩選後所有土壤之孔隙水壓係數(Bq)與正規化錐尖阻抗之分佈情形，圖中清
楚顯示砂性土壤之孔隙水壓係數小於或趨近於0的特性。圖中亦可以發現大部分粘性土
壤具有低正規化錐尖阻抗與高孔隙水壓係數之特性。由此可說明Bq 值可以用來區分土
壤之粘性或砂性之屬性，而且此參數對粘性土壤具有較顯著的意義，但對砂性土壤之的
影響甚微。圖23之水平座標為Schneider et al., (2008)建議之孔隙水壓參數(Δu2 /σv’)，彼等
說明CPTu之孔隙水壓參數比孔隙水壓係數(Bq)可獲得更佳之土壤種類判釋，圖中資料點
分佈情形可以更清楚說明砂性土壤之較高正規化錐尖阻抗與負孔隙水壓參數及大部分
粘性土壤具有低正規化錐尖阻抗與孔隙水壓激發之特性。

第五章  結論與建議 
    本研究計畫的主要研究課題分別為 CPT 土壤分類的特性探討、孔隙水壓激發、CPT
與 DMT 資料之分析等，分別獲致一些重要成果，擇要說明如下： 
1  經過篩選條件後的有效資料分析結果可以得到更明確的對應情形，也可以有效釐清
粉土質土壤的阻抗特性。RW 的 CPT 分類方式應用於砂性土壤或粘性土壤之結果與
鑽探取樣分析結果對應的情形相當良好。 
2  平行資料之分析結果顯示砂性土壤(SP-SM、SM)在 CPT 的分類中大部分落在 Zone 6
與 Zone 5，粘性土壤(CL)的資料點主要分佈在土壤分類圖的 Zone 4、Zone 3 及 Zone 
2。 
3  BJ 的 CPT 分類方法在砂性土壤的應用結果與鑽探取樣分析結果相當一致，亦可有效
的區分粘性土壤，但其分類方式造成粘性土壤(CL)大部分落在有機土或泥炭土之
Zone 2，可能需要進一步檢討。 
4  粉土質土壤(ML 及 CL-ML)在 CPT 土壤分類圖之資料點分佈情形顯示，部分粉土質
土壤類似於砂性土壤的阻抗特性，另一部分則近似粘性土壤的阻抗特性。 
5  砂性土壤與粘性土壤兩種土壤的適當土壤行為分類指數(Ic)界限應用統計分析的方
式，考慮 2 倍標準差採用 RW 法之土壤行為分類指數 2.5 為分界是一個合理值。在土
壤液化分析中，若考慮部份細顆粒土壤亦可能產生液化，可以採用砂性土壤之平均
值加 3 個標準差之土壤行為分類指數為界限，此土壤行為分類指數界限值為 2.71。 
6  BJ 法之砂性土壤與粘性土壤兩種土壤的適當土壤行為分類指數(Ic)界限值約為
2.46。在土壤液化分析中，可以採用砂性土壤之平均值加 3 個標準差之土壤行為分類
指數為界限，此土壤行為分類指數界限值為 2.75。 
7  細料含量低之砂性土壤(SP-SM、SM)及粉土(ML 或 CL-ML)的孔隙水壓係數幾乎接近
0，而細料含量約在 90%以上之大部分的粘性土壤(CL)及部分粉土(ML 或 CL-ML)的
孔隙水壓係數介於 0 至 1 之間。 
8  部分低塑性粉土(ML)類似砂性土壤的無孔隙水壓激發的性質，部分 ML 土壤則類似
粘性土壤(CL)的高額孔隙水壓激發特性。 
9  CPT-Ko 與 DMT-Ko 之比值約為 1.57，CPT-M 與 DMT-M 之比值約為 0.78。在黏性土
壤層中，CPT-Ko 與 DMT-Ko 之比值約為 3.49，CPT-M 與 DMT-M 之比值約為 0.73。
在連續且達某種厚度的砂性或粘性土層，CPT 與 DMT 推估 Ko 值與 M 值的結果對應
相當一致，但是在黏性土層夾砂性薄層或砂性土層夾黏性薄層之情形時，CPT-Ko 變
化則會增大，此種情形值得進一步探討。

Two formulations of Ic are available. The first, developed by
Jefferies and colleagues (Jefferies & Davies, 1991; Been &
Jefferies, 1992; Jefferies & Davies, 1993) is referred to
herein as the Been and Jefferies (BJ) formulation and de-
noted as Ic,BJ. The other is a modification of the BJ formula-
tion by Robertson and co-workers (Lunne et al., 1997;
Robertson & Wride, 1998), referred to herein as the Robert-
son and Wride (RW) formulation and denoted as Ic,RW.
Conceptually, the BJ formulation (Ic,BJ) is more attractive, as
it incorporates all three basic parameters (qt, fs, and u2),
which could be advantageous especially for the characterisa-
tion of the fine-grained soils. However, Robertson (2009b)
pointed out that the accuracy and precision of the u2 meas-
urement for on-shore testing are not always reliable and
repeatable owing to potential loss of saturation of the pore
pressure element, which could complicate the accuracy of
the BJ formulation. Table 1 lists the definitions of soil
behaviour types based on the two formulations of Ic. Both
formulations are examined in this paper.
In geotechnical engineering practice, soils are often
grouped into two classes: sand (cohesionless; typically in
drained condition) and clay (cohesive; typically in undrained
condition). Distinct analysis models are often created and
employed for these two ‘behavioural’ groups. For the pur-
poses of many engineering analyses, soils are often classified
either as ‘sand-like’ or ‘clay-like.’ If a soil exhibits a mixture
of both types of behaviour, it would still be better or
necessary to identify the soil as sand-like or clay-like, and to
perform the analysis accordingly. Classical examples include,
but are not limited to, apparent-pressure envelopes for braced
cuts (Peck, 1969), axial load capacity of drilled shafts
(O’Neill & Reese, 1999), lateral resistance of piles (Broms,
1965) and cyclic liquefaction or softening (Boulanger &
Idriss, 2007), in which the distinct analysis models (i.e. equa-
tions or charts) for the two mechanical behaviour groups,
‘sand-like’ and ‘clay-like,’ are created and employed accord-
ingly. In this study, an effort is made to find the most suitable
Ic cut-off value that can delineate sand-like soils from clay-
like soils.
The significance of the index Ic in liquefaction or cyclic
softening evaluation is further elaborated here. This index
plays an important role in the CPT-based liquefaction evalua-
tion, particularly with the Robertson & Wride (1998) method,
which is perhaps the most widely used CPT-based method.
Liquefaction in sand-like soils during earthquake shaking is
well observed and can be analysed with the conventional
stress-based simplified methods such as Robertson & Wride
(1998). Conversely, for clay-like soils, significant strength
losses and ground deformations during and after earthquake
shaking have also been reported (Ishihara, 1996; Perlea et
al., 1999; Chu et al., 2008). However, a past attempt to
characterise the liquefaction behaviour of clay-like soils with-
in the framework of the conventional simplified methods had
not been as successful. Thus, Boulanger & Idriss (2007)
suggested that a different approach, by means of cyclic
triaxial test or cyclic simple shear test, be taken to analyse
cyclic softening and deformation of clay-like soils during
earthquake shaking. Nevertheless, recent studies (Shuttle &
Cunning, 2007; Robertson, 2009a) showed that a ‘unified’
model that can evaluate both liquefaction of sand-like soils
and cyclic softening of clay-like soils can be created within
the same framework of simplified methods. Such develop-
ment is made possible by an additional insight on the ‘state
parameter’ and soil behaviour type index gained by the
profession over the years.
Because physical samples are usually unavailable with
CPT/CPTu, it would be of particular interest and importance
to be able to delineate sand-like soils from clay-like soils
based upon CPT/CPTu parameters such as Ic. This capability
is also desirable in geotechnical practice as it provides an
objective means for determining the soil behaviour type
(sand-like or clay-like), which is especially significant for
fine-grained soils. Thus, soil classification using Ic is focused
on in this paper, with a special interest in the reliability of
using Ic to delineate sand-like soils from clay-like soils.
DATABASE AND DATA SCREENING
The ‘raw’ database used in this study consists of 133 side-
by-side SPT and CPTu data pairs from seven sites in Tainan,
Taiwan and 275 data pairs from seven sites in Kaohsiung,
Taiwan for a total of 408 data pairs. All sites are located in
the coastal plain of western Taiwan between Tainan and
Kaohsiung (Fig. 1). The subsurface soils are the Quaternary
alluvial deposits, consisting mainly of fine sands, silts and
clays. To ensure the desired side-by-side comparison, the
data have been collected from sites with low soil variability
in the horizontal direction; additionally, the distance between
each pair of SPT borehole and CPTu sounding is less than
3 m. (Note: for sites with significant soil variability, this
horizontal distance could have been too large giving the
intended purpose. This is not the case ‘by design’ in the
present study.)
The selection of a data pair is briefly described below. In
an SPT borehole, testing and sampling is usually taken at an
interval of 100 cm to 200 cm. The SPT blow count is the
sum of the blow counts for the last two 15 cm penetration
(N2 + N3) and the sample is retrieved from the last 30 cm of
the sampler as shown in Fig. 2. Because CTPu sounding
provides data for qc, fs and u2 typically at an interval of
5 cm, approximately seven CPTu data points are available
for a single SPT data point. Additionally, because the CPTu
sounding is affected by the soils immediately below the cone
tip, an additional 15 cm (see Jefferies & Been (2006) for
discussion of the effect of interbedded strata) is included in
the ‘effective’ segment for pairing. Thus, approximately ten
CPTu data points are available to pair with the single SPT
data in the corresponding segment, as shown in Fig. 2. One

that the Robertson and Wride method was created primarily
for sandy soils whose Bq values are generally negligible
(Bq  0). Additionally, the effect of Bq could have been
reflected, to some extent, in the combined effect of Qt and

Ic,BJ. On the other hand, the BJ formulation is conceptually
more attractive as it incorporates all three main CPT meas-
urements including u2. Furthermore, Houlsby (1988) drew
attention to the merit of the term, Qt(1 Bq) þ 1, which
was subsequently shown by Shuttle & Cunning (2007) to be
a fundamental term. Because of these differences in opinion,
both formulations are examined in this paper.
Using the RW formulation as an example, the soils with
known USGS classifications in the database of 201 data
pairs are examined herein. For the 13 data pairs of SP–SM
in the database, which is poorly graded sand with 5% to
12% of fines content, Fig. 6(a) shows that they are all
(100%) located in zones 6 and 5 of the CPT classification
chart. For 92 data pairs of SM in the database, with a fines
content of 12% to 50% with none-to-low plasticity and
plotted below A-line in the plasticity chart, Fig. 6(b) shows
that approximately 98% of data points are located in zones 6
and 5 with the other 2% (two data points in this case)
located in zone 3. An examination of these two data points
reveals that each is located very close to the interface of two
varying soil layers. In this case, the CPTu result is most
likely a reflection of the property of ‘composite’ soil layer,
rather than a single soil type. When the cone is in transition
at or near an interface between soils of significantly different
strength and stiffness, the potential for misinterpretation has
been well recognised (e.g. Robertson, 2009b). In the subse-
quent analysis, these two data points are considered as
‘outliers’ and not included in the analysis (as it is more
desirable to include only ‘clean-cut’ data in the analysis).
One assumption of the data analysis presented herein is
that the variability in the horizontal direction is negligible.
As noted previously, all SPT–CPTu pairs are selected from
sites with low variability in the horizontal direction, and the
distance between borehole and CPTu location is kept at less
than 3 m. Thus, this assumption is deemed reasonable,
although as reported by Jefferies & Been (2006), in some
cases a distance as short as 1.5 m may be required to ensure
the repeatability in the qc measurement.
For the 28 data pairs of ML or ML–CL in the database,
Fig. 6(c) shows that 71.4% of data points are located in
zones 6 and 5, with the rest (28.6%) in zone 3. This
indicates that soils classified as ML or ML–CL will behave
either sand-like (zones 6 and 5) or clay-like (zone 3).
Finally, for 68 data pairs of CL in the database, Fig. 6(d)
shows that 86.8% of data points are in zones 4 and 3 (with
an overwhelming majority in zone 3); while 7.4% (five data
points) are in zone 5 and 5.9% (four data points) are in zone
2. The five data points in zone 5 are considered ‘outliers’,
as they are located very close to the interface of two varying
soil layers. The four data points in zone 2 (organic soils),
however, are likely caused by the selected Ic,RW boundary
between clays and organic soils (according to the RW
formulation and the associated classification criteria, in
which Ic,RW . 3.60 signals organic soils). With proper data
screening to ensure the inclusion of only quality ‘clean-cut’
data in the analysis, results show that soils in the database
can be classified into one of the two major groups, sand-like
(zones 6 or 5) and clay-like (zone 3) with CPT/CPTu data
alone. Table 2 provides a summary of this analysis.
The above examination of 201 data pairs in the database
is based on the Ic defined by Robertson & Wride (1998).
The same analysis can be repeated with the Ic defined by
Been & Jefferies (1992); these results are also shown in
Table 2. For SP–SM and SM soils, there is practically no
difference in the results of CPT/CPTu classification, regard-
less of which Ic formulation is used. For other soils, the
classification results based upon the two approaches are also
quite comparable, with one exception. A large percentage of
CL data points are found in zone 2 using the BJ formulation
and the associated criteria (Table 1), suggesting that in this
formulation, the selected boundary value between clays and
organic soils, Ic,BJ ¼ 3.22, might be too low.
CRITERION FOR SEPARATING SAND-LIKE AND CLAY-
LIKE SOILS
An examination of the soils in the database – including
SP–SM, SM, ML or CL–ML and CL –clearly demonstrates
that these soils can be grouped into sand-like and clay-like
soils based on soil behaviour classification criteria. Because
it is often desirable in many geotechnical applications to
classify soils into sand-like or clay-like soils, it is important
to develop a simple criterion to distinguish between these
two groups. In this paper, a cut-off Ic value, denoted herein
as Ic,c, is envisioned such that a soil is deemed sand-like if
Ic < Ic,c, and clay-like if Ic . Ic,c. Thus, the task now is to
select this boundary or cut-off value.

plotted separately in the CPT classification chart, as shown

Also plotted in this graph are the curve for the mean plus
one standard deviation (labelled as Ic(þ )), the curve for the

‘sand-like’ if Ic,RW < 2.67 and ‘clay-like’ if Ic,RW . 2.67.

Finally, for the zone 3 (clays) and zone 2 boundaries
(organic soils), Ic,RW ¼ 3.60, as recommended by Lunne et
al. (1997), appears to be reasonable, as there are only about
6% of CL data point located beyond zone 3 (into zone 2).
Recall that the mean Ic for the group of soils classified as
CL is 3.26, and the standard deviation is 0.192. Thus,
Ic,RW ¼ 3.60 is 1.77 times standard deviation above the
mean, which means that there is a 3.8% chance that a soil
classified as CL will fall beyond zone 3 (into zone 2). If the
cut-off value is increased to two times standard deviation
above the mean, Ic,RW ¼ 3.64, the chance of misplacing CL
soil into zone 2 is reduced to 1.5%. If three times standard
deviation above the mean is adopted as the cut-off value,
Ic,RW ¼ 3.84, the chance of misplacing CL soil into zone 2
is reduced to 0.13%. Thus, it appears that the appropriate
cut-off selection is two times standard deviation above the
mean, which produces an upper bound of zone 3 approxi-
mately equal to that recommended by Lunne et al. (1997).
Nevertheless, further study with additional data to contrast
this ‘separator’ from the other side (from zone 2 to zone 3)
is considered a worthwhile effort.

The above analysis was repeated using the Been and
Jefferies’ formulation of Ic and the associated classification
criteria. Fig. 13(a) shows the scatter of Ic of SM soils along
with the mean Ic curve and the curves for the mean plus
one standard deviation, two standard deviations, and three
standard deviations, respectively. Fig. 13(b) shows the scat-
ter of Ic of CL soils along with the mean Ic curve and the
curves for the mean minus one standard deviation, two
standard deviations, and three standard deviations, respec-
tively. A suitable cut-off value (Ic,c) should be between the
upper bound from Fig. 13(a) and the lower bound from
Fig. 13(b). Fig. 14 shows a summary of the results follow-
ing this approach, which yields Ic,c ¼ 2.58. Thus, the soil is
deemed ‘sand-like’ if Ic,BJ < 2.58 and ‘clay-like’ if
Ic,BJ . 2.58.
Figure 15 shows a graph that is the same as Fig. 11
except that Ic is computed with the BJ formulation. Similar
observations as described previously with Fig. 11 can be
made. The penetration in the sand-like soils does not gener-
ate excess pore pressure which results in Bq  0; on the
other hand, the clay-like soils can generate excess pore
pressure during the penetration and thus have a significant
Bq. The cut-off value of Ic,c ¼ 2.58 appears to be a suitable
choice. Fig. 16 shows a similar graph to Fig. 15, in which
Bq is replaced with the normalised excess pore pressure
(˜u2= 9vo). Again, Ic,c ¼ 2.58 is seen as a suitable cut-off
value for the mechanical behaviour-based soil classification.
Similar to the Ic,RW-based soil classification by Lunne et
al. (1997), the Ic,BJ-based classification by Been & Jefferies
(1992) also appears to be reasonable. However, in the Been
and Jefferies classification, a large portion of CL data
points are located in zone 2 with Ic,BJ . 3.22 (see Figs 15
and 16). It appears that the boundary value between zone 3
(clays) and zone 2 (organic soils) that they recommend,
which is Ic,BJ ¼ 3.22, is too low. Recall that the mean Ic,BJ
for soils classified as CL in the database is 3.23, and the
standard deviation is 0.201. Thus, the mean Ic obtained in
the analysis is approximately equal to the upper bound of
zone 3 recommended by Been & Jefferies (1992). If the
mean plus two times standard deviation, Ic,BJ ¼ 3.64, is
adopted as the upper bound of zone 3, almost all the data
points classified as CL in the database will be ‘re-located’
into zone 3. However, the view (i.e. the suggestion of using
Ic,BJ ¼ 3.64 as a separator between zone 2 and zone 3) is
somewhat speculative, as the result is yet to be confirmed
by a database that include adequate data points of organic
soils.

additional criterion of ‘PI , 7’ as per Boulanger & Idriss
(2007), 23 of the 28 data points can be characterised as
sand-like in the context of soil liquefaction evaluation.
Alternatively, with an additional criterion of ‘PI , 12 and

data points can be characterised as sand-like. These two
plasticity-based criteria are indeed quite effective in distin-
guishing sand-like soils from clay-like soils. However,
among the data points that are identified as ‘sand-like’ based
on either Boulanger & Idriss (2007) or Bray & Sancio
(2006), only 20 data points are identified as ‘sand-like’
based on the Ic criterion (either Ic,RW ¼ 2.67 or Ic,BJ ¼ 2.58).
For the other eight data points, including the six low
plasticity data points that are shown in Fig. 18 and another
two data points that are identified as non-plastic (NP) fine-
grained materials according to USCS, the CPT soundings all
indicate generation of high positive pore pressure. Thus,
these eight data points are clearly ‘clay-like’ soils.
In summary, the Ic-based criterion is shown to be more
accurate than the physical classification based on USCS
(with additional plasticity-based criteria) in distinguishing
sand-like soils from clay-like soils. The soil behaviour type
index (Ic) correctly reflects the mechanical behaviour of soils
and serves the purpose of distinguishing sand-like soils from
clay-like soils without the knowledge of particle size dis-
tribution and plasticity. Thus, the CPT-based soil classifica-
tion is significant in its own way, and there is no need to
match one-to-one with the USCS classification. Thus, the
reliability of CPT Ic as an index for mechanical behaviour
classification of soils is demonstrated.
CONCLUSIONS
(a) Soil behaviour type index (Ic), either in terms of the
RW formulation (Ic,RW) or the BJ formulation (Ic,BJ), is
an effective parameter for soil classification. Unlike the
physical properties-based USCS classification, the Ic-
based classification reflects grouping of soils by mech-
anical behaviours. This index is shown capable of
distinguishing effectively sand-like soils (cohesionless,
typically in drained condition) from clay-like soils
(cohesive, typically in undrained condition). This
capability is significant, as evidenced by the presented
results of the analysis of liquefaction susceptibility of
soils at the Adapazari, Turkey sites.
(b) In terms of the RW formulation, the most suitable cut-
off Ic,c value for distinguishing sand-like soils from
clay-like soils is Ic,RW ¼ 2.67; in terms of the BJ
formulation, the most suitable cut-off Ic,c value is
Ic,BJ ¼ 2.58. These cut-off values were established based
on statistical analyses of the data set derived and
employed in this paper and verified from multiple
angles. These cut-off values are quite consistent with
the well-recognised limiting value of Ic,RW ¼ 2.60.
However, research with additional quality data to
further validate these cut-off values is desirable. In
this regard, collection of soils that would normally fall
into the ‘zone 4’ of the CPT classification chart to
expand the database for further work is especially
important.
(c) Soils classified as SP–SM or SM using USCS are
almost certain ( 100% probability) to exhibit sand-
like behaviours whereas those classified as CL are
almost certain to exhibit clay-like behaviours. Soils
classified as ML or ML–CL can exhibit either sand-
like or clay-like behaviour. The proposed criterion
based on the cut-off Ic,c value can help ascertain if a
given fine-grained soil will behave in either a sand-like
or clay-like fashion.
(d ) The soil behaviour type index (Ic) correctly reflects the
mechanical behaviour of soils and can distinguish sand-
like soils from clay-like soils without the knowledge of
particle size distribution and plasticity. The reliability of
Ic as an index for mechanical behaviour classification
of soils is demonstrated.
(e) Based on the results presented, it is considered a
worthwhile effort to optimise Ic-based classification of
all soil behaviour types using an expanded database and
a thorough statistical simulation. Further research in
this area is warranted.

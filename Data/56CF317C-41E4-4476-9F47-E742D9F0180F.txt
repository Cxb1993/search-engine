3.1 組織文化之定義 
組織文化係由人類學領域所提出之概念，目前至少有超過 160 種以上之定義
(Kroeber and Kluckhohn 1952)，亦突顯文化定義之多樣性。本研究根據研究學者之分析
觀點，組織文化可分為象徵比喻、共同信念及關鍵特徵等 3 類型定義，如表 1。 
綜合學者之定義可發現，組織文化是組織成員共同擁有之無意識、半意識之意識型
態、語言、信念及價值觀，且組織文化亦會影響及形成組織之外在行為。因此本研究定
義組織文化為：存於特定組織內具公開性、集體性及共享性之信念及價值，並成為組織
作業執行之制度、程序及責任分配框架，以處理組織內部及外在遭遇之問題。

3.3 營建設計專案執行績效 
工程專案具有複雜、動態及不確定性本質，因此業主需根據技術服務廠商所提之建
議，進行許多重大決策，然有近 80%之營建專案成本，係受到初步設計圖說影響(Pilcher 
1994)，若未發現或未處理任何之設計錯誤(或漏項)，可能造成嚴重之工程爭議或拆除重
做之情況，且設計錯誤之影響成本超過施工階段錯誤之成本(設計錯誤成本佔總成本之
9.5%，施工錯誤成本佔總成本之 2.5%)(Burati et al. 1992)。 
許多政府單位為客觀評估營建設計專案執行績效，皆已針對設計專案特性，選擇適
當評量指標，惟因各單位之評量觀點、評量時機及重點不同，造成評量指標內容之差異。 
Cheung et al. (2002)認為評鑑內容之不一致，對其他業主而言，評鑑結果可能不具
參考性，因此需要一致性之績效評鑑標準。Cheung et al. (2002)及 Ling (2002, 2003)是首
先探討工程設計專案通用性評鑑指標之先驅，Cheung et al. (2002)著重建立建築執行之
績效指標，Ling (2002, 2003)則著重建立設計及建築契約之技術服務績效作業與環境。
Chow and Ng (2010)彙整過去研究對於工程設計專案績效之評鑑指標，定義設計專案績
效為下列 8 項指標：(1)符合及瞭解業主要求、(2)符合法令要求、(3)定義業主要求及專
案目標、(4)設計品質、(5)創新及替代方案之可行性、(6)採用有效管理總成本之方法、
(7)圖說/文件之品質、(8)成本估算之有效性。 
惟根據過去研究之評鑑指標可知，工程設計專案績效之評鑑採用指標與一般工程專
案不同，皆屬於定性之敘述性指標，亦即評鑑得分主要為業主主觀感受，此種現象應與
設計專案之特性有關，因此設計專案之執行績效主要為業主之滿意度。

5.關聯性分析 
利用總影響矩陣(T)之數值，將其以斜線深色部份為主對半切開，將下半部之數值
減去上半部之數值，即可得到其關聯分析值；此關聯分析值代表各項構面及各個準則間
之相對支配關聯性。 
再加以計算總影響矩陣(T)之各行、列之總和，並求得各組之列與行總和之和(d+r)
與差(d-r)，利用此列、行總和之和與差，以列、行總和之和為橫軸，以列、行總和之差
為縱軸，即可繪出決策試驗與實驗評估法分析圖；列、行總和之和代表各項構面及各個
準則對於其它構面及準則之總影響值，即其對其他構面及準則之影響程度；列、行總和
之差代表各項構面及各個準則對於其它構面及準則之淨影響值，即其對其他構面及準則
之影響支配方向性。並將其關聯分析值繪入決策試驗與實驗評估法分析圖中，即可了解
各項構面及各個準則彼此間之支配關聯影響方向及影響程度。 
 
6.繪製淨影響矩陣 
利用主要構面總影響矩陣(T)之數值，將其主對半切開，並將下半部之數值減去上
半部之數值，即可得到其關聯分析值；此關聯分析值代表各項構面及各個準則間之相對
支配關聯性。完成後再計算主要構面總影響矩陣(T)之各行、列之總和，並求得各組之
列與行總和之和(d+r)與差(d-r)，利用此列、行總和之和與差，以列、行總和之和(d+r)
為橫軸，以列、行總和之差(d-r)為縱軸，配合淨影響矩陣數值，即可繪出主要構面之決
策試驗與實驗評估法分析圖；其中列、行總和之和(d+r)代表各項構面及各個準則對於其
他構面及準則之總影響值，即其他構面及準則之影響程度；列、行總和之差(d-r)代表各
項構面及各個準則對於其他構面及準則之淨影響值，即其對其他構面及準則之影響支配
方向性。

Abstract 
Numerous fabrication plants for semiconductors have supported the booming development of the 
national economic growth in Taiwan in recent decades. And semiconductor hookup construction is a 
critical job in completing each plant. During the conceptual phase of semiconductor hookup 
construction, it is difficult to conduct an accurate cost estimate due to a great amount of missing or 
uncertain data of the cost items for constructing the piping activities of process modules. The study 
proposes a new model to estimate the costs of semiconductor hookup construction projects. The 
developed model integrates the component ratios method, Fuzzy Adaptive Learning Control Network 
(FALCON), fast messy genetic algorithm (fmGA), and the three-point cost estimating method. The 
results show that the proposed model has achieved an estimation accuracy of 86.73%, which is 
approximately 18.63% more accurate than the conventional average cost method.  
Keywords: Semiconductor hookup construction, cost estimates, fuzzy adaptive leaning control network, fast 
messy genetic algorithms. 
1 Introduction 
The booming development of high technologies of semiconductors has been keeping the national 
economic growth in Taiwan for past decades. Numerous fabrication plants (or fabs) for 
semiconductors support such development. Semiconductor hookup construction is a critical job to 
complete a fab (Hong, 2001). 
During the conceptual phase of a semiconductor hookup construction project (i.e., constructing a 
process tool of a process module), it is preferable to conduct an accurate cost estimate for effective 
cost control. However, in practice, due to the requirement of rapid construction of semiconductor 
plants, the management team of semiconductor plants may not be able to ascertain the semiconductor 
process and/or the detailed requirements of a process module at that time. In summary, estimating the 
costs of the tools of process modules is under the environment of missing or uncertain estimation data. 
The conventional methods used to estimate the cost of process module are either taking the average 
cost of historical data or relying heavily on experienced estimators’ intuition. Such a method performs 
poorly.

Genetic Algorithms (GAs) have been widely used in project cost estimations (Kim et al., 2005; Yu 
and Lin, 2006; Cheng et al., 2009). Generally, NNs provide the ability to learn from past data and

4 Review of related techniques 
This section reviews the techniques related to the proposed model, including: the component ratios 
method, FALCON, and fmGA.  
4.1 Component ratios method 
In the component ratios method, it is assumed that there exists a ratio (or factor) between the whole 
project cost and the cost of a major cost item (Barrie and Paulson, 1992; Yu, 2006). Hence, when the 
cost of the major cost item and the ratio (= total project cost divided by item cost based on historical 
data) are known, the total project cost can be calculated by multiplying the item cost by the ratio 
(greater than 1.0). Following the concept of component ratios method, Yu (2006) further developed a 
principal-item ratio estimating method by considering only the selected 20% cost items (called 
principal items) and their associated principal item ratios for calculating the overall cost. Notably, the 
concept of principal items is adopted by this research with a difference that the sum of the costs of 
principal items is not exactly equal to 80%. 
4.2 FALCON 
FALCON combines the neural network learning ability with fuzzy logic decisions (Lin and Lee, 
1991). FALCON’s learning ability is based on the use of the Kohonen learning rule and supervised 
learning algorithm. In the traditional FALCON methodology, there is no mechanism for rule 
refinement after the Kohonen learning. As indicated by Yu and Skibniewski (1999), it has been found 
after many computational experiments that the two learning algorithms (the competitive learning for 
initial rule connection and the back-propagation for fine-tuning of membership functions) of the 
FALCON methodology encounter severe local minimum problems.  
Since there is no mechanism to revise the fuzzy logic rules by means of back-propagation once 
they are determined, the problem of local minimum cannot be improved in the traditional FALCON 
method (Lin and Lee, 1996). Based on literature reviews, the FALCON’s fuzzy membership functions 
and fuzzy logic rules are optimized through the fmGA’s mutation and cut-splice operators for 
improving the accuracy of cost estimation in the current study. 
4.3 fmGA 
GAs are search algorithms and they search through a decision space for optimal solutions based on 
the mechanics of natural selection and genetics. To explore an individual gene’s contribution to the 
fitness value during the evolution process, messy genetic algorithms (mGA) was developed (Goldberg 
et al., 1989). Unlike the simple genetic algorithms which uses fixed length strings to represent 
possible solutions, Goldberg et al. (1993) further developed the fast mGA (fmGA) to apply messy 
chromosomes to form strings of various lengths. 
The chromosome of fmGA is divided into two parts: allele locus and allele value. The major 
difference between fmGA and the traditional GAs lies in the fact that fmGA allows for variations in 
the lengths of chromosomes, and the evolution of the allele locus and allele value may happen 
simultaneously. Such a characteristic of variable chromosome lengths is very desirable for structure 
revision of FALCON, since an optimum structure of precondition and consequence links for the fuzzy 
rule base may be obtained via the evolution process of fmGA.

5.3 Step 2: applying FALCON 
Figure 2 presents the network structure of FALCON. The structure consists of five layers of nodes 
and two links, including: the input linguistic nodes (Layer 1), input term nodes (Layer 2), IF-part 
condition links (Link 1), rule nodes (Layer 3), THEN-part consequence links (Link 2), output term 
nodes (Layer 4) and output linguistic nodes (Layer 5) (Lin and Lee, 1991; Yu and Lin, 2006). Layer 2 
and Layer 3 are connected by Link 1, while Layer 3 and Layer 4 are connected by Link 2. The 
computation results of each node will be passed on to the next layer of nodes through the synaptic 
weights of the neuron and become the input value for the next layer. 
The description of these FALCON layers and links can be found in Lin and Lee (1991) and Yu and 
Lin (2006). For example, in Layer 1 (input linguistic nodes): The nodes at this layer just transmit the 
input values (i.e., cost data) to the next layer directly. No transformation is performed at this layer. 
For example, the actual costs of the four principal cost items (i.e., bulk gas, UPW, exhaust, and 
foundation) for a CMP project are transmitted directed into the network. 
5.4 Step 3: applying fmGA 
After completing the FALCON operations, the fmGA’s mutation and cut-splice operators then are 
applied to optimize the FALCON’s parameters including fuzzy membership functions and fuzzy logic 
rules in order to improve the accuracy of cost estimation. To do so, the variable-length chromosome 
of fmGA is utilized to revise the fuzzy partitions (i.e., the number of input term nodes of Layer 2 in 
Fig. 2) and fuzzy decision rules (i.e., the consequence links of Link 2 in Fig. 2) of FALCON. The 
global search capability of fmGA is employed to optimize the parameters (means and spreads) of the 
membership functions in input and output term nodes of FALCON. 
As also presented in Fig. 1, fmGA includes two operation loops, outer loop and inner loop. 
Finishing an outer loop is called an epoch, while conducting an inner loop is called an era. 
Furthermore, an inner loop consists of three phases (Goldberg et al., 1993): (1) the initialization 
phase — to produce sufficient population size (n) and chromosome length (l) for evolutionary 
operations; (2) primordial phase — bad genes are filtered out in order to maintain only the 
chromosomes with good fitness; and (3) juxtapositional phase — cut-splice and mutation operations 
are performed to attain a high quality generation.

Experiments 
The growth of GaN films on Si(111) substrates were conducted on Oxford CLUSTERLAB 
600 system using an radio frequency (RF) plasma source to activate the nitrogen and standard 
Knudsen-cells for the solid sources. The growth chamber was pumped down to a base pressure of 
5×10-11 Torr. Nitrogen partial pressure in the growth chamber was stabilized during the growth of 
GaN films at approximately 10-5 Torr. Silicon (111) substrates were cleaned before being loaded 
in the MBE system using a modified RCA method and heated in the growth chamber at 920℃ 
for 30 min to desorb native oxide. For the growth of AlN buffer layers the plasma power was set 
to 270W, 240W, and 200W for sample A, B, and C, respectively. The III/V ratio was set close to 
unity. After the buffer layer growth a GaN epilayer was grown at a substrate temperature of 725
℃ and a slightly gallium-rich condition. The deposition rate was approximately 0.3μm/hr for a 
total film thickness of 0.6μm. After growth, the film thickness was characterized using scanning 
electron microscopy (SEM). The optical performance was investigated by photoluminescence 
measurements. PL was excited by the 325 nm line of a He-Cd UV laser, using a typical power of 
8 mW. A Siemens D5000 x-Ray diffractometer was used to determine the structural quality of 
grown films. The radiation used is copper k-alpha with a wavelength of 1.5418 Angstroms. 
GaN films were also grown on ZnO templates prepared by MOCVD. 0.4μm ZnO was 
deposited on both silicon substrate. ZnO started to decompose at temperatures higher than 600℃ 
in growth chamber, therefore low temperature GaN was grown at 500℃ under slightly gallium 
rich condition prior to the growth of high temperature GaN. The growth rate was 0.1μm/hr. The 
high temperature GaN was grown at a substrate temperature of 750℃ and a slightly gallium-rich 
condition. The deposition rate was approximately 0.3μm/hr for a film thickness of 0.45μm.  
 
Results and discussion 
All samples were grown to determine the effect of varying the effective III/V ratio on the 
optical properties of the grown layers. Table 1 provides the growth parameters of all samples. 
The III/V ratio can be adjusted by modifying the RF plasma-source power. Prior to the nucleation 
of AlN buffer layer, the substrates were exposed to one monolayer of aluminum flux. The AlN 
buffer layers, grown at 850  under stoichiometric flux conditions℃  showed streaky RHEED 
patterns indicating a 2D growth mode.

Cleaved Nitride Semiconductor Laser Mirrors 
 
Yi-Cheng Chang1; Wei-Li Chen1*; Che-Chin Chen1; Ming-Hua Lo2; Tien-Chang Lu2; 
Jong-Ching Wu1;  
1National Changhua University of Education, Taiwan 
2National Chiao Tung University, Taiwan 
Wei-Li Chen* and Yi-Cheng Chang 
*email: weili@cc.ncue.edu.tw
 
ABSTRACT 
It is challenging to fabricate nitride laser mirrors on foreign substrates due to the 
lack of naturally cleaved facets. We demonstrate smooth cleaved gallium nitride facet 
mirrors on Si(111) substrate fabricated by a micro-cleaving technology. The cleaved 
surfaces follow the crystallographic direction of the nitride crystal. The fabrication of 
laser facets on a full wafer scale with precise control on mirror position can be achieved. 
The resonance characteristics of a Fabry-Perot laser such as threshold current density and 
slope efficiency will be drastically affected if both end reflectors are not smooth. The 
approach demonstrated in this study can be applied to sapphire substrates. A 
micro-cleaving approach was applied to create smooth facet mirrors. Designed cantilever 
features on a gallium nitride thin film were defined by photolithography, followed by a 
photo enhance electrochemical (PEC) etch until the substrate was exposed. Lateral 
undercuts underneath the patterned features were created by isotropic wet etch of the 
silicon substrate. The designed nitride suspended cantilevers were isolated from the 
substrate completely. Small notches were located near the fixed end of the hung 
cantilevers to facilitate the micro-cleaving process by concentrated stress. Mechanical 
forces were applied to break the cantilevers. GaN based laser mirrors fabricated by 
directly cleaving sapphire and GaN substrates were compared. The facets made by 
micro-cleavage showed improved roughness confirmed by surface morphology 
characterization. 
 
Key words: micro-cleaving; gallium nitride; cleaved facets; laser.

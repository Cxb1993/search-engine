INTRODUCTION
Greenhouse gases such as CO2, CH4, and CFCs are the primary causes of global
warming. In response, the Kyoto Protocol of the United Nations Framework
Convention on Climate Change mandated a goal for industrial countries to decrease
emission level of CO2. Chemical conversion of CO2 to fuels or chemicals is a
challenging subject in connection with the mitigation of greenhouse effect and the
recovery of carbon resource after the exhaustion of fossil materials. In the conversion
of CO2, some factors are of significance including low energy requirement in the
reaction, high reaction rate, and high yields of valuable products. The use of fossil
fuel to provide the required energy is not promising, because the secondary generation
of CO2 from fossil fuel is feared. The advantage of photoreduction of CO2 is to use
inexhaustible solar energy. Plants can use solar energy to perform natural
photosynthesis, but the rate of transformation is low. The most desirable process is an
artificial photosynthesis in which CO2 is convertible to valuable compounds using a
solar cell as the energy source at room temperature. Even under the optimal artificial
conditions, energy efficiency can only reach about 10% in macroalga under full
sunlight1. To put this process to practical use, however, the rate of artificial
photosynthesis is required to be much higher than that of natural photosynthesis2.
Consequently, the photoreduction of CO2 is particularly interesting. The ultimate goal
is to demonstrate that artificial photosynthesis may be implemented via the
photoreduction of CO2 to produce hydrocarbons, such as methanol and methane. That
is, solar energy is transformed and stored as chemical energy3.

CONCLUSIONS
This study investigated the application of the ultraviolet (UV) with reduction
photocatalyst to convert the greenhouse air pollutants to the useful chemicals. Carbon
dioxide, which has a highest concentration among greenhouse air pollutants, was taken
as a model compound. The emission of CO2 to the atmosphere was substantially
found in the industrial waste gaseous streams. The photocatalyts used in this study were
Ag/TiO2 and Cu/TiO2 made by the impregnation method. The photocatalytic
reductions of CO2 were performed via three different methods. The major product via
methods 1 and 2 was methanol. An addition of CuCl2 to the solution containing
suspended Cu/TiO2 catalyst can produce CuCl/Cu/TiO2 which further catalytically
enhanced the reduction of CO2 and CH3OH, producing CH4 and increasing its yield.
The determining step for the production of methane is the additions of Na2CO3 and
CuCl2 in the aqueous solution. The orders of concentrations of Na2CO3 enhancing the
methane yields were 0.1 > 0.2 > 0.3 > 0.05 M Na2CO3 at 0.1 M CuCl2. The results
obtained from this study are useful for better understanding the UV/photocatlyst process
for the reuse of CO2. The technology for the reduction of CO2 to useful chemicals
elucidated by this study is environmentally favorable.

Abstract 
 
An organically modified 
carbon-nano-tube (CNT) was compounded 
with Polyamide 66(PA66) in a twin-screw 
extruder. The PA66-CNT nanocomposites 
were then microcellular injection molded. 
The effect of CNT content on the mechanical 
and thermal properties was investigated. The 
results showed that when the CNT content 
was 4 wt%, the nanocomposites have 
maximum tensile strength and wear resistance. 
The addition of CNT also increased the 
thermal stability of the nanocomposites. 
Keywords:Compound, Carbon-Nano-Tube, 
Nanocomposites, Twin-screw extruder, 
Polyamide 66.

幾年來一種新興的塑膠射出成型技術 。超
臨界微細發泡最早是在 1980 年代由 MIT
的 Nam P. Suh [1,2]教授所研發之技術，後
來美國 Trexel公司將這項新技術商業化並
稱為 Mucell®微細發泡技術，如圖一所示。 
塑膠材料目前已普遍應用於日常生
活上，而其良好的機械、電子及耐腐蝕等
特性[3]，已充分的運用於汽車工業、電子
產業及航空工程。然近年來，塑膠材料更
廣泛添加了奈米黏土、玻璃纖維及碳纖
維，使其成為奈米複合材料[4]，以提昇其
機械性質及熱性質，亦成為塑膠材料新的
研究領域。 
尼龍 66 是尼龍系列產品中開發較早
之產品，其已經廣泛使用於電子、儀器、
儀表等工程零件和消費性產品，因尼龍 66
具有高剛性、耐磨性、抗腐蝕、耐高溫及
成型加工性較佳等優點，所以用量逐年持
續增加，且新的用途正不斷的被開發中。
雖尼龍 66 具有許多優越之性質，然其尺
寸穩定性及電性能較差，故近年，已有將
玻璃纖維、碳纖維和奈米材料加入尼龍 66

Abstract 
 
This study investigated the influence of 
montmorillonite (MMT) content on the 
mechanical/thermal properties of microcellular injection 
molded Polylactide (PLA)/clay nanocomposites. Carbon 
dioxide (CO2) was the blowing agent. The PLA/MMT 
nanocomposites were prepared by twin screw extrusion. 
The results showed that as MMT content is increased, 
tensile strength, impact strength, and cell density 
decrease. This is caused by the speed degradation of PLA 
due to the addition of MMT. MMT decreases the 
crystallization temperature but increases the 
decomposition temperature of the nanocomposites. The 
XRD results showed that the layer spacing of the clay 
increases as  MMT content increases.  
 
Introduction 
 
The environmental impact of petrochemical based 
plastic wastes has become a global concern in recent 
years. As a result, biodegradable materials such as 
polylactide (PLA) have found favor with many end users. 
PLA is a linear aliphatic thermoplastic polyester 
manufactured by the ring opening polymerization of 
lactide. Lactide is a cyclic dimmer prepared by the 
controlled polymerization of lactic acid, which can be 
obtained from the fermentation of sugar feed stocks, corn, 
and similar [1,2]. PLA has high thermal plasticity, 
mechanical properties, and biocompatibility. A promising 
candidate in the field of environmentally sound plastics, 
it is made from renewable agricultural products and is 
biodegradable.  
 
At the same time, polymer-clay nanocomposites 
(PCN) have become a popular area of research. Layered 
materials used in the preparation of PCN, such as 
smectite clays (e.g., montmorillonites, MMT), have 
attracted great interest. Adding a small amount of clay to 
the polymer gives PCN enhanced physical and 
mechanical properties in aspects such as Young’s 
modulus, tensile strength [3], and flame retardancy [4], 
in comparison with neat polymers.  
It is well documented that the improvement of clay 
as a reinforcing agent depending on the degree of clay 
dispersion on polymer matrix. Three major methods can 
be used to produce PCN: melt intercalation, solution 
intercalation, and in-situ polymerization. It is reported 
that in-situ polymerization results in the best dispersion 
in creating an exfoliated layer structure [5]. 
In addition to that, PCN is also applied during the 
foaming process where the clay serves as the nucleation 
agent, leading to a small cell size [6] that is useful in 
acoustic and thermal insulator applications [7]. 
Microcellular foaming blends polymer and supercritical 
fluid, creating millions of microcells whose size is less 
than 100 μ m[8]. M. Okamoto and S. S. Ray [9] 
investigated polylactide nanocomposites foaming with

Characterization 
 
Fig. 1 shows the wide-angle powder XRD patterns 
for raw clay and organoclay.  Fig. 2 shows the 
wide-angle powder XRD patterns for a series of foamed

existed in an intercalated layer structure. For the

Thermal stability 
Fig. 5 shows the TGA curves of the MMT/PLA 
nanocomposites, as well as the neat PLA, under 
atmosphere. In general, one major stage of weight loss 
appears, starting from 300℃, and ending at 600℃. This 
corresponds to the structural decomposition of the 
polymer. For PCN materials [12], clay has been found to 
enhance the thermal stability of the polymer. We found 
that the beginning of thermal decomposition in these 
nanocomposites shifted slightly, to a higher temperature 
than that of neat PLA. We also found from DSC 
measurements that the addition of the organoclay results 
in a decrease in the crystallization temperature, relative 
to neat PLA, as shown in Fig. 6. However, the content of 
the organoclay does not have a major effect on the 
melting temperature, as shown in Fig.7. 
 
Mechanical Properties 
 
The ASTM standards D 638-99 and D 256-99 were used 
as the tensile and impact tests, respectively. The 
relationship between the mechanical properties and the 
clay content of solid and foamed PCN is shown in Figs. 
8-9 respectively. We found that the incorporation of 
MMT into the PLA matrix decreases the tensile strength. 
MMT clay makes the nanocomposites more brittle than 
neat PLA under both solid and foamed molding, and the 
tensile strength of the foamed material is slightly less 
than that of the solid molded material. As the organoclay 
content increases from 0.5 wt % to 2 wt%, the PCN 
stiffness slightly decreases, compared with pure PLA. 
However, if the organoclay content is larger than 3 wt%, 
the PCN stiffness is slightly larger than that of pure PLA.

Most of the polymer-nanocomposite foaming

which have no commercial value (no mass production). In

Experimental work 
Materials 
 
MMT with a cation-exchange capacity of 94 
mequiv/100g was used as the clay, while 
Cetyltrimethyl-ammonium bromide (CTAB) (Aldrich, 
99.0 %) was used as an organic modifier of MMT. Pure 
PLA, NCP 005, was supplied by Nature Works and 
PLA/MMT nanocomposites were prepared by twin screw 
extruder (KTX-30, Cobel Co., Japan). The mixing ratios 
of the MMT/PLA were 0 wt%, 0.5 wt%, 1 wt%, 2 wt%, 3 
wt%, and 5 wt% respectively.. 
 
Injection Molding Machine 
 
The injection-molding machine used was the 
Arburg 420C Allrounder 1000-350, equipped with Mucell 
capability. Carbon dioxide was used as the gas source. In 
this study, process conditions were kept constant in order 
to examine the effect of the organoclay content on the 
mechanical and thermal properties. A mold of ASTM 
standard tensile and impact sample was used in this study.   
 
Instrumentations 
 
An SEM was used to observe the morphology of 
the cell structure in the foamed PCN material. The cell 
structure in the SEM image was taken on a JEOL 
JSM6360. Specimens were cut into smaller pieces and 
gold was sputtered onto the surface. They were then 
inspected using the SEM. The samples for TEM study 
were prepared by placing the bulk of the PCN materials 
into epoxy resin capsules, followed by curing the epoxy 
resin at 100 °C for 24 h in a vacuum oven. The cured 
epoxy resin containing PCN materials was microtomed 
with a Reichert-Jumg Ultracut-E into slices 60~90 nm 
thick. Subsequently, one layer of carbon about 10 nm 
thick was deposited on these slices on 300-mesh copper 
nets for TEM observations on a JEOL JEM-2010 with an 
acceleration voltage of 200 kV. Wide-angle X-ray 
diffraction study of the samples was performed on a 
Panalytical X'Pert Pro X-ray diffractometer with copper 
target and Ni filter at a scanning rate of 2°/min.

PLA and PCN materials were microcellular injection

results were obtained by W. Zheng and C. Park [14] using 
batch foaming with nylon PCN. This is in contrast to most 
previous foaming results, where well dispersed clay 
particles in polymer nanocomposites were shown to have 
smaller cell sizes than neat polymer [6,15].  
Pure PLA foam had the smallest cell size of the 6 
batch foams studied here, on the order of 33μm, as 
shown in Fig. 11. Foamed cell size was approximately 52
μm for the PLA nanocomposite foam with 0.5 wt% 
organoclay under identical process conditions. As the 
organoclay content in the PLA nanocomposites increased, 
the cell size in the nanocomposite foams also increased. 
Fig. 11 also demonstrates the foam cell density changes 
with pure PLA and its nanocomposites at different 
organoclay contents. Due to the inferior melt strength of 
the PLA, cell coalescence happened in the foaming 
process, increasing the cell size and reducing the cell 
density. Fig. 12 shows the weight reduction changes of the 
PLA and its nanocomposite foams at different organoclay 
contents. Pure PLA foam had the highest weight reduction, 
40%. The weight reduction of the nanocomposite foams 
decreased with the addition of organoclay.  
 
 
Conclusions 
 
 In this study, a series of PCN materials consisting of 
commercial PLA and organoclay were successfully 
prepared using the melt intercalation method. The 
as-prepared PCN materials were then subjected to 
microcellular and conventional injection molding. The 
samples were characterized by XRD and TEM. We found 
that addition of organoclay to the PLA resulted in a 
decrease in mechanical strength and an increase in 
thermal stability, based on the studies of tensile, TGA, and 
DSC tests. Furthermore, the incorporation of organoclay 
into the PLA matrix showed an increase of cell size based 
on the cell morphology measurement.

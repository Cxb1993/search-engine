Abstract  
Keyword: supercapacitors of next generation, 
transition metal oxides, graphene, carbon nanotubes, 
battery-type electrode materials, nanostructures. 
This project develops and integrates the key 
technologies for supercapacitors of next generation. 
The key technologies being developed and integrated 
include: synthesis and characterization of nano- 
structured materials of high specific capacitance and 
power density as well as asymmetric supercapacitors, 
beneficial to capacitors and relative industries. 
According to E = CV2/2, enhancing the specific 
capacitance (i.e., C) and increasing the operation 
voltage of devices (i.e., V) by using organic electrolytes 
or asymmetric type supercapacitors are the main routes 
in promoting the energy density of supercapacitors. 
Based on the above understanding, two main subjects 
are studied in Prof. Hu’s group. (1) Pseudocapacitive, 
nanoporous oxides and composites are prepared by 
several routes, such as electrodeposition, sol-gel, 
aero-gel, hydrothermal and microwave-assisted 
hydrothermal techniques, for promoting the energy and 
power densities. This subproject focuses on 
developing/improving new/existing techniques for 
synthesizing mesoporous oxides for promoting the 
utilization of active materials. The microstructures, 
compositions, and porosity, hydrophilic properties, and 
capacitive properties of oxides and composites born 
from aqueous and nonaqueous media are systematically 
compared. (2) Newly designed mesoporous 
carbon-oxide composites are synthesized through 
self-assembly of tri-block copolymers and huge 
bio-molecules for pore size control, especially focusing 
on tailoring hierarchical structures for enhancing the 
specific capacitance, electronic conductivity, and 
electrolyte penetration rate of composites.  
Two series of carbon-based materials/composites 
are developed by Prof. Teng’s group. (1) The highly 
porous carbon materials are modified with 
O-/N-containing functional groups for enhancing 
energy and power densities. To reduce the electron 
resistance, porous carbons are grown with CNTs for 
promoting the power density. Under the protection of 
CNTs, the energy and power densities can be increased 
by surface modifications. The electrochemical and 
capacitive properties of these specially designed porous 
carbon structures are examined in organic, gel, and 
solid-state electrolytes. (2) New-type graphene-based 
composite electrodes are prepared for supercapacitors 
because the specific surface area of graphene is highest 
among all carbon materials. Graphene are produced by 
an efficient exfoliation technique, which are 
impregnated with surface functional groups and metal 
oxides to utilize both double-layer capacitance and 
pseudocapacitance. Since the power density of 
graphene-based electrodes is strongly dependent on the 
electron density and penetration rate of ions among 
graphene sheets, intercalation of polymers among 
graphene sheets is believed to promote the graphene 
utilization and ion-transport rate for constructing 
advanced, dry-type supercapacitors. 
Two main ideas are tested by Prof. Wu’s group. (1) 
Synthesis and characterization of mesoporous oxide 
nanocrystals of Li-ion cathodes are performed for 
hybrid supercapacitors in aqueous electrolytes, showing 
the promising increase in the energy density (>50%). 
Due to the high solubility and excellent ionic 
conductivity of Li ions in aqueous solutions (ca. 5N), 
Li-ion based systems are suitable for ultrahigh power 
applications. Since the morphologies and micro- 
structures of commercial Li-ion-based oxides cannot 
reach the requirements of high-rate performance and 
good cycle-life, new oxide-carbon composites prepared 
by aqueous chemical synthesis processes are developed 
for high-rate applications with a long cycle life. (2) 
Synthesis and characterization of redox 
"pseudocapacitive" materials for organic electrolytes 
are investigated because of the higher specific 
capacitance in comparison with EDLCs. However, 
most active materials are suitable for aqueous 
electrolytes with the exception of spinel MnFe2O4. The 
operation voltage can be extended from 1.2 to 3 V, 
resulting in that the energy density obtained in the 
organic electrolytes is several times of that measured in

Kuezma reports silicate cathode materials: 
- able to provide 2 electrons providing high capacity 
- theoretical capacity 167.5 mah/g for one electron and 335 mAh/g for two 
- agglomeration appears to be the limiting factor for low capacity  
- introducing C in the precursor can help to reduce grain growth 
- still Li2MnSiO4 gives 120 mAh/g and Li2FeSiO4 <50 mAh/g 
 
Prof Huang reports the formation of Polyanilin coated LiFePO4 with ammonium persulfate as 
dopant. 
- the An:APS ratio is critical in affecting the performance of the composite 
- the acid used in particle preparation also affects the performance; HCl, H3PO4: enhance; 
H2SO4 reduces. 
 
Prof. Mitra: discuss replacement of intercalation reaction with conversion reation material 
- two problems: large 1st cycle irreversible capacity; large polarization 
- regarding reducing polarization: design new materials with weakened M-X (i.e., M-O) bond, 
which is believed to have to do with the polarization 
- using the nanostructured materials does not solve the polarizationproblem, meaning that it is 
not due to transport resistance 
 
Dr. Li Hong: formation of spherical MnO 
- use of RuO2 which undergoes conversion reaction at 0.8 V as a test vehicle to see the effect of 
formation of SEI on polarization and 1st irreversible capacity. The results indicate that 
polarization prevails. Also, the high ionic and electronic conductivities of this material do not 
play a role either. 
- Adding small amount of Ir in IrO to increase the oxygen deficiency was found to increase the 
coulombic efficiency dramatically to near 100%- oxygen deficiency appears to be critical 
 
Dr. Lou nanomaterials for energy materials 
- mesoporous TiO2 plates works well to give high power capability 
- TiO2 single crystals having different morpjologies, i.e., different dominant surface planes, 
shows different capacity at high rates, suggesting different Li insertion kinetics.

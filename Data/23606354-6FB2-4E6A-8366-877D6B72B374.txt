之 Ga2O3 及 Ga 摻雜、Ge 摻雜、Cu 摻雜之

Abstract 
The Ga2O3- and In2O3-based 
(GaxIn1-x)2O3 NWs, i.e., In-doped Ga2O3 and 
Ga-doped In2O3 NWs were grown on Si and 
Au-coated Si substrates at 650-800°C by 
modulating the amount of water vapor in 
flowing Ar via carbothermal reduction of 
Ga2O3/In2O3 powders with a fixed weight 
ratio. Meanwhile, the growth of Ge- and 
Cu-doped In2O3 NWs was also studied. The 
growth of In-doped Ga2O3 NWs was 
enhanced by the Au catalyst, while on 
introducing water vapor into flowing Ar the 
Ga-doped In2O3 NWs became dominant. The 
Ga concentration in In2O3 NWs decreased 
with the amount of water vapor. The growth 
of In-doped Ga2O3, Ga-, Ge-, and Cu-doped 
In2O3 NWs was governed by the 
vapor-liquid-solid process. The In doping 
induced a redshift in the photoluminescence 
(PL) emission and optical bandgap of Ga2O3 
NWs. The Ga and Cu doping yielded a 
redshift in the PL and optical bandgap of 
In2O3 NWs, while the Ge doping induced a 
blueshift in that of In2O3 NWs. This 
approach offers a promising method to 
synthesize In-doped Ga2O3 and Ga-, Ge-, 
and Cu-doped In2O3 NWs for applications in 
optoelectronic nanodevices. 
 
Key word: Ambient-controlled growth、PL 
and energy bangap 、 In-doped Ga2O3

nanowires

 1. Introduction 
Thermoelectric materials have become more important in recent year for applications in power 
generation and electronic cooling. The quest for new thermoelectric materials with high performance is 
still ongoing. Recently, the chalcopyrite-like compounds such as Cu2ZnSnSe4 (CZTSe) [1,2], 
Cu2CdSnSe4 (CCTSe) [3-5], and Cu2SnSe3 [6] have shown reasonable thermoelectric properties at 
medium temperatures. Meanwhile, the CZTSe is also one of the most promising alternatives to 
conventional absorber layer in thin film solar cells. For the fabrication of thin film solar cells and 
thermoelectric devices, there is a great demand for the development of deposition techniques. The 
nanocrystals of CuInSe2 and Cu(InxGa1-x)Se2 synthesized by the solvothermal method have been used 
in the form of a nanocrystal ink and then painted on a substrate to make devices without the 
post-deposition annealing at a high temperature in selenium atmosphere [7]. The non-vacuum 
particle-based deposition has the advantages of low cost and simple processing compared with the 
vacuum-based deposition.  
Few studies about the synthesis of CZTSe and CCTSe nanocrystals by various solvothermal routes 
were reported [4,5,8-13]. Among these approaches, the synthesis of nanocrystals in an autoclave is a 
low temperature and relatively simple processing, but their quality is still a challenge to overcome 
[10-12]. Hydrazine as a reactive solvent can break the extended metal chalcogenide system into a 
lower-dimensional array of metal chalcogenide anions accompanied by small and volatile hydrazinium 
at 3500 rpm for 3 min and then washed with distilled water and ethanol several times to remove the 
dissoluble by-product, and finally dried at about 45˚C. Thin films (1-2 μm) of nanocrystals were 
deposited onto the glass substrates by spin coating of a hexanethiol dispersion at 2000 rpm with the 
nanocrystal concentration of 100 mg/10 ml.  
The microstructure and chemical compositions of samples were observed using scanning electron 
microscopy (SEM) and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS)/SEM. The phases in the samples were 
analyzed using the X-ray diffraction (XRD) method. The Raman spectra of samples were measured at 
room temperature using a laser with the wavelength of 532 nm. The spectral resolution of the 
spectrometer was about 3 cm-1. The optical properties of samples were characterized using UV-vis 
absorption spectroscopy. 
3. Results and discussion 
3.1. Effects of hydrazine on the growth of CZTSe and CCTSe nanocrystals 
As shown in Fig. 1, the XRD peaks of the sample synthesized in the hydrazine-added solution at 
190˚C for 24 h are consistent with those of CZTSe (JCPDS 52-0868), while those of the sample 
synthesized in the hydrazine-free solution at 190˚C for 24 h comprise CZTSe and other unknown 
phases. It is known that the XRD pattern of CZTSe is compatible with those of Cu2SnSe3 (CTSe) 
(JCPDS 72-8034), Cu2Se (JCPDS 88-2043), and ZnSe (JCPDS 37-1463). Therefore, the Raman 
spectroscopy analysis was also carried out to clarify the structure of the samples. In Fig. 2(a), the 
Raman spectrum of the sample synthesized in the hydrazine-added solution at 190˚C for 24 h shows 
24-72 h in an autoclave, pure CZTSe and CCTSe nanocrystals could be readily acquired in the 
hydrazine-added solution, whereas in the hydrazine-free solution the intermediate products such as 
ZnSe, CuSe, Cu2Se, and Cu2SnSe3 for CZTSe nanocrystals and CdSe and Cu2SnSe3 for CCTSe 
nanocrystals were observed respectively. It is conceived that the introduction of hydrazine into the 
ethylenediamine solvent can dismantle the extended framework of the binary and ternary metal 
chalcogenides formed in the initial reactions into a lower-dimensional structure of metal chalcogenide 
anions accompanied by small and volatile hydrazinium cations [14-16]. On heating at 190˚C, the 
lower-dimensional structure allows various metal chalcogenide species to readily react with each other 
and thus enhances the final formation of quaternary CZTSe and CCTSe nanocrystals.  
3.2. Microstructures and UV-vis spectra of as-deposited CZTSe and CCTSe films 
The CZTSe and CCTSe films deposited at room temperature by spin-coating without further 
annealing at a higher temperature were smooth. One example is shown in Fig. 4, where most 
nanocrystals are about 20-600 nm in size and the film is about 2.5 μm in thickness. The plots of (αhν)2, 
the square of the absorption coefficient (α) multiplied by the photon energy (hν), versus photon energy 
for these films are shown in Fig. 5. The band gaps of CZTSe and CCTSe films were determined to be 
about 1.2 and 1.0 eV, respectively, which are in agreement with the reported values [3,9,13,21,22].

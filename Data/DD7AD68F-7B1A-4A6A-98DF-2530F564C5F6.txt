（英文）In this project, we intend to employ the sputter deposition 
method to prepare barrierless Cu alloy thin films. We will study the 
effect of the addition of principal alloying elements during Cu 
metallization under different preparation conditions (isothermal 
annealing and cyclic annealing). In particular, we will study the effect 
of Cu alloying elements as a suppressor and on the high thermal 
stability of silicon bronze in addition to the effect of alloying elements 
on the cross-sectional microstructure, crystallinity, and adhesive 
properties of the Cu alloy thin films. Using these results, we can 
explore the possibility of using a Cu alloy thin film as a low-resistance 
material that facilitates barrierless Cu metallization.

High-thermal-stability copper alloy film as buffer layer for realizing stable 
flip-chip solder joints in barrierless metallization of microelectronic devices  
 
中文摘要： 
本研究係於矽上藉由共濺鍍技術於 Ar/N2 雰圍下製備具高溫穩定性之 Cu(TiW)與
Cu(TiWNx)銅種仔層。Cu(TiW)與 Cu(TiWNx)薄膜分別經 600°C 與 700°C 退火後一小時後並
無觀得有銅矽化合物生成於 Cu–Si 界面。含微量 TiWNx 之銅薄膜高溫穩定性可達 700°C，且
具低電阻與漏電流，可應用為未來之先進無阻障層銅金屬化製程。高溫穩定性藉由 XRD、
FIB 與 TEM 評估，並證實 Cu(TiWNx)薄膜經 700°C 退火一小時後於 Cu 薄膜與矽間並無反應，
Cu(TiW)與 Cu(TiWNx)薄膜分別經 600 與 700°C 退火後之電阻值分別為 3.19 與 2.02 µΩ-cm。 
進一步將其用為緩衝層，可用於減緩 Cu-Sn 間之交互作用。 
關鍵詞：銅種仔層，半導體線材，無阻障金屬化製程，緩衝層 
 
英文摘要： 
This study reports the superior properties of TiWNx-bearing Cu films deposited by 
co-sputtering for barrierless Si metallization. The Cu alloy films also have the potential to replace 
both the wetting layer and the diffusion barrier layer in the conventional under bump metallurgy of 
flip-chip solder joints, which would provide a metal layer that can serve more than one function and, 
hence, reduce costs. Cu(TiWNx) film exhibits excellent thermal stability and adhesion to barrierless 
Si, with no detectable Cu/Si interfacial interactions after annealing at 700°C for 1 h. This suggests 
that the Cu(TiWNx) film is inherently a very stable material. Furthermore, experimental 
characterizations show that Cu(TiWNx) exhibits solderability comparable to that of pure Cu. Most 
importantly, during evaluation on Sn/Cu(TiWNx)/Si structure at 200°C, Cu(TiWNx) showed a 
dissolution rate at least one order of magnitude slower than that of pure Cu. In fact, this 
consumption rate was even comparable to that of Ni, a diffusion barrier material commonly used in 
solder joints. Hence, Cu(TiWNx) is a promising material for use in both barrierless Si metallization 
and solder bump flip chip applications. 
Keywords: Cu Seed Layer, Liner Material, Barrierless Metallization, Buffer Layer

3. Results and Discussion 
I. Fabrication and analysis of Cu(TiWNx) film 
 Figure 1 shows the XRD pattern of the as-deposited Cu(TiWNx) film; this pattern confirmed the 
presence of a WN phase (JCPDS 25-1256) and TiN phase (JCPDS 87-0633). Figure 2 shows the XPS spectra 
of an unannealed Cu(TiWNx) thin film obtained after 20 min of sputter etching. The elemental scan of Cu 2p3 
revealed that there was no change in the binding energy of pure Cu (Fig. 2(a)). The W 4f (Fig. 2(b)), Ti 2p 
(Fig. 2(c)), and N 1s (Fig. 2(d)) spectra show the presence of a bond between Ti, W, and N in the TiWNx 
phase. Thus, the combination of the XRD pattern and XPS spectra confirmed the presence of the WNx and 
TiNx phases.

The resistivities measured for the Cu(TiWNx) films deposited on barrier-free Si substrates before and 
after the annealing are shown in Fig. 3(a). We clearly observe that the resistivity of the Cu(TiWNx) films 
before annealing was higher (~39.46 µΩ-cm) than that after annealing because of the formation of the WNx 
and TiNx phases. After annealing, the resistivity decreased noticeably because of grain growth. When the Cu 
films were annealed at 400°C, the resistivities of the Cu(TiWNx) and Cu(TiW) films continued to decrease 
with an increase in the temperature; the resistivities were ~2.53–3.32 µΩ-cm at 600°C and ~3.86–4.25 µΩ-cm 
at 530°C for isothermal annealing and cyclic annealing, respectively. The resistivities, however, increased 
abruptly once the annealing temperature exceeded the aforementioned temperatures, as shown in Fig. 3(a),

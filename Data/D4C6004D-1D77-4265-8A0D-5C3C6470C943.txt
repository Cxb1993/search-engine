一、前言  
我國的風力規範：「建築物耐風設計規範及解說」，對於建築物設計風力有重要影
響的參數可分為三大類：設計風速及風場特性，風壓（風力）係數，陣風反應因
子（順風向風力）或是風力頻譜（橫風向與扭轉向風力）。其中影響最為重要，
且具有本土特性的是設計風速及風場特性。其中設計風速是根據全國各氣象站的
多年風速記錄，經由極值統計分析所得。所謂風場特性則是包括了在不同地況條
件下，平均風速在高度上的分佈以及紊流特性。由於我國缺乏相關資料，風力規
範中所描述的的風場特性是依據 ASCE-7 的相關規定修訂而成。然而，我國的設
計風速主要受到颱風的影響，與美國的大陸型氣候不盡相同，台灣地狹人稠的社
會發展型態與地廣人稀的美國都市鄉鎮型態亦不盡相同，直接採用美國的風場特
性，未必能夠正確的反應本土的風場特性。因此進行相關研究，建立本土的自然
風場數據做為未來風力規範的修訂依據，實有其必要性。 
 自然風場特性的研究方法不外乎實場監測、風洞模擬與數值模擬，三種
研究方法各有其優缺點。實場監測的數據最為真實可信，然而確有設備昂貴、設
點不易、量測困難、強風難求等諸多難處。風洞縮尺實驗最為便捷，然而僅採用
風洞試驗進行自然風場之模擬亦有不及之處。一般風洞實驗室模擬已知風場之特
性，多利用人造物之不同組合以製造出所需之風場。然而對於未知風場特性時，
則需使風洞發展段之氣流自行通過足夠長度之真實地形地物來產生一合理之邊
界層。在數值模擬方面，現階段以 CFD 數值模擬根據實際地況發展紊流邊界層
特性仍存在極大困難，然而以風工程之發展潛能為出發點，本計畫仍以 CFD 數
值模擬作為研究風法之一。 
本計畫希望經由三年系統化的實場量測、風洞模擬與數值模擬，以及三者之
間的交叉比對驗證，達成下列目標： 
1. 建立研究自然風場的系統性方法； 
2. 取得台灣地區在颱風與常年季風作用時，在海岸線、鄉村開闊地況、鄉鎮地
況與都會地區等自然風場特性之相關數據。 
 
 
風洞試驗預計使用淡江大學一號風洞實驗室，該實驗室適合執行本計劃之試
驗段長約 10 公尺~12 公尺。本案目前預計以 1/400~1/500 之長度縮尺進行實場地
形地物之模擬，預計可形成相對於實場約 4~6 公里之發展段長度。該部分之風洞
模擬，儘量藉由非自然地形之人造物來強制產生流場之改變，而是根據所選定之
實場量測區域之實際地形地物所製作之縮尺模形，自然發展出邊界層。亦即在提
供足夠長度之試驗段而使氣流與地表粗糙物充分作用下，讓該紊流邊界層能得到
完全之發展。風洞實驗資料與實場監測數據之比較，鑒於本案實場量測設備(Lidar)
僅能提供平均風速剖面，故將以此做為二者數據比對之標地。預期透過該項風洞
實驗與實場數據之比對，能確認出本案使用之風洞實驗室，於模擬大氣邊界層風
場特性時，所需具備之相關條件，以做為後續風洞實驗之標準。 
風洞試驗預計於淡江大學一號風洞實驗室執行，實驗室可供執行本計劃之試
驗段長約 12 公尺~14 公尺。實驗將依據前述實場地況風洞模擬之結論，遵循能
準確模擬自然風場特性之條件，設計可行之實驗縮尺與上游地況模擬範圍。製造
邊界層所需之地表粗糙物，則根據建築物高度與分布密度二種參數進行製作。

 六、結論 
本研究完成在符合我國風力規範所定義之都市（地況 A）、鄉鎮（地況 B）以及
平坦開闊（地況 C）等三種標準地況風場的真實地形風洞縮尺模擬，風洞所得之
平均風速剖面與實場監測數據相當吻合，本文並量測包括紊流強度與紊流尺度的
高度分佈以及紊流頻譜等風場紊流特性。惟限於可行的風洞縮尺以及上游發展段
長度，模擬都市（地況 A）與鄉鎮（地況 B）的風場時，必須依賴入流處的錐形
擾流板方能得到足夠的邊界層厚度；只有平坦開闊（地況 C）才能完全以地形縮
尺得到完整的邊界層厚度。

 LIDAR is a meteorology instrument available in recent years. The principle of LIDAR is 
similar to weather radar. The differences are LIDAR use the shorter electromagnetic wave in 
wavelength and get the information via the scattering of the aerosol in the atmosphere. Less op-
eration noise, safer electromagnetic waves and better portability are the advantages of LIDAR. 
At the same time, the shorter wavelength means much more attenuation especially met the thick 
and moist atmosphere. Although LIDAR has weakness in some specific weather condition, it is 
one of the better remote sensing instrumentation for the measurements of low-level atmospheric 
data. 
2 LIDAR SPECIFICATIONS, MEASUREMETN SCHEME AND DATA ANALYSIS 
 
The Mitsubishi model LR-08FS  Ⅲ LIDAR was used in this project. The wavelength is 1.5 
to 1.6 micrometer; the pulse width is 200 nanosecond; the spatial resolution is 30 meter; the 
possible observation rage is from 30 to 600 meter above the ground; the maximum radial wind 
velocity is 30 m/s; in theory, the maximum horizontal wind velocity could reach 173 m/s; the 
maximum wedge speed is 20 degree per second, 18 second to make a full circle.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
LIDAR measure the movement of aerosol to calculate the wind field data. In order to com-
pute the wind field on each layer, the VAD (Velocity-Azimuth Display) method was used. The 
diagram of VAD (Browning and Wexler, 1968) is shown in Figure 1. The center on the ground is 
the location of LIDAR. LIDAR system emits laser beams in a circular motion at constant wedge 
speed with elevation angle α. An imaginary horizontal circle with radius r can be established at 
various altitudes. Since LIDAR measures the radial wind velocity Vr only, the horizontal and 
vertical wind speed at the center of the imaginary circle were decomposed from the radial wind 
speed through VAD method. It is necessary to use the full circle measurement data to estimate an 
accurate mean wind speed at center, LIDAR gives estimated wind speed output at 18 seconds in-
terval. Therefore, it cannot produce turbulence feature in the current data processing scheme. 
During field measurement, the LIDAR unit will automatically discard those data cannot pass 
the preset S/N threshold, i.e., normally there will be less valid wind speed data in higher altitude. 
It makes judgment of gradient height more difficult. The first step of the data analysis is to calcu-

4 WIND PROFILES OF MONSOON WIND 
 
4.1 Wind profile at urban terrain 
During the last two monsoon season, form 2009/12 to 2011/4, several wind profile measure-
ments were taken at a monitoring site with urban environment. This monitoring site locates in a 
residential-commercial mixed region. Most surrounding buildings are 4 to 7 stories residential 
apartment buildings, with some 10 to 12 story commercial buildings and very few higher build-
ings. In the wet monsoon weather condition, the LIDAR measurement range (with acceptable 
S/N ratio) is no more than 420m. For example, shown in Figure 4 is the 10-minute mean wind 
speed and wind direction at of the 2010/4/13 measurement. Based on the S/N ratio report, it was 
determined the highest meaningful data is at 390m, and only those data are presented. The wind 
direction data was used to ensure the wind records are subjected to the similar terrain effects. 
Shown in Figure 5 is the hourly mean wind speed profile normalized with respect to the wind 
speed at 150m, U(z = 150m). Total of 24 one-hour wind records were taken for the wind profile 
analysis. Mean and standard deviation for power law index α were found to be 0.281 and 0.035, 
respectively. Mean and standard deviation for the surface roughness length were found to be 
1.29m and 0.77m, respectively. At this monitoring site, the measurement heights were always 
less than the gradient height. The only conclusion can be reached is that, for this terrain category, 
boundary layer thickness is greater than 420m.

6 CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK 
The LIDAR system was introduced for the field monitoring of wind profiles at sites in urban, 
suburban and open terrains. The initial results indicate that LIDAR can produce mean wind 
speed profile up to 300 to 400 meters in rainy and windy weather. Several measurements had 
been taken during the monsoon season. Mean values for power law index, α, were found to be 
0.28, 0.22 and 0.15 for monitoring site A (urban), site B (suburban) and site C (open country), 
respectively. Surface roughness length, z0, were found to be 1.29m, 0.58m and 0.22m for urban, 
suburban and open country, respectively. Only two measurements had been made during ty-
phoon. In one of the typhoon measurement taken at site C, the wind speed profile is similar to, 
but slightly more smooth and shallower than, the wind profile of monsoon. The typhoon data is 
not conclusive yet. More measurements will be taken in the future.  
 Since during in drizzling weather, LIDAR can only produce reliable mean wind speed up to 
300-400m. The gradient heights of suburban and urban terrain are still out of reach in most of 
cases. In order to capture the entire features of the atmospheric boundary layers at the designate 
sites, two additional works are currently undergoing. The first one is installing ultrasonic ane-
mometers at building top near the current monitoring sites to measure the atmospheric turbu-
lence. The second one is to perform scale-down wind tunnel simulation of the turbulence boun-
dary layers of the three monitoring sites.

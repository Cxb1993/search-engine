摘 要 
雪山隧道為臺灣及東南亞第一長公路隧道，全長約 12.9 公里。通車後大幅
縮短臺北與宜蘭兩地之往返時間，帶來旅運上之便利；但同時也帶來移動車輛數
目和污染源增加，且可能經長隧道累積，污染物濃度更為提升。 
本研究於雪山隧道南下及北上車道及排氣豎井口進行採樣，其中第一時段
為上午(8:00 − 10:00)、第二時段為中午(12:00 − 14:00)、第三時段下午(16:00 − 
18:00)，分析 56 種揮發性有機物。隧道量測中南下出口濃度最高之物種為乙烯
113.03 ppb，其次分別為異戊烷 45.6 ppb、乙炔 44.88 ppb、丙烯 42.49 ppb及甲苯
35.30 ppb；北上出口濃度最高之物種為乙烯 93.07 ppb，其次分別為異戊烷 50.19 
ppb、丙烯 44.09 ppb、乙炔 39.38 ppb及甲苯 32.18 ppb。隧道中有機物成分區分
後南下北上路段近似，百分比由大至小順序為烷類(36.69% − 39.20%)、芳香族類
(34.14% − 36.33%)、烯類(20.27% − 21.95%)、炔類(3.35% − 4.11%)及環烷類(1.06% 
− 1.35%)。

mg/veh-km)、甲苯(5.32 ± 2.39 mg/veh-km)、丙烯(3.55 ± 1.67 mg/veh-km)以及間,

長隧道之污染物傳輸路徑與對人體之健康影響等各方面與一般道路不同，隧
道內之污染物在半封閉空間中擴散路徑易受四周牆壁之限制，使不易向三維方向
擴散，如在通風系統運轉不如預期之情況下，又受到活塞效應之影響，則會導致
污染物向車行方向擴散並累積，尤其是累積於抽風井及兩端出口處。因此，隧道
愈長，污染物累積於隧道內之時間相對提高，則可能對車行狀況不佳時之乘客健
康風險造成危害。另外，隧道之豎井通風系統亦會將移動源之污染物排放至周界
大氣中，經擴散使得當地之居民在長期暴露下，亦可能對健康造成致癌風險，不
能輕忽之。因此，國內在長隧道陸續通車後，已有學者發表隧道內污染物調查之
相關研究 (林順信等，2006；顏有利等，2006)，但若能將隧道內污染物及排放
豎井口之排放一併調查，未來在降低國內民眾對於長隧道健康安全之疑慮方面，
可有更完備之研究結果可參考。 
移動源之排放係數之推估分成三類：(1)動力計；(2)隧道內；(3)道路旁。動
力計法主要是作為法規標準為目的，建立單一排放標準，並且評估不同引擎設計
或燃料組成不同，污染物排放率所受到的影 
響。其缺點是無法獲得真實路上車輛排放，舉例來說，動力計無法計算車輛組成
的變化或行駛時蒸發量 (Na, 2006)。動力計法無法描述一群不同車齡、里程數、
引勤替換或維修、排氣混合狀態下的車輛排放係數，因此無法有效的評估車輛在
真實交通狀態下排放污染物的程度  (Chiang et al., 2007)。隧道空氣品質量測的
優點是採樣含蓋了在正常操作條件下大範圍的車種，隧道量測可以提供較具代表
性的樣本，且包含車輛通過隧道期間尾氣排放及非尾氣排放 (McGaughey et al., 
2004；Na, 2006)。缺點是受到周界大氣中的影響（移動車輛活塞效應所帶入的周
界空氣）所測得的揮發性有機物(VOC)可能是周界與隧道內揮發性有機物的混
合，造成揮發性有機物濃度誤判。但相較於道路邊的量測所受到的影響較小 (Na, 
2006)。由於隧道屬半封閉空間且移動源亦以真實情況行駛於道路上，碳氫化合
物(hydrocarbon)的物種較不受到光化降解影響，所獲得的物種可以用來評估尾氣
排放及揮發排放的組成 (McGaughey et al., 2004)。故三者中以隧道內之量測最具
代表性。 
因各隧道之特性與車速等因子亦會對排放係數產生差異，Pierson et al. (1996)
在 Tuscarora隧道研究指出，輕型車在上坡路段污染物平均排放係數是下坡的 1.6
 16

5.4  受體點空氣污染物分布特性 
為瞭解雪山隧道鄰近地區空氣污染物之分布特性，以及其與雪山隧道內移動
車輛尾氣排放之關聯性，本研究選定於靠近隧道南邊出口處之頭城工務段管理中
心與靠近北邊出口處之坪林行控中心進行同步採樣分析，其空氣污染物之濃度變
化和分布情形於下列各節進行討論。 
5.4.1採樣日氣象概況 
由於本研究之兩處受體點(頭城工務段管理中心與坪林行控中心)，受雪山山
脈分隔所影響，南邊受體點(頭城)以宜蘭測候站之氣象資料為主，北邊受體點(坪
林)則是以坪林行控中心自行量測之氣象資料為主。圖 5.4−1至圖 5.4−3為受體點
採樣日(2008 年 11月 14 − 16日)之中央氣象局地面天氣圖。由圖可知，採樣期間
臺灣受大陸高壓影響，其天氣型態分類為東北季風型(廖秀怡，2005)。 
由表 5.4−1與圖 5.4−4可知，氣壓以 11月 14日 9時之 1016.5 hPa為最大，
11月 15日 14時之 1011.5 hPa最小，平均氣壓為 1014.1 hPa，一日內氣壓有 2次
波峰。氣溫以 11月 15日 13時之 27.7 ℃為最高，11月 16日 19時之 19.9 ℃為
最低，平均氣溫為 23.0 ℃。前兩日氣溫約從上午 7時開始增加，至中午約 12 − 13
時為最大，之後開始下降，於清晨時段達最低點；而 11月 16日為陰天，導致氣
溫普遍較前兩日低，氣溫最低點也發生於此日。相對溼度與氣溫約略呈相反關
係，日出時降低，日落時增加，以 11月 14日 6時最高，達 86%，11月 15日 12
時最低，為 67%，平均相對溼度為 77%；11月 16日之相對濕度則為全日皆偏高。
日照時數與全天空日射量之趨勢略與氣溫相同，日照時數於 11月 15日之 9.5小
時為最多，11月 16日之 0.2小時為最少；全天空日射量則以 11月 15日 12 − 13
時之 2.40 MJ/m2最大。

9. 雪山隧道臭氧生成潛勢(OFP)於各豎井口受到甲苯濃度影響，在第一豎井及
第二豎井芳香族類最高(46.0% − 68.1%)，其次為烯類(25.9% − 42.6%)及烷類
(7.0% − 10.3%)。芳香族類 OFP百分比較高為受到甲苯濃度影響，於第三豎
井甲苯濃度較低時，OFP最主要貢獻為烯類。 
10. 頭城工務段管理中心與坪林行控中心之大氣採樣結果顯示，CO、NO2、SO2
及 O3濃度皆遠低於空氣品質標準。於隧道南端(頭城)，非假日之 SO2濃度
(7.47 ppb)較週末(4.20 ppb)高，推測是因非假日為工作日，運送貨物之柴油
車較多。隧道北端(坪林)，假日時 NOx 平均濃度(16.88 ppb)明顯高出假日
(10.60 ppb)許多，CO週末平均濃度(0.66 ppm)也略高於假日(0.49 ppm)，但
SO2濃度卻無顯著差異，推估成因為假日時有較多人潮前往坪林觀光旅遊，
但以一般自用小客車數量增加所致。非假日時，南端之空氣污染物濃度皆略
高於北端，但差異不顯著，推測因盛行風向為東北風，污染物多往南邊擴散，
並且頭城附近之車流量較高。而假日時，風向包括東北風、西風與南風，使
得車流量增加所產生影響不明顯，污染物濃度並沒有顯著差異。 
11. 由模擬結果顯示，雪山隧道內移動車輛尾氣排放對於鄰近空氣品質影響不顯
著，惟兩端出口處附近約 200公尺內，CO和 NO2濃度偏高，甚至超過空氣
品質標準。 
12. 本研究所使用之 ADMS-Roads模式，於案例期間(2008 年 11月 14 − 16日)，
車輛尾氣排放物模擬值與採樣值之相關係數(R)介於 0.34 − 0.81之間，於頭
城工務段管理中心(南端)之模擬成效皆優於坪林行控中心(北端)。此結果顯
示模式對於 CO、NO2和 NOx等污染物之模擬成效較佳，可達高度相關(最
高 R=0.81)，相對於 SO2 之模擬成效較差，但亦達中度相關(R=0.34 & 
R=0.57)。而一致性指數(IOA)介於 0.58 − 0.77；正規化均方根誤差(NMSE)
介於 0.03 − 0.25之間，顯示模擬結果良好。

Using ADMS Model for Assessment of Pollutant Dispersion from 
a Long Freeway Tunnel in Taiwan 
 
Paper 2009-A-116-AWMA 
 
Chia-Hsiang Lai*, Pei-Chun Tsai

ABSTRACT 
 
The study was to simulate the pollutant dispersion of vehicular emissions and was compared to CO, 
NOx and SO2 measurements on two sites (Tou-Cheng and Pin-Ling management centers) in 
Northern Taiwan in winter of 2008 by using the Atmospheric Dispersion Modelling System for 
Roads (ADMS-Roads). ADMS-Roads is based on ADMS-Urban and is the most widely used 
advanced dispersion model for calculating NO2 concentrations and allows the user to model 
explicitly defined roads, several point sources and other emissions as volume sources. Data sets are 
used to investigate the performance of ADMS-Roads model and predict hourly pollutant 
concentrations from the traffic exhaust and ventilation systems of the long freeway tunnel. 
Comparisons between simulations and measurements at both sites were satisfactory. Simulated 
values were generally in agreement with measured values, with a correlation coefficient of 
R = 0.67–0.93, and an index of agreement (IOA) = 0.77–0.96. In addition, the modeling results 
could account for the health effect of dwellers. The ADMS-Roads will be applied to assess the 
environmental impact while the tunnel will be allowed more types of vehicles to drive in the future.

Methods of sampling and analysis

Traffic flow measurement 
Three video cameras were mounted at the roadside near both monitoring sites and Hsue-Hshan 
tunnel to record the flow of traffic during the sampling periods. The tape was then replayed on the 
laboratory television to enable the number of each type of vehicle to be counted. Vehicles were 
classified as light-duty gasoline vehicles (LDGV) and heavy-duty diesel vehicles (HDDV). 
ADMS model 
ADMS is a UK regulatory model developed to model the dispersion of buoyant or neutrally 
buoyant particles and gases 11. The model predicts the boundary layer structure using the similarity 
scaling approach in a similar method to Berkowicz et al.12. The model uses an advanced Gaussian 
approach with a normal Gaussian distribution in stable and neutral conditions whilst the vertical 
dispersion is approximated by two different Gaussian distributions in a CBL. Gaussian type models 
are widely used in atmospheric dispersion modeling, in particular for regulatory purposes, and are 
often “nested” within Lagrangian and Eulerian models. 
 148
The ADMS-Roads pollution model is a comprehensive tool for investigating air pollution problems 
due to small networks of roads that may be in combination with industrial sites, for instance small 
towns or rural road networks. ADMS-Roads include buildings downwash algorithms and can model 
the effect of buildings, near wake recirculation and changes in the plume centre line due to 
streamline deflections from the buildings. The required input variables for ADMS-Roads are 
wind-speed and wind-direction as well as the Monin–Obukhov length at a specified location in the 
modeling domain. However, the changing wind flow over complex terrain is calculated using 
FLOWSTAR, an advanced airflow model developed by CERC13. Carruthers et al.14 have shown 
that FLOWSTAR models the flow well between tens of meters up to several kilometers typically 
for gradients between 1 in 2 (upwind slopes and hill summits) and 1 in 3 locally in hill wakes. The 
horizontal layer had a 64 × 64 grid. The entire simulation domain covered over 16 km × 16 km 
(Figure 1). 
The simple chemical scheme in ADMS-Roads uses the reaction rates from generic reaction set of 
equation15. A second NO–NO2 conversion scheme is implemented into ADMS-Roads which would 
also consider ozone reactions. The recommended method for a more detailed modeling of the 
chemical reaction would be the generic reaction set scheme as this is the only way of taking into 
account different background NOx and O3 concentrations. 
Estimation of pollutant emission rate by traffic 
It is known that the quality of fuel affects the emission factors (EFs) of vehicles significantly. The 
pollutant emission levels for an oxidizing catalyst passenger car in service of four to seven years 
usually differ from those of a car in service of one to three years by about 10–20%16. Since the EFs 
of automobiles are limited in Taiwan, the differences of EFs due to the vehicle's age were thus 
neglected, but the corrections of EFs due to the change of pollutant contents, e.g. sulfur (S), in the 
fuel were made. In this work, EFs of CO and NOx given by TANEEB17 were used, while only 
one-fifth of those of SO2 in TANEEB17 were used (Table 1) because the sulfur content in mobile 
fuel was about 0.1 g l−1 in 1991, but about 0.018 g l−1 in 2002, corresponding to a five-fold 
reduction. The speed of eighty and ninety kilometers an hour of pollutant EFs are default value of 
ADMS- Road, except SO2. There are three line sources in this study－two for general road (Pei-Yi 
road and Qing-Yun road) and one for freeway. For EFs setting of ADMS-Road, vehicular speed for 
general road, freeway and inside of Hsue-Hshan tunnel was 50, 90 and 80 km h-1, respectively. 
Therefore, the EFs were further combined to calculate the composite EF to provide inputs to the 
source strength of models.

Using ADMS Model for Assessment of Pollutant Dispersion from 
a Long Freeway Tunnel in Taiwan 
 
Paper 2009-A-116-AWMA 
 
Chia-Hsiang Lai*, Pei-Chun Tsai 
Department of Health Safety and Environmental Engineering, Central Taiwan University of Science 
and Technology, Taichung 40601, Taiwan, ROC.  
Chen-Wen Wang 
Institute of Environmental Engineering, National Sun Yat-Sen University, Kaohsiung 80424, 
Taiwan, ROC. 
 
ABSTRACT 
 
The study was to simulate the pollutant dispersion of vehicular emissions and was compared to CO, 
NOx and SO2 measurements on two sites (Tou-Cheng and Pin-Ling management centers) in 
Northern Taiwan in winter of 2008 by using the Atmospheric Dispersion Modelling System for 
Roads (ADMS-Roads). ADMS-Roads is based on ADMS-Urban and is the most widely used 
advanced dispersion model for calculating NO2 concentrations and allows the user to model 
explicitly defined roads, several point sources and other emissions as volume sources. Data sets are 
used to investigate the performance of ADMS-Roads model and predict hourly pollutant 
concentrations from the traffic exhaust and ventilation systems of the long freeway tunnel. 
Comparisons between simulations and measurements at both sites were satisfactory. Simulated 
values were generally in agreement with measured values, with a correlation coefficient of 
R = 0.67–0.93, and an index of agreement (IOA) = 0.77–0.96. In addition, the modeling results 
could account for the health effect of dwellers. The ADMS-Roads will be applied to assess the 
environmental impact while the tunnel will be allowed more types of vehicles to drive in the future.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: Vehicular Emission, ADMS, Air Dispersion Model, Tunnel

where only ambient conditions were measured; these included concentrations of CO, NOx and SO2.

were many vehicles during holidays. Pin-Ling management center is located at a rural style town 
with few populations and vehicles. 
 
Methods of sampling and analysis 
The Sampling of air quality was performed over 3 days on 14–16 November in 2008 concurrently at 
the two sites. Each day included three 2-h sampling periods－ in the morning (08–10), the 
afternoon (12–14) and the evening (16–18). A total of 36 individual 1-h integrated air samples were 
collected. Two sets of equipment comprising instruments for analyzing CO, NOx and SO2 were 
used at two sampling points. Samplings of gases at two sites were conducted simultaneously. The 
CO was analyzed using a CO monitor (API model 300) with a detection limit of 0.05 ppm, 
according to the infrared absorption principle (US-EPA method 10). The NOx was analyzed using 
an ultra-violet spectral-photometer (API model 100A, US-EPA method 7B), with a detection limit 
of 2.0 ppb. SO2 was analyzed using an ultra-violet spectral-photometer (API model 100B, US-EPA 
method 7B), with a detection limit of 2.0 ppb. The accuracies of all analyses were within 7%.  
Meteorological parameters required for modeling were collected from the Regional Meteorological 
Center of Yi-Lan located at about 20 km away from the monitoring site for the period of 
measurements. The meteorological parameters included wind speed, wind direction, sunshine hours, 
temperature, and humidity, which were processed for the preparation of inputs to the models. 
Traffic flow measurement 
Three video cameras were mounted at the roadside near both monitoring sites and Hsue-Hshan 
tunnel to record the flow of traffic during the sampling periods. The tape was then replayed on the 
laboratory television to enable the number of each type of vehicle to be counted. Vehicles were 
classified as light-duty gasoline vehicles (LDGV) and heavy-duty diesel vehicles (HDDV). 
ADMS model 
ADMS is a UK regulatory model developed to model the dispersion of buoyant or neutrally 
buoyant particles and gases 11. The model predicts the boundary layer structure using the similarity 
scaling approach in a similar method to Berkowicz et al.12. The model uses an advanced Gaussian 
approach with a normal Gaussian distribution in stable and neutral conditions whilst the vertical 
dispersion is approximated by two different Gaussian distributions in a CBL. Gaussian type models 
are widely used in atmospheric dispersion modeling, in particular for regulatory purposes, and are 
often “nested” within Lagrangian and Eulerian models. 
 164
The ADMS-Roads pollution model is a comprehensive tool for investigating air pollution problems 
due to small networks of roads that may be in combination with industrial sites, for instance small 
towns or rural road networks. ADMS-Roads include buildings downwash algorithms and can model 
the effect of buildings, near wake recirculation and changes in the plume centre line due to 
streamline deflections from the buildings. The required input variables for ADMS-Roads are 
wind-speed and wind-direction as well as the Monin–Obukhov length at a specified location in the 
modeling domain. However, the changing wind flow over complex terrain is calculated using 
FLOWSTAR, an advanced airflow model developed by CERC13. Carruthers et al.14 have shown 
that FLOWSTAR models the flow well between tens of meters up to several kilometers typically 
for gradients between 1 in 2 (upwind slopes and hill summits) and 1 in 3 locally in hill wakes. The 
horizontal layer had a 64 × 64 grid. The entire simulation domain covered over 16 km × 16 km 
(Figure 1). 
The simple chemical scheme in ADMS-Roads uses the reaction rates from generic reaction set of 
equation15. A second NO–NO2 conversion scheme is implemented into ADMS-Roads which would 
also consider ozone reactions. The recommended method for a more detailed modeling of the 
chemical reaction would be the generic reaction set scheme as this is the only way of taking into 
account different background NOx and O3 concentrations. 
Estimation of pollutant emission rate by traffic 
It is known that the quality of fuel affects the emission factors (EFs) of vehicles significantly. The 
pollutant emission levels for an oxidizing catalyst passenger car in service of four to seven years 
usually differ from those of a car in service of one to three years by about 10–20%16. Since the EFs 
of automobiles are limited in Taiwan, the differences of EFs due to the vehicle's age were thus 
neglected, but the corrections of EFs due to the change of pollutant contents, e.g. sulfur (S), in the 
fuel were made. In this work, EFs of CO and NOx given by TANEEB17 were used, while only 
one-fifth of those of SO2 in TANEEB17 were used (Table 1) because the sulfur content in mobile 
fuel was about 0.1 g l−1 in 1991, but about 0.018 g l−1 in 2002, corresponding to a five-fold 
reduction. The speed of eighty and ninety kilometers an hour of pollutant EFs are default value of 
ADMS- Road, except SO2. There are three line sources in this study－two for general road (Pei-Yi 
road and Qing-Yun road) and one for freeway. For EFs setting of ADMS-Road, vehicular speed for 
general road, freeway and inside of Hsue-Hshan tunnel was 50, 90 and 80 km h-1, respectively. 
Therefore, the EFs were further combined to calculate the composite EF to provide inputs to the 
source strength of models.

Traffic flow measurement 
Three video cameras were mounted at the roadside near both monitoring sites and Hsue-Hshan 
tunnel to record the flow of traffic during the sampling periods. The tape was then replayed on the 
laboratory television to enable the number of each type of vehicle to be counted. Vehicles were 
classified as light-duty gasoline vehicles (LDGV) and heavy-duty diesel vehicles (HDDV). 
ADMS model 
ADMS is a UK regulatory model developed to model the dispersion of buoyant or neutrally 
buoyant particles and gases 11. The model predicts the boundary layer structure using the similarity 
scaling approach in a similar method to Berkowicz et al.12. The model uses an advanced Gaussian 
approach with a normal Gaussian distribution in stable and neutral conditions whilst the vertical 
dispersion is approximated by two different Gaussian distributions in a CBL. Gaussian type models 
are widely used in atmospheric dispersion modeling, in particular for regulatory purposes, and are 
often “nested” within Lagrangian and Eulerian models. 
 8
The ADMS-Roads pollution model is a comprehensive tool for investigating air pollution problems 
due to small networks of roads that may be in combination with industrial sites, for instance small 
towns or rural road networks. ADMS-Roads include buildings downwash algorithms and can model 
the effect of buildings, near wake recirculation and changes in the plume centre line due to 
streamline deflections from the buildings. The required input variables for ADMS-Roads are 
wind-speed and wind-direction as well as the Monin–Obukhov length at a specified location in the 
modeling domain. However, the changing wind flow over complex terrain is calculated using 
FLOWSTAR, an advanced airflow model developed by CERC13. Carruthers et al.14 have shown 
that FLOWSTAR models the flow well between tens of meters up to several kilometers typically 
for gradients between 1 in 2 (upwind slopes and hill summits) and 1 in 3 locally in hill wakes. The 
horizontal layer had a 64 × 64 grid. The entire simulation domain covered over 16 km × 16 km 
(Figure 1). 
The simple chemical scheme in ADMS-Roads uses the reaction rates from generic reaction set of 
equation15. A second NO–NO2 conversion scheme is implemented into ADMS-Roads which would 
also consider ozone reactions. The recommended method for a more detailed modeling of the 
chemical reaction would be the generic reaction set scheme as this is the only way of taking into 
account different background NOx and O3 concentrations. 
Estimation of pollutant emission rate by traffic 
It is known that the quality of fuel affects the emission factors (EFs) of vehicles significantly. The 
pollutant emission levels for an oxidizing catalyst passenger car in service of four to seven years 
usually differ from those of a car in service of one to three years by about 10–20%16. Since the EFs 
of automobiles are limited in Taiwan, the differences of EFs due to the vehicle's age were thus 
neglected, but the corrections of EFs due to the change of pollutant contents, e.g. sulfur (S), in the 
fuel were made. In this work, EFs of CO and NOx given by TANEEB17 were used, while only 
one-fifth of those of SO2 in TANEEB17 were used (Table 1) because the sulfur content in mobile 
fuel was about 0.1 g l−1 in 1991, but about 0.018 g l−1 in 2002, corresponding to a five-fold 
reduction. The speed of eighty and ninety kilometers an hour of pollutant EFs are default value of 
ADMS- Road, except SO2. There are three line sources in this study－two for general road (Pei-Yi 
road and Qing-Yun road) and one for freeway. For EFs setting of ADMS-Road, vehicular speed for 
general road, freeway and inside of Hsue-Hshan tunnel was 50, 90 and 80 km h-1, respectively. 
Therefore, the EFs were further combined to calculate the composite EF to provide inputs to the 
source strength of models.

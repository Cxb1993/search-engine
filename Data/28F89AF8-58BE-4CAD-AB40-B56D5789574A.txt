摘 要
本計畫之研究目的在整合銀髮族照顧服務管理模式，期望透過國內外文獻資料之蒐
集與實務作為之考察瞭解，經過專家學者共同研討，以整合出適合之照顧服務管理模式
(請參考附錄一，第一年結案報告)。第二年度之工作乃將第一年「國內外老人福祉事業
發展狀況之調查」之調查結果整合，並於去年舉行「銀髮族社會照顧服務模式之建構論
壇」邀請官、產、學術界代表參與此次論壇，聆聽專家學者之意見，並針對我國安養與
照養護等議題展開討論，以期暸解我國社會照顧服務在安養與照養護所面臨之難題，以
及探討我國銀髮族晚年社會照顧服務之需求（請參考附錄二，第二年結案報告）。，比
較國內外現有社會照顧服務模式之匯整結果，並於今年針對銀髮族需求發放問卷，對使
用居家式、社區式、機構式照顧服務之銀髮族做需求調查與分析，並比較兩世代銀髮族
在需求上的差異，在分析其結果提出結論與建議。
關鍵詞：福祉士、養生村、日間照護、新世代銀髮族。
- 4 -
研究目的
總計畫名稱「整合福祉科技與提昇照顧服務管理之研究」點出對銀髮族福祉事業的關
懷發展著重在科技研發以俾提昇銀髮族生活品質與社會對銀髮族照顧的服務管理模式
發展。本子計畫「銀髮族社會照顧服務模式之建構」之研究目的即在整合銀髮族照顧服
務管理模式，期望透過國內外文獻資料之蒐集與實務作為之考察，經過專家學者共同研
討，以整合出適合之照顧服務管理模式，並發放問卷，對使用居家式、社區式、機構式
照顧服務之銀髮族做需求調查與分析，並比較兩世代銀髮族在需求上的差異，再分析其
結果提出結論與建議。
本研究對象分為兩群：一群是 50~64 歲間的新世代銀髮族（即準銀髮族）即將成
為銀髮族同時也是所謂的家庭照顧者（carer），仍須照顧家中的更高齡者。第二群是
65 歲以上的現代銀髮族，本研究將兩個世代針對五個不同需求層面做探討，比較銀髮族
之需求與差異。研究對象分別來自全省若干縣市。共計 600 名參加此次的調查（新世代
銀髮族 300 人；現代銀髮族 300）。現代銀髮族之調查資料來源又分為居家銀髮族、社
區銀髮族與機構銀髮族。
本研究僅針對目前及理想的（被）照顧方式加以探討，以暸解各人口變項（性別、
年齡、婚姻狀況、經濟狀況、教育程度、健康狀況、居住狀況等）在需求變項上的差異，
在更進一步比較兩代之間在照顧的需求上是否存在有明顯的差距。
以下為本次調查研究結果。兩世代銀髮族，除了在有無聽過復健服務的知曉度、在
法律或老人福利相關方面的問題需求、有無聽過法律咨詢服務的知曉度、最想瞭解的法
律問題上無顯著差異，其它問題均為顯著，顯示我國兩世代在銀髮族需求變項上，有很
大的差異，顯示現在式的照顧服務模式並無法滿足新世代銀髮族的需求。

三、研究步驟：
(一)抽樣與施測：本研究的問卷施測由南開技術學院福祉科技暨服務管理研究所的學生
組成，共 9 名，分為 3 小組。問卷由各組學生親自前往各縣市施測，並同時進行訪談，
為必免抽樣對象不懂問卷問題涵意與不識字等因素影響效度，故每份問卷在發放時均由
同學對銀髮族進行解說與翻譯。本研究共發出問卷 636 份，使用者有效問卷為 600 份。
(二)本研究之施測對象雖非隨機抽樣，但其涵蓋的縣市、職業教育及社經地位別的多樣
性、足夠大的樣本群，應可彌補非隨機抽樣之部份缺失，其結果分析應具相當之代表性
與參考性。
(三)施測時間：民國九十六年五至九月間。

1 Introduction
The ageing societies are defined by the UN when those aged 65 or above people reaches more than
7% of the size of the population. As in Taiwan, it happened earlier than 2006 because in early 2006 the
said percentage climbed up to 9.9% (See Fig. 1.) The forecast of population aged 65 and above at 2020 with
deeper color indicating an even higher rate, and Taiwan is one among them [12]. (See Fig. 2) Facing these
unresisting trends, an ageing society as Taiwan will pose comprehensive challenges over society, economy
and social welfare. However, changes did come when our birth rate declined and life expectancy
expanded. Thanks to technologic advances in medical care, better living facilities are required and
qualities of recreational and mental satisfaction are demanded. Such abundant living environment
motivates the authors to launch the present study. The research was conducted within the framework
referred to as“social care model for the ageing society in Taiwan.” With comparative data from
healthcare industries among Taiwan and other countries, the main objective of the present paper is to map
out a more satisfactorily manageable model to provide health care for the older people in an ageing society
like Taiwan.

3. 3 Elderly Population in Taiwan
Long worried by the rapid growth of its young population, a more affluent Taiwan is increasingly
watchful of its swelling ranks of ageing citizens nowadays. Population ageing is far from just an issue
of numbers. It means more elderly people are living longer, raising the concerns over the issues of
quality of life and health services. Elderly people are more economically independent from their
children in some regions which bring about changes in traditional social means of caring for the
elderly. Elderly Taiwanese parents once commonly lived with children, but their attitudes changed
due to the rapid social economic changes and the large number of young adults leaving rural villages
and flocking to cities.
While most Asian families still consider taking care of the elderly as their responsibility, the
elderly Taiwanese parents gradually accept publicly operated residences for senior citizens; the rate
increased from 7.47% in 2000 up to 9.74% of all the gray population at the end of 2005. [8] Having
the senior citizens stay alone in old-age homes becomes a common phenomenon.
4. Healthcare Industry for the Elderly in Taiwan
4.1 Present Status
Healthcare industry has become an increasingly eminent industry in Taiwan. There is a heated
debate over healthcare for the elderly by policymakers and members of the society during the past two

(1) Care at Home
Homecare services conducted by local governments provide services for the senior citizens with
low-income and limited assets, chronically disabled, or living alone. The services include assistance
with basic activities of daily living, such as bathing, dressing, cleaning, or other personal care. Meal
preparation and regular phone calls are also designed to minimize or compensate for loss of
independent physical or mental functioning.
(2) Care in the Community
Local government also offers community care services for well elderly who still live with their
busy children. These primarily low-tech services may also help with instrumental activities of daily
living, including day care, rehabilitation, shuttle buses, medication management, assistive technology,
and carer break vouchers.

(3) Care in the Institutions
There are two main types of institutional services, residential and nursing homes. Residential
homes are for people who can no longer cope at home with their day to day activities at home and
need a little extra help. A nursing home is for people whose illness or infirmity requires nursing care
on a regular or daily basis. Some homes are doubly registered which means they have a mixture of
people who either need nursing or need residential care. Many homes can offer a replacement on a
short term basis if the carer goes on holiday etc, or is away for a longer term, or a need for permanent
care arises. These are all supervised by local governments, as shown in Table 2.

7.2 Care in the Community
In addition to rehabilitation counselors, mental health counselors are functionally necessary
service providers in community care programs. Mental health counselors are trained with a variety
of therapeutic techniques used to address a wide range of issues, including depression, addiction,
suicidal impulses, stress management, problems with self-esteem, issues associated with ageing and
alienation. Other services include hands-on and stand-by or supervisory human assistance; and
technology such as computerized medication reminders and emergency alert systems that warn family
members and others when an elder with disability fails to respond. They also include home
modifications like building ramps and the installation of grab bars and door handles that are easy to
use. Moreover, potential assistance on matters such as law, finance, recreation, Internet games, films,
travels, and high-tech devices are on the waiting list to strike a perfect match between technology and
client networking for the elderly in Taiwan. [9]
7.3 Care in the Institutions
Institutional care offers plus advantages integrated from home and community care services.
Same with the above service model, these services require care givers to provide support on matters
such as of laws, insurance coverage, technologic advances, and living environments.
8. Conclusions
There is little doubt that the pace of population ageing is expected to accelerate greatly in the
coming ten years in Taiwan, though the actual rate of growth in older people is difficult to quantify.
At the same time, advances in biomedical, communications, and other technologies are happening at
an ever-increasing rate. (Keenan, 2005) The presence of a growing elderly population means seeing
physical illnesses from cardiovascular diseases to arthritis, and to mental problems like loneliness,
disregard, alienation and dis-integration in society. It is not clear whether the elderly are living a
longer life of good quality, or just enduring a painful existence. Thus, more friendly and respectful
social services are critical to the independence and the overall well-being of the elderly people. After
comparing service models among developed countries, numerous options in a new social service
model as shown in Fig. 7 are added which are instrumental for the care services in an ageing society,
such as rehabilitation and mental counselors at home or improved communications in the community
or comfortable living environment at the care-giving institutions as we had in traditional living
environments; and social activities and further learning should be designed for the elderly to bring
about a fuller meaning out of a longer, healthier life.

一、前言 
癌症即是惡性腫瘤，乳癌是由乳房乳腺管細胞或腺泡細胞由於不正常分裂、繁殖
後逐漸形成惡性腫瘤。除了會感染局部器官，更有可能會轉移到遠處的器官，如骨骼、
肝臟、胃、肺臟或甚至腦部等，破壞人體器官的功能，造成身體健康的危害，嚴重時
更會威脅到人類生命。現在仍不清楚乳癌的成因，所以無法採取有效預防措施，唯有
早期診斷及有效治療才能降低乳癌造成的傷害。 
目前健保給付的 HER2 檢測方式[1]可以使用免疫化學染色法(簡稱 IHC)或熒光原
位雜交(限 IHC 檢測在 2+以上的病人)。IHC 以觀察被染色細胞數量的多寡來判斷，依
染色程度，分為 0+(0 價)、1+(1 價)、2+(2 價)、3+(3 價)的檢驗結果。3 價表示 HER2 為
陽性，而 0 與 1 價則是 HER2 陰性。而 2 價的患者，則屬於弱陽性。然而因為目前檢
體保存和染色判讀等因素的影響，容易出現偽陽性或偽陰性，因此若檢驗結果為 2 價
的弱陽性，必須更進一步的以 FISH 來檢查，以免誤判。 
二、研究目的 
以人工的方式大量進行判讀工作，是相當耗費人力及時間，也可能會因為疲勞、
醫生經驗、知識的不同或主觀意見等因素造成判別結果不一，甚至導致誤判。因此為
了減輕病理科醫師判斷的誤差及負擔，我們在免疫組織化學染色法中，利用數位影像
處理技術分割出細胞核，分析細胞核的特徵，然後輔以細胞學原理以及定量或定性的
分析方式，提供預後分析所需相關資訊予病理科醫師，以期能達到輔助診斷的功用，
並增加診斷的正確率。’

摘要 
 
本研究之主要目的在利用影像處理中型態學
技術、K-Means 分群演算法以及反卷積方式，針對
IHC 染色法後的乳房組織切片影像，進行細胞核以

先我們先透過 Sigmoid Filter 對影像作 R、G、B 各
別顏色通道的對比色彩強化，然後我們先對細胞核

我們直接擷取乳房組織切片之 Her2 彩色成像進行
分析，對褐色部分做初步 Hue 通道的閥值分割，接
著再進行 Otsu 統計分閥值、以及 Maximum Entropy
的方法確認反應最強的細胞膜像素(深褐色反應)，

Abstract 
 
According to the statistics of Taiwan’s govern- 
ment, breast cancer of women has become the fourth 
leading cause of death. The immunohistochemically 
(IHC) stained cell provides a simple and reliable 
method for pathologist in clinical diagnosis treatment 
and prognosis. The major goal of this study is to detect 
and analyze IHC stained cells using image processing  
 
 
techniques. The proposed system efficiently extracts 
nuclei of interest including positive stained nuclei and 
negative stained nuclei, and measures the stained ratio  
of the number of positive membrane and nucleus to all  
nuclei. The index may provide clues for pathologist 
whether adopt hormone treatment or gene treatment or 
not. This paper presents two automated methods for 
segmentation of immunohistochemical tissue. Before 
that, we enhanced color image contrast by using 
sigmoid filter for segmentation approach for IHC 
stained cell nuclei image. At first part, we performed 
Otsu’s thresholding on hue color planes in order to 
extract membrane of interest. And, the threshold 
determined by Local Otsu’s thresholding algorithm for 
overcome the limitations caused by some artifacts. 
The remained artifacts were successfully removed by 
using mathematical morphology and Maximum 
Entropy algorithm. In the second part, to compare 
nuclei and background, we specific information by the 
hue color planes and blue components of the image. 
We applied a specific approach to based on color 
deconvolution target is to eliminated stained. This 
provides accurate quantifyication for IHC stained cell 
images. And then, we apply unsupervised Clustering 
to partition the nucleus and background pixels. Finally, 
after completing part one and part two, the stained 
ratio was measured by calculating the number of 
positive cell nuclei to all cell nuclei. This 
measurement provides pathologist a criterion for 
diagnosis and prognosis.

ABSTRACT. 
Students with disabilities encountered many difficulties in learning activities, especially in reading. To help 
them participate in reading activities more effectively, this study proposed an integrated reading support system 
based on the principle of multiple representations. An integrated E-reading support system provides physical, 
sensory, and cognitive supports to learners. Meanwhile, this system also served as the convenient interface for 
material developers and instructors. Twenty fifth and sixth grade students with learning disabilities in Taiwan 
participated in the experiment to explore if this E-reading system could assist them to understand the nature 
science articles. All the students read the articles presented with and without multiple representations on the E-
reading system. The results indicated better comprehension performance when the participants read with 
multiple representations. The students also expressed a preference to use this system to read the articles with 
cognitive support. 
Keywords: 
             Reading difficulties; Multiple representation; Individualized reading system; Students with disabilities 
 
Introduction 
 
The skill to read is essential to a successful learning activity. However, without any help, it is difficult for learners 
with special needs to read effectively due to their limitations or disabilities, such as dyslexia, mental retardation, 
trembleness, visual impairment, visual perception difficulties, or palsy. Their disabilities prevent these students from 
meeting the challenge of general curriculum (Bender, 2004; Mastropieri & Scruggs, 2002; Lerner, 2006). Therefore, 
remedying the deficiencies of reading and improving the reading skills, including word recognition and reading 
comprehension, has been one of the major services provided at school.  Recent studies have supported various 
effective strategies, such as word recognition instruction methods (Browder, Spooner, Wakeman, Trela, & Baker, 
2006; Hung & Huang, 2006; Van der Bijl, Alant, & Lloyd, 2006), reciprocal teaching (Ledere, 2000; Plinscar & 
Brown, 1984), drawing concept map (Chang, Sung, & Chen, 2002; Guastello, Beasley, & Sinatra, 2000). However, 
though effective, these strategies remain inadequate to certain disabled users. Therefore, researchers explored 
alternative solutions which would allow users to bypass their disabilities or augment their residual capabilities 
(Lewis, 1993). 
 
To help special needs students access the reading material, the following typical assistive technology devices are 
provided. Popular alternative readers include WYNN and Kurzweil 3000; the screen magnifier software can help 
people with low vision read electronic texts by enlarging the characters; the switch equipped with a scanning 
program allows people with severe motor impairments to read the electronic texts (Alliance for Technology Access, 
2004). However effective, the method is of little use for those cognitively-challenged, especially those lacking 
lexical knowledge. They need extra cognitive supports to comprehend the article besides having access to materials 
(Rose & Meyer, 2002). Extra supports should include the following: providing important concepts with pictures, 
audios, and videos, or teaching students to read concept maps instead of drawing.  
 
Therefore, besides providing accessible supports, such as adjustable font size, font color, and reading the text aloud, 
the individuals facing reading difficulties need extra cognitive supports. “Bottom-up” and “top-down” are two 
Accommodations in a case-by-case and lesson-by-lesson manner often overwhelm professionals. It would greatly 
ease their workload if a system is to be developed where individualized supports are provided across lessons.  
 
Noteworthily, universal design for learning (UDL), advocated by Center for Applied Special Technology (CAST), 
could offer thoughts that address the abovementioned considerations. UDL consists of three principles: multi-
representations, multi-engagements, and multi-expressions (Rose & Meyer, 2002). The principle of multi-
representations emphasizes that the learning material should be displayed in multiple representations and provides 
flexible cognitive supports responding to users’ needs. The principle of multi-engagements highlights that learners 
should participate in learning activities and interact with the material in his own manners. The principle of multi-
expressions accents that learners could demonstrate their learning outcomes in multiple methods. Besides, CAST 
believes that affectivity has great impact on learner’s engagement. The past studies also indicate that giving choices 
to the learners could encourage and motivate them to participate in learning activities (Kim & Wei, 2010).  As the 
accessible and supportive learning material is the keystone of creating UDL, it is noted that only when the material is 
displayed with multiple representations can learners properly interact with the learning material based on their needs 
or preference. Therefore, among these three principles, developing material in multiple representations is regarded 
the initial step to assisting students.  
 
Focusing on universal design for learning, the authors considered it a vital approach to create learning material not 
only for special needs students but for all learners. More importantly, learning with multiple representations material 
needs team collaboration between material developers and instructors. Material developers must create appropriate 
cognitive supports in advance. Then, the instructors could customize a reading environment for their students; 
moreover, the learners are allowed to set up proper environment by themselves. However, no study has focused on 
exploring the relationship among the material developers, the instructors, and the learners in the past. Meanwhile, 
nor has a system been developed to integrate the needs of the material developers, the instructors, and the learners. 
 
Based on the above research and observations, this paper was set to develop an individualized E-reading 
environment which can provide not only essential supports for Mandarin readers but also a convenient interface 
which allows the material developers to create adjustable material. Experiment was also conducted to explore the 
effectiveness of the system on reading comprehension for students with learning disabilities. 
 
 
System development 
 
Adopting the principle of multi-representations, this study aimed to develop an integrated, web-based E-reading 
system named “TriAccess system”, which could not only provide individualized physical, sensory, and cognitive 
access for special needs learners but also attend to the needs of instructors and material developers.   
 
 
Accessibility supports 
 
Based on previous studies, the authors proposed sixteen potential supports, as shown in Table 1,  to address special 
needs learners’ potential difficulties deriving from their physical, sensory, and cognitive problems.“Bottom up” and 
“top down” supports were included. Seventeen professionals in curriculum design, special education, and instruction 
fields, who were familiar with interventions for individuals with special needs, were invited to participate in a 
modified Delphi survey to validate these supports. They were asked to rate the importance of the 16 potential 
strategies. Two rounds of survey disclosed 14 items as ‘important’ or ‘very important’ strategies. Therefore, these 14 
supports were then embedded into TriAccess system.

Instructor. The major responsibility of the authorized instructors is to set up individualized, appropriate reading

equipped with screen reader. 
 
 The special educator version assessed the usability of the three different interfaces: material developer, instructor, 
and learner. The dimensions of evaluation focused on satisfaction, remembrance, efficiency, recognizability, and

operate the system; the dimension of efficiency tested user productivity with the system; the dimension of 
recognizability tested whether the function of the system could be identified easily; and the items of learnability 
tested whether the user could learn how to operate the system very quickly.  
 
The version for students evaluated four dimensions in terms of satisfaction and memory as well, besides user 
preference and assistance. The items of user preference tested whether the user likes to use the interface; the 
dimension of assistance examined whether the users felt the system benefited them when they read the article. 
 
All the questionnaire participants rated the items on a scale of 5 to 1, with 5 meaning strongly agree and 1 meaning 
strongly disagree. The student version was read to student participants, who then answered orally.

Experiment  
 
This experiment aimed to explore whether the cognitive support features in TriAccess system could assist the 
students with learning disabilities in comprehending the nature science articles. A two-factor within subject 
experiment was conducted. The concrete research questions are: 1. Is there interaction effect between reading 
conditions and reading sequence? 2. Is the performance different between two reading conditions if the interaction 
effect is not significant? 
 
 
Method 
 
Participants. The participants should be identified as students with learning disabilities by the local education agent. 
And they had reading difficulties. Twenty six students, in the fifth and sixth grade, met the criteria from four 
elementary schools in southern Taiwan, were invited to participate in this experiment. However, only 20 students (13 
boys and 7 girls) participated in the experiment after acquiring their parents’ consent. Their intelligence quotient 
(WISC-III) were from 70 to 109 (mean=83.85, SD=9.21). All of them had reading difficulties, and received special 
education service in their schools. None of them reported difficulty in vision, hearing and motor control. 
 
Based on the requirement of the curriculum standard in Taiwan, the students began computer and information 
technology education at 3rd grade formally. Therefore, participants in this experiment had at least a 2-year computer 
Due to various school locations and class schedules of the participants, the experiment was conducted individually. 
They read the articles and took the tests on the TriAccess system in a quiet room in their own schools. 
 
Experiment procedure. Each participant was interviewed to gather demographic information and computer 
experience before the experiment. The third author then read the purpose of the experiment to the participant, and 
demonstrated how to interact with TriAccess system. The participant practiced using the system to familiarize 
themselves with the reading environment with the training article. In the training sessions, the third author also 
helped each participant set up his/her favorite reading environment, including font size, character spacing, row 
spacing, and speed of reading by text-to-speech program. These setting parameters were stored and served as the 
individual reading setting profile for the following experiment, for both two reading conditions. The participant 
needed to pass a prerequisite computer skills test in this session. Nineteen participants passed at the first trail and one 
passed at the second trail. Besides, every participant had to complete the six article’s corresponding testes to 
understand their knowledge about the content they would read later. The test scores were all less than 3.3, 
demonstrating their poor background knowledge about these articles.  
 
There were three sessions in the formal experiment period. Each participant needed to read the abovementioned six 
articles, three without cognitive supports and three with cognitive supports, without instruction. The participant was 
required to read one article with cognitive supports and the other one without cognitive supports in random sequence 
in a session. When they read the text with cognitive supports, they were encouraged to use all the supports they liked 
and could operate the specific support as many times as they needed. On the other hand, the participants were 
encouraged to read the article aloud when they read it without cognitive support. There was no time limitation for 
reading the article in both reading conditions. The participants were required to take the test in which questions 
would be read aloud by the text-to-speech program on TriAccess system. Their response of the every question was 
recorded by the system. A three-minute break was given between two reading conditions.  
 
Data analysis. SPSS 12.0 for Windows was used to analyze the data. Two factors with repeated measures ANOVA 
was conducted to analyze the interaction of reading conditions and reading sequence on reading compression. The 
simple main effect will be explored if the interaction between reading condition and reading sequence was found 
significant. On the contrast, the main effect of the reading conditions would be explored when the interaction was not 
significant. The main effect of reading sequence would not be examined, due to the effect combined the scores from 
two reading conditions. 
 
 
Results 
 
The mean of test scores and standard deviations of reading comprehension tests appear in Table 3. For participants, 
there was no interaction effects (F=0.42, p=.66) on comprehension test scores. The main effect of reading conditions 
(F=112.13, df=19, p=.00) was significant. The mean of the two reading condition demonstrated that the participants 
performed better when reading with multiple representations (M=6.15, SD= 1.12 for multiple representations mode; 
M=4.15, SD=.88 for text mode). Therefore, the main effect of reading supports was confirmed.

Fourth, the experimental design tries to control the irrelevant variables to demonstrate the effect of treatment. It 
might not fit the real situation in school, especially for exploring the reading activity. Since reading is a complex 
phoneme, it is hard to control the irrelevant variables and examine a single factor. That being said, the future study 
should adopt other research methods to explore the questions for the individuals with specific disabilities in depth. 
For example, qualitative research might be a good approach to investigate the process of improving motivation when 
learners read with an E-reading system; single-subject research design might be a suitable method to explore the 
effect of integrating reading strategy and E-reading system on reading comprehension for the individuals with 
disabilities.   
 
Last but not least, the features of this E-reading system should also be enhanced in the future, at least in the 
following directions. First, the new version of the E-reading system should record the user behavior automatically. 
Monitoring the conditions where the supports are used by the students during the learning processes could help the 
learner and the teacher to determine the proper supports based on the objective information. Second, integrating the 
strategies of remedy and compensation into a system to assist learners in learning reading skills and participating in 
reading activities in a same environment. Many past studies demonstrated the effectiveness of computer-based 
reading skills teaching software, such like concept mapping, marking the key concepts, taking notes. However, they 
are separated from compensated reading software. The authors believe that an ideal supportive environment should 
provide both the necessary supports and essential reading strategies during the reading activities.

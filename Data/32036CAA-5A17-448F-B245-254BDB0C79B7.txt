Values of these temperature differences are set at the default in 
the program.  The absorption chiller controller turns on the 
chiller when the tank outlet temperature is higher than 60 oC 
and the cooling demand is positive.  The load calculator 
computes the water temperature returning to the tank 
according to the load demand.  Then the energy calculator 
calculates the cooling loads provided both by solar and 
auxiliary.  An integrator individually sums the two portions of 
the loads, and the solar fraction calculator calculates the solar 
fraction. The monitoring printer is used to print out the values 
of interest to monitor during the simulation, e.g. collector 
outlet temperature, storage temperature, etc.. The result-printer 
outputs the cumulative solar and auxiliary cooling loads and 
the solar fraction.  The simulation calculation is made for 
every fifteen minutes. 
RESULTS 
Weather data in TMY2 form for five major cities in 
Taiwan are input to the simulation program for the present 
study.  They are Taipei in northern, Taichung in central, 
Chiayi in central southern, Tainan in southern and Hualien in 
east. The sizes of the storage tank from 1 to 4 m3 are tested 
when the collector area is fixed at 100 m2.  The results in 
Fig.2 indicate that solar fraction increases with the storage 
size.  However, 2 m3 may be sufficient for cost consideration. 
Sizes higher than 2 m3 do not produce significant increase in 
solar fraction.  Therefore, the storage size is chosen for 20 
liters per m2 of collector area.  This is much lower than the 
storage size for a typical hot water system, 50 to 100 liters per 
m2.  The reason is that higher temperature is required to drive 
the absorption cooling system in the present study for ensuring 
a reasonable COP.  In this study, only when the storage outlet 
temperature is above 60 oC, the absorption chiller will be 
turned on. Otherwise, the auxiliary system is operated instead.  
The effect of the collector area on the solar fraction is 
shown in Fig. 3. Collector areas from 30 to 120 m2 are tested 
when the storage size is fixed at 20 liters per m2 of collector 
area. Larger collector area always produces higher solar 
fraction. However, when the area is larger than a certain size, 
the increased solar fraction may not offset the increased cost of 
the increased collector area. 
Fig. 4 shows the monthly solar fraction for the five cities.  
Solar fraction strongly depends upon the weather data.  The 
data in Tainan show an outstanding performance for the entire 
cooling season except in September. 
CONCLUSIONS 
Figure 5 compares the solar fractions for the five cities for 
the system with 100 m2 collector area and 2 m3 storage tank. It 
is shown that Tainan has highest, about 90%, seasonal solar 
fraction. Taipei and Giayi have about 75% solar fraction.  The 
lowest is Hualien, but still has 60% solar fraction.  The 
results indicate that the absorption solar cooling system is a 
feasible cooling system to develop in Taiwan, especially in 
southern Taiwan. 
This study provides reference data for design 
considerations. Future study may address on more detailed 
system simulation including variable COP.  Since the hot 
water can be used for heating and hot water demands in winter 
season, the solar fraction for the combined heating and cooling 
application is also interested for future study.

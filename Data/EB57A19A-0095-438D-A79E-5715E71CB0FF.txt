Latex Composition Effect on the Viscoelasticity of Acrylic
Pressure-sensitive adhesives
*Hou-Hsein Chu and Hector Huang
Department of Chemical Engineering, Feng Chia University
Taichung 407, Taiwan
E-mail: hhchu@fcu.edu.tw
Acrylic copolymer latexes were prepared by emulsion polymerization process using either
cetyltrimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB, cationic surfactant), or sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS,
anionic surfactant) as the emulsifier. The latex was applied as pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSAs)
and both the mechanical and rheological properties were investigated.
With a fixed amount of 2-EHA (50% wt. in monomer), recipes contained various weight ratios of
MMA/BA (3/7, 5/5, 7/3). Increasing MMA content, the peel strength and tack of PSA decrease, and
the shear strength of PSA and the Tg of polymer increase. In addition, the viscoelastic window
moves toward to the upper-right direction. Meanwhile, the window of homopolymer (P(2-EHA)) is
located at the central region, featuring the best PSA behavior.
Increasing the surfactant content, the viscoelastic window moves toward to the upper-right
direction; while increasing the SDS content, the viscoelastic window does not change much.
Increasing the CTAB content, the temperature range for the viscoelastic properties of PSA fitting
into the general purpose PSA is narrowed and moved to the higher temperature region.
Keywords： Acrylic, Pressure-Sensitive Adhesives (PSAs), Rheometric, Viscoelastic window, Emulsion polymerization
Introduction
Pressure-sensitive adhesives (PSAs) will
adhere to surfaces by application of light hand
press [1]. The performance of PSAs depends
on the composition, molecular weight and
distribution, and the glass transition tempera-
ture (Tg) of polymer [2]. Usually, the acrylic
PSAs may perform in the absence of tackier.
The performance of PSAs may be charac-
terized by tack, peel adhesion and shear
strength. The tack (or quick tack) is defined as
that property of a PSA to adhere a surface spon-
taneously under very light pressure and to resist
the removal of it. Peel adhesion can be meas-
ured by peeling the PSA tape at a constant rate,
usually at an angle of 180to the substrate.
Shear strength means the force required to
remove a PSA tape from a surface in a direction
parallel to the surface to which it is affixed [1].
According to empirical study [3], the PSAs
with storage modulus (G’) in the following
ranges will have good peel, tack, and shear
properties:

Also, the viscoelastic window approach [4]

Using either cationic surfactant cetyl-

The PSA tapes were used to determine tack,

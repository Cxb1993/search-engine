Sedan drivers’ attention and response to variable message signs on freeway in Taiwan 
C. J. Lai, K. T. Yen  
National Chin-Yi Institute of Technology, Taiwan, ROC 
laicj@chinyi.ncit.edu.tw 
 
Variable message signs (VMS) have been installed rapidly in Taiwan to notify motorists of planned 
events and current network problem. This study employed questionnaires to investigate sedan drivers’ 
attention, preference and response to VMS on freeway in Taiwan. Results of statistic analysis found that 
69.23?of drivers were aware of the existence of VMS while driving on freeway. Display characteristics 
of VMS affected drivers’ attention and response significantly. Drivers were prefer to standard Kai-type 
and Middle-Black-type for Chinese typography, red color and orange color for font colors, and leading 
display method for display format. Driving experience, route familiarity and traffic crowd also affected 
drivers’ attention to VMS. Age, gender and education were also significant factors for drivers’ preference 
and response to VMS.  
Keywords: Variable Message Signs, Attention, Driver response 
 
1. Introduction 
Variable message signs (VMS), which are programmable traffic control devices that display dynamic 
messages composed of letters, symbols, or both, are important parts of advanced traveler information 
system (ATIS). The most common use of VMS is to provide information about current travel conditions, 
such as traffic congestion, weather-related road conditions, and notification of construction ahead. VMS are 
also used for safety messages, notice of future road construction, and notice of public events (Benson, 
1996). 
 
A survey of more than 500 motorists in the Washington D.C. region found that demographic variable have 
little influence on motorists’ attitude toward VMS, except for a few cases involving level of education. 
Respondents are well disposed to VMS messages that are simple, reliable, and useful (Benson, 1996). In 
London, a questionnaire survey  showed that 80? of drivers considered the information presented to be 
useful, 95?could correctly understood the abbreviations (Chatterjee et al, 2002). A interview survey 
conducted in Paris revealed that 97? of drivers were aware of the existence of VMSs, 62? of drivers 
completely understood the information presented on VMSs, 84?considered the information presented to 
be useful, and 46?had on at least one diverted in response to the travel time information (MV2, 1997). 
 
In order to enhance traffic safety and efficiency, many developed countries, such as the United States, The 
United Kingdom, Japan and Singapore, were gaining widespread uses in VMSs. Several studies have been 
carried out evaluating the effectiveness of such systems (Armstrong and Upchurch, 1994; Chatterjee et al,

4. Conclusions and recommendations 
 
This study employed questionnaires to investigate sedan drivers’ attention, comprehension, preference and 
response to VMS on freeway in Taiwan. The total sample were 124, and the results are as follow? 
(1)There were 69?of sedan drivers aware of the existence of VMS whiling driving on freeway. 
(2)VMS often affect drivers’ driving behaviors, such as changing driving lane, changing driving route and 
decreasing driving speed. 
(3)The order of information category of VMS that driver expected to present were traffic report, alterative 
routes, weather condition, position indication and trip caution. 
(4)Drivers preferred standard Kai-type and middle Black-type to thin Ming-type for Chinese typography, 
preferred red and orange to green for display characters color, and preferred leading display method to 
R.S.V.P and flashing display method for display format.  
(5)Driving experience, driving propose, level of route familiarity, level of traffic jammed and weather 
condition affected drivers’ attention for VMS.  
(6)Gender, age, and education were significant factors to drivers’ comprehension and preference for VMS.  
 
Although the results of this study showed that 69% of drivers aware of the existence of VMS while driving 
on freeway, the results also showed that 45.83?of sedan drivers seldom and 15.87?never heard of VMS 
via media. For the more widespread to the drivers, related authorities have to advertise the facility of VMS 
further. There were 86.54?and 66.35?of drivers expected VMS to present traffic report and alterative 
routes. Relative authorities should consider to display this kind messages more often. The other findings of 
this study should also be considered in the setting of VMS. Besides, this study was conducted through a 
questionnaire, a design of experiment approach to evaluation the display of VMS in laboratory should also 
be conducted.

to read and understand the message within a short time (Wang and Cao, 2003).

human factors design considerations such as legibility distance, target value, and 
viewing comfort for fiber-optic and LED VMSs on freeways. It concluded that the 
optimum number of words contained in a message varied with the VMS technology, 
the lighting conditions, and the prevailing traffic speed. For VMSs using other

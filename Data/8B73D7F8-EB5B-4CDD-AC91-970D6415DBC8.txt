摘要 
本計畫採用白、紅、綠、藍發光二極體作為背光
模組的光源，以白光LED為主，RGB-LED為輔的方
式，搭配系統單晶片PSoC與數位色階調光電路，研
製一具色溫調控功能的RGBW-LED背光源，以提升
背光源亮度與色彩的調控準度。本計畫研製LED背光
模組光源光電特性自動量測系統，以此系統對
RGBW-LED進行定量分析，並應用色度學理論發展
數位色階調控RGBW -LED混光技術，可迅速地控制
xy色度座標於黑體輻射曲線上。並應用功率因數修正
器與返馳式轉換器，製作一具高效率及高穩定度的電
源供應器，防止負載變動時造成電壓不穩定的情況。
本計畫以4個8吋模組化單元製作1個16吋背光源，開
發一具廣色域兼高亮度的RGBW-LED背光源。 
 
關鍵詞：發光二極體、返馳式轉換器、數位色階調
光、色溫 
Abstract 
In this report, a RGBW backlight control module is 
developed based on W-LED along with RGB-LED. Use 
PSoC and digital level dimming circuit to develop a 
RGBW-LED backlight module which has color 
temperature control. The quantitative analysis and control 
of mixed–light technology for RGBW LED based on 
digital color level control technique is proposed and 
studied to output white light by rapidly adjusting xy 
chromaticity diagram into black body locus. Accordingly, 
the brightness and color-shifting problems resulted from 
temperature-rise of RGBW-LED can be improved and 
overcome. A high efficiency power supply is an essential 
prerequisite for a high-performance mixed-light LED 
backlight source. Apart from being energy-conserved, the 
voltage fluctuation caused by RGBW LED output change 
must be prevented as well. The research is engaged in the 
improvement and implementation of large-sized LED 
backlight source and a 16" backlight source is developed 
in module. The purpose of wide color space and high 
brightness for RGBW-LED backlight module can be 
completed.    
 
Keywords ： light emitting diode 、 flyback converter 、
digital color level dimming、color temperature 
 
I. 文獻探討與研究方法 
目前常見的背光源有冷陰極管（Cold Cathode 
Fluorescence Lamp, CCFL）與發光二極體（Light 
Emitting Diode, LED）兩種，不過由於 CCFL 有諸多
的缺點，如穩定時間長、含汞、色域範圍小、色彩飽
和度低及需要搭配適當的安定器（Ballast）才能穩定
工作等；而 LED 則有壽命長、直流低電壓驅動、反
應時間快、不易破損、耗電量少、環保無汞、體積小
及色域豐富等等優點[1-3]，以致於 CCFL 背光源逐漸
被 LED 背光源取代 [1-5]。為使色彩再現率提升，色
域（Color Gamut）範圍的大小是個很重要的關鍵，
因此於 1952 年，由美國國家電視標準委員會
（National Television System Committee, NTSC）制定
出類比視訊的規範，色域範圍與其規範相同者，則色
彩飽和度（Color Saturation）為 100%；CCFL 背光源
搭配濾光片後的色域範圍大約只有 70%～75%的
NTSC 色域，而以紅、綠、藍（Red、Green、Blue） 
LED 為背光源則可以輕易超越 100%的 NTSC 色域，
可高達 118%的 NTSC 色域[3-8]。根據文獻指出，再
增加其它原色可使色域範圍更為提升至 175%的
NTSC 色域以上[9-11]。 
LED使用於背光源上一直存在著溫升的問題，由
於LED會隨著溫度的上升，其內阻則隨之下降，導致
流經LED的電流變大、溫度又提高，在如此的惡性循
環下，LED將快速地老化、甚至燒毀[12]。不僅如
此，LED的光學特性跟溫度亦有相當大的關聯，如：
主波長、亮度、色度座標、發光效率等等，而且各個
顏色LED的製作材料不同，溫度的影響性也不盡相同
[13]。 
溫度對 LED 最主要的影響為亮度，所以發光效
率 也 會 跟 著 變 動 ， 又 根 據 國 際 照 明 委 員 會
（International Commission on Illumination， CIE）訂
定之規範，可得知亮度的變化會造成色度座標的飄
移。因此有許多研究都對亮度及色度座標作探討，尋
找將其調整回目標值的方法，主要的調光種類分為
（1）類比調光法（2）數位調光法[4-5,14-18]。 
由於 LED 的跨壓有些微變動時，將使 LED 上的
電流有大幅度的變化，因此需要一穩定的電源供應
器，維持 LED 的驅動電壓，而且根據文獻的研究，
LED 以直流驅動會有較高的發光效率，故本計畫採用
直流驅動 LED 背光模組[1,19-23]。 
類比調光與數位調光方法以數位調光的精準度較
高，由於高精準度的關係，故數位調光適合做定量分
析；然而目前的研究大多是使用數位調光，不過都有
個共同缺點，就是缺乏定量分析，在調光的過程中並
無數值的分析可以作為依歸[1,10,17-18]。 
本計畫將採用 RGB 與白光 LED 製作一 RGBW-
LED 背光源，並研製一功因修正器與一對多返馳式轉
換器提供背光源工作電源，再運用色彩工程學理論開
發一 RGBW-LED 背光源數位色階自動混光系統調整
背光源色溫，完成一高亮度且可調色溫的 RGBW-
LED 背光源。

VI.結論與討論 
本計畫以紅綠藍與白光發光二極體製作一 16 吋
RGBW-LED 背光源，並運用 LED 背光模組光源光電
特性自動量測監控系統對 RGBW-LED 做定量分析，
再應用色彩工程學理論開發一 RGBW-LED 背光源數
位色階自動混光系統，提供一可調色溫與亮度之背光
源。本計畫利用主動功因修正器與一對多返馳式轉換
器製作 16 吋 RGBW-LED 背光源電源供應器，完成紅
綠藍與白光發光二極體應用於液晶顯示器背光源混光
調控之研究。茲就本計畫之總結如下： 
1. LED 背光模組光源光電特性自動量測監控系統：本
計畫之光學量測系統不僅可以量測光學、色度學、
電壓、電流、溫濕度資訊，亦能降低人力的浪費、
提高系統的精準度及可靠性。 
2. RGBW-LED 背光源調光理論：本計畫運用色彩工
程學理論推導 RGBW-LED 之色階與色度座標及其
變化量關係式。 
3. RGBW-LED 背光源數位色階自動混光系統：以
RGBW-LED 背光源調光理論與數位色階調光電路
製作一可調色溫與亮度之 RGBW-LED 背光源。 
4. RGBW-LED 背光源電源供應器：本計畫所研製的
主動功因修正器與一對多返馳式轉換器可提供一高
功因及多組輸出電源。 
本計畫對於紅綠藍與白光發光二極體應用於液晶
顯示器背光源混光調控之研究，提出未來可進一步研
究之方向，如以黃光 LED 與 RGB 三原色 LED 進行
探討，比較加入黃光或白光的優缺點。

I. INTRODUCTION 
Insofar, the traditional fluorescent lamps (FL) still dominate 
in the domestic lighting. The traditional fluorescent lamps are 
equipped with filament to preheat emission electrons and, thus, 
featured with the disadvantage of low life-cycle. In this 
research, a filament-free CCFL will be employed to replace the 
traditional T8 FL with no need to alter the lamp holder. 
The commercial FLs ranging from T8 to T12 possess the 
same lamp holder and lamp plug-in and can exchange one 
another. In replacing the inherited traditional FLs, the cost 
includes not only removal costs but also destruction of 
upholstery, thereby drastically discouraging the incentives for 
users. Consequently, this project aims to develop a T8-CCFL 
lamp by implementing CCFL light source inside a traditional 
T8 lamp. The CCFL can directly replace traditional FL by 
utilizing transparent tube of T8 as the outer package. 
Inʳ lighting applications, the fluorescent lightings are 
generally installed on the ceiling and project light downward in 
single direction. The commonly used reflection plate possesses 
only 70% efficiency when reaching the floor. In addition, the 
dust can pollute the top side of fluorescent lamp and further 
reduce the lighting efficiency under the long period use. To 
improve this drawback, the proposed CCFL lighting set 
implements a high efficiency reflection plate inside the 
transparent light tube. The plate reflects the incoming light 
directly from CCFL in the same direction and promotes the 
lighting efficiency accordingly.   
In this research, a 13 W, 110 cm long, U-shaped CCFL will 
be installed into a traditional T8 transparent tube with 60 cm in 
length. For retaining the original 18 W lighting capability, a 
well-performed reflection plate will be installed at the center to 
project the emitting light of CCFL in the same direction to 
promote the lighting efficiency. For holding the T8 U-shaped 
CCFL within traditional 60cm long T8 lighting set with the 
original holding set being kept, the space between reflection 
plate and light tube will be used to accommodate the CCFL 
driver circuit, thus inspiring the incentives of users to replace 
the traditional fluorescent lamp with CCFL. The product size 
and cost can therefore be reduced and the competitiveness can 
be promoted.   
The traditional fluorescent lamps are always implemented 
with magnetic ballast which is inherited with poor power factor 
and power quality. This research is intended to fully exploit the 
inductor of the original lighting set. In combination with 
appropriate capacitor, the passive filter can enhance the power 
factor considerably and encourage users to refurbish the 
existing traditional FL lighting set. A passive high-power-
factor correction circuit is proposed to improve power factor 
performance. Basically, the power factor corrector consists of 
an inductor and a capacitor. The designed LC filter utilizes the 
original inductor inherited in the magnetic ballast.  Merely by 
introducing appropriate capacitor, the PF performance can be 
improved significantly. 
A high-voltage, low current drive circuit is always essential 
for driving a long CCFL tube. Unavoidably, the leakage current 
problem will accompany. This study employs a Class-D half 
bridge resonant zero-voltage-switching scheme along with 
voltage boosting function of isolated transformer to reduce 
leakage current by connecting the center-tapped transformer 
with a single-tube CCFL. The T8 U-shaped single-tube CCFL 
circuit loop is, therefore, completed. To minimize the influence 
of dimming function on drive circuit design, this research 
makes use of burst mode dimming technique to regulate light 
output, the CCFL output voltage was intermittently outputted 
and the required dimming functions can be achieved.  To 
reduce the unnecessary energy consumption, the dimming 
design is based on sectional type to meet different brightness 
requirements.

Abstract-This paper proposes a high power-factor electronic 
ballast for metal halide lamps. In the proposed circuit, two 
buck-boost converters and a buck converter are integrated with 
a full-bridge inverter of which the four active switches are 
shared. Two active switches are switched at high frequency, 
while the others are switched at low frequency. The buck 
converter is used to step down the dc-link voltage of the full-
bridge inverter and filter out the high-frequency voltage 
components to drive lamps with a low-frequency square-wave 
(LFSW) voltage.  The buck-boost converters are operated at 
discontinuous-conduction mode (DCM) to perform the function 
of power-factor-correction (PFC) to ensure nearly a unity 
power factor at the input line. By tactful arrangement of diodes, 
the dc-link capacitors are charged in series and discharged in 
parallel. It helps to operate the high-frequency active switches 
at a high duty ratio to have a small peak value of the inductor 
current of the PFC converters, and then smaller conduction 
losses. 
Detail operation modes and the design equations are 
provided. A prototype electronic ballast for a 70 W metal halide 
lamp is built and tested. Experimental measurements have 
proven that the proposed ballast has the advantages of high 
power factor, and free of acoustic resonance. By integrating the 
active switches of the converters and the inverter, the product 
cost of the proposed electronic ballast can be effectively reduced. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Owing to the benefit of high luminous efficacy, high 
intensity discharge (HID) lamps have become an attractive 
lighting source and are popularly used as outdoor and indoor 
lighting sources. Among various kinds of HID lamps, metal 
halide (MH) lamps have the advantages of long lamp life 
(>12000 hrs), high luminous efficacy (>85 lm/W), good color 
rendition (color rendering index Ra>85), and have been widely 
used in many lighting applications [1]-[3]. Since the MH 
lamp presents the characteristics of negative incremental 
reactance, it requires a ballast to stabilize the lamp current. 
Recently, traditional electromagnetic ballasts have 
numerously been replaced by the electronic ballasts which 
are operated at high frequency and then, have the advantage 
of small size, light weight and high energy conversion 
efficiency over the electromagnetic ones. Besides, driving 
the MH lamps by a high-frequency voltage can improve the 
light performance [4]-[8].  
However, MH lamps driven by a high-frequency voltage 
may suffer from problematic acoustic resonance. Acoustic 
resonance is a physical phenomenon that is related to 
pressure-wave propagation in the discharging tube of the 
lamp. This phenomenon can result in an unstable arc, flickers, 
extinguishing, or even worse, lamp damage and sometimes 
lamp explosion [6]-[8]. Many approaches have been 
proposed to eliminate the acoustic resonance. Among them, 
driving MH lamps with a low-frequency square-wave 
(LFSW) voltage has been considered the most effective 
method [9]-[17]. 
Typically, the electronic ballasts widely found in industrial 
market consist of three stages as shown in Fig. 1. These 
stages are usually 1) PFC stage, 2) dc-to-dc conversion stage, 
and 3) full-bridge inverter stage. In order to comply with the 
more stringent regulations on current harmonics, such as 
IEC61000-3-2 class C standards, and to improve the power 
factor, a low-pass filter and a full-bridge rectifier are 
followed by the PFC stage.  A boost or buck-boost converter 
is served as the PFC which waveshapes the input line current 
to be sinusoidal and in phase with the input line voltage. 
Since the output dc-link voltage of the PFC is always higher 
than the amplitude of the ac input line voltage, the second 
stage is usual a dc-to-dc buck converter used to step-down 
the dc-link voltage for complying with the lamp voltage and 
regulating the lamp power. Both the first and the second 
stages operate at high switching frequency to enable the use 
of small magnetic components. The final stage is a full-
bridge inverter of which the active switches operate at low 
switching frequency to drive the MH lamp with a LFSW 
voltage. Such three-stage electronic ballasts can ensure a 
high power factor and drive the MH lamp with a low-
frequency square-wave voltage to avoid the acoustic 
resonance. However, each stage incurs losses in energy-
conversion process and then, limits the overall circuit 
efficiency of the electronic ballast. Moreover, each stage 
requires independent active switch/switches and the 
corresponding control circuits. They are not cost-effective 
products.

Since the operation modes in the positive half-cycle of the 
input line voltage and those in the negative half-cycle are 
similar, only the operation modes in the positive half-cycle 
of the line voltage are discussed. At steady-state, the circuit 
operation can be divided into four modes in each high 
frequency cycle. For simplifying the circuit analysis, the 
low-pass filter, the lamp igniter circuit and transformer T1 
are omitted and the MH lamp is represented by its equivalent 
resistance, Rlamp. Fig.4 shows the operation modes and Fig. 5 
illustrates the theoretical waveforms for each mode. The 
circuit operation is described as follows: 
Mode I (t0<t<t1):  
Mode I begins at the instant of turning on switch S1. The 
rectified input voltage is across the inductor L1. Since L1 is 
designed to operate at DCM, the inductor current ip1 
increases linearly from zero with a rising slope which is 
proportional to the line voltage. Meanwhile, both S1 and S3 
are on, and the voltage across the buck inductor Lb is equal 
to Vdc/2 minus Vb. Capacitors C1 and C2 supply current to 
charge Lb which is also operated at DCM, thereby, the buck 
inductor current ib rises from zero. 
Mode I ends when S1 is turned off. There could be two 
different operation modes following Mode I depending on 
whether S3 is on or off.  When S3 is kept on, the circuit 
operation enters Mode II-A. Contrarily, in the dead time 
betweenVgs2 and Vgs3, both S1 and S3 are off and the circuit 
operation will enter Mode II-B. 
Mode II-A(t1<t<t2):  
Mode II-A begins when S1 is turned off. For making PFC1 
and PFC2 perform as buck-boost converters, both the 
voltages across C1 and C2 should be higher than the 
amplitude of the ac input voltage. Then, diode D5 is reverse-
biased. Current ip1 will freewheel through diode D7 to charge 
C1. Meanwhile, current ib flows through S3 and diode D4 to 
supply current to Cb and the lamp. The voltages across L1 
and Lb are equal to –Vdc/2 and –Vb, respectively. Therefore, 
both currents decrease linearly. 
Mode II-B(t1<t<t2):  
The difference between Mode II-B and Mode II-A is only 
the current loop of ib. Since both S1 and S3 are turned off, ib 
will freewheel through diodes D2 and D4 to charge 
capacitors C1 and C2. The voltage across Lb is –Vdc plus –Vb. 
Hence, ib decrease faster than it does in Mode II-A. 
Mode III-A & Mode III-B (t2<t<t3): 
As stated above, ip1 should decline to zero before ib does. 
When ip1 reaches zero, Mode III-A and Mode III-B follow 
Mode II-A and Mode II-B, respectively. Only ib and lamp 
current keep flowing. 
Mode IV (t3<t<t4): 
The circuit operation enters Mode IV when ib reaches zero. 
During this mode, only the lamp current supplied from Cb 
exists. When Vgs1 goes back to high level to turn on S1, the 
circuit operation returns to Mode I of the next high-
frequency cycle.

V.  CONCLUSIONS 
A novel electronic ballast for driving MH lamp with a 
LFSW voltage is presented. The proposed circuit is derived 
by integrating two buck-boost converters, a buck converter 
and a full-bridge inverter. The buck-boost converters which 
perform as power-factor-correction circuits are operated at 
DCM to achieve high power factor and low total current 
harmonic distortion.  
The circuit operations are described and the design 
equations are derived. A prototype circuit designed for a 70-
W MH lamp was built and measured to verify the theoretical 
analyses. Experimental results show that the electronic 
ballast performs satisfactorily. A nearly unity power factor 
(>0.99) and low THD (5.5%) can be achieved. The lamp is 
driven by a low-frequency (60Hz) square-wave current to 
avoid the occurrence of acoustic resonance.

與高延伸溫度 (drawing temperature)下，為 31螺旋結構，同屬斜方晶系
(orthorhombic)[5, 6]。γ型態則由外延結晶(epitaxial crystallization)形成，由六甲基苯
基材中而得[7]。PLA α’型態與α型態的差異在於單位晶胞ab軸較長[8]，結構類似103
螺旋結構，但有些許扭曲(distortion)，呈現較不規則的排列。因此在DSC熱分析
中，還有WAXD與FTIR檢測中皆會出現差異；DSC熱分析中會在熔融峰附近出現
額外的放熱峰，這是因為結晶的再次排列所產生[8, 9]，WAXD繞射峰則因為晶胞
參數不同而有所位移[10-12]，FTIR吸收峰則因為晶胞較為鬆散，故分子間作用力有
所差異，吸收峰也會有所位移[10, 12, 13]。

結果與討論 
 
1.非等溫結晶測試 
fig.1為樣品熔融後快速冷卻再分別使用升溫速率5℃/min與10℃/min的二次
升溫曲線。從fig.1(a)(c)可以看出Tg沒有明顯變化，約為58℃。而從(a)可以發現冷
結晶曲線因為DBS的添加使之變得寬廣(broad)，相對來說也就是結晶時間拉長。
而從(b)可以觀察到兩個熔融峰，分別是Tm1與Tm2，隨著DBS的添加量增多而熔融
峰溫度有所降低，且仔細觀察熔融峰強度比也有所變化，Tm1與Tm2的強度隨著DBS
添加量變化而有所消長；未添加DBS的樣品熔融峰強度Tm1明顯比Tm2來的要高，
DBS添加量上升至2 wt%時兩個峰強度已經十分接近，而DBS添加到3 wt%、4 wt%
時可以發現Tm2熔融峰已經高於Tm1。而(c)結晶曲線因為升溫速率較慢的原因而較
為狹窄(narrow)，且因為升溫速率較慢而提供了足夠的結晶時間，這時PLA本身
主導了結晶程序，DBS的影響能力相對下降，因此結晶曲線在添加各比例DBS下
並無明顯差異。接著看(d)的熔融曲線，同樣的Tm1與Tm2的peak溫度隨著DBS的添
加量增多而有所降低，而在此升溫條件下Tm1 peak強度明顯較Tm2強，並且注意看
Tm2的peak強度十分顯著的隨著DBS含量上升而上升。 
目前文獻中提到 Tm2與 Tm1熔融峰皆為 α型態的貢獻，但 Tm2屬於在結晶過程
中所形成的 α型態熔融峰；而較高溫的 Tm1則是屬於 α’型態在接近熔融溫度時轉
變為 α型態後再熔融的熔融峰[11]，因此 Tm2熔融峰強度越大則代表結晶過程所形
成的 α型態較多。在較快升溫速率(10℃/min)中，因為 DBS的添加而延緩了 PLA

4. PLA/DBS中DBS纖維之形成 
  我們選定添加3 wt% DBS的樣品，分別給予40分鐘以下的120℃等溫結晶時間，
之後迅速的降溫至室溫使結晶終止，藉此觀察DBS纖維的出現情形。其POM圖與
SEM圖如fig.8，可以看出結晶時間達40分鐘時出現了同心圓結構，而相對應的
SEM圖中只有在同心圓出現時的40分鐘樣品才有DBS纖維聚集出現，接著我們把
此球晶結構由圓心向外分成三個區域，分別為A.球晶內圈、B.球晶外圈、C.非球
晶區，fig.8(c)中C區域出現了不平坦的現象，這是在未添加DBS的樣品與結晶時
間20分鐘的樣品中所不曾出現的，而fig.8(d)中B、C區域則明顯出現較為混亂的
結構。 
由以上的結果我們可以推論出DBS纖維形成與PLA球晶成長的時間關係。在
結晶時間20分鐘以前，DBS小分子受到PLA結晶排他的影響逐漸向外排出，因此
   
從fig.10(b) SEM圖中可以發現已經無法觀察到DBS纖維，但仍清晰可見球晶內外
圈之顏色差異，fig.10(b)可以看見A區域仍為標準PLA結晶結構，而B區域則有絲
狀凹槽出現，而內圈則無絲狀凹槽結構。凹槽寬度約為20 nm，也就是恰好與DBS
纖維直徑相當，因此我們研判這些絲狀凹槽為原始DBS所佔據位置，但因為DBS
遭乙醇蝕刻去除，所以留下了這些絲狀凹槽。但是從乙醇蝕刻前SEM圖，如fig.8 
(d)，可知其表面並無明顯纖維出現，因此這些絲狀凹槽的出現必定與原先球晶外
圈的混亂結構有關，且從fig.10中可以看出絲狀凹槽分佈於球晶外圈，且分佈的
十分均勻，這顯示出未蝕刻球晶外圈分佈著均勻的DBS纖維，確實與球晶外圈混
亂結構的分佈區域相符。 
我們將上述乙醇處理後的3 wt% DBS/PLA樣品進行1H-NMR檢測，並不能在
此樣品中發現DBS的特性峰，可以肯定乙醇蝕刻過程已經將DBS完全去除，因此
再次確認POM中出現的同心圓結構與DBS存在與否無關，由此推論較稀疏的DBS
纖維確實能夠影響PLA結晶的雙折射性質，以致於可在POM中觀察到同心圓結
構。

二、與會心得 
國際光學與光電學會（International society for optics and photonics (SPIE)主辦每年舉
行 
一次之國際會議。本次參加的人數超過 4950 人，是歷屆最多參予的人數，共有超過 3175
篇的論文投稿，除了 Yasuhiko Arakawa 等重要學者在內的 24 場專題演說，另有近百場的
研究報告。 
本屆大會主題內容共分成 4 大領域，分別為奈米科學與工程、太陽能技術發展、光
電設備應用與光學工程應用，內容涵蓋範圍極廣。所有投稿中數目最多者是奈米科學與
工程，可見相關研究目前仍相當受到重視，本人發表的部份也屬於此領域，因此受益良
多，得到許多研究相關的知識。 
    我這次會議的論文題目是 The nanostructure and morphology of biodegradable PLLA films 
with DBS nanofibrils，屬於奈米科學與工程之領域。研究主要是探討二苯亞甲基山梨醇
(1,3:2,4-Dibenzylidene sorbitol,DBS)奈米纖維形成與聚乳酸(polylactic acid, PLA)結晶成長
之間的關係。添加不同的 DBS 含量與選用不同的結晶溫度，來進一步觀察 PLA 球晶成長
之變化。我們發現在結晶溫度大於 120℃且 DBS 含量大於 3 wt％時，會在偏光顯微鏡
(POM)的觀測下出現罕見的同心圓結構。接著進一步利用掃描式電子顯微鏡(SEM)來觀察
球晶成長與纖維形成的時間關係，進而推得一系列 DBS 纖維與 PLA 結晶成長關係的時間
軸。再經由核磁共振光譜儀(NMR)確認 DBS 的添加確實改變了 PLA 結晶的形態與雙折射
性質。本次為壁報形式發表，在解說時段以英語和不同國家的學者交換心得並討論後續
可能的研究方向。 
    參加此次的國際性會議讓我視野更開闊，也了解國際間在學術上的競爭與交流，每一
個與會人士都是有備而來，對於有興趣的主題大家都不會輕易讓機會消失，非常踴躍而
主動的討論，因此更顯得英文的聽說讀寫都相當重要，演講的技巧也很重要，可以讓台
下的聽眾容易有共鳴，親身參與會議更可以直接獲得與研究相關的訊息和交流，以及意
見的回饋。最後，由衷感謝國科會提供補助，減輕大部分的經濟負擔。

The nanostructure and morphology of biodegradable PLLA films with 
DBS nanofibrils 
 
Wei-Chi Lai* 
Dept. of Chemical and Materials Engineering, Tamkang University, No.151 Yingzhuan Rd., 
Danshui Dist., New Taipei City, Taiwan 25137 
*wclai@mail.tku.edu.tw; phone+886-2-2621-5656 ext.3516; fax+886-2-2620-9887 
ABSTRACT   
The nanostructures and morphologies of neat 1,3:2,4-dibenzylidene-D-sorbitol (DBS) and DBS/poly(L-lactic acid) 
(PLLA) films have been investigated by polarizing optical microscopy (POM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). 
The morphology of neat DBS samples prepared from solution had unspecific structures, and no fibrils formed. The DBS 
nanofibrils were found in DBS/PLLA systems, and the average diameter was only around 20 nm. The DBS architectures 
could be well tuned by varying the DBS contents and PLLA crystallization temperatures. In addition, nanoporous PLLA 
structures could be obtained by solvent extraction of the DBS nanofibrils.   
Keywords: DBS, PLLA, fibrils, crystallization, porous 
 
1. INTRODUCTION  
1,3:2,4-dibenzylidene sorbitol (DBS) is an amphiphilic molecule, which is derived from the sugar alcohol D-glucitol. 
Figure 1 displays the chemical structure of DBS. DBS could self-assemble into nanofibrils through the hydrogen 
bonding in a wide variety of organic solvents and liquid polymers.1 Electron microscopy and atomic force microscopy 
show that the diameter of these DBS nanofibrils generally ranges from 10 nm to 0.8 μm. On the other hand, the 
microstructures of neat DBS prepared from the melt have been also discussed.2 The diameter of neat DBS fibrils, ranging 
from 100 nm to 1 μm, is much larger than that of DBS nanofibrils found in organic solvents or liquid polymers. In some 
cases, DBS molecules dissolved in organic media can cause physical gelation. The resulting materials are a type of 
“organogels”. The DBS organogels could provide some applications such as gel antiperspirants, gel electrolytes, 
cosmetics and so on.3  
In our earlier studies,4 DBS organogels were used as self-assembled scaffolds for preparing polystyrene (PS) 
nanocomposites and porous materials. DBS molecules were dissolved in styrene/initiator mixtures at high temperature. 
After cooling to room temperature, DBS organogels were obtained as a result of the DBS nanofibrillar networks formed. 
The PS materials with DBS nanofibrils were then synthesized using thermal-initiated polymerization. DBS nanofibrils 
could act as reinforcing materials for improved mechanical properties. In addition, the porous PS materials were 
prepared by the solvent extraction of DBS nanofibrils. 
The sample preparation here is much easier than that in our previous studies: DBS and a crystalline polymer, poly(L-
lactic acid) (PLLA), were directly blended by solution casting. The temperature range of the formation of DBS fibrils 
were very close to that of crystallization temperature of PLLA spherulites. The temperature range for DBS fibrils 
formation depends on the DBS concentration and cooling/heating rate. Usually, the range is between 100-200 oC. 
Therefore, in this study, we examined the interaction between DBS fibrils and PLLA crystals as well. Moreover, we 
attempted to prepare porous PLLA materials by the removal of DBS nanofibrils. PLLA has attracted much attention due 
to their potential applications as biomedical and green materials,5 and such porous PLLA materials could be used as 
scaffolds for the cell transplantation and tissue regeneration.6 There are a lot of ways to prepare porous PLLA films, such 
as solution-casting-leaching, thermal precipitation, solvent evaporation, immersion precipitation, and so on.7 The method 
proposed here might provide useful information on the fabrication of porous PLLA of different morphologies. 
 
 
 
 
Samples crystallized at 120 oC with 0-2 wt% DBS exhibited typical PLLA spherulites, and samples with 3 and 4 wt% 
DBS had concentric-circled spherulites as observed by POM. No ringed-banded structures were found (POM 
photographs are not shown). Figure 4 displays a series of SEM photographs of DBS/PLLA samples with different DBS 
contents crystallized at 120 oC. The positions of aggregated DBS nanofibrils are related to the contents of DBS.  Few 
DBS nanofibrils were observed in the sample with 1 wt% DBS.  However, a large amount of DBS nanofibrils formed as 
DBS content reached 2 wt% (see Figure 4 (c)). The fibrils segregated between spherulites, but POM showed no obvious 
differences. Moreover, the PLLA spherulites almost completely filled the space at the final crystallization stage. It 
seemed that the aggregated DBS nanofibrils formed after the PLLA crystallization, and thus hardly influenced the 
growth of PLLA spherulites. 
When the concentration of DBS reached 3 and 4 wt%, the fibrils aggregated to form circles inside spherulitic regions 
(Figures 3 (b) and 4 (d)). The concentric-circled structures were also observed by POM. The DBS aggregated structures 
were different from those in samples with 2 wt% DBS. The DBS nanofibrils in samples containing 3 and 4 wt% DBS 
aggregated into μm-sized “fibrillar rings” or “fibrillar disks.”  The high-magnification SEM micrographs of samples 
containing 2 and 4 wt% DBS are shown in Figures 4 (e) and (f). Independent of the location of the DBS self-assembled 
inside or outside spherulitic regions, the diameter sizes of these nanofibrils ranged from 10 nm to 200 nm, and the 
average diameter size was near 20 nm. Furthermore, the average diameter of the DBS fibrillar rings and disks in samples 
containing 3 wt% DBS was around 100 μm (Figure 3 (b)). In addition, the average diameter of fibrillar rings and disks in 
samples with 4 wt% DBS was about 50 μm (Figure 4 (d)). 
Figure 5 displays a schematic representation of the DBS/PLLA samples with different DBS contents crystallized at 120 
oC. As the PLLA crystallizes, DBS molecules are excluded outside the crystals; hence the concentration of DBS in the 
amorphous region increases. It is supposed that the local DBS concentration must be high enough to form the nanofibrils.  
Thus, at 1 wt% DBS, few nanofibrils form. As the DBS content reaches 2 wt%, the local concentration of DBS is still 
not sufficient at the early stages of PLLA crystallization. However, when the PLLA crystallization is complete, the 
concentration of DBS molecules ejected outside the PLLA crystals is relatively high. As a result, DBS molecules self-
assemble to form the nanofibrils between spherulites. On the other hand, as the DBS contents are 3 and 4 wt%, the 
formation of nanfibrils is much easier due to the higher DBS contents. Therefore, DBS nanofibrils form earlier during 
the PLLA crystallization process and aggregate inside the spherulites. Moreover, the formation of DBS nanofibrils in 
samples containing 4 wt% DBS is much earlier than that in samples containing 3wt% DBS due to the higher DBS 
contents, which is evident in SEM observations. The average diameter of the fibrillar rings or disks (the primary location 
where DBS nanofibrils aggregate) in samples with 4 wt% DBS was much smaller than that in samples with 3 wt% DBS 
(Figures 3 (b) and 4 (d)). 
The PLLA samples crystallized at 120 oC with 3 wt% DBS were immerged into ethanol, a good solvent for DBS, for a 
week to remove the DBS fibrils. Figure 6 shows the SEM photographs of such a sample after DBS extraction by ethanol. 
As seen in Figure 6 (a), the concentric-circled PLLA spherulites could still be observed by POM, though the DBS 
nanofibrils were removed, as reconfirmed by SEM (Figures 6 (b)-(d)). Thus, it seems that the change in birefringence is 
caused by the irregular PLLA lamellae. Figure 6 (b) shows that there were no pores in region A and that the porous 
structure only appeared in region B.  Figures 6 (c) and (d) are the SEM photographs of the same sample at different 
magnifications near the spherulitic boundary in region B, clearly showing the porous structure. The shape of the pores 
was similar to that of the DBS nanofibrils. The average diameter was around 20 nm, also very close to that of nanofibrils. 
Therefore, the porous structure seems to be the result of the removal of nanofibrils.  Also note that although the DBS 
nanofibrills could aggregate into fibrillar disks (see Figures 3 (b) and 4 (d)), nanofibrils were not trapped in PLLA 
spherulites inside the circles. Actually, the nanofibrils seemed to be ejected out of PLLA at the circles to form the 
fibrillar disks. We will investigate the cause of this intriguing behaviors in our further studies. 
In addition, the porous structures in region B also confirmed that some DBS nanofibrils did form beyond the circles and 
were dispersed in the spherulites. The dispersed nanofibrils affected the orientation of PLLA lamellae and led to a 
change in birefringence. As a result, the regions outside the circles in the concentric-circled PLLA spherulites were 
slightly different from the typical PLLA spherulites).

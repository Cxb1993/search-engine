六、附錄: 本此次與會的論文。 
  
 
 
One technique to interrogate the distributed sensors is by Brillouin scattering measurement. 
Brillouin scattering-based distributed sensors have been applied in safety monitoring of structures [3, 
4] and smart building implementation [5]. 
In typical pulse-based Brillouin system, the limiting spatial resolution comes from the damping 
time of the acoustic wave.  When the pulse becomes shorter than the damping time of the acoustic 
wave, the interaction between the pulse and the acoustic waves becomes shorter and the gain 
spectrum becomes broadened. Improvements on the resolution may be achieved by an analysis of 
the coherence interactions of waves [6]. In the current work, the sensing system employed is based 
on the Brillouin optical correlation domain analysis (BOCDA) technology proposed by Hotate et al. 
[7, 8]. This optical fiber sensing technology has the capability of distributed sensing. The detecting 
area and the spatial resolution are adjustable. The system is based on the interaction of frequency-
modulated continuous waves for both probing and pumping waves. The BOCDA shows unique 
features of random accessibility of measuring position with high spatial resolution (1cm). 
In a correlation-based technique, the frequency-modulated light source is divided into the pump 
and probe. The pump and probe lightwave counter-propagate along the fiber, stimulated Brillouin 
scatting (SBS) in the fiber is generated at the correlation peak and the probe obtains Brillouin gain 
from the pump. The resulting Brillouin gain spectrum (BGS) is predominantly contributed from the 
correlation peak within the fiber section under test. We can selectively stimulated Brillouin scatting 
at any arbitrary section along the fiber by changing the modulating frequency. The Brillouin 
frequency shift νB , which is determined by the peak of a BGS, is known to be linearly related to the 
temperature and strain prevailing in that section of the fiber. 
 
                                    νB(T0, ε) = Cε (ε - ε0) + νB0(T0, ε0)     (1) 
 
                                    νB(T, ε0) = CT (T - T0) + νB0(T0, ε0)     (2) 
 
where Cε and CT are the strain and temperature coefficients respectively, and T0 and ε0 are the strain 
and temperature that correspond to a reference Brillouin frequency νB0. 
In this paper, we apply the BOCDA technique to interrogate fiber deployed in a spirally wound 
fashion along a pressurized steel pipe, with the aim to detect the occurrence, location and the degree 
of change in wall thickness in the pipe. 
 
 
EXPERIMENTAL PROGRAM 
 
Wall-Thinning Defects and sensor lay-out on the Steel Pipe 
 
The steel pipe employed was 1.8 m long; with an outer diameter of 9.2cm and a wall thickness of 
6mm. To simulate the localized deteriorations of pipeline thickness, rectangular patches of depths 
from 0.6mm (10%) to 3.6mm (60%) were excavated from the inner wall of the pipe. The resulting 
thicknesses reduction varies from 10% to 60%. The longitudinal and circumferential distribution of 
these simulated wall-thinning defects is shown schematically in Fig. 1a & b. The exact details of 
these artifacts are documented in Table 1. An Optical fiber was wound around the pipe in a spiral 
course with a pitch of 20cm. To independently assess the fiber sensors, uniaxial strain gages were 
stuck alongside with and in the direction of the optical fiber. Strain gages A, C, E, G, I and K were 
stuck inside the thinned regions. Strain gages B, D, F, H and J were stuck at positions away from the 
cutout areas. Layout of the strain gages is also shown in Fig.1a. Table 2 lists the lengthwise positions 
of the optical fiber each strain gage corresponds. Two thermocouples were attached to the steel pipe 
to monitor pipe temperature changes during testing. 
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure2. Photograph of the pipe with fiber sensor, strain gages and thermocouple. 
 
 
DISTRIBUTED BRILLOUIN SCATTERING SENSOR SYSTEM 
 
Fig.3 shows the scheme of the distributed Brillouin Scattering System employed. The sinusoidal 
frequency modulation (FM) of the laser diode (LD) generates the correlation peak at a specific 
position along the fiber under test. The pump and probe lightwave interact at the point which is 
related to the correlation peak. By changing the modulated frequency, we can move the position of 
the correlation peak along the fiber. The stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS) is generated 
exclusively at the specified position corresponding to the correlation peak along the fiber. The probe 
obtains the Brillouin gain from the pump. The probe gain is measured with the photodetector. The 
shift in Brillouin frequency will reflect the change in the strain and temperature.

As shown in Fig.1b, we have placed 11 strain gages on every cutout region and some non-cutout

appears to be larger. There are several causes for discrepancy: firstly, the resolution and accuracy of 
the BOCDA system is about ±50µε. Secondly, although temperature was kept as stable as possible 
but still the BOCDA is liable to exhibit a ±10µε error. On the other hand, strain gages are self 
temperature compensated. Thirdly, the gage length of the strain gage is 5mm, which much shorter 
than the spatial resolution of 3.5cm of the fiber sensor. Lastly and most importantly, due to possible 
installation errors, the axis of the strain gage may not be exactly the same as that of the optical fiber. 
Thus the strain recorded by both kinds of sensors may be different. The latter fact may also explain 
why the discrepancy is smaller in the cutouts with less severe wall-thinning, as the strain values are 
lower there.  
Fig.6 compares the typical strain readings from the strain gages at pipe positions where the 
internal wall has full thickness with the fiber results. Strains recorded by the strain gages at full 
thickness segregated into two groups. Strain gages D and H gave significantly higher readings while 
the remaining gages showed more or less similar readings under the same pressure. The positions of 
strain gages D and H roughly corresponds to that of the peaks P and Q noted before in Fig.4. This 
confirms the above explanation that strain concentration at the cutout boundaries extended to these 
positions. In Fig.6 we can see that the strains indicated by the fiber at these two locations are 
considerably lower than that of the strain gage. This is understandable as stress/strain concentration 
usually involves a steep gradient. The much longer gage length of the fiber tends to average out the 
results and give a lower strain than the pointwise gage. At position F, the agreement between the 
fiber and strain gage is much better as this position is relatively far away from any strain 
concentration feature.

Abstract—The solidly mounted resonator (SMR) is composed of 
a piezoelectric AlN thin film sandwiched between two electrodes 
and a Bragg reflector consisting of alternating high and low 
acoustic impedance layers of quarter-wavelength thickness. 
According to the different arrangements of high and low 
acoustic impedance layers, SMR devices form two resonance 
modes, i.e. 1/2 λ and 1/4 λ mode configurations. The purpose of 
this study is to compare the resonance characteristics of these 
two configurations. The thickness of the piezoelectric layer for 
1/4 λ mode configuration is set equal to a half of that for 1/2 λ 
mode configuration. Therefore, the piezoelectric thin film for 1/2 
λ mode configuration exhibits better crystallization owing to its 
film thickness. It leads to a high coupling coefficient, k2, and a 
wide bandwidth if piezoelectric thin film is fabricated with good 
crystalline characteristics. As to 1/4 λ mode configuration with 
moderate film thickness, it has a better surface smoothness and 
a lower stress resulting in a slight scattering of the acoustic wave. 
As a result, the SMR device with 1/4 λ mode configuration leads 
to a high quality factor. The return loss outside passband is 
obviously close to zero for the 1/4 λ mode configuration. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
In recent years, the thin film bulk acoustic wave resonator 
(TFBAR) plays an important role in the field of the modern 
communication systems due to the advantages of low insertion 
loss, high power handling capability and small size. Besides, 
TFBARs can offer a more stable performance and a higher 
operation frequency at GHz band ranges, compared with 
microwave ceramic devices and surface acoustic wave (SAW) 
devices. Furthermore, the fabrication processes of TFBARs 
are similar to semiconductor manufacturing technologies. 
Therefore, TFBARs have the potential for monolithic 
microwave integrated circuits (MMICs) with other 
communication devices. 
TFBARs can be fabricated with two conformations. The 
first structure is formed using surface or bulk micromachining 
technique to etch air groove or cavity, which is named a thin 
film bulk acoustic resonator (FBAR). The other structure is 
solidly mounted resonator (SMR), introduced by Newell in 
1965 [1], which is composed of a piezoelectric thin film 
sandwiched between two electrodes and a Bragg reflector 
consisting of alternating high and low acoustic impedance 
layers of quarter-wavelength thickness. The function of the 
Bragg reflector is to transform the impedance of the interface 
between the resonator and Bragg reflector to a lower or higher 
value, thereby producing partial isolation. Furthermore, the 
interface between the resonator and Bragg reflector may 
behave a free or clamped interface depending on the detailed 
arrangement of the reflector layers.  
It forms a free interface, i.e. 1/2 λ mode configuration, 
while a low acoustic impedance layer is fabricated beneath the 
piezoelectric film, and therefore the piezoelectric film is set as 
a half-wavelength thickness at the fundamental resonance 
frequency, as shown in Fig. 1(a). On the other hand, 1/4 λ 
mode is formed a clamped interface by constructing a high 
acoustic impedance layer beneath the piezoelectric film and 
therefore the thickness of the piezoelectric film is set as a 
quarter-wavelength at the fundamental resonance frequency, 
as shown in Fig. 1(b). The purpose of this paper is to analyze 
the resonance characteristics of both configurations. 
II. EXPERIMENT 
The fundamental SMR device is composed of a 
piezoelectric layer which is sandwiched between two metal 
electrodes and then attached to a Bragg reflector which has 
been formed on a Si substrate. The SMR device is fabricated 
using dc/rf magnetron sputtering and patterned by three masks 
of photolithography processes.

the substrate depends on the Bragg reflector, which consists of 
multi-layers of high and low acoustic impedances. Each layer 
of Bragg reflector can be considered as a transmission line 
with acoustic impedance and acoustic phase velocity. The 
effective input impedance is related to the following 
transmission line equation [2]:

According to the different arrangement of high and low 
acoustic impedance of Bragg reflector layers, the effective 
acoustic input impedance will be transform to a value near 
zero or near infinite. As a result, the interface between the 
bottom electrode and Bragg reflector will transform to a free 
or clamp interface, which constructs the 1/2 λ and 1/4 λ mode 
configurations. In this study, Mo and SiO2 were employed as 
the materials of high and low acoustic impedances, since they 
had the advantages of quite large acoustic impedance ratio, 
conspicuous interfaces between high and low acoustic 
impedance layers and flat surface morphologies.

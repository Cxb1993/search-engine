A two-stage fermentation strategy, based on batch cultures conducted first under high salt condition, and 
later under low salt condition, was used to improve carotenoids production by Haloferax mediterranei. Using 
2% glucose as carbon source and appropriate NaCl in medium allowed to obtain a maximum biomass 
concentration of  in the first stage of cultivation. In the second stage, a same volume of fresh medium with 
0.1% butyrate but without NaCl was fed rapidly that led to half of the NaCl concentration in culture medium. 
Under the osmosis-shock conditions, the productions of red carotenoids were improved. If glucose was fed in 
second stage, its carotenoids concentration was only about half. The butyrate and osmosis shock were 
effective for the induction of carotenoids, and the two-stage fermentation process could be a feasible strategy 
for the production of carotenoids for H. mediterranei. 
The extracted carotenoids from H. mediterranei (hmERP) are composed mainly of the C50 carotenoids 
which are biosynthesized from lycopene. The hmERP were tested at different concentrations that decreased 
the cell proliferation of melanoma B16-F0 cell in a dose dependent manner.  Incubation of the cells for 48 h 
with 0.03 ppm hmERP reduced 45 % of cell growth assayed by tryan blue dyeing. Additionally, the melanin 
content of the treated melanoma cell by 0.03 ppm hmERP for 48 h was dropped off 27% than one of untreated 
cell, and the total melanin of treated cell was decreased to 45% of untreated cell.  When zebrafish as a 
whole-animal model for phenotype-based evaluation of melanogenic inhibitors, the pigmentation of zebrafish 
was obviously inhibited from the observation of the embryos of 9 h post-fertilization (hpf) treated with 400 
nM ??? for 48 h. And tyrosinase activity was spectrometrically determined using about 100 zebrafish 
embryos treated with 400 nM bacterioruberin for 48 h, sonicated in Pro-Prep protein extraction solution, and 
quantified. Tyrosinase activity was also slight decreased. It is obvious that bacterioruberin was effective to 
inhibit the pigmentation in vitro and in vivo.

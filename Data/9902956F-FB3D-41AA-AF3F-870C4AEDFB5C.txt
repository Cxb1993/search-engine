3.2 訪談結果與討論 
3.2.1 工作轉換歷程 
工作轉換歷程係指畢業生在工設職業中，工作轉換
情形與選擇、更換工作之原因。研究發現，除環境
因素如公司地點偏僻及公司經營問題外，同事間因
工作方式無法協調也為導致更換工作之主要因素。 
3.2.2 畢業後求職情形 
尋找工作所花費時間是指扣除準備作品集時間，從
投遞履歷到應徵上所花費的時間。受訪者找尋第 1
份工作，平均所花費時間為 1.6 個月。對於在學時
期對工設行業之想像與實際工作後落差主要為產
品製程問題。在工作時間方面，12 位受訪者中，
有 3 位（ID3, 8, RE2）表示該工作需靠常態加班才
能完成所派任務，其他受訪者均表示，只有在案子
進度落後時，需視情況偶爾加班，ID8 表示，若公
司須靠工業設計師常態加班才能完成設計工作，該
公司應考慮招募新進人員。在工作中學得實務經驗
及設計產品被量化生產的成就感，為工業設計師對
於目前工作主要滿意之項目。 
3.2.3 學用適配及建議 
受訪者對於在學時期學校課程對工作幫助程度的
看法上，綜合 12 位受訪者發現，在傳統產業中，
創意思考、3D 及製程相關課程對工作較有幫助；
在設計公司裡則以基本技法與製程方面課程較有
幫助；科技產業對於課程的看法則較多元。 
 
四、問卷調查 
本階段以問卷調查法，調查台灣工業設計系畢業生
就業情形，由文獻探討及訪談結果擬出「台灣工業
設計畢業生就業情形調查問卷」，作為本三階段調
查研究之工具。 
4.1 研究對象 
本研究第一階段以台灣設有 4 年制工業設計相關
科系，且該校之畢業生作為研究對象，共 15 所學
校，有效問卷回收 328 人。第二階段以台灣 4 年制
工業設計相關科系，新增加之該校畢業生達到 4
屆以上共 5 所學校，有效問卷回收 88 人。兩階段
共寄發問卷 2461 人，有效問卷回收 416 人，有效
回收率 16.9%。受試者基本資料分析如表 2。 
4.2 結果與討論 
4.2.1 就業狀況分析 
就業狀況是由受試者中擁有工作經驗者 357 人填
答，再就其填答情形分析各項之人數與百分比分配
情形，並與基本資料各變項進行卡方檢定，檢驗各
項就業狀況與此 5 變項的差異情形。受試者就業狀
況各題分析結果如下： 
1）畢業(或役畢)後多久找到第 1 份正式工作 
在 1 個月以內找到工作的佔 47.5%。3 個月以
內找到工作者累加至 84.5%，6 個月以內累加
至 93.5%，而花費半年以上者僅佔 6.5%。就畢
業生尋找工作的速度而言，情況頗為良好，在
與基本資料各變項的檢定上，均無顯著差異。

5.1 研究分析 
本研究從 2008 年 9 月至 2009 年 1 月間，利用職務
名稱關鍵字搜尋，收集台灣兩大求職網站(104 人力
銀行及 1111 人力銀行)上之設計相關工作徵才訊
息，並將重複刊登的訊息予以刪除。分析每個設計
相關工作的徵才條件，藉以了解業界所要求之工作
條件及知能。總計收集兩大人力網站共計 58 天，
包含 1382 筆徵才訊息，平均一天提供 23.83 個工
作，顯示就業市場提供相當的設計工作機會。

Taiwan established the first collegiate level of industrial design department in 1964 and the development of 
industrial design education in Taiwan has a history spanning more than forty years. As shown in Figure 1, 
there was six ID related programs in Taiwan in 1987 and has been increased up to 26 schools with 29 
programs now. The number of ID graduates from the years of 1980 to 1990 was less than two hundred 
annually, and it is about thirteen hundred annually nowadays [Huang & You, 1985; Ministry of Education 
Department of Statistics, 2007]. According to the estimation of the Chinese Industrial Design Association, 
the annual need of the ID talents in Taiwan is about 600-800 people, compared to 1,300 graduates each year, 
the supply should be more than sufficient. However, the design departments in industries have claimed that it 
is not easy to find qualified ID talents in the job market [Weng, 2003]. In addition, the R.O.C. Council for 
Economic Planning and Development [2006] estimated that the supply of ID talents would be short of 
demand in Taiwan for the next 10 years. While the industry is eagerly in demand of ID talents, can the 
quality of ID graduates satisfy the industry’s need? A study found that 63 percent of respondents considered 
a gap existing between the quality of design graduates and the industry’s need in Taiwan [Chang, 1997]. 
Kaufman [1998] also reports that the quality of ID graduates in the U.S. is less than expected. This shows 
that there is a lack of link between what design students’ learn in school and the real requirement in 
employment after graduation [Ball, 2002; Yeh, 2003]. In other words, design students are not necessarily 
well equipped with the required knowledge and skills for employment when they graduate. 
Employment preparation is about all the acts for the future development of vocation or work, and it can be

aspired to, liked, or for which one has an interest”; 4) self-concepts: “the belief held about an ability one has

ANALYSIS OF INTERVIEWS 
3.1 PARTICIPANTS 
The authors compiled the information about the tasks, required competency, and work returns via the 
approach of literature review. However, most of the above information derives from the government, 
professional organizations, and academic institutions. This study conducted 15 in-depth interviews, including 
7 ID practitioners and 8 ID students. A semi-structured outline was used to collect their viewpoints about the 
ID profession. The seven design practitioners come from six companies, including four from the Information 
and Communication Technology (ICT) industry, one from domestic appliances industry, and one from shoe 
industry. This study used snowball sampling to recruit ID students and selected those with various 
backgrounds in high schools and entrance channels to the university.  
3.2 SUMMARY OF INTERVIEW RESULTS 
Comparing the interviewees’ viewpoints with the literature about the work content of the ID profession, both 
are quite consistent. However, the actual content about design tasks and required competences would vary 
based on the type of industry and the scale of enterprises. These detailed differences are not shown in current 
information about ID profession. For example, most of the ICT industry or big scale enterprises have set up 
various task teams to design and develop new products together; therefore, there is a need for different 
specialists in the design domain. On the contrary, designers work in the traditional industry or small and 
medium sized enterprises usually have to handle a design project from the beginning to the end so they are 
expected to be equipped with comprehensive competences.  
The summary of interview with the students are: 1) Students perceive differently about the ID profession 
before and after entering the university; 2) Students seem to be limited in identifying the job titles that ID 
graduates can be qualified; 3) Students perceive that the ratio of female designers is out of proportion to male 
ones and the opportunity of promotion for two genders is different; 4) Part of the female students claim they 
are not comfortable to deal with mechanism design; 5) Students perceive that the distribution of industry, 
employment opportunities, salary, and available information, etc. for industrial designers vary from region to 
region. In addition, there is inconsistence among the interviewed students about some tasks that industrial 
designers should be responsible for. Some students claim industrial designers should personally take care of 
everything about design projects, such as model making, advertising, package design, and marketing, etc., 
while some consider industrial designers are only responsible for their own core expertise. In terms of 
industrial designers’ lifestyle, most of the interviewed students claim that it is common for designers to work 
longer hours, staying up late, without distinction between workday and holidays, flexible schedule on or off 
duty, etc.  
In sum, practitioner and student interviewees agree most of the descriptions about the ID profession, but 
there is a need to supplement the details and differences about the ID tasks, working environment, salary, 
and competences within the various type of industry and scale of enterprises. In addition, those student

CONCLUSION AND SUGGESTIONS 
The ID students’ career awareness is fairly acceptable in the 4 aspects of knowledge, values, preferences, and 
self-concepts. However, there are variations in opinions of lifestyle of industrial designers, which is also 
lacking in the literature. In addition, there are about one fourth of the surveyed items got low mean ratings. 
The students’ awareness in some of the tasks, required competency, and lifestyles of industrial designers 
does not match the reality. As to the aspect of career values, the ID students generally have a positive 
evaluation of the ID profession. Some identified values are mainly in opportunity of personal growth, 
contribution to the public, high social esteem, etc. In the aspect of preferences, the students like ID because 
they personally feel comfortable in oral presentation and also like the flexibility and freedom of time control 
in ID activities. Finally, in the aspect of self-concepts, though the students are confident in their professional 
skills, they still feel a deficiency in some areas of design related knowledge, such as the second language, 
website design, sales and marketing, material and processing, ergonomics, etc. This study links between 
students’ academics and employment, therefore, the outcome is significant to both design education and 
profession. It would help to propose necessary adjustment and suggestions for educators in order to assist the 
ID students to better understand this profession, and hence be more competent in the work world. 
A few suggestions are raised as follows:  
1) To provide up-to-date information about the ID occupation on related websites. 
Though ID students have basic understanding of the ID occupation and its work dimensions, they have 
different opinions of lifestyle of industrial designers. It would be beneficial if the government agencies can 
update the ID occupation in the Dictionary of Occupational Titles in ROC and, in addition, compile a 
Handbook of Occupational Outlook, including the ID occupation. In the Outlook, information contents as 
exemplified by the US Department of Labor [2008] and the UK Graduate Prospects [2008] can be adopted.

2.1 量子秘密分享
量子秘密分享的問題分為兩類： (1) 將古
典訊息利用量子狀態編碼後傳輸， 參與者
合作藉由量子態的操控與測量獲得原有的
古典訊息，稱為Quantum secret sharing；
(2)量子資訊的量子秘密分享，也就是量子
狀態分享，稱為 Quantum state sharing。
在 Quantum secret sharing 方 面 ，
Hillery 等人於 1999 年提出以 GHZ states
設計古典訊息 (2, 2) 的量子秘密分享協
定 [22]， 同時做到資訊分割、竊聽偵測
並建立秘密分享者共有的金鑰。 同年，
Karlsson 等人發表使用 EPR pairs 設計量
子分享古典秘密協定 [27]。 以非糾纏態設
計量子分享古典秘密協定則於 2003 年由
兩位中國大陸學者發表 [21]。 理論方面，
Gottesman 證明了量子秘密分享的一些性
質與限制 [18]。
在 Quantum state sharing 方面， Cleve
等人最早證明 ((l, n))-threshold 方案存在
的條件是 n ≥ l > n/2， 否則將違反量
子態不可複製定理 [12]。 近年來量子秘密
分享的問題也運用連續變數系統的量子理
論 [41, 40]。
現有的量子秘密分享協定設計的方法，

Yuan-Jiun Wang and D. J. Guan
Department of Computer Science and Engineering, National Sun Yat-sen University, Kaohsiung,

Abstract. A quantum anonymous voting scheme for two voters is proposed. Two
voters, Alice and Bob, vote on a question with either “yes” or “no”. A third party
called Charlie determines whether or not they have the same vote. If they have dif-
ferent choices, our scheme can keeps the anonymity such that no one can trace back
except that Alice and Bob cooperate. Our scheme uses quantum GHZ states that
are the maximal entanglement of three particles. If there exist eavesdroppers, the
proposed scheme can prevent intrusion and detect eavesdropping.
Keywords: quantum mechanics, anonymity, voting, quantum entanglement, GHZ state
1 Introduction
In real world, a secret voting processes the following procedure: Each voter
secretly marks his vote and casts into a ballot box. After every voter finishes
his vote, count the votes publicly. Four conditions are necessarily satisfied: (1)
Each voter votes just once; (2) The choice of voter is securely transmitted,
that is voting will not be interpolated; (3) A trusted voting center counts the
votes fairly; and (4) Every voter keep anonymity, that is the vote cannot be
traced back.
We consider a simplest scenario called two-voter ballot: Alice and Bob are
two voters, and Charlie is an honest (non-cheating) authority who determines
the result of the votes. A classical procedure is like this: Alice and Bob give
their votes on certified and identical papers secretly and independently, and
then they put their papers into a black box. Charlie opens the black box after
the vote finishes and counts the votes to see the final result. How to design
this black box in a classical computing environment? The following is a simple
method: If Alice/Bob votes for “yes” then she/he sends a number 1, otherwise
sends 0. The black box executes the addition operation. The black box outputs
the number of “yes”. If the output is 1, it means that one “yes” and one “no”.
The black box satisfies the above third and fourth conditions: offering the final
result and hiding the identity of voters, but lacking non-reusability and secure
transmission.

presented a quantum protocol for sending and receiving classical bits anony-
mously [3]. Hillery et al. analyzed quantum communication privacy and pro-

使用者身份認證 (entity authentication) 的技術可分為三大類 [2]： (1)

有的特徵 (something inherent)，例如手寫簽名、指紋、聲音、視網膜圖像
等。 其中通行碼認證是使用最普遍的方式。

則這次認證失敗。 Eve雖然能夠獲得 r 與 H(r+ s)的部份對映資訊，因為
H 是一個安全的雜湊函數， 她想要依賴部份的資訊假冒 Alice 進入系統，
機率是很小的。 這個方法能夠避免 Eve 竊聽後假冒攻擊 (impersonation
attack)、重送攻擊與 pre-play attack。

A Quantum Password Authentication Scheme
with Trusted Servers
Yuan-Jiun Wang, E. S. Zhuang, and D. J. Guan
Department of Computer Science and Engineering, National Sun Yat-sen University, Kaohsiung,
Taiwan, ROC
Abstract. Password authentication is conventional means of identifying users, and
is extensively investigated topic in classical cryptography. This work addresses a situ-
ation in which users authenticate themselves using a password with a trusted server,
that is, unilateral authentication and excluding the fake server. A quantum password
authentication scheme based on the theory of quantum mechanics is proposed. The
BB84 quantum key distribution protocol is utilized for detecting eavesdroppers in
this scheme. If there is no eavesdropper between users and servers, a user can trans-
mit a response to certificate him. The approach is also tested against eavesdropping,
replay, impersonation, and man-in-the-middle attacks, so that the proposed scheme
is justified secure to be claimed.
Keywords: quantum mechanics, password, authentication, quantum key distribution protocol
1 Introduction
Users generally utilize passwords stored in servers beforehand to identify them-
selves. A password is typed to draw money from automatic teller machines,
or to login to computer systems. Eavesdropping devices may be installed by
adversaries to steal passwords. If adversaries steal the password, then they can
impersonate users to draw money from the bank, or login to computer to crack
systems. To address this problem, user entity authentication protocols have to
protect the transmission of passwords, so that attackers cannot impersonate
legitimate users to authenticate successfully, even when messages transmitted
between communications are intercepted.
Entity authentication techniques can be divided into three categories [8]:
(1) based on users having something known, for example passwords, Personal
Identification Numbers (PINs), and secret or private keys; (2) based on users
having possessed, for example magnetic cards, smart cards, and IC cards, and
(3) based on inherent characteristics of users, such as handwritten signatures,
fingerprints, voice and retinal patterns. The most popular method is to use
password to authenticate one.
The security of classical cryptography protocols is typically based on some
mathematical hard problems, such as factoring and discrete logarithms prob-
lems. By the steady progress of computation, once these hard problems are
solved by efficient methods, the security of protocols based on these hard prob-
lems is threatened. As an important discovery, Shor proposed algorithms for
quantum computation [12], in which problems involving factoring and dis-
crete logarithms were solved in polynomial time by quantum computation.
Therefore, if quantum computers are built, then many classical cryptography
3In password files, cipher f(s) is stored in place of s. This approach can protect

A one-time password is a dynamic password. A user transmits an encoded
string of s, called the authentication password, rather than s. The encoded
string is sent only once, that is, the encoded strings are different for every trans-
mission. Lamport presented a password authentication protocol in 1981 [5]. For
the ith authentication, Alice encrypts s by a secure one-way function i times
to obtain wi, and sends it to Bob. As well as the password s, Alice and Bob
share a secure one-way function. This protocol can defeat eavesdropping and
replay attacks, but still has a weakness, that attackers can impersonate Bob
to obtain responses from Alice, and then apply the responses to impersonate
Alice successfully. This attack is called a pre-play attack.
“Challenge-response” identification is called strong authentication. If Alice
wants to enter a system, then Bob selects some messages randomly and sends
them to Alice. Alice then computes the answers according to the messages
and the password, and then sends them to Bob as responses. Both messages
and responses can be classical or quantum information. A secure and simple
password challenge-response authentication method is as follows. Alice and
Bob share a password s and a secure one-way hash function H. If Alice asks
to identify herself, then Bob randomly chooses a number r and transmits it to
Alice. When Alice receives r, she calculates H(r + s) and sends the value to
Bob. Bob compares the value with the result that he has computed. If both are
identical, then Alice authenticates successfully; otherwise she fails. Although
Eve can obtain partial information of r and H(r+s), the probability of entering
system by the very litter information available to impersonate Alice is very
small. This approach can avoid impersonation, replay and pre-play attacks.
Cre´peau and Salvail developed the first mutual identification protocol based
on quantum mechanics in 1995 [4], based on quantum oblivious transfer and
coding theory. However, quantum oblivious transfer was later demonstrated
to be insecure against collective attack of quantum computers. Ljunggren et
al. presented a user authentication protocol with trusted authority [6]. Mihara
proposed quantum identification schemes with entanglement in 2002 [9]. His
schemes assume quantum that entangled states have been transmitted between
the user and the system. Therefore, he did not consider the problem of qubit
of interception between communications. Dan later pointed out that Mihara’s
methods only use the classical NOT operation and the computational basis
for measurement, and that same schemes can be implemented by replacing
entangled states with classically correlated states. Therefore, the security of
Mihara’s schemes cannot be based on quantum entanglement or any quantum
properties [13].

In a stratified fluid system, when an 
ISW with both elevation and depression 
components encounters the seabed 
topography, then interactions occur. Figure 
2 shows a satellite image illustrating the 
interaction between ISWs and coral reefs  
near an island in the South China Sea. Note 
the diffraction and refraction of internal 
waves in relation to the bathymetry of the 
ocean floor. However, more field 
observations of these exceptional signatures 
of internal waves are still needed so they can 
be commented on in a quantitative way. The 
internal wave propagates along the 
thermocline until it interacts with variations 
in the bottom topography, such as 
underwater mountains, sills, and other 
submerged structures. The aim in this 
current study is  to determine which parts of 
a depression-type ISW are reflected, 
transmitted, or dissipated by turbulence as 
they approach the lip of a submerged 
triangular. Such interactions are important 
because turbulent vortices can help to 
transport oxygen and nutrients from the 
epilimnion (upper layer) into the 
hyperlimnion (lower layer). Furthermore, 
this interaction may also affect the 
horizontal transport of water and suspended 
matter across the structure. Vortices are 
important  not only for their role in the 
attenuation and completed destruction of an 
internal wave, but also as an important 
dynamic mechanism in our quantitative 
discussion.

Once an ISW is generated by the 
Kuroshio current in the Luzon Strait, the 
area between the southern tip of Taiwan and 
the Philippines, it propagates westward, first 
passing two submarine ridges in the strait. 
The top of the first ridge is estimated to be 
about 750 m below and the second 1,100 m 
below the mean sea surface; the water is  
about 3,500 m deep; the ridges are 
approximately 110 km apart; see Figure 2. 
The current then moves onto the margin of a 
continental slope and finally onto a shallow 
shelf-slope (see the transect in Figure 12). 
Based on the observations we reproduce a 
similar geometry for the numerical 
experiments. The evolution showing the 
computed leading component of the ISW at 
various locations along this path of 
propagation in the northern SCS is 
illustrated in Figure 13. The fluctuations in 
wave amplitudes of the leading ISW are 
complex. Not only can we find wave 
augmentation but also reflection; see Figure 
8. The ISW amplitude is augmented from 
the onset where it enters the first ridge and 
also after the second one, and the same 
effect continues further until reaching the 
top of the shelf-slope. Trailing oscillations 
also become rather strong in the rear of the 
leading wave as the ISW reaches the corner 
of the trapezoidal shelf and further on the 
slope itself. Although the first and second 
triangular ridges seem to have little  effect on 
the leading wave, the basin between these 
two ridges may trigger some minor 
oscillations perhaps due to localized vortex 
formation.

4. Statistical results 
 
The effects of the ridge height, the 
depth of the lower water layer, and the 
potential energy on the propagation of the 
ISW are all considered. The results from the 
laboratory experiments are shown in the data 
sets. The amplitudes of the incident and 
reflected waves are also included. The 
dependent variables for the binary logistic 
regression model are classified into two 
groups, weak and strong, based on the 
amplitude incident rate. When the 
hypothetical incident rate >0.5 it is 
considered strong and when it is < 0.5 it is 
considered weak. The frequencies for the 
strong and weak levels are 35 and 28, 
respectively.

deviance Chi-squares test are used. The

When testing the null hypothesis for

have coefficients of zero. According to the

The results obtained after rerunning the

after the removal of some of the

(Likelihood ratio, Score, and Wald tests). 
For the likelihood ratio and score tests, the 
null hypothesis that ? is zero is rejected, 
but not for the Wald test. The seeming 
discrepancies in p-values obtained between 
the Wald test and the other two tests are a 
sign that the large-sample approximation is 
not stable.  
Exact logistic regression for binary 
outcomes can be utilized to provide an exact 
score test and an exact probability test for 
hypotheses where the parameters are equal 
to zero; these tests produce an exact p-value 
and a mid p-value.  
To test whether individual parameter 
estimates are zero, we also require point 
estimates of the parameters, an odds ratio 
that contains two-sided confidence limits, 
and the p-value.     
The results of exact conditional

shelf-slope relationship, waveform

hydraulic jump on a slope with strong vortex 
formation. The  remaining momentum 
produced an ISW of elevation-type over the 
trapezoidal obstacle  and further on the 
horizontal crest. Therefore, a similar process 
of internal hydraulic jump with breaking and 
waveform conversion could be expected on 
a continental shelf-slope which could help 
resuspend nutrients and pollutants at rest on 
the seabed.

I（一）計畫中文摘要
潛沒式導流板為河岸保護工法的一種，應用設置在溪流底床上的一系列導流板，在下
游流場中產生尾跡渦流，並利用此尾跡渦流抵消河川彎道中的二次流，消除造成凹岸淘刷
的原因，達到保護河岸的目的。在前人的研究中，曾指出導流板的形狀對其導流效果乃至
保護河岸的功效有重要的影響，並陸續有研究者開發出各種型式的導流板，本研究擬針對
前人研究中尚未觸及的導流板形狀因子如板頂束縮(taper)、前傾(forward swept)、後傾(aft
swept)等形狀的變化對徑向床形所造成的影響加以研究。此外，由於單一導流板的影響範圍
有限，欲達到消除二次流的效果，通常須在一河道斷面上橫列設置數個導流板，才能達到
預期的效果。在已往的理論研究或現場案例中，皆將各導流板以等間距排列的方式加以設
置，然而，由於導流板彼此之間的交互作用及各板的入流條件不同，等間距排列可能並非
最佳的設置方式，本研究擬探討導流板系統在各種不同形狀及不同排列方式下對彎道徑向
床形所產生的影響，並應用此研究結果結合最佳化設計(Design Optimization)的理論，探討
不等間距排列的導流板系統的最佳設置位置，使導流板系統的導流效果能發揮最大的功
效。本計劃的研究結果可以對不同的導流板形狀及排列方式對彎道徑向床形所產生的影響
有更進一步的瞭解，並提供設置最佳導流板系統的準則，包括導流板的最佳形狀、最佳尺
寸、大小與水深的關係，以及最佳排列方式與設置位置等，以提高導流板系統在河岸保護
或其他應用上的效果，研究結果可提供相關研究者或工程師在理論研究或現場應用時的參
考。
關鍵字：潛沒式導流版、彎道徑向床形、導流板最佳形狀、導流板系統最佳設置
（二）計畫英文摘要
Submerged vane system is one of the techniques for river bank protection. Vane works by
changing the direction of the bend flow to eliminate the secondary current that is responsible for
erosion at the concave bank. It has been pointed out by previous researchers that vane shape has
great effect on flow training. This project plans to study the effect of vane shapes on bed profile
in river bends. Also, by adopting the technique of optimal analysis, the study plans to
investigate the possibility of developing an optimal vane that is efficient in flow training as well
as bank protection. Previous researchers also noted that, in a vane array, interactions between
vanes emerge. The study plans to investigate this phenomenon of vane interaction and its effect
on bed profile, and furthermore, by introducing the technique of optimal analysis, to investigate
the possibility of determining the optimal configuration of the vane system.
Keywords: submerged vane, transverse bed profile, optimal vane shape, optimal configuration
of vane system

方向形成一垂直分佈，最後在板頂降至零。根據 Helmholtz 的渦流定理，一渦流的強度在
流場中必須維持定值，因此，當範束渦流的強度由潛板的基部向板頂逐漸減弱時，在潛板
尾部的下游區域將形成一漩渦層(Vortex Ssheet)，以保持整體渦流的強度不變。潛板即藉由
此漩渦層發揮導流的功能，同時也藉由漩渦層在河床上產生側向剪應力，使底床沉滓產生
側向運移，達到調整床形的作用。

Problem Formulation
Following Sanders et al.’s suggestion (1983), as the first stage of design for the
placement of a monitoring network, this study will focus on the determination of the
macrolocations only, i.e., to decide which river reach, or link, of a river should place a
sampling station. For simplicity, two assumptions have been made: (1) the flow is fully
mixed at the downstream end of each river reach, and (2) there is no backwash upstream
into the river and the pollutant materials flow downstream only. Based on these
assumptions, the sampling stations will be placed at the downstream end of a reach,
immediately before the next confluence. Also, one sampling station is always located
at the outlet of the river system ensuring that all the river reaches are within the
surveillance range of the monitoring network.
Geometrical data of the river reaches
Basic geometrical data for the river system is first extracted to serve as a data base
for later use in the optimization process. The data required are (1) the connection
relations of the river reaches in the river system, and (2) the lengths of each river
reaching the system. To construct the connection relation between the river reaches,
each river reach is labeled with an integer serial code number. These serial numbers
are then recorded in pair for each river reach and the associated downstream reach. A
computer code was developed to reconstruct the connection relation of the river system
based on these records. The digital elevation model (DEM) for the study area is
analyzed by using the ARC/GIS geographic information system to extract the lengths of
the river reaches. The data is then recorded in associated with the serial codes for each
of the river reaches. These data, together with the record of the downstream link for
each river reach, are then exported as an ASCII file for later use. Table 1 gives an
example for the data structure extracted from a simple hypothetical river system shown
in Fig. 1.

4A Genetic Algorithm (GA) is adopted in this study to serve as an optimization tool
due to its ability on searching for the optimal solution in a discontinuous search space
(Goldberg, 1989), which is a feature particularly important to present study since the
objective function defined in Eq. (2) is not varied continuously with the spatial
distribution of the monitoring stations. Also, since a river system usually consists of a
large number of river reaches and each river reach could potentially be selected as a
monitoring site, the search space of the problem could be quite large and the problem
might possibly be having a number of local optimal solutions. To find the global
optimum, the optimization tool must have the ability to jump out of the local optima.
GAs have shown good performance in this aspect (Goldberg, 1989).

After a population of chromosomes for the candidate monitoring networks has been
produced, each binary chromosome is decoded back to the integer serial code numbers
of the selected river reaches each will be a monitoring site. The river system is divided
into several subcatchments based on the spatial distribution of these monitoring sites.
The accumulative length of the reaches in each subcatchment is then calculated based on
the geometrical data of the river system extracted by GIS. The performance of each
candidate monitoring network is evaluated by the objective function of Eq. (2). The
next generation of monitoring networks is then generated according to the performance
of the monitoring networks in present generation.
By utilizing the Genetic Algorithm in the optimization procedure, the searching for
the optimal monitoring network becomes an evolution process. The population of
monitoring networks evolves and gradually improves their performance. After several
generations of evolution, the optimal monitoring network might eventually emerge out
of the others. The iteration procedure is described in the following:
Step 1. The chromosomes for the first generation of monitoring networks are generated
randomly.
Step 2. The chromosome segments for each candidate monitoring network are then
decoded back to the sN integer serial code numbers of the river reaches
selected as monitoring sites.
Step 3. For each candidate monitoring network, the river system is divided into several
subcatchments based on the spatial distribution of the monitoring sites. The

In this case study, the methodology is extended for use in more restrictive situations.
The spatial distribution of a 3-station monitoring network is to be decided for the
Lanyang River System. However, due to budgetary constraints, the possible locations
to setup the sampling stations are restricted to the 7 existing flow gauge stations on the
river system only. The computation time for this case study took about 2 minutes on
the same machine. The optimal placement of the network thus obtained is presented in
Fig. 3. As shows, the best locations for the sampling sites are the Jiayuan Bridge
station, the Lanyang Bridge station, and the Singlang Bridge station. The expected cost
for this monitoring network to locate a point source of pollution in the area is 8.29,
which is a little higher than that of the previous case study due to the extra constraint.

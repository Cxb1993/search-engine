中文摘要 
 
本計畫之主要目的是期望在資訊不完整的代理人協商環境中，藉由學習對手
的行為模式，來更為貼近現實世界中的解題情境，提昇協商結果的品質及代理人
的整體效能。 
首先，本研究以模糊限制為基礎，建構完整的代理人協商架構，包括協商流
程、訊息交換協定及協商計算模式。然後，在此協商架構下，探討學習對手行為
模式的相關議題，涵括對手行為模式的學習架構及其計算模式。藉由對手的交談
訊息中，代理人可以歸納出對手的(1)喜好函數、(2)議題重要性、及(3)協商策略
等特性來掌握對手的行為狀態。另外，本計畫也利用協商案例庫，比對出近似案
例，進而預測對手可能的讓步行為，加速達成協商的預期目標。 
針對學習計算模式，本計畫提出兩種學習演算法：貝式及模糊機率，探討對
於不同協商情境的效能。實驗數據，包括收斂速度及協商結果，顯示貝氏學習比
較適用在長期穩定的協商環境，而模糊機率學習則比較有利於變動頻繁的協商環
境，推理出接近對手的行為模式。 
 
關鍵詞：智慧型系統、多重代理人系統、軟體代理人協商、模糊限制 
 
Abstract 
 
This project presents a belief learning model to approach the real-world scenario of 
problem solving. The main purpose of this work is to improve the quality of 
negotiation and the overall performance of the agents by learning the opponent’s 
behaviors model during an incomplete information environment. 
 
We first propose a fuzzy constraint-directed framework of agent negotiation 
including basic definitions, the process, the protocol of message exchange, and the 
computational models of negotiation. Then, we investigate the relative issues of 
exploring the opponent’s behavior model involving the learning element and the 
computational models of the opponent based on the framework. According to the 
messages coming from the opponent, an agent can deduce the features of opponent’s 
(1) preference functions, (2) the importance of issues, and (3) the negotiation strategy 
to handle the opponent’s behavior state. Furthermore, we also construct the instance 
repository for matching the proximate instances to predict the opponent’s next 
feasible proposals and to speed up the achievement of the expected negotiation goal. 
 
We propose two kinds of learning models for the learning computational models, 
the Bayesian and the fuzzy probability learning models, to apply to different 
negotiation environments. The data of numerical experiments including the speed of 
convergence and the quality of negotiation reveal that the Bayesian learning method is 
good at regularizing the opponent’s behavior models in long-term stable environments 
and the fuzzy probability learning approach is adept in approximating the opponent’s 
behavior patterns in volatile environments.

2.以模糊限制為基礎之軟體代理人協商 
 
  軟體代理人協商達到彼此間互相可接受的協議，與模糊限制滿足問題找出滿
足在分散式模糊限制網路中所有限制式的解可被視為是相同的。因此，許多方法
[5, 7, 9, 15, 16]將軟體代理人協商模塑成一個分散式模糊限制滿足問題，或是一
個按優先順序處理的模糊限制滿足問題，來找出軟體代理人共同可接受的滿足結
果或協議。

  綜合表 1 至表 4 所得的實驗結果得知： 
 
 未具學習能力之模糊限制代理人協商方法，以相似度函數(similarity 
function)來促使代理人協商目標的達成，它只利用每一回合對對手短視
的認知，來調整代理人自己的讓步值，期使達成協商目標；然而，由於
缺乏對對手長期行為規則的預測，致使平均的協商結果並未能如代理人
的協商目標所預期。 
 貝氏學習法，不僅蒐集對手協商行為規則的變化，再根據其發生的頻率
賦予相對的發生機率，以機率的高低預測對手可能協商行為；然而，貝
氏學習法雖可對對手進行長期行為規則的預測，但它的方法是以各個可
能協商值的假設乘以其發生的機率，因此，常會忽略發生機率小的可能
協商值假設，而有誤判情勢的情形發生，故貝氏學習法較適合運用在具
長期穩定協商行為模式的協商環境。 
 模糊機率學習法以叢集的觀念為基礎，以與現階段對手行為模式相類似
或鄰近的模式，來預測對手的行為模式，也可對對手進行長期行為規則
的預測；但因使用叢集為學習的依據，當類似個案發生過，不論其機率
高低，亦可延用類似個案的行為模式來進行協商，故較適合運用在變動
頻繁的協商環境中。 
 
由表 1 至表 4 的實驗結果也驗證了觀察的結果，在長期穩定的協商環境中，
雖然具學習能力的協商方法均較未具學習能力者為佳，但明顯地，貝氏學習法的
協商結果優於模糊機率學習法；相對地，在變動頻繁的協商環境中，模糊機率學
習法的協商結果反優於貝氏學習法的協商結果。

The intent of a distributed fuzzy constraint network
(U ,X,C), written ΠU,X,C, meaning the set of solutions of
DFCN can be regarded as an agreement among agents that
satisfy all of demands. No agent knows about its opponents’
feasible proposals and possible agreements a priori. Agents
take turns to propose offers to explore potential agreements,
thereby moving the negotiation toward a consensus.
The overall course of agent negotiation is a consecutive
process of offer exchange. The following aggregated satis-
faction value of the solutions is defined to evaluate offers or
counteroffers and to find more satisfactory outcomes.

If the aggregated satisfaction value of counteroffer is
superior to the one of offer at current or next round for
agent k and the counteroffer is one of feasible proposals or a
potential agreement in ΠU,X,C , then agent k will accept the
counteroffer as an agreement. Otherwise, agents will perform
the negotiation until one of agents withdraws.
B. Agent Negotiation Strategy
Negotiation strategies determine how agents evaluate and
generate offers to reach an agreement that is most in their
self-interest.
1) Concession Strategy: An agent makes a concession
by decreasing its previously aggregated satisfaction value to
generate an offer from a certain solution space. In that space,
the satisfaction degrees of the constraints associated on the
solutions equal or exceed a certain threshold of acceptability.
2) Tradeoff Strategy: An agent generates and develops
alternatives in a specific solution space without reducing its
aggregated satisfaction value. In that space, the degrees of
satisfaction in the constraints associated with the solutions
equal or exceed a particular threshold.
III. OPPONENT’S BELIEFS LEARNING
Learning opponent’s profile, it may be useful to reason
not only about what is true and what is false, but also
about what is not known. This research proposed a fuzzy
constraint-directed approach, including two learning mech-
anisms, strategy identification and fuzzy instance matching,
and one domination method, adaptive interaction, to present
the learning abilities of an agent.

A. Strategy Identification
In negotiation, the negotiator will deploy specific strategic
profiles in response to actions from the opponent. The strate-
gic profiles depend on a certain rule of negotiator behavior.
Many researches [4], [10], [19] introduce the different views
of concession scale making by an agent as the negotiation
strategies. To capture an opponent’s negotiation strategy, the
variances of concession scales coming from the concession
values at adjacent negotiation rounds is inspected . The
Bayesian learning approach uses the probability distributions
of concession value to classify such variances and to identify
the opponent’s strategy. However, it still has the problem of
slow convergence toward a correct prediction.
Instead of using concession value like Bayesian method,
this research adopts a fuzzy concession value to cluster
proximate paradigms for alleviating the problem of slow
convergence toward a correct prediction. To demonstrate the
speed of convergence toward proximate behavior regularities,
there are 500 negotiation instances to be observed and the
missing rate of locating in a specific classification or a fuzzy
cluster for a counteroffer is illustrated as Fig. 1.
In this experiment, the Bayesian classification and the
fuzzy clustering approaches are measured. The set of classi-
fications/fuzzy clusters of the prior 50 negotiation instances
are used as the base of comparison. The missing rates upon
the previous 100 instances for the Bayesian classification and
the fuzzy clustering are 50.69% and 14.9%. Apparently, the
missing rate for the fuzzy clustering is less than the Bayesian
classification. Even though the 500 instances have been run,
the missing rate for the fuzzy clustering 2% is also superior
than the Bayesian classification 10.26%.
The Bayesian learning approach classifies counteroffers
with probability distributions in some of the mutually ex-
cluding and exhaustive categories. However, the offers or
the counteroffers may be changeable among the real world
instances even in the same negotiation scenario. Hence, the
missing rates for the Bayesian classification method are
frequent. The fuzzy clustering approach discriminates cluster
from the proximate fuzzy concession values to identify the
proximate cluster. It relaxed the boundary that a counteroffer
either belongs or does not belong to a cluster. Therefore, the
missing rates for the fuzzy clustering would be smaller. The
problem of slow convergence toward a correct prediction on
the opponent’s behavior regularities would be also alleviated.

To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed learning
model, experiments are conducted in Section 4 followed by
an example application in Section 5.
IV. EXPERIMENTS
A simple multi-issue negotiation consisting of one buyer
and one seller is described as follows.
• Negotiation issues are price and delivery time.
• The interval values are [1000-4000] for price and [0-14]
for delivery time. The membership function associated
with each issue is monotonic decreasing and generated
by random distribution.
• The urgency values which partially represent mental
state of negotiating agents are 0.1 for buyer agent and
0.07 for seller agent.
• The initial order is random. Then, the buyer and the
seller take turns to submit proposals.
In these experiments, the Bayesian approach is based on
Zeuthen’s negotiation strategy in [16] along with a tradeoff
strategy presented by Faratin et al. in [5]. Based on the work
in [11], fuzzy constraint-based agent negotiation (FCAN) ap-
plying a simple learning heuristic, (i.e. a similarity function),
is also used as a baseline model to compare the performance
with the proposed model. Additionally, with same evaluation
standard, the utilities of negotiating issues for the Bayesian
learning are normalized between 0 and 1. After 100 instances
for each arrangement has been run, the negotiation outcomes
are summarized in Table I.
Each dotted-line block in Table I denotes the results by
various beliefs learning model of the buyer agent, while the
seller agent adopts a fixed particular learning model.
In block 1, the seller agent selects the Bayesian learning
approach to negotiate with the buyer agent. Owing to a
smaller urgency (0.07) resulting in a less concession, the
seller agent easily dominates the buyer agent employing
either Bayesian or FCAN approaches. It can be observed
that, while buyer agent applies FCAN-Comp and FCAN-
Win approaches, making a less concession, to keep its ASV
higher and DASV lower respectively, seller agent has less
to lose from a conflict and will be less willing to concede.
Such results, as expected, bring a higher ASV for the buyer
agent and a small DASV, but it takes more iterations to
reach an agreement. When the buyer agent adopts the FCAN-
Coop approach to make more concessions for reaching a
less negotiation rounds, the seller agent also makes many
concessions for avoiding running into conflict. Expectably,
the number of negotiation rounds is the lowest.
Similarly, the urgency of an agent determines the negotia-
tion outcome for Bayesian and FCAN approaches in block 2.
Additionally, the buyer agent will not be willing to concede
more in Bayesian because of a larger urgency (0.1). The
outcome takes slightly more iterations. Meanwhile, due to
a higher initial urgency, the FCAN-Comp and the FCAN-
Win approaches will expend more iterations than the one
of FCAN to gain the advantage of ASV and minimize the
DASV, respectively. Since the FCAN-Win approach is to
come up with the ASV of opponent agent not overtake it,
the average rounds of FCAN-Win is smaller than the one of
FCAN-Comp. The FCAN-Coop approach concede as more
as it can to speed up reaching an agreement. Hence, the
outcome gets a less average number of rounds more easily
than other approaches.
In other blocks, a similar result can be observed from
the data in other blocks. The outcomes of the FCAN-
Comp approach show that seller agent obtains higher av-
erage utilities/ASVs than the ones of buyer agent but it
takes more negotiation rounds. The overall results of the
FCAN-Win approach for seller agent reveal that the average
difference of utilities/DASV and the average JASV are both
superior than others. The FCAN-Coop approach for seller
agent, the overall consequences demonstrate that the average
negotiation iterations have the smallest costs against the other
approaches.
In summary the results from Table I suggest that
• the Bayesian learning method makes concession deci-
sion based on how much it has to lose by running into
conflict at that time. It usually results in a compromise
between agent’s and the belief of opponent’s utilities. It
is good at achieving a less conflict bargain.
• The FCAN approach applies the variance of satisfaction
degrees for counteroffers to get the opponent’s simple
beliefs, and utilizes the similarity function to support
negotiating agent’s desire. It does not further refine the
opponent’s beliefs to modify its concession value toward
expected outcome.

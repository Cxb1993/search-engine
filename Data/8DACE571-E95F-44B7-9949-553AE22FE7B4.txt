應用Mitchell利害關係人理論建構一個考量 
多元顧客需求之改良式QFD技術 
Developing a Multiple-customer-needs-based Revised 
QFD Technique Using Mitchell’s Stakeholder Theory 
 
摘要 
 
本文之主要目的係使用Mitchell的利害關係人理論，發展一個考量多元顧客
需求之改良式 QFD 技術。在日益競爭的環境下，追求顧客滿意已成為組織成功
的關鍵因素。QFD 是透過標準作業程序滿足顧客需求的技術，但是大部分研究
都僅針對如何滿足單一顧客群的需求進行探討。為達成本研究目的，本研究使用
Mitchell 的利害關係人理論，嘗試發展一個考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD 技
術。本研究提出之改良式 QFD 技術包含三個階段的多元顧客需求展開過程。最
後，為說明本論文所提出技術之可行性，本研究以國道五號蘇花高速公路興建決
策做為實證個案。 
關鍵詞：利害關係人理論、QFD、顧客滿意、公共政策 
 
ABSTRACT 
The objective of this paper is to develop a multiple-customer-needs-based 
revised QFD technique by using Mitchell’s stakeholder theory. In responding to this 
enhanced competition, enterprises increasing recognize that customer satisfaction is 
crucial for success. QFD is a well-established technique on developing standard 
operational procedures to meet customers’ needs. However, most studies have been 
restricted to satisfy single customer needs. To fulfill this purpose, a 
multiple-customer-needs-based revised QFD technique is proposed in this study by 
using Mitchell’s stakeholder theory. This revised QFD technique involves a 
three-stage of multiple-customers’ needs development. In order to demonstrate how 
the proposed technique works, an empirical case study is conducted. A policy analysis 
for the no.5 national highway construction in Taiwan is chosen as the subject of the 
empirical case study. 
Keywords: Stakeholder Theory, QFD, Customer Satisfaction, Public Policy

一、利害關係人理論 
利害關係人被認為是公司必須對其負責的人，通常包含許多不同類型的關係
人。Freeman（1984）定義利害關係人為－「可以影響或是被影響來達成組織目
標的任何組織或是個人」， Johnson ＆ Scholes（2002）對利害關係人所做的描
述是－「這些個人或群體是依賴組織履行他們的目標，相反地，組織也必須依賴
這些個人以及群體才能達到目標或是生存」。對於利害關係人的確認，Mitchell
等人（1997）曾針對利害關係人管理提出三個主要的關係屬性：權力、合法性，
以及急迫性，並將之做為利害關係人確認的基礎（請參見圖 1）。

參、建構一個考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD技術 
基於研究背景與動機的論述，本研究使用Mitchell利害關係人理論建構一個
考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD技術。主要是由於過去 QFD對於顧客需求的滿
足展開活動中往往只考慮單一類型顧客需求，並無滿足及考量多元顧客需求。為
達成本研究目的，本研究利用Mitchell利害關係人理論的分類方式區分顧客類型
以及需求，並將以區分出的顧客類型及需求分階段導入 QFD，以確保每一個展
開階段中都能確實的考量到多元顧客的需求。 
首先，藉由利害關係人屬性的組合所組成之七類利害關係人進行區分，分
為：潛在的利害關係人（如：公司的員工）、自由的利害關係人（如：義工）、索
求的利害關係人（如：消費者）、主控的利害關係人（如：代理人，像是：董事
長、債權人等）、危險的利害關係人（如：對公司有訴求的人，比方說：罷工的
員工，環保人士等）、依賴的利害關係人（如：地方協會，像是：工會，社區協
會）以及決定的利害關係人（如：股東）七類，並且加以探討這些利害關係人的
態度（贊成、中立、反對）以及所關注的議題（如：蘇花高、兩岸三通、核四興
建案）。 
其次，並利用利害關係人顯著性理論得擁有屬性的多寡分成三群，並依照潛
伏、期待以及決定的利害關係人順序分三個階段導入顧客的需求。本研究採用分
階段導入不同利害關係人群體的原因是，現今顧客導向的時代中最終顧客需求必
須加以優先考量，因此在利害關係人需求展開階段首先將潛伏的利害關係人（即
最終顧客）加以導入，希望可以優先考量這一類型之利害關係人，雖然他們並不
會立即對組織營運產生影響，但是，在顧客導向的時代環境下，他們卻是非常重
要的一群人。接下來才將期待的利害關係人導入品質機能展開階段，主要的目的
是確認出潛伏的利害關係人所提出的需求設計方案亦符合期待利害關係人之需
 7
利害關人需求以及滿足程度的強弱，之後透過與權重（相關報導的次數）的計算
這些方案設計項目將可以進行排序並且同樣地將排名較為前面的項目（如：排名
前二分之一、三分之一）的方案設計項目進入到決定的利害關係人需求展開階
段，做為決定的利害關係人需求展開階段的方案設計項目。

歸類之方式分成七類的利害關係人（1.潛在的利害關係人、2.自由的利害關係人；
3.索求的利害關係人、4.主控的利害關係人、5.危險的利害關係人、6.依賴的利害

關報導資料所得出的關注議題（1.生態環保：1-1維護自然環境、1-2採行生態工
法；2.決策程序：2-1遵循行政程序、2-2擬訂配套措施、2-3注重環境評估；3.經
濟建設：3-1縮短城鄉差距、3-2發展觀光產業、3-3增建經濟建設；4.交通建設：
4-1強化鐵路系統、4-2維護現有公路）。

陸、結論與建議 
由於現今民主意識的高漲，政府機關在制定公共政策時所需考量的需求面也
越來越大，因此公共政策在制定時必須同時關注到不同利害關係人的需求，以全
面滿足各方面需求為首要目標。本研究以Mitchell利害關係人理論結合品質機能
展開法，建構出一個考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD 技術，主要是藉由滿足潛
伏的利害關係人需求方案設計，找出了可以同時滿足三種不同類型（潛伏的利害
關係人、期待的利害關係人以及決定的利害關係人）利害關係人的方案設計以達
到顧客導向之目的。最後本研究以國道五號蘇花高速公路興建案為例進行分析，
證明本研究發展出的考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD 技術可行性。實證個案分
析結果顯示，可以藉由某些關注議題的方案設計確實滿足不同類型之利害關係
人。本研究提出之考量多元顧客需求之改良式 QFD 技術，有助於確認公共政策
制定時考量多元利害關係人類型，以及瞭解利害關係人之對於公共政策之態度與
關注議題，透過品質機能展開的導入，篩選出能夠確實執行並且滿足多元利害關
係人需求之方案。除此之外，本研究與過去的四階段模式相比，除了考量多元顧
客需求之外，亦考量了滿足多元顧客需求之優先順序，透過從利害關係人顯著性
理論從最外圈的顧客需求逐一的篩選以及確認，讓組織考量顧客需求時更能達到
顧客導向的目的。 
此外，根據個案分析之結果，本研究認為並根據篩選出的利害關係人需求滿
足方案設計來滿足不同的利害關係人需求，透過三階段篩選所得出的方案設計將
可以確實的符合民意潮流，以及能夠確實滿足基層民意以及需求。而在制訂公共
政策時，民眾關注議題的方案設計是一項重要的工作，本研究在實證個案分析中
發現，由於各種不同利害關係人之間所關注的議題不儘相同，往往因為不同的需
求導致政策難以滿足或窒礙難行，因此透過本研究所提出的改良式 QFD 技術可
以逐漸篩選出符合民意的方案設計並且可以經由篩選的步驟找出能確實執行的
需求方案設計。建議可多透過各方面的專家評估以及配套方案的規劃，滿足各種
不同顯著性利害關係人的需求，以提升政府機關制定公共政策時可以達到最大效
益並且順應民意達到雙贏的局面。

Abstract. The objective of this paper is to develop a pubic policy analytic model by using Mitchell’s 
stakeholder identification and salience theory. Under the democratic political environment, the 
development of a public policy becomes a tough job to public managers because it is necessary to 
meet the conflicting needs which are derived from the multiple stakeholders. According to the 
empirical studies by Chen et al. [7, 8], it was evidenced that most public managers paid little 
attention on this issue due to it is too complicated to deal with. In order to resolve this dilemma, a 
public policy analytic model is developed in this paper by using Mitchell’s theory as the conceptual 
framework. Mitchell et al. [18] proposed a theory of stakeholder identification and salience which 
intended to generate a typology of stakeholders from three relationship attributes: power, legitimacy, 
and urgency. The model proposed in this paper is involved a two-stage and two-layer analytic 
procedure. 
1   Introduction 
Under the democratic political environment, the development of a public policy becomes a tough job to 
public managers because it is necessary to meet the conflicting needs which are derived from the multiple 
stakeholders. The word ‘stakeholder’ has assumed a prominent place in public and nonprofit management 
theory and practice in the last 20 years, and especially in the last decade. The term refers to persons, 
groups or organizations that must somehow be taken into account by leaders, managers and front-line 
staff [5]. Moreover, attention to stakeholders is important throughout the strategic management process 
because ‘success’ for public organizations — and certainly survival — depends on satisfying key 
stakeholders according to their definition of what is valuable [4, 19]. Managers in the public sector are 
therefore under constant pressure to improve the performance of their organizations. They are expected to 
satisfy the various stakeholders, increase the efficiency, effectiveness and quality of services, achieve 
organizational goals, and establish a culture of continuous improvement [6]. 
Despite the prevalence of stakeholder management, most studies have been restricted to private 
enterprise [5]. The issue of multiple stakeholders in the public sector in comparison to the focus of the 
private sector on customers presents difficulties that must be addressed in attempting to adapt private 
sector approaches [17]. Simply translating the language of the private sector with minor modifications is 
unlikely to be sufficient. Such an approach could result in the public sector reality being subsumed within 
that of a contorted private sector reality, which does not accurately or appropriately represent the interests 
and needs of the stakeholders [1]. In addition, stakeholder theory development has centered around two 
related streams [23]: (i) defining the stakeholder concept, and (ii) classifying stakeholders into categories 
that provide an understanding of individual stakeholder relationships. Bryson [5] has indicated that there 
is relatively little in the public and nonprofit literatures on exactly how to systematically identify and 
analyze stakeholders. According to the empirical studies by Chen et al. [7, 8], it was also evidenced that 
most public managers paid little attention on this issue due to it is too complicated to deal with.

2.2   The Social-Network Model 
One approach for understanding stakeholder environments is using concepts from social network model 
to examine characteristics of entire stakeholder structures and their impact on organizations’ behaviors, 
rather than individual stakeholder influences [22]. Employing social network concepts will generate an 
explicit theory of stakeholder influences based on the structural characteristics of an organization’s 
network of relationships [22]. The theory’s logic is derived from Oliver’s [21] examination of 
organizational responses to external influences and addresses this question: How does the structure of an 
organization’s stakeholder relationships affect its response to stakeholder pressures? This theory con-
tributes to stakeholder research by providing a mechanism for conceptualizing the simultaneous influence 
of multiple stakeholders and predicting organizational responses to these forces [22]. 
2.3   The Cause-Effect model 
The cause-effect model attempts to investigate the relationships between stakeholder management and 
firms’ financial performance or competitive advantage. Managing relationships with primary stake-
holders, however, can result in much more than just their continued participation in the firm. Effective 
stakeholder management—relations with primary stakeholders to include customers, employees, 
suppliers, community residents and the environment—can constitute intangible, socially complex 
resources that may enhance firms’ ability to outperform competitors in terms of long-term value creation 
[14]. Kaplan and Norton [15] argue that the drivers of financial performance are the relationships a com-
pany develops with customers and the relationships internal to the firm that shape customer relations and 
impact customer service. Legnick-Hall [16] also emphasizes the importance of loyalty-producing 
relationships with customers that extend beyond traditional firm boundaries as a source of competitive 
advantage.

3.3  Auxiliary layer 
There are seven stakeholder types that emerge from various combinations of the attributes: power, 
legitimacy, and urgency. The seven stakeholder types indicate [18]: (i) dormant- the relevant attribute of 
a dormant stakeholder is power. Dormant stakeholders possess power to impose their will on a firm, but 
by not having a legitimate relationship or an urgent claim, their power remains unused; (ii) discretionary- 
discretionary stakeholders possess the attribute of legitimacy, but they have no power to influence the 
firm and no urgent claims; (iii) demanding- demanding stakeholders, those with urgent claims but having 
neither power nor legitimacy, are the “mosquitoes buzzing in the ears” of managers: irksome but not 
dangerous, bothersome but not warranting more than passing management attention, if any at all; (iv) 
dominant- in the situation where stakeholders are both powerful and legitimate, their influence in the firm 
is assured, since by possessing power with legitimacy, they form the “dominant coalition” in the 
enterprise [9]; (v) dependent- stakeholders who lack power but who have urgent legitimate claims as 
“dependent,” because these stakeholders depend upon others (other stakeholders or the firm's managers) 
for the power necessary to carry out their will; (vi) dangerous- Mitchell et al. [18] suggest that where 
urgency and power characterize a stakeholder who lacks legitimacy, that stakeholder will be coercive and 
possibly violent, making the stakeholder “dangerous,” literally, to the firm; and (vii) definitive-

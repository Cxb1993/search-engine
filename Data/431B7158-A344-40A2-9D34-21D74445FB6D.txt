它利用氫與氧的電化學反應產生電力，可是現今科技仍有待重大突破，才能克服燃料電
池壽命過短和成本過高兩大關鍵問題。何時可有重大突破，使燃料電池從目前的展示階
段提升到商業階段，恐怕是誰也無法保證的。所以如何利用現有已成熟的技術，將之加
以改善後，使其可直接利用氫能，以達到潔能並減少二氧化碳排放之目的，是一個較快
速且具效率的首選解決方案。而氣渦輪發電機就是一例，因它已是一成熟的技術，由於
它可適用多種燃料(如天然氣、生質燃料和合成氣等等)，加上其廢氣汙染物排放低，因
此已經被普遍地用於發電及動力推進系統上。其中微型氣渦輪發電機為分散式發電系統，
若能將其改良，使之適用於無碳之氫氣燃料，也就是本研究欲研發之前瞻氫微型氣渦輪
機發電技術，將是未來滿足電能需求又可同時達到減少二氧化碳排放的少數有效方案之
一。  
氣渦輪發電機組，主要是由壓縮器、迴流式燃燒室、燃燒室內艙、渦輪及永磁發電
機等五個主要的組件組合而成，發電是利用被帶動的渦輪壓縮機將空氣壓縮後，經一大
型熱交換器預熱後送入燃燒室內，與噴入的天然氣混合燃燒後產生的高溫氣體推動渦輪
機，此渦輪機同時帶動產電用發電裝置及壓縮空氣用的壓縮機，最後排出的高溫廢氣再
通入熱交換器以利用其廢熱。轉動件係固定在單轉軸上，此單轉軸則由氣墊式軸承所支
撐，轉速可高達 96,000 rpm，發電機組係由空氣冷卻，故整個機組沒有使用潤滑油、冷
卻劑、馬達、齒輪箱或其他機構，若操作在貧油條件下，具有超低 NOx廢氣排放，故不
需要後置觸媒燃燒或其他的脫硝廢氣處理設備。而微型渦輪發電機的廢氣污染極低，且
維修需求為同等級發電機中最少者，加上它的體積小、佔地空間小、重量輕，運作時震
動低、操作時安靜，容易安裝於室內/室外/屋頂等任何地方，而且多機組組合時無需額
外設備即可擴充至 600 kW 以上，並可利透過遠端監控系統了解運作情況。雖然微型渦
輪機的優點甚多，但它運作時無法避免產生二氧化碳，因此我們利用此一裝置之優點並
加以改善它的循環，並使用重組器將二氧化碳轉換成氫與可燃之一氧化碳，以達到二氧
化碳減量之目的。  
天然氣燃氣渦輪機加入少許氫可提升燃燒效率並減少污染排放，這方面已有相關研
究[5-7]，但將純氫作為氣渦輪機之燃料，仍屬於研究階段，尚未有實際的產電裝置。主
要是因為氫的燃燒速度遠高於一般碳氫燃料，且火焰溫度也較高，加上它低燃點及低引
燃能量，進入渦輪機燃燒室後極易自燃產生爆震而損壞燃燒室及渦輪葉片。此外，若以
體積容量來估算，氫的熱值約是天然氣的三分之一(若以每公斤質量來估算則氫的熱值
約是天然氣的3.5倍)，因此要達到同樣發電量的渦輪轉速可能需要較大的氫體積流量，
否則無法達到預定之發電效率，故燃燒器之設計需要修改，以達到氣渦輪機最佳之發電
條件。由前述之研究背景，本研究所欲研發之前瞻氫微型氣渦輪機發電技術應具前瞻性，
因氫燃燒器在氣渦輪引擎內燃燒，是有可能超溫爆震，而氣渦輪引擎是不容超溫，否則
葉片容易燒壞或斷裂，故必須有獨特可行的氫燃燒器設計，而利用觸媒重組器同時作熱
的回收與CO2的化學回收，對整體效率的提升與CO2的減量將有重大的助益，目前國際
上尚未有類似的設計。

在無二氧化碳排放的渦輪發電機系統設計上，純氫氣燃燒或者混合碳氫燃料的氫氣
燃燒的溫度相當高，所以氮氧化物的排放以及燃燒室整體設計仍然是必須考慮的重點，
要避免氮氧化物的排放並減少渦輪機的損害就必須採用貧油低溫燃燒。由於氫氣可以在
超貧油(ultra lean)狀態下燃燒，故可直接引用超貧油燃燒，除此之外也可以將觸媒燃燒
的觀念引進本研究中。觸媒反應主要是藉著降低反應的活化能(activation energy)來增加
反應速率，觸媒的使用對我們來說並不陌生，因為車輛的廢氣排放系統中就有它的存在，
而運用在燃燒技術上則發展較晚。Davy [13]是首位在實驗中發現到白金線可以在常溫下
與可燃性氣體發生表面反應並釋放出溫度，到了 1970 年代 Pfefferle [14]利用汽車用觸媒
來進行觸媒燃燒實驗，經過實驗驗證發現污染物甚低而且燃燒效率非常高。利用白金作
為觸媒可以促進碳氫燃料以及氫氣燃料在貧油情況下進行燃燒，所以利用觸媒做為燃燒
室的內部結構也是一種相當可行的方式。 
目前在能源使用上，為了達到乾淨而且可再生的需求，初期利用氫搭配碳氫燃料在
渦輪引擎中是有潛力的燃料，然而因為回收燃料成份比例的不穩定性使得燃燒性能沒有
完整的理論可以加以描述，因此如果能夠針對這些燃料間互相影響的燃燒特性以及控制
這些燃燒現象之參數做詳細的釐清，並探討設計此類燃料燃燒之回授控制，則對於將來
利用此類多重成份燃料之鍋爐、燃燒器與渦輪機達到高效率高潔淨之設計將有極大的幫
助，最後藉著減少碳氫類燃料的使用更進一步推向全氫氣燃燒系統作為本研究之目標。 
1.3 研究規劃 
由研究背景與研究構想的討論中，我們了解氫氣渦輪機為一低污染且高效率之發電
技術，相較於燃料電池，此技術應更快能商品化及普及化。是故，我們進行短、中、長
期研究規劃(請參考表一之技術發展路程圖)，內容說明如下： 
(1) 短期研究規劃(2006/11 ~ 2009/10，第一階段三年期研究計畫)  
我們鎖定發電範圍為1 ~ 10 kW的氫微型氣渦輪動力機組為短程之研發目標，並透過
重組器化學熱能回收技術進行二氧化碳減量技術之研發。依據短期規劃目標，本研究計
畫(前瞻氫微型氣渦輪機發電技術研發)將以三個子計畫來整合執行，子計畫一(機械-施
聖洋)為零CO2排放前瞻氫燃燒器研發：第一年度以氫燃燒器實作設計和觸媒燃燒為研究
重點；第二年度以分析氫加碳氫化合物(合成氣)燃燒特性為研究重點，並與子計畫二和
三(微型氣渦輪機和重組器)相互搭配組合調整；第三年度以純氫之氫燃燒器最佳化分析
和設計為研究重點，並與子計畫二和三相互配合作系統整合。子計畫二(航空-趙怡欽)
為前瞻氫燃氣輪機組合設計與分析測試：第一年度以微型氣渦輪機的循環分析與操作測
試為研究重點；第二年度以微型氣渦輪機之改裝、組合與測試為研究重點，並與子計畫
一(氫燃燒器)相互搭配組合調整；第三年度以重組器(子計畫三)併入微型氣渦輪機並完
成整體系統測試分析為研究重點。子計畫三(化材-陳郁文)為微型氣渦輪機尾氣化學熱回

3-1-a： 以鎳金屬觸媒，在反應溫度大約在500~600°C左右進行，高溫反應容易發生

第二年計畫：研發H2/CO的分離薄膜技術  
3-2-a： 使用鈀膜來進行分離研究，僅允許氫氣通過之鈀金屬裝配在薄膜內，故可
生產高純度氫氣而無需轉化器和純化系統之幫助。  
3-2-b： 利用吸附劑來做分離，例如分子篩對CO與H2的吸附能力不同，可以選擇對

具相似性特徵。因此，所有碳氫燃料火焰面之位置均會脫離燃燒器噴嘴，並維持在燃燒

的燃燒特性與碳氫燃料全然不同，由於氫之燃燒速度高於碳氫燃料，氫火焰易附著於燃
燒器噴嘴，因而影響氫火焰之穩定性，故原先適用於碳氫燃料的操作條件必須作進一步
的修正以適用以氫為主的燃料。 
除了提高燃燒效率並降低汙染物排放外，改善燃燒器之燃燒穩定性也一直是研究重
點方向。因燃燒不穩定所引起的其中一個特徵就是大振幅的壓力振盪，且通常會與熱釋
放所引起的壓力擾動相互作用，導致燃燒器效率降低並形成噪音。採用添加氫氣的方法
來增加燃燒器效率與穩定性方面的研究也已有不少文獻[37-39]。其中 Schefer et al. [38]
便指出加氫可在不影響汙染物的排放情形下，提高天燃氣之貧油燃燒穩定性。雖然，
Barbosa et al. [40]於燃燒器中央以 coflow 配置加裝一氫燃料噴射器，並在噴射氣出口安
裝葉片式漩渦產生器，作為主動控制此類易發生於貧油預混燃燒中之不穩定特性的方法。
研究結果指出添加氫氣確實有助於快速降低壓力振盪的振幅，但對於熱膨脹所致的擾動
卻無直接的改善。此外，加氫對於 NOx排放並未造成顯著的影響，但卻引起 CO 之排放
量大幅提升。

圖十為本研究第一代系統測試平台實體照片圖，照片右上方為渦輪增壓器，其下方
有一主燃燒器(未顯示於圖上)，右下方是氣體分流器，而照片左上方左、右分別是發電
機與動力渦輪，最後，在動力渦輪下方依序為燃燒室及燃燒器。此系統之工作原理簡述
如下。主燃燒器的功用為提供轉動渦輪增壓器所需的動力，當甲烷或甲烷加氫燃料與空
氣注入其內後，負責在燃燒室內艙中維持穩定的燃燒反應。燃燒反應所產生的高溫生成
物氣體在離開內艙前，會與環繞其外壁流動的大量冷卻空氣混合降溫，藉此降低生成物
氣體溫度防止葉輪材料因超溫燒壞。高溫氣體離開燃燒室後緊接著會進入渦輪增壓器膨
脹作功，進而轉動渦輪增壓器之渦輪葉輪，同一轉軸另一側的壓縮機葉輪獲得動能後，
便可吸入外界空氣進並壓縮進而提高氣體整體壓力。與渦輪增壓器之壓縮機吐氣口相連
接之氣體分流器，便負責將壓縮空氣分配至主燃燒器與次燃燒器。離開渦輪增壓器之渦
輪側的氣體會經由廢氣排放口(照片中央)排出，並藉由抽風設備帶出實驗室外排放至大
氣中。待渦輪增壓器轉速高至足以提供主燃燒器穩定運作所需之空氣量，便達成渦輪系
統之起動，此時外接之空氣源便可以移除。主燃燒器驅動渦輪增壓器以製造大量的壓縮
空氣的主要功用，一為提供主燃燒器自身運轉所需之燃燒用空氣與冷卻用空氣，二為提
供次燃燒器運轉所需之空氣，故主燃燒器與渦輪增壓器可視為一氣源供應器。當有大量
的壓縮空氣被分配至次燃燒器作為燃燒用空氣與冷卻用空氣時，在次燃燒器所進行之燃
燒反應所產生的高溫氣體便全用來推動動力渦輪。動力渦輪獲得能量後渦輪葉輪便開始
轉動，當轉速不高時輸出的軸功也不高，必須待渦輪轉速拉高後再經由減速機將低扭矩

本研究所採用之發電機主要是選用汽車用之交流發電機，除了發電機外，整個電力

使轉動之轉子線圈產生時變磁場，促使發電機開始發電，二為將交流發電機之部分輸出
電能轉化成可儲存之化學能。一般來說，相較於直流發電機，交流發電機具有在低轉速
條件下仍可輸出大電流的優點，因此交流發電機在扭矩足夠的條件下，應該是個不錯的
選擇。不過，本研究之重點為微型氣渦輪發電系統，受限於燃燒器尺寸、燃料種類與進
氣流量等，動力渦輪之輸出軸功無法與汽車活塞引擎相比，故我們也使用一些改裝自散
熱風扇發電量較小的直流馬達。而本研究所選用的車用交流發電機有多種款式，主要差
異在其發電量，範圍介於 500 W~1,250 W。交流電發電機之基本發電原理簡介如下，當
發電機內部之轉子線圈有電流通過時便會產生磁場，經由動力渦輪機提供動力使得轉子
線圈開始轉動，根據電磁感應原理得知，在受到不斷變換的磁場方向與強度作用下，位
在發電機機殼上的定子線圈內便會形成不斷變換方向與強度的感應電位，進而產生交流
電流，然後藉由二極體整流形成直流電流。此外，隨著動力渦輪提供的轉速不斷升高，
發電機之感應電位也會持續增高，可能導致輸出電壓過高損壞負載端組件。因此，發電
機內建有(或需搭配)一個具調節電壓能力，可以依轉子線圈轉速大小，調整進入轉子線
圈之激磁電流的大小，使發電機維持穩定電壓輸出的電壓調整器。例如，在低轉速時要
提高激磁電流，但在高轉速時便要降低激磁電流。負載元件則是讓電流得以輸出的耗能
電器，如電瓶本身就是一個負載元件，為了估算發電機輸出功率，於實驗中我們並聯數
個可任意開關之負載電路，藉以瞭解在每個操作條件下之最大輸出功率。整個電路量測
裝置內共使用 1 個伏特計與 6 個安培計，可量測發電機之輸出總電壓與總電流、轉子線
圈之激磁電流、定子線圈(Y 型接法)之中性線電流以及負載電路之電流等 5 種數據。

4.1 改良前後燃燒器火焰特性 
噴嘴混合式燃燒器的燃燒狀態屬於非預混之擴散火焰，其噴嘴出口原始外觀如圖十

提高燃燒器噴嘴出口的混合效率，藉此提高火焰的燃燒速度進而縮短火焰長度。因此，
我們於燃燒器之燃料管出口處安裝一燃料噴射器，安裝後燃料噴射方式以及燃燒器噴嘴

排每排 8 個呈輻射狀分佈之燃料噴射孔，所有噴射孔之孔徑皆為 1.5 mm。相較於燃燒
器原先的平行噴流混合機制，燃料噴射器可以讓燃料與空氣噴流以 90°夾角正交對沖，
進而提升混合效果。另外，為了近一步提高混合、縮短火焰長度，我們進一步設計安裝
一漩渦產生器，用以搭配燃料噴射器使用，其安裝位置位於燃料噴射器上游，安裝後之
噴嘴出口外觀如圖十八(c)所示(以 Type III 表示)。 
圖二十四為此三種不同燃燒器型式之火焰影像，由左至右依序為 Type I、II 與 III，
實驗所採用之燃料/空氣當量比(φ)皆為化學劑量燃燒條件(φ = 1)，並固定流場雷諾數 Re = 
LV/ν = 8800，其中 L 為燃燒器噴嘴內徑、V 為噴嘴出口平均流速而ν為空氣之運動黏滯

度比未經改良的圖二十四(a)之 Type I 火焰的一半長度還要小。此外，我們可以發現三種
不同型式的混合方式，也會改變火焰的結構。Type I 的火焰由於混合較慢，且在噴嘴出
口處僅有局部混合燃燒反應較不強烈，因此火焰根部較細，而混合較佳的位置則發生在
噴嘴出口往下游方向約 8 cm 處，亦即火焰寬度最寬的位置。會導致前述結果的主要原
因為，此噴嘴混合式燃燒器混合最強的位置，會發生在中央燃氣噴流與圍繞周圍之空氣
噴流的交互作用最強烈的區域，而噴流本身在剛自噴嘴噴射出時其擴張角較小，因交互
作用的區域小所以交互作用便不強烈，故交互作用最強烈的區域通常會出現噴流擴張角
較大的地方，也就是在比較下游的位置。整體而言，Type I 燃燒器由於混合能力較差的
緣故，其火焰長度較 Type II 與 III 長。然而，Type II 的火焰乃因噴嘴內混合效率提高，
火焰最寬部位乃位於噴嘴出口處，並可推斷噴嘴內的燃燒狀態相當劇烈。Type III 藉由
漩渦流場可進一步強化混合，所以火焰長度又大幅縮短(圖二十四(b))，此外，由於漩渦
流場會使噴嘴出口流場發散，將原本較長且窄細的火焰轉化成較短且寬的火焰，故火焰
最寬處發生於發散流場最強的區域。

最後，我們於 Type III 燃燒器中使用甲烷燃料並添加不同比例之氫氣，進行一系列
定性的火焰觀測。圖四十顯示在固定 Re = 8,800 與φ = 0.6 的條件下，純甲烷以及四種不
同加氫比例之火焰影像。仔細比較圖四十各影像我們可以發現，。在使用部分氫氣取代
甲烷時，火焰外觀會逐漸顯現出兩種結構，分別是位於火焰中心的噴焰結構與火焰根部
向外發散的漩渦火焰結構，但即使火焰結構被改變了，火焰的燃燒狀態依舊相當穩定。
並且，我們也發現火焰可穩定燃燒的當量比界限，隨著加氫量的提升有明顯延伸的趨勢，
且延伸的幅度取決於氫氣添加量的多寡。雖然由實驗測試結果得知，本 Type III 燃燒器
在未作進一步改良前，便可同時適用於甲烷與部分甲烷加氫燃料，但這種因添加氫氣所
形成的雙重火焰結構，仍必須經過嚴謹的實驗觀察以及審慎地研究評估後，才能確認現
階段的燃燒器是否可以適用於更高的加氫比例，因此甲烷加氫燃料的特性的深入研究也
是我們在不久的將來會繼續努力的課題。

tion, such as the increase of burning velocities, the
extension of lean flammability limit of methane com-
bustion, and the reduction of unburned hydrocarbons,
several unresolved problems still remain.
It is still a challenge for experimentalists to mea-
sure an accurate value of the turbulent burning ve-
locity (ST), which influences virtually all important
properties of premixed turbulent flames, especially
when hydrocarbon fuel doping with hydrogen or other
diluents is considered. What are the effects of gas
velocities and pressure rise due to turbulent combus-
tion in a confined chamber on ST measurements with
hydrogen addition at high Reynolds numbers? What
exactly is the effect of hydrogen addition on values
of ST at different values of φ? How does the so-
called bending effect of ST/SL vs u′/SL plots vary for
lean turbulent premixed methane flames with H2 or
CO2 addition? Is there a general correlation of ST in
terms of strain rates for these lean premixed turbulent
CH4/H2/air flames? The objective of this study is to
address the above questions by measurements of tur-
bulent flame speeds, gas velocities, and pressure rise
during turbulent combustion using pairs of ion-probe
sensors and pressure transducers in a large fan-stirred
cruciform burner. Particular interest is on investiga-
tion of the bending effect at high u′/SL (the leveling
off of ST/SL curves), for which H2 and/or CO2, as
additives are applied in an attempt to diminish and/or
enhance such bending effects.
The difficulties in measuring values of ST/SL ac-
curately are mainly due to very wide ranges of tempo-
ral and spatial scales in high-Reynolds-number chem-
ically reacting flows [16,17]. This is why previous
experimental ST data (e.g., [18–20]) have all fea-
tured a very large scatter even for a given mixture in
the same burner with fixed values of u′/SL and φ.
These burners, like all other burners, suffer various
degrees of unsteadiness, inhomogeneity, significant
mean strain, and/or narrow space that can compli-
cate the accuracy of ST measurements. To improve
the accuracy of ST measurements, Shy et al. [21,22]
proposed a new methodology for direct and quan-
titative measurement of ST using two sets of ion-
collecting electrodes in a cruciform burner that con-
sisted of a long vertical vessel and a large horizontal
vessel. The long vertical vessel is used to provide a
sizable downward-propagating premixed flame. The
large horizontal vessel, equipped with a pair of coun-
terrotating fans and perforated plates, is used to gen-
erate intense isotropic turbulence in the central region
between the two perforated plates. Thus, flame propa-
gation and its interaction with intense isotropic turbu-
lence are not influenced by the ignition source and the
unwanted turbulence from the walls. This burner is
used in the present study. We measure quantitatively,
for the first time, turbulent burning velocities (ST/SL)
of lean premixed methane–air mixtures with various
hydrogen additions from 10 to 30% in volume at three
different equivalence ratios, φ = 0.6, 0.7, and 0.8,
over a wide range of turbulent intensities (u′/SL) up
to 40. These results with H2 addition are compared to
data without H2 addition as well as data with CO2 or
N2 addition, so that effects of diluents, φ, and u′/SL
on ST/SL and the associated bending effect can be
investigated.

the burner, and (3) pressure and emission measure-
ments. These data are then applied to investigate
effects of gas velocities, pressure rise, and hydro-
gen or carbon dioxide additions on turbulent burning

Mixture compositions are determined by both the
total fuel/air equivalence ratio (φ) and the hydrogen
volume fraction (δ) as an additive in the total fuel
gases:
(1 − δ)CxHy + δH2 + Xo(O2 + 3.762N2).
Here φ = [(x + y/4)(1 − δ) + δ/2]/Xo, where x = 1
and y = 4 for methane and Xo is the oxygen mole
number. This means that hydrogen is used as a fuel to
replace methane in the total fuel gases for the present
lean methane combustion with hydrogen additions.
Before a run, lean methane and air mixtures with var-
ious hydrogen additions (δ = 0–30%), at three differ-
ent values of φ = 0.6, 0.7, and 0.8, were well mixed in
the mixing chamber (see Fig. 1). Then these mixtures
at fixed δ and φ were injected into the evacuated cru-
ciform burner. A run began with ignition from the top
of the burner. For CO2 or N2 diluents, the experimen-
tal procedures are the same. It should be noted that ap-
propriate diluting percentages of CO2 (large radiation
heat loss) or N2 (small radiation heat loss) are applied,
so that 41% CO2-diluted or 56% N2-diluted flames at
φ = 0.7 can have the same value of SL = 0.1 m/s [17]
and nearly the same value of adiabatic flame temper-
ature (Tad ≈ 1776 K) for comparison. The reader is
directed to Ref. [17] for detailed treatment of differ-
ent percentages of N2 and CO2 dilution gases, which
were used to alter the degree of radiation heat losses
from small to large. Thus, the effect of radiation heat
losses on the bending effect of ST/SL curves can be
studied and compared with that measured for the ef-
fect of hydrogen additions. Note that we have made an
effort to hold the mixtures’ Tad fixed while adding hy-
drogen. There are a total of 15 flame groups studied,
each having a wide range of u′ for ST measurements.
Values of Tad for each flame group are calculated us-
ing the premix code of Chemkin and C1 mechanism
proposed by Dryer [29], as shown in Table 1. Table 1
lists compositions and properties of all mixtures used
in the study, so that the reader should be able to judge
the credibility of the results measured by adding H2,
CO2 or N2.

2.3. Pressure and emission measurements
In any closed vessels with constant volume, turbu-
lent combustion can result in a pressure rise, and it
is essential to evaluate the influence of the pressure
rise on the value of ST. To modulate such pressure
rises during turbulent combustion in the cruciform
burner, as many as 10 pressure-released valves are
used. There are four valves on the top of the burner
(see Fig. 1), two on the sides of the horizontal ves-
sel, and an additional four valves on both the front
and back windows of the central uniform region (see
Fig. 2). Here the original front and back quartz win-
dows are replaced by two stainless steel plates, each
with two valves for pressure measurements. There-
fore, a series of simultaneous measurements using
the pressure transducer and pairs of ion-probe sen-
sors are performed to investigate the effect of pressure
rise on ST. Three different cases, with 4, 6, and 10
valves opened, are independently studied. Data for
one complete set of lean mixtures at φ = 0.7 with
δ = 10% over a wide range of fan frequencies up to
120 Hz, corresponding to a turbulent Reynolds num-
ber ReT = u′LI/ν ≈ 16,300, are presented with re-
sults from other equivalence ratios used where appro-
priate to augment the conclusions.
We found that it is very difficult to measure the
local concentration of NOx and/or CO for these tran-
sient turbulent flames inside the cruciform burner.
The best we can do is to measure an overall con-
centration of NOx and/or CO of burned gases that
are accumulated from ignition to the end of turbulent
combustion. After many tests, we close all pressure-
released valves to avoid additional air dilution from
outside and use two 10-liter sampling bags to accom-
modate thermal expansion gases, as schematically
shown in Fig. 2. These accumulated burned gases in-
side the burner with or without hydrogen additions
at various values of turbulent intensities are sampled
to measure overall (accumulated) NOx and CO con-
centrations using gas chromatography (GC) and/or a
NDIR (nondispersive infrared) gas analyzer to study
the effect of hydrogen addition on emissions. How-
ever, these measured [NOx ] and [CO] data need to
be viewed with caution because of complicated wall,
transient, and accumulated effects during turbulent
combustion. This paper only points out that the quali-
tative trends of [NOx ] and/or [CO] emissions increase
and/or decrease with the percentages of hydrogen ad-
dition at any fixed values of u′/SL without giving
quantitative data.

4. Conclusions
This work reports the bending effect of ST/SL
plots, using H2 and CO2 or N2 as additives to alter the
degree of bending, over a wide range of u′/SL for lean
premixed turbulent methane combustion at three dif-
ferent equivalence ratios, φ = 0.6, 0.7, and 0.8. These
measurements reveal the following.
(1) The accuracy of the ST measurements with hy-
drogen additions has been proved using pairs of
ion-probe sensors and pressure transducers si-
multaneously, for which gas velocities in the cru-
ciform burner are negligible and the pressure rise
due to turbulent burning has little influence on
ST/SL over a wide range of u′/SL up to 40.
(2) Results at φ = 0.7 with various hydrogen ad-
ditions show that values of the ST/SL slope
can vary gradually from n = 1.05–1.46 > 1 for
u′/SL < 6 to n = 0.58–0.61 < 1 for 6 < u′/SL <
20 to very close to zero (n ∼ 0) or even drop to
be slightly negative (n < 0) for u′/SL > 20, re-
vealing the bending effect even when hydrogen
is added.
(3) The bending of ST/SL vs u′/SL plots is di-
minishing with increasing hydrogen addition,
whereas the bending is strongly promoted when
the additive is replaced by CO2, with large radia-
tive heat losses. Moreover, when N2- and CO2-
diluted flames are compared at φ = 0.7 and/or
φ = 1.4 under the same SL, u′, and Tad, it is
found that the bending effect is much stronger
in rich flames than in lean flames because rich
flames are very sensitive to the effect of radiation
losses due to the fast depletion of H radicals, re-
sulting in much smaller values of ST/SL in CO2
flames than in N2 flames at any given values of
u′/SL.
(4) These measured ST/SL data for u′/SL up to
40 with various hydrogen additions at φ = 0.6,
0.7, and 0.8 can be well approximated by a
general correlation, (ST − SL)/u′ = 0.17Da0.43,
covering both corrugated flamelet and distributed
regimes, with little data scattering. A modified
distribution reaction zone model is described,
which agrees well with the experimental data
over a range of the Damköhler number from
about 10 to 100.

It has long been anticipated that there are different modes of turbulent com-
bustion which may influence all key features of premixed turbulent flames (Williams,
1985; Bradley, 1992; Ronney, 1995; Peters, 2000). Traditionally, there are two
major regimes, ‘‘turbulent-flamelet’’ and ‘‘turbulent-distributed’’ regimes for high
Reynolds number turbulent flows in the so-called phase diagram or Borghi diagram
(Williams, 1985), which are separated by a Ka ¼ 1 line where Ka is the turbulent
Karlovitz number commonly defined as Ka ¼ ðu0=SLÞ2ðReTÞ0:5.
Here the turbulent Reynolds number ReT ¼ u0LI=v and u0, LI, n, and SL are the
characteristic turbulent intensity, the integral length scale of turbulence, the kine-
matic viscosity, and the laminar burning velocity, respectively. This traditional
criterion, namely Ka > 1 for the transition of combustion modes from flamelet
to distributed, has recently been modified by Peters (2000) based on a scaling
description of the size of the reaction zone. He argued that flame broadening by
turbulence in the ‘‘thin reaction zone’’ regime occurs only in the preheated zone with-
out much influencing the reaction rate and this thin reaction zone regime before
transforming into the ‘‘broken reaction zone’’ regime could sustain for Ka up to 100.
How to prove experimentally the existence of the distributed-combustion
regime or the thin=broken reaction zones and how to find the correct criterion for
the transition are important and still remain open issues for turbulent combustion.
This motivates the present work that aims to investigate experimentally different
combustion modes based on measurements of ignition energies and observations of
spark-ignition, flame kernel development and its subsequent flame propagation in
a large fan-stirred cruciform burner. Since the cruciform burner can be used to
provide a very intense near-isotropic turbulent environment with very large values
of u0 up to 8m=s while having negligible mean velocities, we are able to study flame-
turbulence interactions over a very wide range of u0=SL from 0 to 100 with a very wide
range of LI=d from about 40 to 1300, where d is the laminar flame thickness.
Thus, centrally-ignited sparks, flame kernels, and subsequent outwardly-
propagating flames in different combustion regimes, including such as laminar,
turbulent-flamelet, and turbulent-distributed regimes, can be measured and com-
pared. Particular interest is placed on a transition of minimum ignition energy
(MIE) due to different combustion modes depending on both Ka and the equivalence
ratio (/). Here MIE is commonly defined as the ignition energy of 50% ignitability
in a given reactive mixture (e.g., Maly and Vogel, 1979; Lewis and von Elbe, 1987;
Ishii et al., 1992; Huang et al., 2007).
It is known that MIE is an extremely important property for safety standards
as well as for the fundamental understanding of the ignition process of combustible
mixtures (Loeb, 1939; Lewis and von Elbe, 1987; Fisher, 2000). In addition, accurate
MIE data are essential for optimization of ignition systems especially when lean pre-
mixed turbulent combustion is considered (Maly and Vogel, 1979; Ishii et al., 1992).
Recently presented at the 31st Combustion Symposium, Huang et al. (2007) showed
that measurements of MIE and observations of the flame kernel formation and its
subsequent flame propagation in intense isotropic turbulence without mean veloci-
ties can provide an excellent opportunity to scrutinize different combustion modes
in the phase diagram of premixed turbulent combustion.

between 100% and 0% ignitability is first captured and then 
continuously narrowed down to identify the ignition energy of 
50% ignitability, Eig(50%) ( ≡  MIE). In this study, each data 
point of Eig(50%) for a given mixture at a fixed flow condition is 
determined by more than 30 repeated runs to ensure the 
accuracy of statistics.

3. Turbulent Burning Velocities 
Turbulent burning velocities, which are extremely difficult to 
obtain accurately because thermal expansion and heat losses at 
the flame front may induce global as well as local changes of 
the turbulent flow field. Consequently, large scattering data of 
ST/SL are commonly observed even at a fixed u′/SL in a given 
flow configuration [1,19-21]. To reduce some difficulties, 
several researchers attempted to create high-intensity, near-
isotropic turbulent flow fields (simplest turbulence with 
unique characteristics) for observing turbulent flame 
propagation at large values of u′/SL. These experiments at least 
included the constant-volume fan-stirred explosion vessel 
flames [19], the ring-slot jet stabilized flames with weak-
swirling [21], and the turbulent Taylor-Couette flames [20]. 
Unfortunately, these flows still suffer various degrees of 
unsteadiness, inhomogeneity, or having significant mean flow 
velocity or mean strain, as discussed in [1,3]. 
Three different methods for measurements of ST, not shown in 
this paper but will be presented in the speech, include (1) near-
spherical turbulent flame propagation from point ignition (see 
Fig. 3b) using high-speed image processing system, (2) 
stabilized turbulent flame with weak swirling using high-speed 
particle imaging velocimetry, and turbulent flames 
propagating downwardly into 3-D fully developed 
homogeneous turbulence using pairs of ion-probe sensors. 
Comparisons among these three different means will be 
discussed in the presentation, revealing that the ion-probe 
method has the least uncertainty in measuring ST. Using pairs 
of ion-probe sensors in the central uniform region of the large 
fan-stirred cruciform burner, complete data sets of ST/SL as a 
function of u′/SL for both CH4/air and C3H8/air mixtures at 
various equivalence ratios (φ = 0.7~1.4 and 0.65~1.6) were 
obtained and reported [3]. Unlike previous turbulent ignition 
experiments, the ignition of given mixtures was initiated from 
the top of the long vertical vessel to generate a sizable 
premixed flame propagating downwardly through the central 
near-isotropic turbulence region without influences by the 
ignition source and the unwanted turbulence from walls.

 5. Conclusions 
We have reviewed dynamics of turbulent premixed flames 
from ignition to flame propagation and to flame global 
quenching by turbulence. All presented experimental results 
are obtained from the cruciform burner, which can provide 
homogeneous turbulence with large turbulent intensities 
greater than hitherto measured. These measurements, starting 
from turbulent ignition to turbulent burning velocities and then 
to global quenching, reveal the following. 
1. (a) Before the transition, values of MIE only increase 
gradually with u′/SL at which MIE ~ (u′/SL)0.3. Across the 
transition when u′/SL > 25 and/or Ka > 7, values of MIE 
increase abruptly at which MIE ~ (u′/SL)7. (b) There is a 
similar transition on MIE existing for both φ = 0.6 and 
0.7 due to different modes of combustion. These 
transitions occur at Ka ~ 10 which is much greater than 
the Klimov-Williams criterion (Ka = 1). (c) Values of 
MIE should be relevant to the size of the reaction zone at 
least in the beginning of combustion for which MIE ~ δ3. 
This is why a drastic increase of MIE is found in 
distributed-like or broken reaction zone regimes. Thus, 
the present finding may provide the experimental 
evidence for the existence of both thin and broken 
reaction zones regimes proposed by Peters [10]. 
2. A general correlation of the form, (ST-SL)/u′ = 0.05 Da0.61, 
is proposed, which is better than previous correlations 
covering both distributed (small Da) and corrugated 
flamelet (large Da) regimes. 
3. (a) For pure CH4/air flames, the critical value of Ka for 
global quenching of rich/lean CH4 flames must be greater 
than 9/39. Lean CH4 flames are much harder to quench 
globally than rich CH4 flames, indicating an influence of 
Le. (b) Values of Ka are very sensitive to φ, because Ka 
increases largely as φ gradually approaches towards φ =1 
from either lean or rich sides, with the maximum Ka 
occurring possibly near φ =1. 
4. Radiation heat losses can influence turbulent burning 
velocities and global quenching of premixed flames but 
in an opposite manner. Namely, RHL has much stronger 
influence on ST for rich flames than lean flames, but the 
reduction of Kac due to RHL is more profound for lean 
flames than rich flames. 
These results are important to the understanding of dynamics 
of turbulent premixed combustion.

5 Conclusions 
This paper presents a portable modified SRB using a newly-
designed flame-holder near the central combustion region, 
such that the flashback problem frequently occurred in the 
SRB when continuously operated at fixed φ due to heat 
recirculation can be significantly improved. Thus, a long time 
operation of the modified SRB for many hours can be 
achieved, as confirmed by both temperature measurements at 
various positions in the modified SRB and numerical results. 
This new improvement is a useful contribution for the 
practical use of heat-recirculating burners. Finally, the present 
portable TE power generator using the modified SRB and lean 
propane/air fuels can easily and stably provide a power density 
of 240 mW/cm2 with very low [NOx] < 5 ppm for many hours.

A typical flow pathway of interconnects in a planar SOFC includes three parts, 
starting from the feed header to multi-flow channels usually divided by many rectangular 
ribs to the exhaust header. The optimal design of interconnects for increasing the cell 
performance requires uniform distribution of fuel and large electrode-interconnect 
contact area on the anode side as well as uniform air flow at higher speed on the cathode 
side to remove the heat during a long-term operation. Two commonly-used flow 
distributors of interconnects deserve to comment, as presented in Figs. 1a and 1b, a 
module proposed by Yakabe et al. (3) with a single-inlet/single-outlet design having 
thirteen parallel rectangular channels in the middle and a module adopted by de Haart et 
al. (4) with a double-inlet/single-outlet design with similar parallel rectangular flow 
channels. These two flow distributors have been applied in many numerical simulations 
of the cell performance relative to different flow arrangements (e.g., co-flow, counter-
flow and cross-flow at an angle of 90o). Under the assumption of uniform velocity 
distributions in interconnects, Lin et al. (5) studied analytically the effect of rectangular 
ribs on the concentration polarization and Li et al. (6) investigated numerically the shape 
of cylindrical ribs in attempt to increase the reactant contact area with the 
electrode/electrolyte surface. Quite a few researchers have also made an attempt to study 
the complex thermo-fluid electrochemical transport phenomena in planar SOFC with 
various fuel cell boundary and interfacial conditions, such as the combined thermal 
boundary conditions on solid walls, mass transfer associated with the electrochemical 
reaction and gas permeation across the interface, see Refs. (2,3,5-7) among others. 
Furthermore, Teller et al. (8) proposed the use of the Nickel mesh (Ni-mesh) as the 
contact and fuel distributor for the anode to further improve the cell performance, as 
shown on Fig. 1c. Note that all of these aforementioned studies on flow uniformity of a 
series of parallel rectangular flow channels and/or Ni-mesh in interconnects for planar 
SOFC were either numerical simulations or mathematical models. Yet no experimental 
data are available to test the uniformity of the flow in these different designs of 
interconnects. Therefore, there is a need to devise an idealized experimental platform, 
allowing direct imaging and velocity measurements for the flow in these rib-channels 
and/or the Ni-mesh of interconnects that can be used to test the accuracy of numerical 
flow data. This is the first objective of the present work.

Concluding Remarks 
(1) The present hydraulic platform is a useful tool for improving the flow uniformity in 
interconnects.
(2) The effect of Re plays an important role on flow uniformity in interconnects as well 
as on temperature gradients for both electrode surfaces. 
(3) A new design using small guide vanes equally-spaced around the feeder of the 
double-inlet module which features a very high degree of flow uniformity is proposed. 
(4) The use of Ni-mesh at anode can improve significantly flow uniformity, making 
temperature distributions uniformly and thus minimizing thermal stresses.

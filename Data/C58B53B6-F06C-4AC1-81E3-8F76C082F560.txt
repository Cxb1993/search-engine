Abstract 
In the present work, the parameters controlling the Alumina-W nanopowders 
Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) process are studied. The important and crucial role of C 
diffusion from the SPS dye in the microstructure, fraction of porosity and new phases 
formation (W2C) is discussed. It is also pointed out the importance of the processing 
parameters of the starting powder on the density and mechanical properties of the final 
compacts. In this regard, dense Alumina-nW compacts containing 4 vol.% W with a 
hardness value as high as 24.6  ± 0.9 GPa were obtained.

2. Nano-composites Processing and Characterization 
 2.1. Powder processing  
 2.1.1. Starting materials 
The following commercially available powders were used: (1) WCl6 (Tungsten 
(VI) chloride 99.9 %, Aldrich, Germany) and (2) α-alumina powder (99.99 %,  Taimei 
Chemical Co., Ltd., Japan) with an average particle size of d50 = 300 ± 50 nm.  
 
 2.1.2. Synthesis of nano-powders 
Before the deposition of tungsten nano-particles onto the alumina grains, the 
alumina powder was:  
(1) Not processed labelled as “as received” below 
(2) Calcined at 750 ºC for 2 h in order to remove any organic additive present in the 
commercial powder and subsequently passed through a 63 μm sieve, and  
(3) Calcined at 750 ºC for 2 hours, sieved down to 63 μm and attrition milled during 30 
minutes in alcoholic media (anhydrous 99.97% ethanol) in order to break aggregates.  
Powder mixtures with different metallic contents (1 – 10 vol. %) were prepared. 
Alumina powders, pretreated in one of the two ways described above or “as received”, 
were weighed and dispersed in anhydrous 99.97 % ethanol and a solution of tungsten 
(VI) chloride (WCl6) in ethanol (with the corresponding concentration needed for 
obtaining the desired final metallic content) was added drop wise4. When alumina was 
calcined first and then attrition milled in ethanolic media, the powder was not dried but 
was directly composed with the corresponding WCl6 solution. The slurries were first

ºC higher than the temperature needed for obtaining dense compacts when a pressure of 
100 MPa was applied. 
 
 2.3. Compacts characterization 
 2.3.1. Microstructural characterization 
The microstructural features of the obtained nano-sized powder as well as the 
SPSed samples were studied in a transmission electron microscopy TEM (Jeol 
microscope, model FXII, JEM 2000, operating at 200 kV). For high resolution 
transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM), a Jeol JEM-200CX microscope using a 
200 keV accelerating voltage was employed. Reflected light optical microscopy was 
performed with a Leica DMRM microscope. 
Fracture surfaces for the different compacts were studied in a SEM model DSM 
960, Zeiss Thornwood, NY. 
 
 2.3.2. X-Ray Characterization 
X-Ray diffraction analysis (XRD) of the starting and processed powders and the 
final compacts obtained was performed using Cu Kα radiation (Bruker D8 
diffractometer). In the final compacts case, cross-sections or the external dense surfaces 
were analyzed by this technique.

The Vickers hardness, HV, of the SPSed samples was determined by 
microindentation (Buehler model Micromet 5103) on sample surfaces polished down to 
1 micron, applying a 1.96 N load with an indentation time of 10 s. For each sample, 30 
 A more extended microstrusture study was performed using the SEM. SEM 
micrographs of polished surfaces of the 2 and 4 vol.% W compositions prepared 
according route (2) are shown in Fig. 2 . Peculiar microstructures were observed 
containing large alumina aggregates (<63 μm) surrounded by W nano- and sub-micron 
sized particles attached to smaller alumina grains.  
Fracture surfaces of compacts prepared according to routes (1) to (3) were also 
studied in the SEM. Fracture surfaces of the dense composites are shown in Fig. 3. It 
could be determined by EDS- analysis that tungsten was present, in a less significant 
amount, inside the alumina aggregates (Fig. 4). A more extended study of these large 
aggregates by TEM, showed that very small W nano-particles (d50 = 16 nm) were 
mostly located at triple points but also at alumina grain boundaries providing a pinning 
effect and constraining alumina grains sizes to <300 nm, as shown in Fig. 5. The W 
concentration inside the aggregates was 50% the theoretical one. The presence of W 
nano-particles inside the aggregates is not so unexpected, due to the preparation 
procedure of tungsten nano-particles. When the alumina slurry is prepared, the 
aggregates present certain porosity allowing penetration of WCl6 solution.  
In the case of composites processed according to route (1), the outer dense parts 
of the specimens did not present such aggregates, tungsten was well dispersed yielding 
homogeneous composites (Fig. 6(a)). On the other hand, in the porous inner parts of the 
specimens, abnormal alumina grain growth, also known as “dynamic ripening” was also 
observed as shown in Fig. 6(b). This micrograph shows how alumina has grown in an 
exaggerated way embedding tungsten nano-particles during the sintering process. Thus 
well-faceted large alumina grains of sizes between 2 and 10 μm are present. On the 
contrary, in between these large grains, alumina grains of sizes between 300 nm and 1 
μm are present. 
 Fracture surfaces of this composition and of monolithic alumina are shown in Fig. 8. 
Intergranular fracture features are mainly observed. 
   
 3.2. Compositional characterization 
All SPSed compacts were X-rayed. The presence of a tungsten carbide, W2C, 
was always noticed, independently of the processing route used or SPS furnace 
employed. The X-ray patterns of selected compacts are given in Fig. 9. 
 As the sintering of the compact is taking place in a graphite dye, it is reasonable 
to think that carbon can diffuse into the sample from the dye and this process is 
promoted by the applied pressure. The formation of tungsten carbide, W2C, is 
thermodynamically feasible in the temperatures range where the sintering takes place. 
ΔG value varies between -21.736 kJ/mol at room temperature to -34.694 kJ/mol at 1350 
ºC. By quantitative XRD analysis, the fraction of W2C formed ranged from 15 – 35 
vol.%. 
 The presence of W2C does not favour the densification of the composites. Its 
density, 17.14 g/cm3 is lower than the one for W, 19.25 g/cm3, so a volume expansion is 
going to be induced inside the material during its formation. The molar volume change 
produced is around 16 vol.%. This expansive magnitude is quite large and, to a certain 
extent, can counteract the uni-axial load applied during SPS process, yielding a non 
homogeneous material densification. On the other hand, W2C is a very rigid compound 
and, in this sense, it could also hinder grain boundary sliding during sintering.  
 
 3.3. Densification and powders packing 
 densifications are inhomogeneous, as seen in Fig.6., since plastic phase is replaced by a 
rigid one as carbon diffusion front propagates hindering densification.  
Sintering kinetics is not perfectly understood due to the amount of parameters 
involved in the process and, moreover, carbon diffusion that in this particular case takes 
part as a reactant, is an external and uncontrollable process.  
 
 3.4. Mechanical properties 
Hardness and Toughness have been evaluated. Due to the observed porosity in 
all the specimens processed by routes (1) and (3), toughness could only be determined 
for the fully dense samples prepared according route (2).  
 
 3.4.1. Vickers Hardness 
The samples prepared at 1350 and 1400 ºC exhibit a similar trend in the plot of 
HV versus vol.% W as seen in Fig. 10. The SPSed samples prepared at 
1350ºC/3min/100 MPa were the one with highest HV-value. This fact is supported by 
the observation that the mean grain size for this composite (2.5 ± 1 μm) is smaller than 
for the one prepared at 1400ºC/3min/60MPa (d50 ≈ 5.7 ± 1 μm).  
The observed trend in Fig. 10 is similar to the one obtained in the case of 
alumina-nNi11. An increase of the HV-value with the nW content is observed. Even 
considering that the theoretical model proposed by Pecharromán et al.11 can not be 
applied in this particular case, the obtained value for the apparent aggregation threshold 
fci ≈ 4 vol. % is not far away from the one expected if the nW particles would be 
homogeneously distributed (2.6 vol.%) as in the case of Al2O3-nNi with a similar matrix 
grain size (~ 0.3 μm). We have to take into account that in the alumina-nW (4vol.%) a 
fraction of metal is clearly agglomerated and other fraction is presented as W2C.

數一數二的汽車零件供應商, 他介紹了multilayer acuator, oxygen sensor, ceramic filter
三項產品的開發,他指出藉由與學術界的合作, 是BOSCH產品開發成功最主要的關
鍵, 這個事實很值得國內廠商參考.  
第三位為美國GE公司Global 研究中心的主管K.L. Luthra博士演講, GE公司為全
世界最大的發動機供應商, 他以SiC/SiC複合材料在發動機的發展為題, 他指出一
個新的零件, 須經十年以上的評估才能進入市場, 但只有藉由SiC/SiC複合材料的
使用, 發動機的操作溫度才能超過1200C, 他也指出有SiC/SiC複合材料的發動機, 
即將在一兩年內上市. 目前已有許SiC/SiC複合材料的通過實驗室評估, 即將進入
實機測試.

預計每兩年舉辦一次.  
 
本屆國際會議由武漢理工大學承辦, 武漢理工大學投入大量人力及資源, 全
力辦好此國際會議. 會議選在武漢市郊之武漢湖濱花園旅館召開, 於2008年11月
14日至11月18日舉行, 本屆計有10個國家共150人參加, 以第一屆而言, 參加人數
及規模已相當不錯.  
 
二. 參加會議心得 
 
本屆國際會議共邀請四位Keynote講者, 第一位為美國Harvard大學藝術中心主
任 Nancy C. Selvage教授, 他以”The global impact of historical Chinese porcelain technology 
and trade”為題, 介紹中國陶瓷技術對世界科技及商業交流之影響, 她指出中國
陶瓷技術在經由馬可波羅帶回義大利之後, 世界各地才陸續發生陶瓷技藝, 但各
地出土的陶瓷藝品, 最後皆因地方, 依原料不同而有些許差異, 但陶瓷技術是源
起於中國, 則是毋庸置疑的.  
 第二位為美國National Academies’s Board的Stephen A. Merrill, 他以 “Asia’s 
role in the era of global science” 為題, 介紹美國對亞洲科技進展的觀點, 此位先生
為美國華盛頓的政治人物, 他以平淡的聲音說了近一個小時, 似乎只證明一件事
情, 政治人物都自以為自己高高在上, 並不屑於因聽眾的組成不同而做演講內容
的調整. 他的演講是筆者所聽過最糟的演講.  
 第三位為中國科學院上海硅酸鹽研究所的江東亮院士, 他以 “Hot points on 
advanced ceramics” 為題, 介紹陶瓷科技的最新進展, 他的演講內容涵蓋結構陶
瓷及功能陶瓷, 他並指出具多功能之陶瓷複合材料是個值得投入資源的重要研
究方向.  
 第四位為澳洲Monash University的Yibing Cheng博士, 他以 “Applications of 
nanomaterials and nanotechnology in dye sensitized solar cells” 為題, 介紹奈米陶瓷科技
對染料電池發展的影響, 他們以sol-gel技術製作奈米TiO2 電極, 得到不錯的研究
成果.

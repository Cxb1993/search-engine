Abstract 
The characterization of nano-sized TiO2 prepared by a sol-gel method was characterized in 
this study. The synthesized photocatalysts were used for the photodegradation of 
1,2-dichloroethane (1,2-DCE). From TG/DTA data, A broad exothermic peak at around 460-520 
°C and extending to 570 °C is attributed to the phase transformation of amorphous to anatase 
phase. Further, Therefore, a broad exothermic peak at around 590-800 °C is attributed to the 
phase transformation from anatase to rutile phase. From X-ray powder diffraction data, the 
crystal phase presents a mixture of anatase and rutile with anatase the dominant phase. As seen in 
TEM images, the crystallites of photocatalysts are spherical particles with a crystallite size about 
10-20 nm. From 1,2-DCE photocatalytic degradation, The photocatalytic activity of TiO2 tested 
at RT = 120 s is the highest (conversion, η = 98%), followed by that tested at 60 s (η = 79%) > 
Degussa P25 at 30 s (η = 59%) > TiO2 at 30 s (η = 49%) at the end of experiments. It indicates 
that the conversion is a function of RT within the range from 30 s to 2 min. The conversion of 
1,2-DCE photodegradation decreases with increases in water vapor. It follows the sequence of 
relative humidity = 10% (η = 97%) > 30% (η = 50%) > 50% (η = 32%) > 70% (η = 26%) at the 
end of experiments. The water will be produced in the process of photocatalytic degradations of 
the chlorinated organic compounds, and competitive adsorption on the active site of TiO2 
between water vapor and 1,2-DCE will happen in all cases. The byproducts of 1,2-DCE 
photodegradation include H2O, CO, CO2, C2H5Cl, CH2Cl2, and HCl. 
Keywords: Photocatalyst; Photodegradation; 1,2-dichloroethane, TiO2; sol-gel method

In this study, the characterization of TiO2 prepared by a sol-gel method was performed. 
Further, a continuous-flow reactor was used to evaluate parameters affecting photocatalytic 
activity including retention time (RT) and water vapor. In addition to the traditional 
characterization instruments, such as X-ray powder diffraction (XRD), fourier transform infrared 
spectroscopy (FTIR), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and BET Surface area, other 
instruments such as thermogravimetric/differential thermal analysis (TG/DTA), and transmission 
electron microscopy (TEM) were employed to characterize modified photocatalysts. Lastly, the 
byproducts of 1,2-DCE photodegradation are qualitatively identified via FTIR. 
2. Materials and methods 
2.1. Chemicals. 
All chemicals used in laboratory were analytical grade, and the titanium isopropoxide (TPIP, 
> 97%), nitrite acid (> 65%), and iron nitrate nonahydrate (> 99%) were purchased from Aldrich, 
Merck, and Riedel-de Haën, respectively. 
2.2. Photocatalysts preparation 
In the preparation process of Ti-precursor sol, 30 mL TPIP was mixed with 15 mL absolute 
ethanol. Then the mixture was slowly added into a de-ionized water-ethanol solution 
(water:absolute ethanol = 12:1 v/v). After stirring vigorously for 1 min, 1.2 mL HNO3 was 
slowly added into the mixture. The final solution was stirred vigorously for 24 h until the 
Ti-precursor sol formed. The Ti-precursor sol was then dehydrated and the alcohol removed in a 
rotary vacuum evaporator (EYELA, N-1000S) in a 75 °C water batch. The samples were dried 
for 24 h at 120 °C in an oven.  
2.3. Photocatalysts Characterization 
Simultaneous TG/DTA data were recorded using a TG/DTA instrument (Pyres Diamond 
TG/DTA, Perkin Elmer). The temperature ranged from room temperature to 1100 °C in order to 
obtain crystallization and phase-transformation data. All analyses were performed in a flowing air 
atmosphere with the heating rate of 10 °C min-1. The crystalline structures of photocatalysts were 
determined by XRD (D/MAX III-V, RIGAKU) using CuKα radiation with a nickel filter. The 
applied current and voltage were 30 mA and 40 kV, respectively. The scan ranged from 10 to 80° 
with a scan rate of 6° min-1. All peak data measured by XRD analysis were assigned to known 
crystalline phases by comparing with those of JCPDS database. The surface area was measured 
with a Micromeritics ASAP 2010 instrument using adsorption of nitrogen at 77 K. Prior to 
adsorption measurements, the samples were degassed under a vacuum of  7×10-1 MPa at 110 
°C for 2 h. The surface area was calculated by the BET equation. The surface morphology was 
observed with a SEM instrument (S3000-N, HITACHI). In order to prevent the charge build-up 
during SEM observations, samples were coated with gold. Further, TEM images of nano-scale 
samples were obtained on a TEM instrument (CM-200 TWIN, PHILIPS).

3.2. X-ray diffraction patterns of TiO2 
Figure 3 shows the XRD patterns of TiO2 calcined at 250-750 °C for 3 h in air atmosphere. 
The XRD pattern at low calcination temperature (< 400 °C) shows broad peaks of anatase, as the 
dominant phase, and brookite phase with small crystalline size. The presence of broad peaks at 
low calcination temperature is ascribed to the amorphous nature of the gel and the small crystal 
size anatase (Pal et al., 1999). Further, the XRD patterns of the gel calcined at 500 and 600 °C 
show that the crystal phase presents the mixture of anatase, as the dominant phase at 500 °C, and 
rutile, as the dominant phase at 600 °C. However, the XRD pattern of the gel calcined at 750 °C 
shows narrow and sharp peaks of rutile phase with the disappearance of the anatase phase. These 
results are supported by the observations from our TG/DTA analysis. 
3.3. Surface morphologies of TiO2 
The surface morphologies of TiO2 film is shown in Fig. 4. It is clear that the surface of the 
film has dense-packed flat pieces composed of the aggregation of nano-particle TiO2. Figure 5 
shows the TEM images of TiO2, separate nano particles of photocatalysts can be seen (ca. 10-20 
nm). Unfortunately, the particles were also found to aggregate during either synthesized process 
or pre-treatment of TEM samples. 
3.4. Photocatalytic activities of TiO2 
The effects of RT on 1,2-DCE photodegradation are shown in Fig. 6. The commercial 
photocatalyst (Degussa P25) was also used in the experiment to compare with synthesized 
photocatalysts. When the UV lamp was turned on (t = 0), the production rate of photo-induced 
electrons and holes was suddenly excited, resulting in immediate 1,2-DCE removal, although 
reaction rate tended to stabilize or decrease later on. The photocatalytic activity of TiO2 tested at 
RT = 120 s is the highest (conversion, η = 98%), followed by that tested at 60 s (η = 79%) > 
Degussa P25 at 30 s (η = 59%) > TiO2 at 30 s (η = 49%) at the end of experiments. It indicates 
that the conversion is a function of RT within the range from 30 s to 2 min. Since the 
photocatalytic reaction occurs on the surface of TiO2, surface area may play another important 
role in the photocatalytic reaction. The surface area difference between TiO2 and P25 was

Research and development of photocatalytic technologies  
applied to indoor VOCs removal 
 
Hsin Chu, Wen-Chi Hung 
 
Department of Environmental Engineering  
National Cheng Kung University 
1 University Rd., Tainan 70101, Taiwan 
E-mail address: chuhsin@mail.ncku.edu.tw 
Abstract 
Total volatile organic compound (TVOC) is one of indoor air suggesting 
regulated pollutants announced by Taiwan EPA in 2005. The exposure of human body 
to VOCs may cause irritation, poisoning, tumor or even cancer to the various human 
organs. TiO2 is widely used for environmental applications because of its physical and 
chemical stability, lower cost, non-toxicity, resistance to corrosion, and operating 
capability in room temperatures. A continued-flow reactor was used to evaluate various 
parameters including retention time and relative humidity which would affect the 
photocatalytic activity. From an example of 1,2-dichloroethane photocatalyitc 
degradation, the retention time and the presence of water vapor influence the 
photocatalytic activity quite much. The intermediates and prodcuts of 1,2-
dichloroethane photodegradation include C2H5Cl, CH2Cl2, HCl, Cl2, CO, CO2 and H2O. 
 
Keywords: Indoor air pollution, VOCs, Photocatalytic degradation

2. EXPERIMENTAL PREPARATION 
There were four groups of cement mortar specimens being studied in this paper, including plain cement – 
rectangular (PCR), 20% weight of fly ash – rectangular (FAR), plain cement – cubic (PCC), and 20% weight 
of fly ash – cubic (FAC). CNS 61 Type I Portland cement, C190 standard sand, and F grade fly ash were 
selected and used in the preparation of specimens, respectively. Specimens of the dimension (height by width 
by length) 20x20x80mm and 50x50x50mm were prepared and cured in sealed bag at room temperature for 1, 
7, 14, 21, 28 days before tests. The mixing proportions of all groups are summarized in Table 1. 
Conductive silver pastes provided by AG PRO were employed for the electrode instrumentations. Copper 
tapes were then applied onto the electrodes so as to ensure the measuring tips can be firmly attached. Keithley 
2611A Single-channel System Source Meter Instrument was used for the constant electric field excitation, 
resistance measurement, and polarization response recording. In this paper, the commonly used two-point 
probe methods was adopted with the current input and voltage output carried at the same electrode pairs. For a 
constantly applied voltage, the resulting electric field of the cubic specimens would be 1.6 times 
(80mm/50mm), slightly larger than the rectangular specimens, while the capacitances is of 10 times 
(50x50/50 over 20x20/80) difference – cubics are significantly larger. This suggested that polarization of 
cubic specimens would require longer durations to attain the steady state of poling saturation. 
 
3. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS 
Figure 1 shows the resistivity growth of mortar specimens measured under low and high electric fields. In 
this study, high electric field is 2.0 kV/m for rectangular specimens and 3.2 kV/m for cubic specimens, while 
low electric fields are set as 125 V/m and 200 V/m, respectively. It can be observed that the geometric 
differences of the mortar specimens seem to have no significance to the electrical resistivity as compared to 
the influences of electric fields and compositions. The discrepancy caused by specimen geometries became 
more prominent after 7 days of curing. This might be attributed to the consequences of early hydration due to 
the geometric dissimilarity that has resulted in the changes of initial porous distributions, while it requires 
further validation and qualification works. Nevertheless, magnitudes of electric excitation used to acquire the 
resistance of mortar specimens do have certain influences to the material resistivity. Figure 1 indicates higher 
electric fields would result in lower resistivity measurement of cement mortars. In this study, the drops are 
about 14% for PCR, 7% for FAR, 22% for PCC, and 13% for FAC, respectively. While this observation can 
be traced back to the fact that higher electric excitation agitates more to the free ions; specimen geometry has 
again shown to be a factor of this phenomenon. 
 The poling results of cement mortars are illustrated in Figure 2. As seen, specimen geometries hold 
significant effects to the material polarizations. In particular, cubic specimens tend to achieve the poling 
saturation in a faster manner than rectangular ones. This observation can be attributed to the higher 
capacitance of cubic specimens, and is true for both plain cement and fly-ash added mortars. In addition, the 
poling resistivity of cubic specimens are greatly raised than rectangular ones, reflecting the fact that free ions 
per unit volume are more abundant in cubics.
